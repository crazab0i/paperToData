As whole genome sequencing can assist to differentiate and trace the origin of outbreaks of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains, the whole genomes of retrospective isolates (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;19) from previous studies were sequenced. Sequences were analysed using average nucleotide identity (ANI), pangenomics, and whole genome single nucleotide polymorphism (wgSNP) to trace the geographical origin of cases of brucellosis circulating in human, cattle, goats, and sable from different provinces in SA. Pangenomics analysis of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;69) and <italic>B. abortus</italic> (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;56) was conducted with 19 strains that included <italic>B. abortus</italic> from cattle (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> from&#xA0;a human (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1), cattle (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1), goat (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1), Rev1 vaccine strain (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1), and sable (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;12). Pangenomics analysis of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> genomes, highlighted shared genes, that include 10 hypothetical proteins and genes that encodes for acetyl-coenzyme A synthetase (<italic>acs</italic>), and acylamidase (<italic>aam</italic>) amongst the sable genomes. The wgSNP analysis confirmed the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> isolated from human was more closely related to the goat from the Western Cape Province from the same outbreak than the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> cattle sample from different cases in the Gauteng Province. The <italic>B. melitensis</italic> sable strains could be distinguished from the African lineage, constituting their own African sub-clade. The sequenced <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains clustered in the C2 lineage that is closely related to the isolates from Mozambique and Zimbabwe. This study identified genetically diverse <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. among various hosts in SA. This study expands the limited known knowledge regarding the presence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in livestock and humans in SA, further building a foundation for future research on the distribution of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. worldwide and its evolutionary background.</p><sec><title>Supplementary Information</title><p>The online version contains supplementary material     <sec id="Sec1">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p id="Par2">The genus <italic>Brucella</italic> is a highly monomorphic genus of pathogenic species affecting a wide range of hosts including humans, livestock, and wildlife in SA. Molecular typing test has impacted the differentiation of <italic>Brucella</italic> homogeneity more than biotyping consisting of 25 biochemical tests to identify <italic>Brucella</italic> species and their biovars and the latter&#x2019;s inability to differentiate atypical <italic>Brucella</italic> species (Ledwaba et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>). Furthermore, biotyping is often difficult to interpret due to limited standardization of the typing reagents (Whatmore, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">2009</xref>). Eight distinct <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovars (1&#x2013;7 and 9) have been documented, whereas <italic>B. suis</italic> has five biovars (1&#x2013;4 and 5) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> has three biovars (1&#x2013;3) (Verger et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">1985</xref>). Additionally, there is no biovar differentiation for other <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. (Matle et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">2021</xref>; Verger et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">1985</xref>). Moreover, the efficacy of biotyping is moderate. Since it includes the manipulation of the live agent, it poses a biosafety and public health risks of laboratory infections to the personnel involved (Whatmore, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">2009</xref>). Recently, more researchers&#xA0;are utilizing whole genome sequencing (WGS) to explain the relationships between different strains of <italic>Brucella</italic>, either supplementing or replacing traditional molecular typing techniques such as multilocus sequence typing (MLST) and multiple locus variable number of tandem repeats analysis (MLVA). The use of whole genome sequencing to characterize the genomes of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp., allows to determine additional traits like virulence factors, mobile genetic elements while acquiring the highest genetic resolution (Wattam et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">2014</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par3">To improve strain typing, whole-genome sequencing-based analysis that employs Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI) and whole genome single nucleotide polymorphism (wgSNP) (Abdel-Glil et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">2022</xref>; Uelze et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">2020</xref>) have been employed. The diversity of strains implicated in infections may be revealed by wgSNP analysis of isolates from various geographical locations (Anbazhagan et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">2023</xref>). Pan-genome analysis can be used to determine the genetic diversity of related genomes such as strain tracking, niche specialization and diagnostic marker identification. The fundamental components of the biology behind the main behavioural features were controlled by the genes that make up the core genome (Vernikos et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">2015</xref>). When new <italic>Brucella</italic> genomes are sequenced, pan-genome analysis, as opposed to SNP, can compute, and compare existing genomes to estimate the number of protein-coding genes and aid in the understanding the role of accessory genes such as horizontal gene transfer.</p>
      <p id="Par4">Brucellosis is a controlled disease in SA, focused primarily on mitigating the risk of bovine brucellosis caused mainly by <italic>B. abortus</italic> in cattle through a surveillance and disease control scheme and governed by relevant legislation (R.2483 of 9 Dec 1988 of the Animal Diseases Act 35 of 1984). <italic>Brucella</italic>
<italic>abortus</italic> is endemic in cattle in SA occurring in all nine provinces (De Massis et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">2019</xref>). The wgSNP analysis of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from cattle in SA with global sequences indicated these SA strains grouped closely to <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from Mozambique and Zimbabwe as well as Eurasian countries such as Portugal and India. Furthermore, the wgSNP analysis suggested that the introduction of infected animals might be the mode of brucellosis transmission between farms in SA (Ledwaba et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>). <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> normally associated with small livestock, is not subject to routine surveillance, and testing only becomes compulsory for exports or during outbreaks. <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> has been sporadically reported in sheep and goats in 1965 (Van Drimmelen, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">1965</xref>), 1989 (Ribeiro et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR02">1990</xref>), and 1994 (Reichel et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR01">1996</xref>). <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> outbreaks from 2014&#x2013;2016 occurred in humans linked to sheep and goats (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>) as well as in sables with outbreaks on the same farm from 2004 to 2015 followed by various infected sable being reported from 2013 to 2018 (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>). The latter study reported persistent <italic>B. melitensis</italic> infection in sables (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>). The investigation did not identify local livestock as a source of ongoing infection in sable but rather that the persistent infection is consistent with the disease circulating within herds (ranched wildlife) and is transmitted through the keeping and trading of animals. <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> was reported for the first time in cattle by Kolo et al., (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2019</xref>) implying that mixed farming practises results in spread between cattle, sheep and goats.</p>
      <p id="Par5">The <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains that were found to be circulating in goats, sable antelope, human samples from South African provinces, and seemingly healthy animals from abattoirs were whole genome sequenced in this study. Using publicly accessible genomes from GenBank, particularly the African genomes in the database, comparative genomics was employed in the current study to determine the taxonomic classification of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. Using genome indices like ANI, pangenomics, and wgSNP, the study examined the South African sequenced <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains to determine the genetic diversity and to trace the geographical origin of cases of brucellosis.</p>

      <title>Materials and Methods</title>
      <sec id="Sec3">
        <title>Study Area and Sample Collection</title>
        <p id="Par6"><italic>Brucella</italic> isolates and/or DNA characterised and sequenced in this study were recovered from human (blood) (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1) (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>), abattoir cattle (tissues) (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3) (Kolo et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2019</xref>), farmer&#x2019;s cattle (tissues) (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1) (Ledwaba et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>), farmer&#x2019;s goat (tissues) (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1), sable antelope (<italic>Hippotragus niger</italic>) (tissues, hygroma fluid, joint fluid, skin lesion, serum) (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;12) (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 vaccine strain used in SA (Table&#xA0;<xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>). These were collected from selected provinces of SA including Gauteng, Limpopo, North West, Eastern Cape, and Western Cape. Cattle tissue samples were collected from apparently healthy animals between the years 2016 to 2022 in Gauteng Province (Kolo et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2019</xref>). The human case of brucellosis was a 27&#xA0;year old male from Western Cape Province who appeared unwell and on examination he presented with vomiting, diarrhoea and fever (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). After a thorough medical history, it was discovered that the patient had been feeding his dog with abattoir waste from sheep, goats, and cattle dumped at an accessible municipal waste facility (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). The goat sample sequenced in this study was collected from animals that tested brucellosis seropositive during the investigation of the animal source of the human case in the Western Cape Province (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>).         <p id="Par7">Isolates for sequencing were selected based on their representation of diverse host origins (human, livestock, and wildlife) and geographical spread across SA to ensure a comprehensive analysis of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. diversity. Priority was given to retrospective isolates from previous studies with confirmed <italic>Brucella</italic> infections, aiming to enrich the study's genomic dataset with genetically varied strains.       <sec id="Sec4">
        <title>Molecular Identification of Isolates</title>
        <p id="Par8">Initially, the genomic DNA from bacterial colonies were verified using <italic>Brucella</italic> genus and species-specific conventional PCR (cPCR) based on the targeted sequences 16S&#x2009;&#x2212;&#x2009;23S ribosomal DNA interspacer region (ITS) and IS711 regions using AMOS-PCR. The genus specific ITS PCR produced a PCR product of 214&#xA0;bp using the protocol previously developed by Keid et al., (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">2007</xref>). With the multiplex AMOS-PCR assay, <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 1, 2 and 4, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> bv 1, 2 and 3, <italic>B. ovis</italic>, and <italic>B. suis</italic> bv 1 were identified and differentiated as previously reported (Bricker &amp; Halling, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1994</xref>). The four species-specific forward primers were used at a final concentration of 0.1&#xA0;&#x3BC;M with 0.2&#xA0;&#x3BC;M reverse primer IS711. PCR cycling condition consisted of an initial denaturation at 95&#xA0;&#xB0;C for 5&#xA0;min followed by 35 cycles of 95&#xA0;&#xB0;C for 1&#xA0;min, 55.5&#xA0;&#xB0;C for 2&#xA0;min, 72&#xA0;&#xB0;C for 2&#xA0;min and a final extension step at 72&#xA0;&#xB0;C for 10&#xA0;min. The PCR products were analysed using 2% agarose gel electrophoresis using <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 1 (REF 544 / BCCN R4), <italic>B. abortus</italic> S19 (Onderstepoort Biological Products) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 (Onderstepoort Biological Products) as positive controls.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec5">
        <title>Whole-Genome Sequencing and Biotyping</title>
        <p id="Par9">Following the preceding description, genomic DNA of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. isolates (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;19) were submitted for whole genome sequencing. The genome libraries for the isolates were prepared using the Nextera XT Library Prep kit (Ilumina), following the manufacturer&#x2019;s instructions, and sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq 500 platform with 150&#xA0;bp paired-end chemistry. The recommended Illumina dual index barcoding (Ilumina) protocol was employed on the generated libraries. Furthermore, read coverage ranging from 18 to 356-fold, with an average of 155-fold was generated in the NexSeq 500. The human DNA sample, sequencing was performed by means of the MiSeq Reagent Kit V3 (2&#x2009;&#xD7;&#x2009;300&#xA0;bp) on the MiSeq 2000 (Illumina).</p>
        <sec id="Sec6">
          <title>Quality Assessment and de Novo Assembly</title>
          <p id="Par10">The FastQC software version 0:10.1 (Andrews et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">2010</xref>) was used to access the quality of the sequenced reads. By applying a read&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;Q28 criterion and Trimmomatic (Bolger et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">2014</xref>), the ambiguous nucleotide reads were eliminated. Using the SPAdes v1.1.0 pipeline, the paired end trimmed reads of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains were de novo assembled (Bankevich et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">2012</xref>). Kmer sizes 21, 33, 55, 77, 99, and 127 were employed for the assembly, with a minimum contig length of 500&#xA0;bp. The probable contaminants in each assembled genome were also evaluated using CheckM (Parks et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">2015</xref>). For the comparative analysis, reference genomes were selected based on their high-quality assembly, annotation completeness, and representation of known genetic diversity within the <italic>Brucella</italic> genus. The assembled genomes were assessed using Quast v. 2.3 (Gurevich et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">2013</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="Sec7">
          <title>Multilocus Sequence Typing Analysis (MLST)</title>
          <p id="Par11">The pubMLST database of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. on the website (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://pubmlst.org/">https://pubmlst.org/</ext-link>), [accessed on 22-02-2023] was used to determine the species identity of the sequenced genomes. Identification of species and strains was performed by MLST 2.0 and PubMLST. The assignment of the new sequence types (STs) and subspecies identification were achieved by the Pasteur MLST database curators which were implemented on Pathogenwatch platform (Argim&#xF3;n et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">2021</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="Sec8">
          <title>Pangenomics and Average nucleotide Identity Analysis</title>
          <p id="Par12">Pangenome analysis was conducted using 69 <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains retrieved from publicly available genome in GenBank that included the 16 sequenced strains in this study. Selection was on the completeness of the genomes, African genomes, and some of the reference genomes i.e., <italic>B. abortus</italic> S19 and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 vaccine strains. The pangenome of the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains was conducted on 56 isolates, that included the three sequenced genomes in this study (Fig. <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">S1</xref>). All the retrieved and sequenced <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains in this study were further annotated using Prokka v.1.14.0 (Seemann, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">2014</xref>) and Prodigal 1.20 implemented on Anvi&#x2019;o v 7.1 (Eren et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">2021</xref>). Similarity searches between the coding domain sequences (CDSs) of assembled genomes were conducted using pairwise BLASTp and Markov Cluster Algorithm (MCL) (Buchfink et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">2015</xref>). The tRNAScan-SE (Chan &amp; Lowe, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">2019</xref>) was used to determine the number of RNAs. Clusters were created using paralogs of the genomes and were ordered by the presence/absence of orthologs (Page et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">2016</xref>). Pangenome clusters were defined as follows: Core-genes present in all isolates; soft core-genes present in at least 95% of isolates; shell-genes present between 15 and 95% of isolates; cloud-genes in less than 15% of isolates. Assignment of clusters of orthologous groups (COG) was achieved using DeepNOG v. 1.2.3 (Feldbauer et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">2020</xref>). Pangenomics analysis was carried out using Anvio-7.1 (Eren et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">2021</xref>). Average nucleotide identity analysis was conducted using pyANI implemented in Anvio-7.1 (Eren et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">2021</xref>). Phylogenetic trees of the ANI analysis were visualized using the iTOL v5 (Letunic &amp; Bork, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">2021</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="Sec9">
          <title>Single Nucleotide Polymorphism and Phylogenetic Analysis</title>
          <p id="Par13">The core-genome alignment generated from the pangenomics analysis was used to determine the SNP for the placement of the sequenced <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains. The SNP-sites 2.5.1 (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://github.com/sanger-pathogens/snp-sites">https://github.com/sanger-pathogens/snp-sites</ext-link>) (Page et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">2016</xref>) was used to filter the single nucleotide polymorphism on the core genome alignment. Gubbins (Croucher et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">2015</xref>) was used to identify and remove recombination within the fill alignment. The phylogenetic analysis of <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> was conducted and the phylogenetic trees were constructed based on the filtered SNPs from the alignment using the IQTREE software (Trifinopoulos et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">2016</xref>) applying the GTR&#x2009;+&#x2009;G substitution model and maximum likelihood methodology (1000 bootstraps). The generated phylogenetic trees were visualized and annotated using the iTOL v5 (Letunic &amp; Bork, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">2021</xref>).</p>
        </sec>

    <sec id="Sec10">
      <title>Results</title>
      <sec id="Sec11">
        <title>Molecular Identification of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. Isolates</title>
        <p id="Par14">A 214&#xA0;bp fragment was amplified for <italic>Brucella</italic> isolates using the ITS-PCR test, which is used to screen for <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. The AMOS-PCR was used to differentiate and confirm the isolated <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;19). Of the three cattle samples from Gauteng abattoirs, two were characterised as <italic>B. abortus</italic> and one as <italic>B. melitensis</italic> (Kolo et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2019</xref>). <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> was isolated from cattle in Gauteng (Ledwaba et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>). The human (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>) and goat <italic>Brucella</italic> DNA samples from the Western Cape were identified as <italic>B. melitensis</italic>. <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> was isolated from sable samples from the Eastern Cape, Western Cape, North West, Limpopo and Gauteng provinces (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>).</p>

        <title>Pangenomes of <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> and Average Nucleotide Identity (ANI)</title>
        <p id="Par16">The 19 sequenced genomes in this study were classified as <italic>B. melitensis</italic> (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;16) which include strains Bmel, BS0018, NIS8, BS0214, BS0160, 110, BS0487, BS04SERUM, 113, 114, 115, BS04HY, BS2271, BS2770, BS06SERUM and BS1SKIN.         <p id="Par17">The pangenome of the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> genomes (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;69) was made up of 3,548 total gene clusters that included the sequenced genomes (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;16) in this study (Fig.&#xA0;<xref rid="Fig1" ref-type="fig">1</xref>A).     <sec id="Sec17">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p id="Par23">As one of the most common zoonotic diseases on the globe, brucellosis necessitates early detection and control to reduce the infection&#x2019;s incidences and outbreaks. The presence of brucellosis in humans, livestock, and wildlife in SA raises major concerns to public health. Whole genome sequencing was carried on 19 <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. strains to trace the geographical origin of cases of brucellosis circulating in human, cattle, goat, and sable antelope from selected provinces in SA. The <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. reported in this study were identified as <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, which were isolated from human (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1) (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>), goat (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1) (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>), cattle (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1) (Kolo et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2019</xref>), and sable antelope (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;12) (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>) samples obtained from five different South African provinces. The South African <italic>B. melitensis</italic> sable did not cluster with other analysed worldwide genomes but formed a unique cluster in the African clade. This confirms that this infection is a persistent infection circulating in the sable population and not related to livestock as reported by a previous study (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>), which is further supported by the unique cluster based on ANI and wgSNP analysis. The sequenced <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains are part of the major C2 lineage, which also includes worldwide strains and exhibits two genotypes as previously reported by Ledwaba et al. (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>).&#xA0;Janke et al. (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR900">2023</xref>) refered to C1 lineage as clade C and C2 lineage as clade D.</p>
      <p id="Par24">The comparative genomics and genome study of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. involving <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains that are circulating in SA are the subject of very few investigations (Ledwaba et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>). Furthermore, none of the genomes of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> have been examined in SA and contrasted with genomes from around the world. The pangenomic analysis of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and <italic>B. abortus</italic> revealed a total of 3, 538 and 6,618 gene clusters, respectively. This indicates that <italic>B. abortus</italic> has high number of gene clusters. Both genomes&#x2019; species COG analysis indicated that they have&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;44% of the core genes devoted to metabolism. It has been observed that these two species have metabolism genes with a percentage greater than 44%, suggesting that the metabolism of energy and amino acids is more crucial to the growth and reproduction processes of <italic>Brucella</italic> species (Yang et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">2016</xref>). The use of pangenomics and ANI analysis showed that the sequenced SA <italic>B. melitensis</italic> genomes from sable and cattle form their own distinct cluster.</p>
      <p id="Par25">Sequenced types were identified among the sequenced genomes in this study from both <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and <italic>B. abortus</italic>. This study demonstrates the relation between the human case strain NIS8 which was the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain isolated by Wojno et al. (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>) and goat BS0018 DNA from Western Cape Province. The goat BS0018 isolate was obtained from the Western Cape Provincial Veterinary Laboratory, Stellenbosch. It was isolated from animals testing seropositive during this human outbreak (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). The goat BS0018 strain was collected in Western Cape as to identify the origin of the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> reported in the 27&#xA0;year old male patient (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). It is evident by the whole genome SNP- based phylogenetic tree that the human strain reported in this study, clustered with the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> BS0018 strain of the Western Cape goat. The isolation reports for <italic>B. melitensis</italic> from human and cattle in SA have not been intensively documented. A small percentage of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> bv 1 (3.3%) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> bv 3 (18.2%) were reported in SA from samples collected between 2008 and 2018 using laboratory diagnostic data (Matle et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">2021</xref>). An increased isolation of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> was reported in Gauteng (54.5%) followed by 36.4% in the Western Cape Province (Matle et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">2021</xref>). The frequency of <italic>Brucella</italic> reported in Western Cape is contrary to expectations since the province has some of the best regulation and enforcement of animal movement in the country due to foot and mouth control. The human case NIS8 strain from Western Cape sequenced in this study, further clustered with A1489 strain that was isolated from a human sample in Belgium based on ANI and both strains have the sequence types 12. The African genotypes including those in Tanzania are dominant with ST12, while other few of this sequence types have also been reported in India (Bodenham et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">2020</xref>; Karthik et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2021</xref>). The <italic>B. melitensis</italic> abattoir cattle Bmel S8 strain from Gauteng Province clustered with the human and goat <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain indicating the circulation of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in livestock and human in SA. This branch also caters the Gauteng abattoir cattle BmelS8 strain that has a high number of accessory genes. This may be due to the movement of people and animals across provincial borders as this plays a significant role in the infection&#x2019;s incidence (Sabin et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">2020</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par26">Studies reporting analyses and trace-back of <italic>Brucella</italic> genomes in SA are very limited. The overall incidence of human brucellosis is unknown in SA (Frean et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2018</xref>). Only a small number of studies on patients with febrile illnesses and populations at high risk have been carried out in recent years in SA, where there is currently no human brucellosis surveillance program (Frean et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2018</xref>). Brucellosis patients may exhibit ambiguous signs and symptoms during examination. Patients presenting with symptoms of chronic brucellosis may receive a false diagnosis and inadequate treatment in most developing countries (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). Furthermore, clinicians and laboratory personnel lack awareness and have little expertise diagnosing and managing this pathogen, which causes a delay in diagnosis (Wojno et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2016</xref>). A recent study conducted by Kolo et al., (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">2023</xref>) reported a brucellosis sero-positivity of 20.4% from abattoir workers in Gauteng Province, SA. A similar study conducted in Gauteng Province amongst workers on cattle farms, reported the seropositivity or exposure to brucellosis using multiple serological screening tests (Govindasamy et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">2021</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par27">In this study, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> sable samples BS1 skin, B04 Serum and B06 Serum were collected from a farm during an outbreak that occurred in Limpopo by the year 2016. Strain BS2770 grouped among the other sable samples, this was from an outbreak that occurred in 2015 from a farm in North West. Sample BS04HY (2007), BS0487 (2015) and BS0160 (2015) were obtained from the same farm in the Eastern Cape Province during two different outbreaks that occurred in 2007, 2014 and 2015. The findings demonstrated that the outbreaks (in this instance, those from 2007 and 2015) from the same farm persisted there in spite of the quarantine, testing, and slaughter program which was initiated (Glover et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2020</xref>). Several studies corroborate our findings where the re-emergence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> infection in wildlife was reported following an eradication period (Garin-Bastuji et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">2014</xref>; Mick et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">2014</xref>). According to Strydom (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">2016</xref>), the brucellosis outbreaks in sables that occurred between 2015 and 2016 in SA was a contributing factor to spillover from livestock to sable. A study conducted by Simpson et al (<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">2021</xref>), reported livestock contact as a significant risk factor for brucellosis infection in antelope. This may be attributed by animals (wildlife and livestock) grazing together or sharing water sources during dry seasons (Alexander et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2012</xref>). This is not supported by the wgSNP analysis in this study as <italic>B. melitensis</italic> sable sequences clustered in separate cluster with <italic>B. melitensis</italic> from any small livestock sequences currently available in this cluster. All the sable genomes cluster in one group based on ANI and represent a new African genotype based on wgSNP analysis. The high number of SNPs found indicate the diverse number of African strains within this population. The sable genomes may be distinguished from the previously published African sequences on GenBank, constituting a unique African sub-clade.</p>
      <p id="Par28">In this study, <italic>B. abortus</italic> isolates obtained from cattle collected in Gauteng abattoirs (20S4, 48S5) were closely related to strains from neighboring countries such as Mozambique and Zimbabwe and further clustered together with the cattle isolate (1.9) from a farm in Gauteng based on wgSNP analysis. This indicates that <italic>B. abortus</italic> is persistant, diverse and circulating within the cattle population in SA. <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> most probably were introduced and/or transmitted to cattle herds through the movement of animals within the country and across borders. It further emphasises that the current bovine brucellosis scheme should be expanded not only to bovine but all host species in SA, including sheep and goats to include compulsory vaccination of not only cattle but also sheep and goats to ensure immunity. The test and slaugtering prescribed by the scheme and expansion to include all bovine and other host species might be more difficult due to capacity and economic constrains in SA.</p>
      <p id="Par29">It is evident that the current bovine brucellosis scheme launched in 1979 has not reduced brucellosis, thus alternative control measures to consider might be mass vaccination of all livestock. In order to execute One Health initiatives against any zoonotic disease, awareness amongst the communities, farmers, health workers and veterinary practitioners is necessary. Lack of awareness regarding animal brucellosis causes ongoing financial losses, the disease's persistence in humans, underdiagnosis and or a delay that raises the risk of complications and treatment failure/relapse in human (Solera et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">1998</xref>). Mass vaccination can effectively reduce outbreaks and spread of brucellosis in an endemic country (Sanz et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">2010</xref>). <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> has been reported to be effectively controlled by using a mass vaccination with a decreased dose of Rev 1 (Scharp et al., <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">1999</xref>) which can also be considered. Currently, SA is reporting infections of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in cattle, thus vaccination of livestock could reduce the posibility of future outbreaks and spillover between species.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec18">
      <title>Conclusion</title>
      <p id="Par30">Brucellosis remains a controlled zoonotic infection in SA with a reduced proportion of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> reports as compared to <italic>B. abortus</italic>. This study demonstrates a comprehensive analysis of <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> isolated from human, livestock, and wildlife in SA. We presented a new African branch of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> genotype that is dominant in sable genomes. The presence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in human and cattle requires further extensive investigations and revision of control. In conclusion, this study identified genetically diverse <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. among various hosts in SA. The findings of this study will form the foundation for future research on the distribution of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. worldwide and its evolutionary background.</p>
      <p id="Par31">The limitation of this study included the difficulties of purifying additional <italic>Brucella</italic> isolates for sequencing, as the livestock samples were collected from apparently healthy animals with no abortion history. According to the literature, it has been reported that the organism is highly concentrated in aborted materials.</p>
