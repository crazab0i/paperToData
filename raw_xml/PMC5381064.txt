<italic toggle="yes">Brucellosis</italic>, caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp., is an important zoonosis worldwide. Vaccination is an effective strategy for protection against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in livestock in developing countries and in wildlife in developed countries. However, current vaccine strains including S19 and RB51 are pathogenic to humans and pregnant animals, limiting their use. In this study, we constructed the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>) S2308 mutant strain &#916;22915, in which the putative lytic transglycosylase gene <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> was deleted. The biological properties of mutant strain &#916;22915 were characterized and protection of mice against virulent S2308 challenge was evaluated. The mutant strain &#916;22915 showed reduced survival within RAW264.7 cells and survival in vivo in mice. In addition, the mutant strain &#916;22915 failed to escape fusion with lysosomes within host cells, and caused no observable pathological damage. RNA-seq analysis indicated that four genes associated with amino acid/nucleotide transport and metabolism were significantly upregulated in mutant strain &#916;22915. Furthermore, inoculation of &#8710;22915 at 10<sup>5</sup> colony forming units induced effective host immune responses and long-term protection of BALB/c mice. Therefore, mutant strain &#8710;22915 could be used as a novel vaccine candidate in the future to protect animals against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> infection.</p><sec><title>Electronic The Author(s) 2017</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="Sec1"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is a zoonotic disease epidemic in Asia, South and Central America, and sub-Saharan Africa [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>]. It is caused by the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, which infects millions of livestock and more than half a million people annually [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>]. Infection leads to reduction of animal productivity and debilitating disease in humans and causes economic losses and public health threats. Currently, vaccination of healthy animals is an effective strategy for protecting livestock from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in developing countries and protecting wildlife in developed countries [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>]. Vaccine strains such as S19, RB51 and Rev.1 have been extensively applied over the past decades with promising effects. These results stress the value of live attenuated vaccines. However, residue pathogenicity to humans and pregnant animals, and potential virulence reversion risks require the development of safer and better vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>].</p><p>Site-directed, unmarked deletion is an effective method for identifying virulence genes and constructing attenuated strains as <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines. For example, acid shock protein 24 (<italic toggle="yes">asp24</italic>), ATP-binding/permease protein (<italic toggle="yes">cydC</italic>, a component of the cydDC operon), phosphoribosylamine-glycine ligase (<italic toggle="yes">purD</italic>), nitric oxide reductase activation protein (<italic toggle="yes">norD</italic>), high-affinity zinc uptake system (<italic toggle="yes">zunA</italic>), sigma factor (<italic toggle="yes">rpoE1</italic>, &#963;<sup>E1</sup>) and teichoic acid ABC transporter ATP-binding protein (<italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS18515</italic>) are involved with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>]. Deletion of these genes reduces <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence, but they maintain excellent immunogenicity to activate the host immune response. These mutants provide protection against wild-type, virulent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> challenge in mouse models [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>], making them potential vaccine candidates.</p><p>In a previous study, we identified a series of genes associated with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S2308 virulence using miniTn5 transposon mutagenesis (unpublished data). One mutant with the gene <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> interrupted by miniTn5 showed highly attenuated virulence in BALB/c mice. <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> encodes a putative lytic transglycosylase that is a homolog of membrane-bound lytic transglycosylase B (MltB). MltB cleaves the &#946;-(1&#8594;4)-glycosidic bond between the <italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-acetylmuramic acid and <italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-acetylglucosamine residues of bacterial heteropolymer peptidoglycan [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>]. In addition to bacterial cell wall recycling [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>] and antibiotic resistance [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>], MltB is also involved in assembly of macromolecular transport systems such as the type IV secretion system in Gram-negative bacteria [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">21</xref>]. We expected that deletion of <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> would make the S2308 strain a good potential vaccine candidate; to investigate this, we generated the site-directed deletion mutant strain &#8710;22915. The virulence and protection capability of the mutant strain were evaluated. Our results demonstrated that the mutant strain &#8710;22915 was a highly attenuated strain and provided long-term, effective protection against wild-type, virulent strain S2308 challenge. These results suggested the mutant could be used as a novel vaccine candidate in the future.</p></sec><sec id="Sec2"><title>Materials and methods</title><sec id="Sec3"><title>Ethics statement</title><p>This study was performed in strict accordance with the recommendations in the Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee guidelines set by Shanghai Veterinary Research Institute, Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences (CAAS). BALB/c mice (SLAC, Experimental Animal Inc., Shanghai, China) were kept in cages and given water and food ad libitum under biosafety conditions. The protocol for animal experiments was approved by the Committee on the Ethics of Animal Experiments of Shanghai Veterinary Research Institute, CAAS (shvri-MO-0135).</p></sec><sec id="Sec4"><title>Bacterial strains, cell lines and plasmids</title><p>The virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S2308 strain was from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA). Vaccine strain RB51 was kindly provided by Professor Qingming Wu from China Agriculture University, Beijing. Both strains were cultured in tryptic soy broth (TSB, Difco, Becton&#8211;Dickinson, Sparks, MD, USA) or tryptic soy agar (TSA) at 37&#160;&#176;C with 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S2308 with nalidixic acid resistance was induced with nalidixic acid at 50&#160;&#956;g/mL and preserved in our laboratory. <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> DH5&#945; competent cells (Tiangen, Beijing, China) were cultured in Luria&#8211;Bertani (LB) media at 37&#160;&#176;C. Murine macrophage RAW 264.7 cells were from ATCC and cultured in Dulbecco&#8217;s modified Eagle medium (DMEM, Hyclone, GE Lifesciences, Logan, UT, USA) media with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Gibco, ThermoScientific, Grand Island, NY, USA) at 37&#160;&#176;C with 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. The suicide pSC plasmid with the <italic toggle="yes">sacB</italic> gene [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>] was preserved in our laboratory and used to construct a site-directed mutant strain.</p></sec><sec id="Sec5"><title>Construction of the mutant strain &#8710;22915</title><p>The &#8710;22915 strain was constructed as described previously [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>]. Primers for construction were designed using the sequence of <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> in the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S2308 genome (GenBank Code: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_007618.1">NC_007618.1</ext-link>). Fragments that flanked <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> were amplified in two independent PCR reactions using PrimeSTAR Max Mix (TaKaRa, Dalian, China) with primer pairs BAB_RS22915 UF/BAB_RS22915 UR, and BAB_RS22915 DF/BAB_RS22915 DR. Recovered PCR products were used for overlap PCR to produce joint sequences with primer pairs BAB_RS22915 UF/BAB_RS22915 DR. PCR products were purified, digested with <italic toggle="yes">Xba</italic>I and ligated into pSC. A recombinant plasmid with the correct sequence was designated pSC-&#8710;22915 and introduced into DH5&#945;.</p><p>Allelic replacement was employed to delete <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> from the wild-type strain S2308. According to the method described previously [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>], S2308 was cultured and collected by centrifugation at the exponential phase. After an ice bath for 15&#160;min, S2308 was washed twice with ice-cold sterile water. Bacteria were resuspended in 10% (v/v) glycerin water and 3&#8211;5&#160;&#956;g recombinant pSC-&#8710;22915 plasmid was added on ice. After electroporation, transformed S2308 were immediately transferred to prewarmed TSB media and cultured overnight. Bacteria were cultured on TSA plates with ampicillin at 100&#160;&#956;g/mL. A single exchanged mutant was selected and inoculated into TSB without antibiotics and cultured on TSA containing 5% (w/v) sucrose to produce a second exchange mutant. At least ten colonies per plate were collected for identification with PCR or quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR). Primer pair BAB_RS22915 FF/BAB_RS22915 FR, flanking the gene coding sequence, and primer pair BAB_RS22915 OF/BAB_RS22915 OR, partially overlapping the deleted sequence, were used to identify gene deletions. Colonies with length-reduced fragment from BAB_RS22915 FF/BAB_RS22915 FR pair and no fragment from BAB_RS22915 OF/BAB_RS22915 OR pair were selected as <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> deleted mutant. Primer pairs RT-22910 F/RT-22910 R, RT-22920 F/RT-22920 R and RT-22915 F/RT-22915 R were used for qRT-PCR to identify if BAB_RS22915 deletion had polar effects on flanking gene transcription. Deletion mutants were designated &#8710;22915. Mice vaccinated with strain &#8710;22915 were challenged with nalidixic-acid resistant S2308 at 1.0&#160;&#215;&#160;10<sup>4</sup> CFU at 12 and 16&#160;wpv. This result showed that the mutant strain &#8710;22915 provided BALB/c mice with better protection against S2308 than RB51.</p></sec></sec><sec id="Sec21"><title>Discussion</title><p>We successfully constructed the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> mutant strain &#8710;22915 by deleting a 1036-bp fragment from the <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> gene. Strain &#8710;22915 showed similar LPS phenotypes and adherence and invasion capacities compared to the wild-type strain S2308, but attenuated virulence, determined by in vivo and in vitro survival. We demonstrated that the decreased intracellular survival of strain &#8710;22915 was associated with the altered capacity to exclude lysosomes, an important step before <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reaches a replicative niche in endoplasmic reticulum [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>]. This result suggested that the altered intracellular traffic contributed to decreased survival in RAW 264.7 cells.</p><p>MltB is a member of the lytic transglycosylase (LT) family that is involved in recycling bacterial cell walls to produce 1,6-anhydromuropeptides for bacterial growth [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">35</xref>]. However, inactivation of LTs does not inhibit bacterial growth in medium [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>]. This finding indicates involvement of other pathways or gene upregulation to compensate for bacterial cell growth [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>]. Thus, we investigated the gene expression in the whole genome using transcriptomic analysis, which indicated 16 genes were changed for their expression. qRT-PCR confirmed that four genes were upregulated by more than 10-fold; these were categorized into &#8220;amino acid/nucleotide transport and metabolism&#8221;. Transcription of <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS24460</italic> was upregulated 39-fold in the mutant strain &#8710;22915; this is the substrate-binding component of the branched-chain amino acid ABC transporter, responsible for the uptake of leucine, isoleucine and valine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>]; uptake mediated by this transporter contributes to bacterial growth [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>], intracellular survival [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">40</xref>], and symbiosis between host and bacteria [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">41</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS30485</italic> gene, also known as the <italic toggle="yes">PotD</italic> gene, is the substrate-binding component of the polyamine ABC transporter that preferentially takes up spermidine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">42</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS17430</italic> encodes the &#945; subunit of the NADPH dependent glutamate synthase that catalyzes the reductive transfer of the amide group of <sc>l</sc>-glutamine to C-2 of 2-oxoglutarate to produce <sc>l</sc>-glutamate [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">43</xref>]. This reaction is involved in nitrogen assimilation of <italic toggle="yes">&#945;</italic>-<italic toggle="yes">proteobacteria</italic> to produce glutamine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">44</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS17405</italic> encodes a dihydropyrimidinase that is responsible for the second step of pyrimidine reductive catabolism to produce <italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-carbamoyl-b-alanine and <italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-carbamoyl-b-aminoisobutyric acid, respectively, from dihydrouracil and dihydrothymine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>]. To some bacteria, it functions as an important source of nitrogen [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">46</xref>]. We found that upregulated genes are mainly responsible for bacterial metabolism. Therefore, they might have contributed to the enhanced growth rate of strain &#8710;22915 in stationary stage cultures in TSB media. The upregulated genes might be related to the decreased intracellular survival of the mutant strain &#8710;22915. Bacterial ABC transporters and metabolism-related proteins are reported to provide animals with protection as vaccine candidates [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR47">47</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">49</xref>]. We assume the upregulation of these genes may enhance the antigenicity of &#8710;22915 to activate the immune response of macrophages, resulting in its decreased intracellular survival.</p><p>In vivo experiments indicated that strain &#8710;22915 persisted in mice for longer than 12&#160;weeks, but caused no observable pathological damage. In addition, the mutant strain &#8710;22915 induced fewer inflammatory responses than the wild-type strain. In previous research, live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> with multiple disregulated genes protected against the wild-type strain S2308 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">50</xref>]. This result suggested that testing whether mutant strain &#8710;22915 could be applied as a vaccine would be a worthwhile study. Vaccination with the mutant strain induced an effective immune response against the wild-type strain S2308. After vaccination with &#8710;22915, bacterial loads decreased until after 4&#160;wpv, with specific antibody titers increasing to a peak at 12&#160;wpv. The smooth type of the mutant strain &#8710;22915 induced higher levels of antibody than RB51, due to the dominant antigenicity of the O-antigen [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]. The adaptive cellular response is mainly responsible for the immunity against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, but antibody against O-antigen or serum from smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-infected animals participates in defense against challenge by wild-type <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>]. In challenge assays, coinciding with the humoral response, strain &#8710;22915 provided longer and better protection than RB51 at 12 and 16&#160;wpv. Unlike <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> mutant <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">53</xref>], challenge by S2308 did not rescue the survival of &#8710;22915 (data not shown), confirming its safety as a vaccine candidate.</p><p>In conclusion, using a suicide plasmid, we constructed a smooth-phenotype mutant strain &#8710;22915 with a deletion of the <italic toggle="yes">BAB_RS22915</italic> gene. In addition to altered intracellular traffic and attenuated survival, multiple genes involved in amino acid/nucleotide transport and metabolism were upregulated in the mutant strain. These genes may be associated with the attenuation of intracellular survival and require further research on their mechanism. Virulence of the mutant strain &#8710;22915 was significantly attenuated in BALB/c mice and provided better protection against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S2308 than RB51. This finding facilitated potential use of mutant strain &#8710;22915 as a novel vaccine candidate in the future.</p></sec></body><back><app-group><app id="App1"><sec id="Sec22"><title>Additional file</title><p>
