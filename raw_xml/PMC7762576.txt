The live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain is widely used for the control of brucellosis in the small ruminant population. However, Rev.1 induces antibodies against the O-polysaccharide (O-PS) of the smooth lipopolysaccharide thus, it is difficult to differentiate between infected and vaccinated animals. Hence, rough <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains lacking the O-PS have been introduced. In the current study, we conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis of the genome sequence of two natural Rev.1 rough strains, isolated from sheep, against that of 24 Rev.1 smooth strains and the virulent reference strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. We identified and characterized eight vital mutations within highly important genes associated with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> lipopolysaccharide (LPS) biosynthesis and virulence, which may explain the mechanisms underlying the formation of the Rev.1 rough phenotype and may be used to determine the mechanism underlying virulence attenuation. Further complementation studies aimed to estimate the specific role of these mutations in affecting <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> morphology and virulence will serve as a basis for the design of new attenuated vaccines for animal immunization against brucellosis.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1</kwd><kwd>vaccine</kwd><kwd>comparative sec-type="intro" id="sec1-ijms-21-09341"><title>1. Introduction</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species are Gram-negative, facultative intracellular bacteria that cause brucellosis, a zoonotic world-wide disease [<xref rid="B1-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. As in other Gram-negative bacteria, its outer membrane plays a crucial role in the infection process, being the first point of bacterial interaction with its host [<xref rid="B2-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. The major component of the outer membrane, the lipopolysaccharide (LPS), consists of three structural regions: (1) The lipid A, which forms the hydrophobic anchor of the LPS within the outer membrane and is responsible for many endotoxic properties attributed to LPS, (2) an inner and outer core composed of sugar molecules, and (3) the O-antigen (O-polysaccharide; O-PS) a polymerized sugar chain extending into the extracellular environment [<xref rid="B3-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. Most <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species occur naturally as smooth LPS (S-LPS) strains (i.e., <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus, B. melitensis, B. suis, B. neotomae, B. microti, B. pinnipedialis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic>), which contain LPS that is modified with an O-antigen, while <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis, B. canis, B. abortus</italic> RB51, and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> B115 are typically rough strains (R-LPS) which lack the O-side chain from LPS [<xref rid="B3-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>,<xref rid="B4-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>]. Generally, rough strains were shown to be less virulent than smooth strains, with the exception of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella ovis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>, which are rough but virulent [<xref rid="B5-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>]. Indeed, mutants of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis, B. suis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> lacking the O-antigen modification were shown to be considerably less virulent than their respective parent strains [<xref rid="B6-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B7-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. It has been suggested, that the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> LPS plays a key role in the invasion, intracellular multiplication and protection against complement-mediated lysis [<xref rid="B3-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> LPS is a significantly less effective inducer of the innate immunity compared to that of <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic>. As such, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is able to evade detection by the host immune system and to form a chronic intracellular infection [<xref rid="B9-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>,<xref rid="B10-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>].</p><p><italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, which infects sheep and goats mainly in Africa, the Middle East, Asia, and Latin America, is one of the most pathogenic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species for humans [<xref rid="B11-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>]. In these high-prevalence regions, brucellosis is controlled mainly by vaccination of the host ruminants. Among the widely used brucellosis vaccines is the live attenuated Rev.1 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strain [<xref rid="B12-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>] which successfully protects and reduces abortions in small ruminants [<xref rid="B13-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>,<xref rid="B14-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>]. However, Rev.1 has some undesirable traits: It may be abortifacient when used in pregnant animals, it is infectious for humans, and it induces O-PS-specific antibodies, which interfere with the serological tests that employ S-LPS as an antigen [<xref rid="B5-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="B14-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>]. As compared with the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 smooth strain, rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains may offer a solution to these drawbacks, as they are less virulent and lack O-PS [<xref rid="B6-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B15-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. Because of these features, rough <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> mutants have been considered potential brucellosis vaccines [<xref rid="B14-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>,<xref rid="B16-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>].</p><p>We have recently used RNA-seq to comprehensively compare the transcriptome of smooth Rev.1 vaccine strains to that of natural rough Rev.1 vaccine strains, each grown under either normal or acidic conditions, in order to better understand the molecular mechanisms underlying the virulence attenuation of the rough versus smooth Rev.1 strains [<xref rid="B17-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. We found 18 key genes that were significantly differentially expressed between smooth and rough strains, all associated with bacterial virulence and survival [<xref rid="B17-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. We also showed, in a cell culture model, that the rough Rev.1 strain was highly attenuated compared to the smooth Rev.1 strain [<xref rid="B17-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>].</p><p>In this study, we evaluated the genetic characteristics of two natural Rev.1 rough strains, isolated from sheep, compared to 24 Rev.1 smooth strains (including the original Elberg <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain) and the virulent smooth reference <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M strain, in order to reveal the specific gene mutations in the genomes of the natural Rev.1 rough strains. We identified and characterized several vital mutations within highly important genes associated with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> LPS biosynthesis and virulence, which may explain the mechanisms underlying the formation of the Rev.1 rough phenotype, thereby improving our understanding of the genetic structure relationship in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> LPS, and revealing new insights into the virulence attenuation of these strains.</p></sec><sec sec-type="results" id="sec2-ijms-21-09341"><title>2. Results and Discussion</title><p>Genome-wide comparisons between two natural rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strains (71036 and 44457), 24 smooth <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strains and the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M virulent strain were conducted to identify all the single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) between these genomes. Below, we first report the genes containing unique SNPs in the natural rough Rev.1 vaccine strains compared to their smooth strain counterparts and to the virulent 16M smooth strain. We then characterize each mutation and evaluate their potential effect on protein function in order to better understand the molecular mechanisms leading to the formation of the Rev.1 rough phenotype and to the virulence attenuation of these strains. </p><sec id="sec2dot1-ijms-21-09341"><title>2.1. Genomic SNPs</title><p>The genome-wide comparisons between the natural rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain, 71036, and the original Elberg smooth Rev.1 vaccine strain revealed a total of 12 variants (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Table S2</xref>) of which seven exclusively appeared in this strain and not in other tested Rev.1 smooth strains. Among these, one SNP is a synonymous substitution encoding the same amino acid; four SNPs are missense substitutions encoding a different amino acid; and the remaining two SNPs contain frameshift mutations, which, in many cases, have a deleterious effect on the protein function. A comparison between the natural rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain 44457 and the original Elberg smooth Rev.1 vaccine strain revealed a total of 12 variations of which three exclusively appeared in this strain and not in other tested Rev.1 smooth strains. Among these, one is located in intergenic regions; one is a synonymous substitution; and one is a missense substitution. We next compared the two natural rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strains, 71036 and 44457, and the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M virulent strain and found two additional unique SNPs in 44457 that did not appear in our tested Rev.1 smooth strains. One SNP is an upstream gene variant, and another is a frameshift variant (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Table S2</xref>, rows 21&#8211;22). We assume that these SNPs were not detected in our comparison with the Rev.1 sequence due to incomplete sequencing or annotation of the Rev.1 reference genome. Only a single gene, BMEII0899, was mutated both in 71036 and 44457 rough strains, albeit with different unique mutations. To validate the existence of the detected rough-unique mutations, we applied Sanger sequencing of 8 detected variants to the two Rev.1 rough strains and three Rev.1 smooth strains including the original Elberg smooth Rev.1 vaccine strain. All the variants selected for Sanger sequencing were validated (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="ijms-21-09341-f001">Figure 1</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot2-ijms-21-09341"><title>2.2. Characterization of the Specific SNPs of the Natural Rough B. melitensis Rev.1 71036 Vaccine Strain</title><sec id="sec2dot2dot1-ijms-21-09341"><title>2.2.1. Genes Involved in LPS Biosynthesis</title><sec><title>Phosphomannomutase (BMEII0899)</title><p>We identified a frameshift mutation (c.388delA|p.Ile130fs) in the CUC12_12640 (BMEII0899) gene encoding phosphomannomutase. Phosphomannomutase is encoded by the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> manBcore gene which is responsible for the interconversion of mannose-6-phosphate to mannose-1-phosphate [<xref rid="B5-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>]. Phosphomannomutase enzymes contain four domains with the active site residues located between these domains [<xref rid="B18-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>]. Each domain contributes to the enzyme function in a different way: Phosphoglucomutase_phosphomannomutase (PGM_PMM)_I contains the catalytic phosphoserine, PGM_PMM_II binds the magnesium ion which is important for enzymatic activity, PGM_PMM_III recognizes the sugar orientations, and PGM_PMM_IV binds the phosphate group [<xref rid="B19-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. This frameshift mutation will delete three of the PGM-PMM domains, and thereby most probably cause a non-functional protein. In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, mannose acts as an important precursor in the O-antigen biosynthetic pathway and in the production of the inner core moiety of LPS [<xref rid="B5-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>]. Using transposon mutagenesis in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> H38 and in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308, it has been shown over a decade ago that mutations in this gene resulted in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> rough phenotype [<xref rid="B7-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B15-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> H38 manBcore mutant was unable to grow in bone-marrow derived macrophages (BMDM), and was completely degraded in LAMP1-positive compartments [<xref rid="B15-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. We assume that the detected frameshift mutation may not only highly affect the conversion of smooth to rough phenotype in the Rev.1 rough strain, but also the survival capabilities of this microorganism within its host.</p></sec><sec><title>ABC Transporter Permease (BMEI1416)</title><p>We identified a frameshift mutation (c.151_152dupGG|p.Ile52fs) in the <italic toggle="yes">wzt</italic> (CUC12_10065, BMEI1416) gene encoding ABC transporter permease. The mutation is located on the ABC_trans domain (PFAM PF00005; residues 49&#8211;182). The frameshift mutation is predicted to cause a non-functional protein due to the missing domain. </p><p>The O-antigen homopolymers of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are fully synthesized and assembled at the cytoplasmic face of the inner membrane and then transported across the inner membrane using an ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter system [<xref rid="B2-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="B20-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. In <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, the genes <italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic> (BMEI1415) and <italic toggle="yes">wzt</italic> (BMEI1416) were predicted to form the transmembrane and ATPase domain, respectively, of an ABC transporter required for the translocation of the full length homopolymer O-antigen, from the cytoplasmic to the periplasmic face of the inner membrane [<xref rid="B2-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="B20-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. Indeed, the deletion of <italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic>/<italic toggle="yes">wzt</italic> resulted in a rough phenotype of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M colonies and in the accumulation of intracellular O-antigen [<xref rid="B20-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. The wild type smooth phenotype was restored via mutant complementation experiments, demonstrating the high significance of the transmembrane and ATPase domain in the formation of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> smooth phenotype [<xref rid="B20-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. In contrast, in the natural rough <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> B115 strain, the same complementation failed to restore the smooth phenotype, indicating that additional mutations may be present in this strain that affect the LPS O-side chain transport or ligation to the lipid A-core molecule [<xref rid="B20-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. We assume that the detected frameshift mutation may highly affect the conversion of smooth to rough phenotype in this Rev.1 rough strain.</p></sec></sec><sec id="sec2dot2dot2-ijms-21-09341"><title>2.2.2. Genes Involved in Survival within Environmental Stress Conditions</title><sec><title>Glutathione S-Transferase Family Protein (BMEII0256)</title><p>We identified a missense mutation (c.248G&gt;A) in the CUC12_15955 (BMEII0256) gene encoding a glutathione S-transferase family protein (GST). This mutation leads to the replacement of the amino acid arginine to histidine at position 83 of the protein sequence. This mutation is unique, as when we compared the GST sequences among 16 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species/strains, we found that Arg83 was conserved through all homologs (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="ijms-21-09341-f002">Figure 2</xref>A). To evaluate the possible effect of the detected mutation on protein functionality, we conducted a structural analysis of the amino acid sequence of GST based on the solved 3D structure of this protein from <italic toggle="yes">Sinorhizobium meliloti</italic> (PDB ID 4MDC). The missense mutation at position 83 is located within the GST_N_3 domain (residues 4&#8211;86), which contain two Prosite motifs: GST-C TER and GST-N TER, between residues 1&#8211;79 and 88&#8211;222, respectively (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="ijms-21-09341-f002">Figure 2</xref>B,C). This mutation is predicted to have either an allosteric effect on the catalytic function or a local structural effect due to histidine being a smaller residue than arginine. </p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is a facultative intracellular pathogen, which is able to survive and replicate within various types of host cells [<xref rid="B21-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>,<xref rid="B22-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B23-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>]. Therefore, it has to adapt to a range of different hostile environments, including oxidative and acid pH stresses [<xref rid="B24-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. GSTs are evolutionarily conserved enzymes which play an active role in the detoxification of various xenobiotic compounds and protect cells from oxidative stress by detoxifying some of the secondary reactive oxygen species (ROS), including hydrogen peroxide, hydroxyl radicals, and superoxide anions [<xref rid="B25-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>,<xref rid="B26-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>]. Indeed, a glutathione S-transferase B1-1 null mutant of <italic toggle="yes">Proteus mirabilis</italic> was found to be more sensitive to oxidative stress than its wild-type counterpart [<xref rid="B27-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. Accordingly, it was suggested that this GST plays an important role in protecting against oxidative stress [<xref rid="B27-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. We assume that the detected missense mutation may affect the GST function, resulting in a lower capability to cope with oxidative stress thereby contributing to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec><sec><title>NAD(P) Transhydrogenase Subunit Alpha (BMEII0323)</title><p>We identified a missense mutation (c.464T&gt;C) in the CUC12_15600 (BMEII0323) gene encoding NAD(P) transhydrogenase subunit alpha. This mutation leads to the replacement of the amino acid methionine to threonine at position 155 of the protein sequence. The NAD(P) transhydrogenase subunit alpha protein is very conserved in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and in other Gram-negative bacteria (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S1A</xref>), and specifically high conservation is seen in this methionine residue. NAD(P) transhydrogenase is a tetramer composed of two alpha and two beta subunits. This protein does not have a crystal structure and its 3D structure was modeled using Alpha1 dimer of <italic toggle="yes">Thermus thermophilus</italic> transhydrogenase (PDB ID 4IZH, article is unpublished). The detected mutation is located in the alpha subunit of the enzyme. A Prosite motif was detected within this protein, between residues 172&#8211;197: ALADH_PNT_2, an alanine dehydrogenase and pyridine nucleotide transhydrogenase signature 2 pattern (Prosite pattern ID PS00837; <xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S1B</xref>). Although Met155 is highly conserved and is located in a conserved region of the protein (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S1C</xref>), it does not interact with the NADH substrate of the enzyme and is not located within the specific detected Prosite pattern, and so its role is predicted to be mainly structural. Mutation to threonine, although a polar residue and of a smaller size, is hypothesized to have a local structural effect.</p><p>NAD(P) transhydrogenase, an integral membrane protein found in most organisms, catalyzes the transfer of reducing equivalents between NAD(H) and NADP(H), coupled to the translocation of protons across a membrane [<xref rid="B28-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. The resulting NADPH is subsequently used for biosynthetic reactions or for the reduction of glutathione (GSH) [<xref rid="B28-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. Indeed, the highly essential enzyme, NADPH-dependent glutathione reductase, is known to recycle oxidized glutathione (glutathione disulfide; GSSG) produced during bacterial growth and under oxidative stress, back to the reduced form (GSH) [<xref rid="B29-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>]. It is possible that the detected missense mutation in this rough strain may affect the efficiency of the NADPH production by NAD(P) transhydrogenase, resulting in a lower intracellular GSH/GSSG ratio, reducing the capability of this strain to cope with oxidative stress.</p></sec><sec><title>Magnesium Transporter (BMEII0418)</title><p>We identified a missense mutation (c.464C&gt;T) in the BMEII0418 gene encoding a magnesium (Mg<sup>2+</sup>) transporter. This mutation leads to the replacement of the amino acid threonine to methionine at position 155 of the protein sequence. The mutation is located within the CBS domain (residues 135&#8211;197). This domain&#8217;s 3D structure was modeled using the magnesium transporter MgtE protein from <italic toggle="yes">Thermus thermophilus</italic> (PDB ID 2ZY9, <xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S2B&#8211;D</xref>) [<xref rid="B30-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>]. Thr155 is located in a conserved hydrophobic core of the CBS motif. The mutation is predicted to have a structural effect on the motif, since methionine is larger than threonine. Notably, this mutation is unique since, when we compared the sequence of the CBS domain of 85 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> magnesium transporter sequences, Thr155 was conserved throughout all homologs (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S2A,D</xref>).</p><p>Mg<sup>2+</sup> is the most abundant divalent cation in living cells and the second most abundant cation after K+ [<xref rid="B31-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>,<xref rid="B32-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>]. Mg<sup>2+</sup> plays several essential roles, including stabilizing macromolecular complexes and membranes, neutralizing nucleic acids, and nucleotides in the cytoplasm, and acting as a cofactor in a variety of enzymatic reactions [<xref rid="B31-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. As magnesium is an essential nutrient, bacteria possess multiple transporters for Mg<sup>2+</sup>. In <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella typhimurium</italic>, a subset of Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters was shown to be highly induced upon invasion of this bacterium into eukaryotic cells, and the signal for this induction was shown to be Mg<sup>2+</sup> itself, mediated by the PhoP/PhoQ two component regulatory system, important for virulence, thus implicating Mg<sup>2+</sup> and its transporters in pathogenesis [<xref rid="B33-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>,<xref rid="B34-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. Furthermore, in <italic toggle="yes">Yersinia pestis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Y. pseudotuberculosis</italic>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> was shown to be an important signal controlling gene regulation via the PhoP/PhoQ two-component regulatory system [<xref rid="B35-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>,<xref rid="B36-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>,<xref rid="B37-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>]. Notably, the <italic toggle="yes">mgtC</italic> gene, first described in <italic toggle="yes">S. Typhimurium</italic> as part of the <italic toggle="yes">mgtCB</italic> operon encoding an Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporter [<xref rid="B38-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], was shown to play a major role in intramacrophage survival and growth under magnesium limitation in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> [<xref rid="B39-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. We assume that the detected missense mutation may lead to the formation of a less functional magnesium transporter, resulting in a lower capability of this rough strain to survive within its host, thereby contributing to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec><sec><title>MFS Efflux Pump (BMEI0267)</title><p>We identified a missense mutation (c.1069C&gt;T) in the BMEI0267 gene encoding an MFS efflux pump. This mutation leads to the replacement of the amino acid proline to serine at position 357 of the protein sequence. MFS transporters, which are membrane transport proteins that facilitate movement of small solutes across cell membranes in response to chemiosmotic gradients, contain a 12-transmembrane (TM)-helix core consisting of two pseudo-symmetrical six-helix domains [<xref rid="B40-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>]. A membrane-embedded central cavity is present between these two domains, thus forming the substrate-transport path. This protein does not have a crystal structure and its 3D structure was modeled using <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> multidrug transporter MdfA (PDB ID 4ZP0, [<xref rid="B41-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]). The detected mutation is located within the MFS Prosite motif (residues 2&#8211;395) of the protein. This protein is very conserved in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and in other Gram-negative bacteria (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S3A</xref>), and specifically high conservation is seen in this proline residue. Pro357 is located in TM helix 11 (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S3B</xref>) and although it is highly conserved and is located in a very conserved region of the protein (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S3C</xref>), its role is predicted to be mainly structural, as alignment to other bacterial MFS proteins does not show it to have a role in substrate binding (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Figure S3B</xref>). For this reason, mutation to serine, a polar residue, but of similar size to proline, is hypothesized to have a local structural effect.</p><p>MFS efflux pumps are membrane protein complexes that are conserved in all living organisms [<xref rid="B42-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>,<xref rid="B43-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>]. In several pathogenic bacteria (e.g., <italic toggle="yes">Listeria monocytogenes</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Vibrio cholera</italic>), efflux pumps were shown to be involved in intracellular survival, resistance to stress, antibiotic extrusion and biofilm formation [<xref rid="B44-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>,<xref rid="B45-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>]. Furthermore, the important role of efflux pumps in the bacterial invasion process and survival within macrophages and intestinal epithelial cells was shown in <italic toggle="yes">S. typhimurium</italic> [<xref rid="B46-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>]. We assume that the detected missense mutation of the Rev.1 rough strain may have a negative impact on the invasion capability and survival of this microorganism within its host, thereby contributing to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec></sec></sec><sec id="sec2dot3-ijms-21-09341"><title>2.3. Materials and Methods </title><sec id="sec3dot1-ijms-21-09341"><title>3.1. Strain Information, Extraction of the Genomic DNA, and Sequencing</title><p><italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and 16M genomes and annotations were downloaded from NCBI (accession numbers: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_003317.1">NC_003317.1</ext-link> and <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_003318.1">NC_003318.1</ext-link> for 16M; <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="CP024715">CP024715</ext-link> and <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="CP024716">CP024716</ext-link> for Rev.1). Twenty-four smooth Rev.1 strains and two rough Rev.1 strains were used in this study (<xref ref-type="app" rid="app1-ijms-21-09341">Supplementary Table S1</xref>). SNP Detection</title><p>Following quality control with FastQC v0.11.5 [<xref rid="B49-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>], the reads were processed to trim adaptors and low-quality bases by using Trim Galore v0.5 [<xref rid="B50-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>]. The SNPs were called using Samtools v1.7 [<xref rid="B52-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>]. Next, output SNPs were converted to VCF format and filtered (QUAL &gt; 20) using BCFtools v1.7. The SnpEff database of the 16 M genome (2002 assembly; [<xref rid="B54-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>] and the Rev.1 genome (2018 assembly; [<xref rid="B55-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>,<xref rid="B56-ijms-21-09341" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>] were built based on GenBank files that were downloaded from NCBI. To validate the detected SNPs and to detect additional mutations that were missed, the analysis was repeated using alignment against the 16M reference genome followed by SNP calling. In our analysis, we defined rough-specific mutations as variations exclusively appearing in Rev.1 rough strains and not in Rev.1 smooth strains.</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot3-ijms-21-09341"><title>3.3.
