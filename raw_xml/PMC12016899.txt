Despite the importance of vaccines in preventing brucellosis, no previous systematic evaluation of vaccination in sheep has been conducted. </p></sec><sec><title>Materials and Methods</title><p> Articles were searched in databases such as PubMed, Science Direct, Cochrane, VIP, Wan Fang, and CNKI by screening the articles, and articles reporting <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccination in sheep were included in the study. Meta-analysis was performed using random effects models to calculate pooled risk ratios for vaccines and to calculate vaccine effectiveness. </p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p> A total of 2,605 articles were retrieved, and 17 articles were obtained through screening for analysis. The effectiveness of vaccination was 65% (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.35, 95% CI: 0.27&#8211;0.36; VE&#8201;=&#8201;65%), with the M5 vaccine being significantly more effective at 84% (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.1587, 95% CI: 0.0256&#8211;0.9858; VE&#8201;=&#8201;84%) than the other vaccines, and intramuscular injection could be the best route of immunization. Rev.1 was indicated for female sheep, especially for pregnant ewes (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2016, 95% CI: 0.1139&#8211;0.3569; VE&#8201;=&#8201;80%), and for reduced abortions (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.0978, 95% CI: 0.0459&#8211;0.2085). </p></sec><sec><title>Conclusion</title><p> This meta-analysis was conducted to identify the relevant factors affecting vaccine efficacy. We recommend that sheep be inoculated intramuscularly with Rev.1, different inoculation protocols be adopted for sheep of different ages, and pregnant ewes be inoculated with Rev.1 to prevent abortion.</p></sec></abstract><funding-group><award-group><funding-source>Jilin Provincial Key Research and id="sec1"><title>1. Introduction</title><p>Bacteria of the genus Brucella are members of the alpha-2 Proteobacteria and are parthenogenetic intracellular and Gram-negative bacteria [<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. In recent years, the number of new <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species has increased, and there are currently 12 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella species</italic>, three of which can cause severe brucellosis in humans [<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. Brucellosis, a globally occurring subacute or chronic zoonotic disease that infects cattle, sheep, goats, other ruminants, and pigs, is usually unnoticeable in the initial stages of infection, with abortion, placenta retention, uterine inflammation, and reduced milk production occurring in the later stages [<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>, <xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>, <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>].</p><p>Sheep act as natural hosts for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, and this disease can occur in most major sheep-rearing countries. Brucellosis can be sexually transmitted from infected sheep to other healthy sheep to infect the flock [<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. Outside of the breeding season, the disease is also transmitted directly between sheep via oral and mucosal routes. When sheep are infected, rams can cause testicular inflammation, arthritis, and other symptoms, and the main clinical phenomena in ewes are abortion and stillbirths [<xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>]. In addition to adversely affecting the health of flocks, sheep brucellosis has significant economic consequences, including reduced conception rates, prolonged lambing periods, reduced marketability, and the loss of valuable genes from culled rams [<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>].</p><p>Because of the serious economic and medical consequences of brucellosis, efforts have been made to prevent infection through the use of vaccines [<xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>]. In endemic areas, vaccination against the epidemic is the most economical preventive measure to control brucellosis [<xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>]. The technology used for vaccine development can be divided into traditional vaccines and new vaccines. Traditional vaccines include inactivated vaccines and live attenuated vaccines, and their components generally include the whole pathogen [<xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>]. Live attenuated vaccine strains are the most effective vaccines available for the control of brucellosis in animals and play an important role in the prevention and control of brucellosis in animals and humans [<xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>, <xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>] because live attenuated vaccine strains have the same tissue tropism and cellular tropism as wild-type strains, as opposed to inactivated and subunit vaccines, and can induce immunity through humoral and cell-mediated responses, thus providing a greater level of protection [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. Rev.1 and M5 are widely used as commonly used live attenuated vaccines against brucellosis in small ruminants. In 1957, Rev.1 was obtained by successive passaging of virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 6,056 on streptomycin-containing media and is used in several countries and regions [<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. In 1991, the Harbin Veterinary Medical Research Institute in China produced M5 from the potent strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M28 by acridine yellow treatment and sequential passaging in cultured chicken embryo fibroblasts, which is commonly used for the prevention of brucellosis in sheep and goats in China [<xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. Both Rev.1 and M5 are produced by successive passages of weakened strong strains, which are simple to produce, but the molecular and physiological mechanisms underlying the loss of vaccine virulence are unknown [<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>, <xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. In addition, vaccination can cause abortions in pregnant animals, interfere with serological diagnosis, and make it impossible to distinguish between vaccine and natural infections, thus leading to misclassification of the source of infection.</p><p>The lack of available vaccines hampers the control of human brucellosis. Therefore, the control of animal brucellosis is the most effective way to prevent human infections [<xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. This paper presents the first meta-analysis of the clinical phenomenon of brucellosis vaccination in sheep and the development of rational vaccination recommendations to prevent the spread of brucellosis in sheep better, thereby improving the health and safety of animals and related workers.</p></sec><sec id="sec2"><title>2. Materials and Methods</title><sec id="sec2.1"><title>2.1. Search Strategy</title><p>We searched six databases, including Cochrane, Science Direct, PubMed, VIP, Wan Fang, and CNKI, for eligible studies published before January 2, 2024. The search strategy is detailed in <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary Appendix <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">1</xref></italic>. The database search results were downloaded and put into EndNote (EndNote X9.3.3).</p></sec><sec id="sec2.2"><title>2.2. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria</title><p>Inclusion criteria are as follows:<list list-type="order"><list-item><p>Studies using placebo or other vaccines as control or experimental groups.</p></list-item><list-item><p>This study reports the efficacy of a vaccine against brucellosis in sheep.</p></list-item><list-item><p>Articles published in English or Chinese.</p></list-item></list></p><p>Exclusion criteria are as follows:<list list-type="order"><list-item><p>Failure to conduct bacteriological attack experiments.</p></list-item><list-item><p>No control group.</p></list-item><list-item><p>Unable to download.</p></list-item><list-item><p>The experimental animal is not a sheep.</p></list-item><list-item><p>Repeated articles.</p></list-item></list></p></sec><sec id="sec2.3"><title>2.3. Data Extraction</title><p>We used EndNote X9.3.3 to manage records and filter and exclude duplicates. After article screening through inclusion and exclusion criteria, the following data were extracted from the included articles: first author, year of publication, geographic location, vaccine type, mode of immunization, dose of immunization, region, number of samples, sex, age at the time of vaccination, data on protection from clinical symptoms (abortive and bacteriological), data on protection from infections (abortive and bacteriological), strains of challenge infections, and doses of infections. The extracted information was exported to Microsoft&#174; Excel&#174; 2019 MSO (16.0.14228.20216) 32.</p></sec><sec id="sec2.4"><title>2.4. Statistical Methods</title><p>The quality of the included studies was assessed based on the criteria of the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) method (<italic toggle="yes">Supplementary table <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">2</xref></italic>) [<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>]. The included articles were analyzed in this study using R version 4.0.0. Combined risk ratios and 95% confidence intervals were calculated using a random effects meta-analysis model [<xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. Vaccine effectiveness was calculated as (1&#8201;&#8722;&#8201;risk ratio) [<xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>]. The presence of publication bias was assessed using funnel plots and Egger's test, and <italic toggle="yes">P</italic> &lt; 0.05 indicated statistical significance [<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. When the funnel plot was asymmetric, the shear method was used for correction [<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">I</italic><sup>2</sup> test was used to assess the statistical heterogeneity between studies; the higher the <italic toggle="yes">I</italic><sup>2</sup> value was, the greater the heterogeneity [<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>].</p></sec></sec><sec id="sec3"><title>3. Results</title><sec id="sec3.1"><title>3.1. Selected Studies</title><p>In this study, 2,605 studies were collected from six databases. First, duplicates were removed using EndNote. Second, articles were filtered by article title and abstract. Finally, 17 studies were subjected to data extraction based on the inclusion and exclusion criteria (<xref rid="tab1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref> and <xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). The vaccine types included in the study were mainly live attenuated (M-111, Rev.1, S2, M5, and RB51) and inactivated (<italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>).</p></sec><sec id="sec3.2"><title>3.2. Publication Bias and Risk of Bias Assessment</title><p>Publication bias was shown to exist in the tests by the funnel plot (<xref rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>) and Egger (<italic toggle="yes">P</italic>-value &lt;0.0001) (<xref rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref> and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary table <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">2</xref></italic>), so the stability of the combined results was assessed by the trim-and-fill method, and the results showed good stability (<xref rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4</xref>). The result of the sensitivity analysis is <italic toggle="yes">I</italic><sup>2</sup>&#8201;=&#8201;51%, indicating heterogeneity in the meta-analysis, and the following analysis was carried out to determine the source of heterogeneity (<xref rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Figure 5</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="sec3.3"><title>3.3. Vaccine-Related Outcomes</title><p>The results of this study showed that a meta-analysis of all studies with sheep vaccination as an outcome indicated that the pooled efficacy of the vaccine was approximately 65% (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.35, 95% CI: 0.27&#8211;0.36; VE&#8201;=&#8201;65%) (<xref rid="fig6" ref-type="fig">Figure 6</xref>). The effectiveness of the M5 vaccine was 84% (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.1587, 95% CI: 0.0256&#8211;0.9858; VE&#8201;=&#8201;84%), which was significantly greater than that of the other vaccines (<italic toggle="yes">P</italic>=0.0401) (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figure 7</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p><p>The vaccination doses were analyzed to determine the optimal dose of each vaccine. The results showed that M-111 was more effective than &#8805;2.5&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>10</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2692, 95% CI: 0.1826&#8211;0.3967; VE&#8201;=&#8201;73%) at an inoculation dose &lt;2.5&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>10</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.1545, 95% CI: 0.0629&#8211;0.3795; VE&#8201;=&#8201;85%), with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>, <xref rid="fig8" ref-type="fig">Figure 8</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). The Rev.1 vaccine was less effective than the &gt;2&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>9</sup> vaccine (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2790, 95% CI: 0.1673&#8211;0.4653; VE&#8201;=&#8201;72%) at doses &#8804;2&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>9</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3604, 95% CI: 0.2208&#8211;0.5882; VE&#8201;=&#8201;64%), with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>, <xref rid="fig9" ref-type="fig">Figure 9</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). The S2 vaccine was less effective than the &gt;1&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>10</sup> vaccine (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2999, 95% CI: 0.2016&#8211;0.4461; VE&#8201;=&#8201;70%) at a vaccination dose &#8804;1&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>10</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3260, 95% CI: 0.1671&#8211;0.6358; VE&#8201;=&#8201;67%), with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>, <xref rid="fig10" ref-type="fig">Figure 10</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> was more effective at an inoculation dose of 2.5&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>9</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3736, 95% CI: 0.1216&#8211;1.1482; VE&#8201;=&#8201;63%) than at an inoculation dose of 2&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>9</sup> (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.5399, 95% CI: 0.2626&#8211;1.1101; VE&#8201;=&#8201;46%), with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>, <xref rid="fig11" ref-type="fig">Figure 11</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p><p>As the route of vaccine immunization is an important factor affecting the effectiveness of vaccine immunization, we also analyzed the different routes of immunization for different vaccines. The results showed that subcutaneous M-111 vaccination (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2355, 95% CI: 0.1596&#8211;0.3474; VE&#8201;=&#8201;77%) was more effective than oral gavage (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2913, 95% CI: 0.1678&#8211;0.5057; VE&#8201;=&#8201;71%), with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>, <xref rid="fig12" ref-type="fig">Figure 12</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Rev.1 vaccination via intraconjunctival (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2653, 95% CI: 0.1313&#8211;0.5361; VE&#8201;=&#8201;73%) was more effective than subcutaneous (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3993, 95% CI: 0.2654&#8211;0.3474; VE&#8201;=&#8201;60%) vaccination, with no significant difference (<xref rid="tab4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>, <xref rid="fig13" ref-type="fig">Figure 13</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Intramuscular S2 vaccination (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2414, 95% CI: 0.0969&#8211;0.6012; VE&#8201;=&#8201;76%) was significantly more effective than intraconjunctival vaccination and oral gavage vaccination (<xref rid="tab4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>, <xref rid="fig14" ref-type="fig">Figure 14</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p></sec><sec id="sec3.4"><title>3.4. Sheep-Related Results</title><p>The effectiveness of vaccination is affected not only by the vaccine itself but also by the age, sex, and health of the immunized animals; for this reason, this paper analyses the above factors. The results showed that females (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2639, 95% CI: 0.2059&#8211;0.3384; VE&#8201;=&#8201;74%) were more protected (<italic toggle="yes">P</italic>=0.0008) after vaccination than were males (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.5413, 95% CI: 0.4026&#8211;0.7277; VE&#8201;=&#8201;46%) (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig15" ref-type="fig">Figure 15</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Compared with those vaccinated with other vaccines (M-111, Rev.1, S2, M5), female sheep vaccinated with the M5 vaccine (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.1587, 95% CI: 0.0256&#8211;0.9858; VE&#8201;=&#8201;84%) exhibited greater protection (<italic toggle="yes">P</italic>&#8201; &lt; 0.0001) (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig16" ref-type="fig">Figure 16</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Male sheep vaccinated with Rev.1 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.5049, 95% CI: 0.3844&#8211;0.6633; VE&#8201;=&#8201;50%) were more protected than those vaccinated with the other vaccines (<italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>, S2) (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig17" ref-type="fig">Figure 17</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Pregnant ewes, as an important target for protection, showed that vaccination with Rev.1 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2016, 95% CI: 0.1139&#8211;0.3569; VE&#8201;=&#8201;80%) was more protective than other vaccines (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig18" ref-type="fig">Figure 18</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p><p>Adult animals (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3499, 95% CI: 0.2469&#8211;0.4958; VE&#8201;=&#8201;65%) were more protected than lambs (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.4352, 95% CI: 0.3128&#8211;0.6055; VE&#8201;=&#8201;56%) after vaccination (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig19" ref-type="fig">Figure 19</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Adult sheep inoculated with M5 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.1587, 95% CI: 0.0256&#8211;0.9858; VE&#8201;=&#8201;84%) and S2 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.2558, 95% CI: 0.1873&#8211;0.3493; VE&#8201;=&#8201;74%) were better protected (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig20" ref-type="fig">Figure 20</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>). Lambs inoculated with M-111 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.3107, 95% CI: 0.1636&#8211;0.5897; VE&#8201;=&#8201;69%) and Rev.1 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.4258, 95% CI: 0.3087&#8211;0.5873; VE&#8201;=&#8201;57%) were well protected (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>, <xref rid="fig21" ref-type="fig">Figure 21</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p></sec><sec id="sec3.5"><title>3.5. Abortion</title><p>As abortion is a major feature of brucellosis, this paper analyses the phenomenon of abortion in vaccinated attacking females. The results showed that Rev.1 (RR&#8201;=&#8201;0.0978, 95% CI: 0.0459&#8211;0.2085) vaccination significantly reduced the incidence of abortion in infected females (<italic toggle="yes">P</italic>=0.0299) (<xref rid="tab5" ref-type="table">Table 5</xref>, <xref rid="fig22" ref-type="fig">Figure 22</xref>, and <italic toggle="yes">Supplementary figure <xref rid="supplementary-material-1" ref-type="sec">3</xref></italic>).</p></sec></sec><sec id="sec4"><title>4. Discussion</title><p>Brucellosis, a globally distributed zoonotic bacterial disease, can infect a wide range of mammals [<xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>]. Sheep, the main type of brucellosis-infected animal, are infected, and infection triggers reduced fertility, the loss of young people, and reduced milk production, leading to significant economic losses in the livestock industry [<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>]. Vaccines are used as a primary means of preventing brucellosis in sheep, and this study recalculates the efficacy of these vaccines and analyses the relevant influencing factors through a systematic review and meta-analysis, thus providing important information for the development of sheep vaccination protocols.</p><p>Based on the susceptible&#8211;exposed&#8211;infected&#8211;recovered&#8211;susceptible model of brucellosis, it was observed that it took 16.8 years to eliminate brucellosis from farms after the sheep were vaccinated [<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>] because the complex and insidious dynamics of brucellosis make it difficult to eradicate brucellosis in most cases, making effective vaccination necessary for the control and eradication of brucellosis in livestock [<xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>]. The results of this study showed that sheep vaccination provided effective protection to the animals (VE&#8201;=&#8201;65%). Efficient animal vaccination is undoubtedly an effective control measure that can significantly reduce the prevalence of brucellosis in livestock, thereby reducing human infections [<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>]. In addition, we found that female sheep were significantly more effective than male sheep in terms of vaccine immunity after vaccination. First, the majority of the articles included in this study involved pregnant ewes, which, as important breeding and production animals, receive special attention because they have better dietary and feeding conditions and can be better vaccinated with better results [<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>]. Second, the main animal brucellosis vaccines currently in use are A19, S19, Rev.1, M5, S2, and RB51, of which <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Rev.1 is one of the most effective [<xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>, <xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>, <xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>]. The results of our study showed that both female and male sheep were effectively protected by Rev.1 vaccination, but females were more protected than males, which may explain the significant difference in the protection rates of vaccinated female and male sheep. In our study, it was found that the vaccination of pregnant ewes with Rev.1 produced better protection and reduced the incidence of abortion. Studies have also shown that Rev.1 has good efficacy against brucellosis infections in pregnant ewes, thus reducing economic losses for farmers [<xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>]. Reducing the occurrence of brucellosis-induced abortions in sheep reduces the risk of exposure to infectious vectors and protects the health and safety of the workers involved.</p><p>In our study, vaccination had a better effect on adult sheep, although there was no difference in age. Compared with lambs, adult sheep have better body functions, and when the vaccine enters the body, it stimulates an immune response similar to that of a natural infection, inducing a large number of favorable cytokines and generating a sufficient cell-mediated immune response [<xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>]. In the present study, it was found that adult sheep vaccinated with the M5 vaccine had better efficacy than those vaccinated with the S2 vaccine, while lambs vaccinated with M-111 had better vaccination efficacy than those vaccinated with Rev.1. Relevant studies have shown that M5 macrophages have a faster rate of immune antibody production, longer antibody duration, and better levels of postimmunization antibody production than S2 macrophages [<xref rid="B54" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>]. In small ruminants, the first step in controlling brucellosis is to vaccinate young animals, which provides lifelong immunity to sheep and thus prevents brucellosis [<xref rid="B55" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>]. Since the M-111 vaccine is rarely used in practice, we recommend targeting adult sheep with M5 and lambs with Rev.1, which provides better protection against brucellosis at different ages and thus stimulates a long-lasting protective response in the organism.</p><p>The vaccination dose can affect vaccination effectiveness, and we observed that the vaccination effectiveness of Rev.1, S2, and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> increased with increasing vaccination dose because, for any single vaccine, larger doses induce better protective immunity [<xref rid="B56" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>]. However, we found that high doses of the M-111 vaccine reduced the effectiveness of vaccination. For different vaccines, there are different dosage values; when a certain threshold is exceeded, it can cause poor results. For example, Ebrahimi et al. [<xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>] used different dosage groups to connect the ewes, and after the challenge, the 7.5&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>6</sup> vaccination group triggered a significantly greater proportion of abortions than did the 5&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>5</sup> vaccination group. Therefore, vaccination should be carried out after determining the optimal dose of vaccine required prior to vaccination. Once the vaccination measurements have been determined, different routes of inoculation can also lead to different results. In this study, we found that there was no significant difference in vaccination efficacy between the M-111 and Rev.1 vaccines after using different vaccination methods, while the S2 and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> vaccines were more suitable for intramuscular delivery because the intramuscular route is not only the most basic form of inoculation but also generates a stronger and longer lasting antibody response than other forms of inoculation [<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>]. In summary, we suggest that the optimal vaccination dose should be determined before the inoculation of sheep and that intramuscular injection should be adopted for inoculation to achieve better results and lasting protection of sheep from brucellosis infection.</p><p>Limitations of this meta-analysis include, first, the relatively small number of studies and the possibility of publication bias. This paper lacks a comparison between M5 and other vaccines in terms of abortion rate but still reflects to some extent the effect of different vaccines on the abortion rate in sheep. Second, our analyses were limited to published data, and due to the variable nature of the data, it was not possible to further analyze other relevant data; for example, adverse reactions following vaccination were not analyzed in this paper. Finally, this paper only analyses single-dose vaccines and lacks analyses of booster vaccinations.</p></sec><sec id="sec5"><title>5. Conclusions</title><p>Our study highlights the protection offered by different vaccines against brucellosis in sheep. Rev.1 Vaccines protect against brucellosis infection and reduce abortion rates. Sheep should be vaccinated in adulthood, with an emphasis on ewes, and should be vaccinated by intramuscular injection or an inoculation dose &gt;2&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>9</sup> CFU for optimal inoculation results. The results of this systematic evaluation and meta-analysis are useful for considering recommendations for the use of brucellosis vaccines in sheep of different ages and sexes.</p></sec></body><back><ack><title>Acknowledgments</title><p>This study was funded by the Jilin Provincial Key Research and Development (20230204010YY) and the Jilin Provincial Natural Science Foundation (YDZJ202201ZYTS444).</p></ack><glossary><title>Abbreviations</title><def-list><def-item><term>PRISMA:</term><def><p>Preferred reporting items for systematic reviews and meta-analyses</p></def></def-item><def-item><term>GRADE:</term><def><p>Grading of recommendations assessment, development, and evaluation.</p></def></def-item></def-list></glossary><sec sec-type="data-availability"><title>Data Availability</title><p>The datasets used in this work are accessible upon reasonable request from the corresponding author.</p></sec><sec><title>Additional Points</title><p>List of publications for the author (Lian-Min Li): Li LM, Wang Q, Shi JF, Li T, Zhao B, Ma QX, Liu HY, Su N, Cai RP, Zeng FL, Gong QL, Shi K, Li JM, Liu F, Du R.
