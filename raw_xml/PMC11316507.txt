    <sec sec-type="intro">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>The first effective vaccine against brucellosis was the live attenuated vaccine <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> (<italic>B. abortus</italic>) S19 strain. Although this vaccine protects cattle against <italic>B. abortus,</italic> its side effects have led it to be used cautiously <sup><xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>,<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref></sup>. Despite these complications, the <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> (<italic>B. melitensis</italic>) vaccine strain, Rev-1 leads to the control of brucellosis in sheep and goats, only in endemic areas <sup><xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref></sup>. Although previous studies have examined the live attenuated <italic>B. abortus</italic> 45/20 vaccine, this strain may still cause disease in some cases. <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains RB51 vaccine can protect cattle against <italic>B. abortus</italic> with less side effects <sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup>. The vaccine is a preventive tool used to eradicate brucellosis.</p>
      <p>Classical serological tests detect antibodies against the Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) antigens induced by vaccination with S19 or Rev. 1 or exposure to virulent field strains <sup><xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref></sup>. Therefore, no specific serological tests can distinguish between animals vaccinated with S19 and Rev-1 or a natural Brucella strain infection to detect the disease. Recently, mutant strains of <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 were proposed to be used in the immunization of cattle. Vaccines used for brucellosis are ~70% efficacious and may cause disease in some vaccinated animals <sup><xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref></sup>.</p>
      <p>The major Outer Membrane Proteins (OMPs) of Brucella spp. were first introduced in the 1980s <sup><xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref></sup>. They were soon characterized as potential immunogenic and protective antigens. They were classified according to their apparent molecular mass as group 1, 36&#x2013;38 <italic>kDa</italic> OMPs, group 2 porin proteins and 31&#x2013;34 and 25&#x2013;27 <italic>kDa</italic> OMPs, which belong to the group 3 proteins <sup><xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref></sup>. The use of recombinant protein technology and Monoclonal antibodies (MAbs) has shown that the major OMPs appear to be of little relevance as antigens in smooth (S) <italic>B. abortus</italic> or <italic>B. melitensis</italic> infections, <italic>i.e.</italic> low or no protective activity in the mouse model of infection and low or no immunogenicity during host infection. However, group 3 proteins, in particular Omp31, appear as immunodominant antigens in the course of rough (R) <italic>Brucella ovis</italic> (<italic>B. ovis</italic>) infection in rams and as an important protective antigen in the <italic>B. ovis</italic> mouse model of infection. The major <italic>OMP</italic> genes display diversity and specific markers have been identified for Brucella species, biovars, and strains, including the recent marine mammal Brucella isolates for which new species names have been proposed.</p>
      <p>The <italic>B. abortus</italic> LPS, compared to the Enterobacteriaceae, has an unusual structure that is significant in making T-cell-mediated responses <sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup>. It can induce IgM and IgG. In addition, according to new studies, the <italic>B. abortus</italic> LPS can show adjuvanticity <sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup>. Moreover, it is also a Hapten. The LPS has a crucial role in the pathogenesis of <italic>Brucella</italic> ssp., therefore, by the characteristics described above, it is likely to be considered as a qualified vaccine candidate <sup><xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref></sup>.</p>
      <p>The susceptibility of micro-organisms to antimicrobial agents is often measured <italic>in vitro</italic> by measuring the inhibiting activity of growth. These measurements often do not provide sufficient information to determine the appropriate treatment of certain infections, such as endocarditis <sup><xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref></sup>. In addition, a specific &#x3B2;- Lactam antibiotic, which has bactericidal activity, does not have bactericidal activity for all organisms <sup><xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref></sup>. Therefore, there is a requirement for new experimental methods which can observe the bactericidal activity in optimal conditions. Schlichter and Mac Lean are considered the founders of the Serum Bactericidal Assay (SBA) methods. They discovered the inhibitory effects of blood serum. However, they were unable to define criteria for the characterization of bactericidal effects. Fisher completed Schlichter&#x2019;s discovery path by culturing the tubes, which showed no visible bacterial growth <sup><xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref></sup>. However, Fisher disclaimed the urge to determine the concentration levels of bactericidal effect <sup><xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref></sup>. Since killed or weakened vaccines may cause pathogenicity, the use of biological and immunological compounds that can induce antibody production and cause the immunity is a solution to prevent the side effects of killed and weakened vaccines.</p>
      <p>In this study, we aimed to determine the bactericidal activity of the serum induced by the potential <italic>B. abortus</italic> LPS and OMP&#x2019;s combined subunit vaccine.</p>

    <sec sec-type="materials|methods">
      <title>Materials and Methods</title>
      <sec sec-type="methods">
        <title>Strains used in this study</title>
        <p><italic>B. abortus</italic> S99 was obtained from the Bacterial Cell Bank of Pasteur Institute of Iran (CSBPI) as the strain for smooth LPS extraction. <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 was provided as a live attenuated vaccine, which was used after resuscitation, as the source of the OMP&#x2019;s extraction. <italic>B. abortus 544 was</italic> provided by the Department of Bacteriology, Tarbiat Modares University, and used as the challenge strain for vaccine immunization in mice and rabbits.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec>
        <title>LPS extraction</title>
        <p>The LPS of <italic>B. abortus</italic> S99 was extracted and purified using a modified hot phenol method based on Westphal&#x2019;s (1986) technique. Phenol at 68<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> was added to a batch cultured <italic>B. abortus</italic> S99 in a fermentor (Novo-Paljas Netherland) and then centrifuged at 2900 rpm. To separate the proteins and nucleic acids, cold methanol was added to the phenolic phase after the separation. Trichloroacetic acid (0.5 <italic>g/l</italic> of solution) was added and stirred at 4<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> for 30 <italic>min</italic>. Following centrifugation, the supernatant underwent dialysis (cutoff=20 <italic>kDa</italic>) three times in distilled water over 24 <italic>hr</italic>. Next, the white precipitate collected inside the dialysis bag was placed in 100 <italic>ml</italic> vials and lyophilized <sup><xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref></sup>.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec>
        <title>LPS Detoxification</title>
        <p>The LPS obtained was detoxified by the addition of 0.2 <italic>N</italic> NaOH to the purified LPS, placed at 100<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> for 2 <italic>hr</italic>. After it cooled down, the pH was set to 7 by 0.1 <italic>N</italic> HCL. The fatty acids, released by performing dialysis against distilled water for 4 days, were cleared. The dialysate (containing D-LPS) was subsequently lyophilized. Quality assurance tests, including Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL), Bradford test (it is based on the shift in absorbance maximum of Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 dye from 465 to 595 <italic>nm</italic> following binding to denatured proteins in solution) <sup><xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref></sup>, and bioassay [LPS toxicity was compared with its detoxified form using the LAL (Limulus Amoebocyte Lysate) method], were also performed to ensure LPS detoxification based on reported protocols <sup><xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref></sup>.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec>
        <title>OMP extraction</title>
        <p>Its moisture weight was suspended in 10 <italic>mM</italic> Tris buffer (Sigma) with ethylenediamine, trichloroacetic acid, and 10 <italic>mM</italic> EDTA (W/V). The 100% power for 4 <italic>min</italic> sonication was done within half a minute at 3500 <italic>rpm</italic> at 4<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> and was then centrifuged. Then the cell-free fluid was centrifuged for 60 <italic>min</italic> at 42000 <italic>rpm</italic> and ultracentrifugation was done at 4<italic>&#xB0;C</italic>. The precipitate was suspended in 20 <italic>ml</italic> sodium N-Lauryl sarcosinate 2%, added to 0.1 <italic>M</italic> Tris buffer containing 10 <italic>mM</italic> EDTA and kept at room temperature for 1 <italic>hr</italic>. The sample was ultracentrifuged at 42000 <italic>rpm</italic> for 1 <italic>hr</italic> at 4<italic>&#xB0;C</italic>. The precipitated OPMs in the tube were solubilized in 15 <italic>ml</italic> of water, then dialyzed against 0.2 <italic>M</italic> NaCl with three replacements every 8 <italic>hr</italic> for 24 <italic>hr</italic> at 4<italic>&#xB0;C</italic>. The OMP deposits were then dissolved in 15 <italic>ml</italic> of distilled water and dialyzed against 0.2 <italic>M</italic> NaCl. The dialyzed OMPs were divided into sterile 20 and 50 <italic>ml</italic> glass vials after passing through 0.22 <italic>&#x3BC;m</italic> filters, and stored at &#x2212;20<italic>&#xB0;C</italic>. Quality control tests were also conducted <sup><xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref></sup>.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec>
        <title>Combination of the B. abortus LPS and OMPs</title>
        <p>The combined vaccine contains 25 <italic>mg/ml</italic> LPS and 50 <italic>&#x3BC;g/ml</italic> OMPs. 10 <italic>ml</italic> of the vaccine was provided <sup><xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref></sup>.</p>
      </sec>

      <sec>
        <title>Immunization of mice and rabbits</title>
        <p>One week to 10 days before the challenge, the mice were adapted to the test conditions and then divided into random groups. Five BALB/c mice were each injected with 0.2 <italic>ml</italic> of the combined vaccine, following the same injection schedule as the rabbits. Four groups of two white New Zealand rabbits each were selected. Group 1 received a 25 <italic>&#x3BC;l</italic> LPS injection, Group 2 received a 50 <italic>&#x3BC;l</italic> injection of the OMPs, and the third group received a 1 <italic>ml</italic> injection of the combined vaccine. The injections were administered twice, with a 14-day gap between each. To induce brucellosis in all groups, they were injected with the challenge strain of <italic>B. abortus</italic> 544, two weeks after the initial injection. Two weeks after the final injection, blood was drawn from all groups and their sera were separated to conduct a SBA <sup><xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref></sup>.</p>
      </sec>

        <p>After completing the aforementioned steps, the microplate was incubated at 37<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> for 30 <italic>min</italic>. Subsequently, 1 <italic>&#x3BC;l</italic> of each well was transferred to Brucella agar plates and streak cultured immediately. Following this, all plates were placed in the incubator at 37<italic>&#xB0;C</italic> for 72 <italic>hr</italic>. The colonies present on all plates were then enumerated. Any plate counts equal to or less than 50% of the colonies on the control plates were deemed positive.</p>

As shown in <xref rid="F3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>, the immunogenicity of combined vaccine (OMP+LPS) was 87.71% and the percentage of live vaccine immunogenicity RB51 was 57.14%.</p>

    <sec sec-type="discussion">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>While the S19 and RB51 live vaccines used against brucellosis are generally safe, there have been instances where they have caused disease in animals or humans, particularly in those involved in the cultivation of bacteria. Furthermore, distinguishing between vaccinated and infected animals through natural means becomes very difficult after inoculation with these vaccines. Consequently, there has been a growing focus on developing a subunit vaccine for brucellosis, as the use of conventional vaccines in humans poses significant challenges. As a result, the development of a brucellosis subunit vaccine has become a critical area of research.</p>
      <p>A variety of protein and non-protein antigens of Brucella, or a combination of them, have been proposed as vaccine candidates. Induction of immunity against brucellosis requires both immune responses, particularly cellular immunity. The characteristics of pure Brucella antigen are not sufficient to induce these responses. Therefore, an effective subunit vaccine against brucellosis seems likely to be a combination of different Brucella antigens <sup><xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>,<xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>,<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref></sup>.</p>
      <p>Generally, pure LPS injection can induce some antibodies, but it does not provide strong immune system stimulation. This is because LPS functions as a thymus-independent antigen. The LPS was derived from <italic>B. abortus</italic> S99, a smooth strain used at the Pasteur Institute of Iran to produce antigens. This component of the vaccine was considered the hapten due to its immune system stimulation. To address the vaccine&#x2019;s shortcomings, the OMP&#x2019;s of <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 were utilized, as the candidates do not provide long-lasting immunity.</p>
      <p>Efforts to develop brucellosis vaccines have been made worldwide. Despite these efforts, no effective vaccine with minimal side effects has been developed for use in humans and animals. In order to develop an effective vaccine against brucellosis, now Smooth Brucella strains: <italic>B. abortus</italic> S19, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Rev-1 and Rough Brucella strains: <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 are used in many countries <sup><xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>,<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>,<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref></sup>.</p>
      <p>While S19 and Rev-1 have drawbacks such as inducing abortions in pregnant animals and being pathogenic in humans, the more significant issues lie in the production of specific antibodies against O-LPS, and the cross-immunity observed in conventional serological tests using S-LPS, 9, 10, and 23. The best strain for developing a brucellosis vaccine is <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51, a rough strain without the disadvantages of the S19 and Rev-1 vaccines. Studies have also demonstrated the immunogenicity of the OMPs of the <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 strain. However, each one alone is not an effective immune system inducer. When used together, they exhibit a synergistic effect in the vaccine. All 11 OMPs were utilized in this study <sup><xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>,<xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref></sup>.</p>
      <p>SBA is superior to the ELISA method because ELISA measures the antibody production that can only be achieved by a vaccine. Nevertheless, SBA, antigenicity and the serum bactericidal titers against bacteria can be achieved at the same time, as desired. SBA was the main method for testing vaccine safety against Meningitis, and could likely be used to observe the vaccine immunity for brucellosis. Even though Brucella is an intracellular bacterium, since the newly developed vaccines are killed, it will not be a problem against the intracellular bacteria. Moreover, by the application of both LPS and the OMPs of <italic>B. abortus,</italic> the humoral and cellular immune responses to vaccines would be significant. The colony counts of mice&#x2019;s spleen demonstrated the effectiveness of this vaccine compared to the control plate. Thus, indications are that the use of these two antigens may be useful in vaccine development.</p>
      <p>Compared to the S19 vaccine, this vaccine did not show any sign of abortion. Bioassay in mice and rabbits did not show recurrence. Moreover, this vaccine does not have the risk of transformation of rough strains to soft strains. There are risks of using cattle brucellosis vaccine in humans; however, by using the combination of LPS and the OMPs of <italic>Brucella</italic>, it will be cleared.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="conclusion">
      <title>Conclusion</title>
      <p>The study results show that the vaccine induced a T-cell-mediated response through LPS. The OMPs in the vaccine acted as a carrier for the LPS and enhanced the immune response to LPS. Despite concerns, the hope is that production of meat and dairy products could be increased due to the economic damage caused by brucellosis in livestock. Since conjugation of two or more molecules can lead to better immunogenicity in humans or animals, further studies in the future could be done on the evaluation of the immunity caused by the conjugation of these two antigens by SBA.</p>
