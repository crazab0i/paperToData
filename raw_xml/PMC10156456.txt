      <abstract>
        <sec>
          <title>Background</title>
          <p> Table olives are becoming well recognized as a source of probiotic bacteria that might be used to create a health-promoting fermented food product by traditional procedures based on the activities of indigenous microbial consortia present in local environments. <italic>Methodology</italic>. In the present study, the characterization of probiotic bacteria isolated from mince, chunks, and brine of fermented green and black olives (<italic>Olea europaea</italic>) was done based on morphological, biochemical, and physiological characteristics. </p>
        </sec>
        <sec>
          <title>Results</title>
          <p> Bacterial isolates demonstrated excellent survival abilities at 25, 37, and 45&#xB0;C and at a variable range of pH. However, the optimum temperature is 37 and the optimum pH is 7 for all three isolates. An antimicrobial susceptibility pattern was found among these isolates through the disc diffusion method. Most of the isolates were susceptible to streptomycin, imipenem, and chloramphenicol, whereas, amoxicillin showed resistance to these isolates, and variable results were recorded for the rest of the antibiotics tested. The growth of the isolates was optimum with the supplementation of 3% NaCl and 0.3% bile salt. The isolated bacteria were able to ferment skimmed milk into yogurt, hence making it capable of producing organic acid. </p>
        </sec>
        <sec>
          <title>Conclusion</title>
          <p> Isolates of <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417, <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418 from black olives, and <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 from green olives were characterized as potential candidates for use as starter cultures to induce fermentation of other probiotic food products.</p>

    <sec id="sec1">
      <title>1. Introduction</title>
      <p>Probiotics have a long history of human consumption; for example, cultured dairy products (curds and yogurts) are traditionally consumed in several parts of the world. The word probiotics is of Greek origin, meaning &#x201C;for life&#x201D;, which is the antonym of antibiotics [<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>, <xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. Those living microorganisms that are administered in an adequate amount and have a beneficial effect on human health are known as probiotics [<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>, <xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>].</p>
      <p>According to Ranadheera et al., [<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>] and Oleskin, &amp; Shenderov [<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>], the human gastrointestinal tract contains more bacteria than eukaryotic cells. This gut flora also contains probiotics, which constructively influence the body by offering health encouragement to the host [<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>].</p>
      <p>Microorganisms used as probiotics, specifically lactic acid bacteria (LAB), include <italic>Lactobacillus</italic> species, some species of <italic>Bifidobacteria, Enterococcus, and Streptococcus</italic> [<xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>, <xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. Most of these bacterial species reside in the human intestine [<xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>]. The only probiotic yeast that exists is the nonpathogenic <italic>Saccharomyces boulardii</italic> [<xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>]. LAB has prevalent exploitation in fermented food manufacturing [<xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>] and is a generally recognized as safe (GRAS) organism that can be securely used for medical or veterinary purposes [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>].</p>
      <p>If LAB is exposed to traumatic circumstances in the gastrointestinal tract like acidic gastric juice, bile salt, and/or altered microbial balance of the intestinal tract, then administration of any kind of antibiotic may suppress the probiotic population. So, to get the maximum benefits out of the probiotic bacterial population, the selection of probiotics is made after various <italic>in vitro</italic> and <italic>in vivo</italic> tests [<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>, <xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>].</p>
      <p>There are a variety of traditional fermented food products produced by probiotic fermentation like fruits and vegetables including olives, beetroot, cabbage, and other leafy vegetables. Fermentation is done by keeping the vegetables in a 2% brine solution and allowing the stored vegetables to be fermented with LAB [<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>, <xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. In the food industry, especially for ready-to-eat foods, microbiological quality is a persistent concern. Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) could be used to prevent the growth of spoiling and pathogenic bacteria [<xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. Some LAB exhibits active microbial antagonism through competition mechanisms, the production of bacteriocins, or the production of organic acids [<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>].</p>
      <p>The plant named &#x201C;olive&#x201D; is a small tree of the family <italic>Oleaceae</italic>, and its binomial name is <italic>Olea europaea</italic> [<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>, <xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. Olives are bitter when raw or fresh, so they must be treated and fermented to make them edible. Both green olives, which are full-size olives plucked before ripening, and black olives, which are completely matured, ripened olives, can be fermented. The main reasons for olive processing are the exclusion of bitterness by hydrolysis of some phenolic compounds (like oleuropein), preservation of the fruit, and enhancement of the organoleptic characteristics of the ultimate product [<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>, <xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>].</p>
      <p>Due to the high concentration of dietary fiber, vitamins, antioxidants, and anticancer chemicals in table olives, they have therefore been considered useful food [<xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. Table olives are called pickled vegetables, in which preparation and maintenance are achieved by an amalgamation of salting, fermentation, and acidification. Some studies have been conducted to widen the range of useful food types by utilizing of the microarchitecture of the surface of olives, and the dietary characteristics of olive pulp to produce a flavorsome, vegetable-based efficient food comprising of table olives equipped with probiotic strains [<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>].</p>
      <p>In research, the capacity of seven strains from the probiotic species <italic>Lactobacillus rhamnosus</italic>, <italic>Lactobacillus paracasei</italic>, <italic>Bifidobacterium umbifidum</italic>, and <italic>Bifidobacterium longum</italic> to survive on the olive surface and the suitability of table olives as a biological carrier for probiotic microorganisms were studied [<xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>]. The resultant table olives can be stored with or without refrigeration, and probiotic-dominated fermentations are generally considered the most suitable method of curing olives [<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>, <xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>]. This study is aimed at identifying and functionalizing the characterization of isolated probiotics from commercially available green and black olives.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec2">
      <title>2. Materials and Methods</title>
      <p>The research work was done in the Microbiology &amp; Biotechnology Laboratory at Fatima Jinnah Women University, Rawalpindi. The work is divided into two phases: isolation and identification of bacteria and physiological characterization of probiotic isolates.</p>
      <sec id="sec2.1">
        <title>2.1. Sample Collection</title>
        <p>Fermented pitted Spanish green and black olive samples of the same brand (Figaro Company) were purchased from local superstores located in the Pakistan Aeronautical Complex (PAC), Karma, and Rawalpindi. The sealed fermented green and black olives bottles were opened in sterile condition near a flame to avoid contamination. Using sterile forceps, a few olives from the bottles were taken out and then minced inside a mortar and pestle after disinfecting it with an ethanol (70%) swab. Before being minced, big chunks of both black and green olives were kept inside sterile universal bottles with the help of sterile forceps; later, the olive mince from the mortar was also collected in universal bottles and infused in autoclaved distilled water using a sterile spatula. The brine from black and green olive samples was also stored in a sterile universal bottle following the method of Doulgeraki et al. [<xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>] with slight workable modifications. All the universal bottles containing olive and brine samples were stored in the refrigerator for isolation.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2.2">
        <title>2.2. Isolation of Probiotics</title>
        <p>Strains from olives were isolated on selective de Man, Rogosa, and Sharpe (MRS) media (Merck Millipore Germany, catalog # 110660) [<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>, <xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>]. Isolations from chunks, brine, and minced fermented black and green olives were done on MRS media by the spread plate method [<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>]. Isolates were characterized by morphological, biochemical, and physiological characteristics.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2.3">
        <title>2.3. Morphological and Biochemical Characterizations</title>
        <p>Morphological characterization was studied through the gram staining technique by Hans Christian Gram [<xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>]. The catalase test [<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], Simmons citrate test [<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>, <xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>], methyl red and Voges&#x2013;Proskauer's test [<xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>], indole production test [<xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>, <xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>], and oxidation fermentation test [<xref rid="B43" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>, <xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>] were performed for biochemical characterization of the isolates. The identification of the isolated bacteria was done by the Standard API 50-CHL system [<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2.4">
        <title>2.4. Physiological Characterization</title>
        <sec id="sec2.4.1">
          <title>2.4.1. Determination of Optimal Temperature</title>
          <p>Isolated bacteria were incubated at 25&#xB0;C, 37&#xB0;C, and 45&#xB0;C for 24 hours to determine their optimal temperature for growth, and the results were calculated by the spectrometric method at 600&#x2009;nm [<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>].</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.2">
          <title>2.4.2. Determination of Optimal pH</title>
          <p>Fresh bacterial cells were grown for 24 hours at pH ranges of 4&#x2013;9 to determine whether the isolates were acidophilic, neutrophilic, or alkaliphilic. Optical density was measured by spectroscopic readings at 600&#x2009;nm [<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>].</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.3">
          <title>2.4.3. The Antibiotic Sensitivity Assay</title>
          <p>An antibiotic susceptibility test was conducted using the disc diffusion method [<xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>]. Fresh overnight cultures of bacterial isolates were spread onto Mueller-Hinton (MH) agar media plates (recommended by the National Committee for Clinical Laboratory Standards (NCCLS, CLSI (2018)), and 10 antibiotic discs were placed on the media plates and incubated at 37&#xB0;C for 24&#x2009;h. The antibiotics used were amoxicillin (10&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), gentamicin (10&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), streptomycin (10&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), tetracycline (30&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), kanamycin (30&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), imipenem (10&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), chloramphenicol (30&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), bacitracin (10&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g), erythromycin (15&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g) and neomycin (30&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>g).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.4">
          <title>2.4.4. Antimicrobial Activity</title>
          <p>The antimicrobial activity of all isolates against indicator bacteria was determined by the agar-well diffusion method [<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>]. <italic>Pseudomonas geniculate</italic>, <italic>Microbacterium oxydans</italic>, <italic>Bacillus subtilis</italic>, <italic>Streptomyces laurentii</italic>, <italic>Klebiella pneumonia</italic>, <italic>Bacillus pumilus</italic>, <italic>Bacillus cereus</italic>, <italic>Acaligens facelis</italic>, <italic>Enterococcus faecium</italic>, and <italic>Enterococcus facelis</italic> were used as indicator bacteria (obtained from the Microbiology &amp; Biotechnology Lab, FJWU) against which the antimicrobial activity of isolated strains was assayed. Each test string was inoculated into 5&#x2009;ml of nutrient broth and incubated at 37&#xB0;C for 24 hours on a shaking incubator at 150&#x2009;rpm. After incubation, each culture was centrifuged at 10000&#x2009;rpm for 5 minutes to obtain a cell-free supernatant. Supernatants of isolated species were identified for antibacterial activity besides indicator bacteria. For antimicrobial activity identification, Mueller-Hinton agar medium (for antimicrobial testing) was prepared, autoclaved, and poured separately into the sterile Petri dishes. By the spread plate method, the plates were inoculated with indicator bacterial suspension. Five wells, each with an 8&#x2009;mm diameter, were generated in every nutrient agar plate, and the base of each well was sealed with soft agar (0.7%) plugs. To identify the antibacterial activity of probiotic isolates, 100&#x2009;<italic>&#x3BC;</italic>l of cell-free supernatants of probiotic strain was added to the well. The plates were incubated for 24&#x2009;h at 37&#xB0;C, and the diameter of the zone of inhibition was measured in millimeters on both nutrient agar and MH agar media.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.5">
          <title>2.4.5. Salt Tolerance Assay</title>
          <p>Isolate tolerance to NaCl was determined by supplementing nutrient broth with varying salt concentrations ranging from 1 to 11% [<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>].</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.6">
          <title>2.4.6. Organic Acid Production Assay</title>
          <p>Lactic acid bacteria are known for the production of organic acids, specifically lactic acids. Isolates were considered to have the same property, and to determine this ability, an acid production assay was conducted [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>]. The brine in which fermented green olives and black olives were stored was also assayed for organic acid produced by the isolated strains inhabiting the brine at the beginning, in the middle, and at the end of the research work. Powdered skim milk was purchased from the local market. Autoclaved distilled water was taken and mixed with 10% of powdered skim milk to make sterile skim milk with a pH of 6.68. Five ml of skimmed milk was inoculated with a 24&#x2009;h fresh culture of isolated bacteria and incubated at 37&#xB0;C for 24, 48, and 72&#x2009;h. After incubation, coagulated skim milk was filtered, and the pH of each filtrate was measured with a digital electrode pH meter for lactic acid production. The filtrate was also titrated with 0.1&#x2009;N NaOH, and organic acid production was quantified in terms of percentage strength.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec2.4.7">
          <title>2.4.7. Bile Salt Tolerance Assay</title>
          <p>The ability of isolated species to survive and grow in the presence of bile salts was investigated by growing the isolated strains with different bile salt concentrations (0.1, 0.3, 0.5, and 1%) in nutrient broth as described by Dunne et al. <italic>[</italic><xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">47</xref><italic>]</italic>. The broth was incubated for 4&#x2009;h at 37&#xB0;C, and the optical density (OD) of the cultures was then measured at 600&#x2009;nm. The viability and growth of isolates at this concentration showed tolerance of isolated strains within the gastrointestinal tract of humans [<xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>, <xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>].</p>
        </sec>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2.5">
        <title>2.5. Tolerance to Simulated Gastric Juice</title>
        <p>Stimulated gastric juice tolerance was determined by the method described by Graciela and Maria [<xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>]. Stimulated gastric juice was freshly prepared and sterilized by filter sterilization.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2.6">
        <title>2.6. Numerical Taxonomy of Probiotic Isolates</title>
        <p>Similarity among isolates was checked by taking data from fifty (50) different biochemical sugar fermentation tests from the API CH 50 kit and converting them into binary data (0 or 1) for negative or positive test results, respectively, using PAST (Paleontological Statistics Software Package for Education and Data Analysis software). Similarities amongst the strains were estimated using the Jacquard coefficient, and the unweighted average linkage gave the cluster.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec3">
      <title>3. Results</title>
      <sec id="sec3.1">
        <title>3.1. Isolation and Identification of Potential Probiotic Bacteria</title>
        <p>In the present study, probiotics were isolated from commercially available fermented black and green olives from the Figaro Company. Fermenter probiotic bacteria were isolated on an MRS medium, and a total of 12 isolates were obtained from brine, chunks, and suspension from minced olives (<xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). As shown in <xref rid="tab1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>, potential probiotic bacteria were isolated from black and green olives. Identification was done by cell and colony morphology, which showed diversity ranging from 0.5 to 11.5&#x2009;mm in size, circular to irregular shape, and white to pale color. All the isolated probiotic bacteria were stained as Gram-positive, and most of them were rod-shaped.</p>
        <p>For <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus, Lactococcus lactis,</italic> and <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic>, the results of biochemical tests showed characteristics similar to those of reported probiotics, for example, not being catalase producers (anaerobes or facultative anaerobes that only ferment and do not respire using oxygen as a terminal electron acceptor), being nonspore formers, and having a thick peptidoglycan cell wall structure (gram-positive). The isolates ferment glucose during anaerobic incubation as well as during aerobic incubation, but no gas was produced during the 24&#x2009;h of incubation, both under aerobic and anaerobic conditions (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). Whereas the isolates were identified by carbohydrate fermentation patterns on the API 50 CH panel, the carbohydrate fermentation ability of the isolated strains was analyzed against frothy nine different sugars (<xref rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). The heat map summarizes the sugar fermentation pattern of probiotic bacteria from fermented olives and groups the strains based on similarities and differences in the sugar fermentation profile shown in <xref rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>. The species-level identification was done using API web50 CHL v5.1 (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.2">
        <title>3.2. Characterization of Optimal Temperature and pH</title>
        <p>
<italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 from green olives and <italic>Lactoccocus lactis</italic> MB418 from black olives grew optimally at 25&#xB0;C, and <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 showed optimal growth at 37&#xB0;C (<xref rid="fig4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4</xref>). Optimal growth of <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421, <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417, and <italic>Lactoccocus lactis</italic> MB418 was observed at pH&#x2009;7, showing the neutrophilic behavior of the isolates (<xref rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">Figure 5</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.3">
        <title>3.3. Evaluation of Antibiotic Susceptibility Profile</title>
        <p>The antibiotic susceptibility of isolates to various antibiotic discs by disc diffusion assay was determined in terms of standard inhibitory zones, and the results are shown in <xref rid="tab4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>. All the isolated strains were sensitive to imipenem. <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 and <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 showed resistance to amoxicillin.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.4">
        <title>3.4. Antimicrobial Activity</title>
        <p>The isolates were assessed for antimicrobial properties and tested negative on nutrient agar medium against <italic>Bacillus cereus</italic> MB401, <italic>Streptomyces laurentii</italic> MB319, <italic>Klebsiella pneumoniae</italic> MB081, and <italic>Acaligens facelis</italic> MB090.</p>
        <p>On Mueller-Hinton (MH) agar medium, isolates behaved differently as compared to nutrient agar medium. This might be due to the MH medium, which is the standard medium for the performance of such tests in microbiology laboratories. <italic>Lactobacillus crispatis</italic> MB417 and <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418 isolated from black olives were sensitive to <italic>Streptomyces laurentii</italic> MB319. Isolates depicted no inhibitory zone against <italic>Microbacterium oxydans</italic> MB325<italic>, Klebsiella pneumoniae</italic> MB081, and <italic>Acaligens facelis</italic> MB090, but showed antimicrobial activity against <italic>Bacillus cereus</italic> MB401, <italic>Bacillus subtilis</italic> MB405 and <italic>Streptomyces laurentii</italic> MB319. The growth of <italic>Enterococcus faecium</italic> JH22 and <italic>Enterococcus facelis</italic> OGRE1 was tested on Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) agar medium. <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 produced an inhibitory zone against <italic>Enterococcus facelis</italic> OGRE1.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.5">
        <title>3.5. Bile Salt Tolerance Assay</title>
        <p>Isolated probiotics were able to tolerate 0.1-1% bile salt and flourish at 0.3% gastrointestinal bile salt concentration (<xref rid="fig6" ref-type="fig">Figure 6</xref><bold>)</bold>. The isolates <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 and <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418 grew optimally at 0.3% bile salt concentration with a gradual decrease to higher concentrations that were 0.5% and 1.0%, whereas <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 showed a linearly increasing growth trend starting from 0.1 to 1% bile salt concentration.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.6">
        <title>3.6. Tolerance to Simulated Gastric Juice</title>
        <p>Potential probiotic bacterial isolates were able to tolerate the acidic pH (2) of stimulated gastric juice through the course of incubation, but some isolates were unable to withstand such harsh conditions in the gut. Colony-forming units per milliliter (CFU/ml) were calculated for black and green olive isolates (<xref rid="fig7" ref-type="fig">Figure 7</xref>). <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 showed a gradual reducing trend during incubation inside the artificially stimulated gastric juice; while <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB18 showed the same reducing viability trend till 90&#x2009;min of incubation (might be the acclimatization time). The viability count increased at 120&#x2009;min of incubation and decreased when measured after 24&#x2009;h of incubation. <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 showed a parabolic endurance pattern peaking at 90&#x2009;min of incubation in simulated gastric juice.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.7">
        <title>3.7. Salt Tolerance</title>
        <p>Following the assessment of salt tolerance, isolates were shown to be resistant to high NaCl concentrations (<xref rid="tab5" ref-type="table">Table 5</xref>), even at a 9% concentration of salt, especially the black olive isolate <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417. Isolates <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418 and <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 endure up to 8% and 7% NaCl concentrations, respectively.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3.8">
        <title>3.8. Quantification of Organic Acid Production</title>
        <p>All three isolates exhibited the capability to coagulate skim milk and produce organic acid under gradually decreasing pH (<xref rid="tab6" ref-type="table">Table 6</xref>). Against 0.1&#x2009;M NaOH, <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 produced elevated organic acids during 24 to 72&#x2009;h. Similar results were shown by <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421, but in the case of <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418 decreased with time, measured at 24, 48, and 72&#x2009;h of incubation. The organic acid production ability of residing potential probiotic bacteria inside the brine (in which fermented olives were stored) was assayed during research work, that is, at the beginning (1st stage), in the middle (2nd stage), and at the end of practical work (3rd stage). During incubation, the pH and acidity of the brine remained unchanged; this could be because of the refrigerated storage of the fermented olives (<xref rid="tab7" ref-type="table">Table 7</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec4">
      <title>4. Discussion</title>
      <p>The present study was conducted to isolate and characterize the potential probiotic isolates from fermented black and green olives. Table olives are the best source of probiotic bacteria [<xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>]. Table olives are considered functional foods because of their nutritional value related to the presence of phenolic compounds and monounsaturated fatty acids [<xref rid="B54" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>].</p>
      <p>The oxidation-fermentation test in this study identified that the isolated bacteria as facultative anaerobes about their ability to perform fermentation in aerobic as well as anaerobic conditions. The results indicated that all the isolates were able to conduct fermentation during aerobic conditions. All the isolates ferment lactose to produce acid, which in turn changes the color of the medium from green to yellow, showing the isolates were fermenters. Positive fermentation ability was also reported by other scientists on lactic acid bacteria from table olives [<xref rid="B55" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>] and lactic acid bacterial isolates from yogurt [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>].</p>
      <p>The study showed that most of the isolates were mesophiles, as they prefer 37 &#xB0;C, but some of the isolates were slightly thermophilic. Isolates from another study on green Algerian olives demonstrated tolerance to temperatures ranging from 15 to 45&#xB0;C [<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>, <xref rid="B58" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>].</p>
      <p>In the case of the isolation of probiotics from yogurt, isolates could tolerate pH up to 2.5 with good growth, but according to the current study, after pH&#x2009;4, growth was very low. This was most likely because isolates from yogurt or dairy origins are more adapted to low pH than isolates from vegetables and meat (due to the source's lactose content, which is then converted to lactic acid after fermentation) [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>]. <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417, <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418, and <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 showed good growth at pH&#x2009;7, depicting their mesophilic nature. Similarly, mesophilic (pH&#x2009;6.5-7.5) strains of lactic acid bacteria were reported from various research data [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>].</p>
      <p>The genomes of probiotic bacterial isolates may contain many antimicrobial resistance genes, <italic>van(X)</italic>, <italic>van(E)</italic>, <italic>gyr(A)</italic>, and <italic>tet(M)</italic> genes that code for resistance to the respective vancomycin, ciprofloxacin, and tetracycline antibiotics [<xref rid="B60" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B62" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>]. As a key feature, a good probiotic candidate should not possess or acquire any antibiotic-resistance genes. This research found that most of the isolates were resistant to amoxicillin. This might be because of the wide and nonspecific use of antibiotics that could subsidize the propagation of resistance in the bacterial population used for the fermentation of olives [<xref rid="B63" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>]. Along with this, in Pakistan, the use of unprescribed antibiotics is a common practice, which results in the development of resistance to many of the pathogenic bacteria from antibiotics, including amoxicillin. Whereas, it was also reported that all of the probiotic isolates were sensitive to streptomycin, imipenem, and chloramphenicol.</p>
      <p>Out of a total of 60 combinations of tests for bacteriocin production activity of six isolates against 10 different indicator bacteria, only two gave positive results on nutrient agar medium. This finding was comparable with some other reported probiotic strains, <italic>L. caseishirota</italic>, <italic>L. paracasei</italic> sub spp. <italic>tolerans</italic>, <italic>L. plantarum</italic> [<xref rid="B64" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>, <xref rid="B65" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>], and <italic>L. fermentum</italic> [<xref rid="B66" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>, <xref rid="B67" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>], which also did not produce bacteriocin against other bacteria. These results concluded that there is no existence of bacteriocin-like action [<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>], and inhibition of surrounding microbes was due to the acidic environment produced by probiotic LAB strains [<xref rid="B69" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>].</p>
      <p>The present study showed that all isolates were able to tolerate salt concentrations up to 7%, but few were able to tolerate high concentrations. All isolates showed maximum growth with a 1% NaCl concentration, which is consistent with the findings of other studies [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B70" ref-type="bibr">70</xref>].</p>
      <p>In the present study, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, and 1% bile salt concentrations were used for the growth of potential probiotic isolates. In a healthy human, 0.3% bile salt is present in the GIT, and for any bacteria to be used in probiotic production, it must tolerate this bile concentration [<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>, <xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>, <xref rid="B71" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>, <xref rid="B72" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>]. All of the black olive isolates and a few of the green olive isolates gave maximum growth on 0.3% bile salt, and almost all of the isolates tolerate this bile concentration (up to 1%). This was because of a vital characteristic of <italic>Lactobacilli</italic> which enabled them to survive, grow, and exert their action in the GIT due to the action of bile salt hydrolase, thus reducing the toxic side effects of bile salts. Along with this, some components of food protect and promote resistance among strains to bile salts [<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>].</p>
      <p>To achieve health benefits, probiotic foods must comprise an adequate amount of live bacteria, at least 10<sup>6-7</sup>&#x2009;CFU/g [<xref rid="B73" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. The ability to tolerate harsh conditions and viable cell counts of isolated potential probiotic bacteria were checked while incubating in artificial gastric juice with pH&#x2009;2.2 for 30, 60, 90, 120 minutes, and 24&#x2009;h. The experiments yielded &#x2264;10<sup>5</sup> bacterial counts, and recent studies have provided significant data on the valuable immunological effects derived from dead probiotic cells [<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>]. The viability of isolates from black olives (<italic>Lactoccocus lactis</italic> MB418) and green olives (MB422) gradually decreased with incubation time, while black olive isolates MB416, and <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417; and green olive isolates MB424, MB422 showed stability up to 24&#x2009;h of incubation in acidic gastric juice. Results of the experiment predict that gastric juice in the GIT has fewer effects that could be hostile to most of the probiotic isolates [<xref rid="B74" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>]. Similar viability counts (3.4-5.6 logs CFU/ml) for the fermented olive isolates from the present study were also observed previously for <italic>L. plantarum, L. pentosus,</italic> and <italic>L. paraplantarum</italic>, showing 3-6 log CFU/ml viable bacterial cells [<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>].</p>
      <p>In natural olives, organic acids can be added to create an optimal initial pH for the proliferation of LAB [<xref rid="B69" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>, <xref rid="B75" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. Olive samples used in the study also contained added lactic, ascorbic, and citric acids before packaging. The organic acid production ability of probiotic bacteria present inside the brine of black and green olives was determined to have an idea of the shelf life of the fermented food, which can be spoiled in terms of taste, texture, and smell due to altered lactic acid production. The Acidity of the brine of black and green olives remained the same throughout, but the brine of green olives was more acidic with a lower pH as compared to black olives. Because green olives were un-ripened, the bacteria employed for their fermentation had to work more to make them less bitter than for fully ripened black olives, for example, maturation of olives will condition phenolic compounds, sugar, and cell wall permeability [<xref rid="B69" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>].</p>
      <p>In our study, the data analysis showed that one of the isolates, MB422, from green olives, is the least similar to the rest of the isolates. Isolates from black olives MB416 and green olives <italic>Carnobacterium divergens</italic> MB421 showed 84% similarity, which is 72% similar to <italic>Lactococcus lactis</italic> MB418. The cluster is 60% similar to other clusters, such as MB424 and <italic>Lactobacillus crispatus</italic> MB417 (75% similar).</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec5">
      <title>5. Conclusion</title>
      <p>It is concluded that isolated potential probiotic bacteria are well adapted to the olive surface or brine. Thus, the olives, either green or black, contained good microbial flora of probiotics and could be consumed as an effective probiotic food. As a result, the isolates from our study, specifically MB417, MB418, MB421, and MB424, demonstrated relatively good antipathogenic activity and survival in harsh conditions, suggesting that they could be considered viable &#x201C;next-generation&#x201D; probiotic candidates, which could be beneficial to the pharmaceutical industry.</p>
      <sec id="sec5.1">
        <title>5.1. Future Applications</title>
        <p>It would be advantageous to conduct a detailed study on the identification of isolates using molecular methods. The use of bacteriocin producers as starters is of considerable interest because bacteriocin production is a significant factor that helps to promote the safety and quality of fermented table olives and can also be used as natural antibiotics.</p>
      </sec>
