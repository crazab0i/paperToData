      <abstract>
        <p><italic>Brucella abortus</italic> is the main causative agent of brucellosis in cattle, leading to severe economic consequences in agriculture and affecting public health. The zoonotic nature of the infection increases the need to control the spread and dynamics of outbreaks in animals with the incorporation of high resolution genotyping techniques. Based on such methods, <italic>B. abortus</italic> is currently divided into three clades, A, B, and C. The latter includes subclades C1 and C2. This study presents the results of whole-genome sequencing of 49 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains isolated in Kazakhstan between 1947 and 2015 and of 36 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains of various geographic origins isolated from 1940 to 2004. <italic>In silico</italic> Multiple Locus Sequence Typing (MLST) allowed to assign strains from Kazakhstan to subclades C1 and to a much lower extend C2. Whole-genome Single-Nucleotide Polymorphism (wgSNP) analysis of the 46 strains of subclade C1 with strains of worldwide origins showed clustering with strains from neighboring countries, mostly North Caucasia, Western Russia, but also Siberia, China, and Mongolia. One of the three Kazakhstan strains assigned to subclade C2 matched the <italic>B. abortus</italic> S19 vaccine strain used in cattle, the other two were genetically close to the 104&#x2009;M vaccine strain. Bayesian phylodynamic analysis dated the introduction of <italic>B. abortus</italic> subclade C1 into Kazakhstan to the 19th and early 20th centuries. We discuss this observation in view of the history of population migrations from Russia to the Kazakhstan steppes.</p>
      </abstract>

    <sec sec-type="intro" id="sec1">
      <label>1.</label>
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>Brucellosis is a bacterial zoonotic disease caused by species belonging to the genus <italic>Brucella</italic> and results in high economic impact (<xref rid="ref35" ref-type="bibr">McDermott et al., 2013</xref>). <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. may be transmitted to humans resulting in a severe disease requiring a specific and long-term antibiotic treatment with significant burden to public health systems (<xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">Franc et al., 2018</xref>). The genus <italic>Brucella</italic> currently contains 12 validly published species (<xref rid="ref40" ref-type="bibr">Olsen and Palmer, 2014</xref>; <xref rid="ref69" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore and Foster, 2021</xref>; <xref rid="ref39" ref-type="bibr">Occhialini et al., 2022</xref>). <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic>, <italic>Brucella abortus</italic>, <italic>B. suis</italic>, <italic>B. ovis</italic>, <italic>B. neotomae,</italic> and <italic>B. canis</italic> are often referred to as &#x201C;classical&#x201D; <italic>Brucella</italic> species in the literature. These have been identified between 1887 and 1968 and were differentiated on the basis of phenotypic traits and host preference (<xref rid="ref8" ref-type="bibr">Buddle, 1956</xref>; <xref rid="ref62" ref-type="bibr">Stoenner and Lackman, 1957</xref>; <xref rid="ref9" ref-type="bibr">Carmichael and Bruner, 1968</xref>; <xref rid="ref36" ref-type="bibr">Moreno et al., 2002</xref>). Since 2007, the wider use of genetic methods of identification and differentiation has led to the identification of <italic>B. ceti</italic>, <italic>B. pinnipedialis</italic>, <italic>B. microti</italic>, <italic>B. inopinata</italic>, <italic>B. papionis,</italic> and <italic>B. vulpis</italic>, which also preserve the tradition of naming the species in accordance with their original host (with the exception of <italic>B. inopinata</italic> isolated from a breast implant; <xref rid="ref17" ref-type="bibr">Foster et al., 2007</xref>; <xref rid="ref49" ref-type="bibr">Scholz et al., 2008</xref>, <xref rid="ref50" ref-type="bibr">2010</xref>; <xref rid="ref68" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2014</xref>; <xref rid="ref51" ref-type="bibr">Scholz et al., 2016</xref>). The use of molecular methods made it possible to identify other potential new species recovered not only from mammals but also from amphibians, reptiles, and fish (<xref rid="ref60" ref-type="bibr">Soler-Llorens et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref2" ref-type="bibr">Al Dahouk et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="ref15" ref-type="bibr">Eisenberg et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="ref37" ref-type="bibr">M&#xFC;hldorfer et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="ref16" ref-type="bibr">Eisenberg et al., 2020</xref>), leading to further expansion of our knowledge of the genus <italic>Brucella</italic>. Based on whole-genome comparisons, a merge of the <italic>Ochrobactrum</italic> and <italic>Brucella</italic> genus was recently proposed (<xref rid="ref24" ref-type="bibr">Hordt et al., 2020</xref>; <xref rid="ref32" ref-type="bibr">Leclercq et al., 2020</xref>). Nonetheless, the greatest impact on public health and livestock infections around the globe have to date only been caused by <italic>B. melitensis, B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. suis</italic> (<xref rid="ref21" ref-type="bibr">Godfroid et al., 2011</xref>; <xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">Franc et al., 2018</xref>). An increase of human cases due to <italic>B. canis</italic> is currently suspected (<xref rid="ref22" ref-type="bibr">Hensel et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="ref72" ref-type="bibr">Zhou et al., 2020</xref>).</p>
      <p>The majority of brucellosis cases are reported in the Mediterranean countries, South and Central America, Africa, Asia, Arabian Peninsula, Indian subcontinent, Eastern Europe, and the Middle East (<xref rid="ref41" ref-type="bibr">Pappas et al., 2006</xref>; <xref rid="ref38" ref-type="bibr">Nicoletti, 2010</xref>). Over the past decade, there has been a decline in incidence in many regions previously considered to be highly endemic, but also new reservoirs have been identified, such as in Africa and the Middle East, possibly resulting from a better implementation of diagnostic methods (<xref rid="ref67" ref-type="bibr">Wang and Jiang, 2020</xref>). The real incidence of the disease in the mentioned regions is most probably largely underestimated because registration is based on passively collected data (<xref rid="ref13" ref-type="bibr">Dean et al., 2012</xref>).</p>
      <p>In Kazakhstan, brucellosis remains a major livestock and public health problem. More than 1,300 cases of human brucellosis are registered annually corresponding to an incidence of 7.6 per 100,000 inhabitants. Seropositivity to <italic>Brucella</italic> antigens in cattle and small cattle is 0.6% and 0.4%, respectively (<xref rid="ref10" ref-type="bibr">Charypkhan and Ruegg, 2022</xref>).</p>
      <p>The high zoonotic potential, re-emergence of the infection in previously disease-free regions, and identification of new reservoirs underscore the need for modern molecular epidemiology approaches such as genotyping to trace source reservoirs and paths of introduction. For <italic>Brucella</italic> genotyping at subspecies level, two methods are most widely used, Multilocus Sequence Typing (MLST) and Multiple Loci VNTR polymorphisms (Variable Number of Tandem Repeats, MLVA; <xref rid="ref70" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). Robust phylogenetic relationships can be obtained from nucleotide sequencing data owing to the strictly clonal evolution of classical <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. (<xref rid="ref69" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore and Foster, 2021</xref>). The first MLST scheme included nine loci, seven housekeeping genes, the outer membrane protein gene <italic>omp25</italic> and int-hyp. Six <italic>B. abortus</italic> MLST9 sequence types (STs) were initially described (<xref rid="ref71" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2007</xref>). Twenty-six <italic>B. abortus</italic> MLST9 STs are recorded in the current version of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. MLST database.<xref rid="fn0005" ref-type="fn"><sup>1</sup></xref> Including 12 housekeeping genes in the genotyping resulted in the more discriminatory MLST21. Thirty MLST21 STs were initially described in 172 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains defining three clades (A, B, C including C1 and C2; <xref rid="ref70" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2016</xref>) and 43 STs are recorded in the current version of the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. MLST database (see text footnote 1). The rare clades A and B include strains originating almost exclusively from Africa and allow defining the most ancestral nodes within the <italic>B. abortus</italic> phylogeny. Clades C1 and C2 are found on five continents and their presence in Africa seem to result from livestock importation (<xref rid="ref70" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). Several MLVA genotyping schemes have been proposed for <italic>Brucella</italic>, one most commonly used scheme is MLVA16 (<xref rid="ref52" ref-type="bibr">Scholz and Vergnaud, 2013</xref>). MLVA16 combines two panels of markers: one VNTR panel with a low discriminatory ability allows determining the species and main genetic lineages, and the other VNTR panel with a high discriminatory ability but low phylogenetic value allows differentiating strains in local outbreaks (<xref rid="ref52" ref-type="bibr">Scholz and Vergnaud, 2013</xref>; <xref rid="ref69" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore and Foster, 2021</xref>). The current version (Brucella v4_6_5) of the Brucella MLVA data hosted by MLVAbank<xref rid="fn0006" ref-type="fn"><sup>2</sup></xref> contains <italic>in vitro</italic> data derived from more than 1,400 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains. The eight VNTR loci with the low discriminatory power (MLVA8) cluster <italic>B. abortus</italic> into 28 genotypes represented by at least two entries. MLVA alone is not suitable for phylogenetic reconstructions because of the high homoplasy at VNTR loci (<xref rid="ref29" ref-type="bibr">Keim et al., 2004</xref>), but interestingly, MLVA using the low discriminatory markers (MLVA8, MLVA10 or MLVA11) and MLST clustering are highly congruent allowing to indirectly deduce phylogenetic signal from MLVA data (<xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). Consequently, MLVA may constitute a first-line assay with low phylogenetic resolution until whole-genome sequencing (WGS) becomes sufficiently low-cost to be directly used as first line assay. MLVA genotypes can be readily deduced from WGS data with sequencing reads longer than 200&#x2009;bp so that the highly discriminatory MLVA loci can also constitute a strain identity control tool (<xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="ref23" ref-type="bibr">Holzer et al., 2021</xref>; <xref rid="ref42" ref-type="bibr">Pelerito et al., 2021</xref>).</p>
      <p>The availability of whole-genome sequence (WGS) data opened the way to whole-genome and core genome MLST assays (wgMLST and cgMLST, respectively) with much higher resolution and phylogenetic value than these classical genotyping tools (<xref rid="ref25" ref-type="bibr">Janowicz et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="ref1" ref-type="bibr">Abdel-Glil et al., 2022</xref>). Genome-scale MLST assays as well as genome-wide SNP-genotyping confirmed the presence of the four major clusters A, B, C1, C2 in a collection of <italic>B. abortus</italic> with strains collected at a global scale (<xref rid="ref69" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore and Foster, 2021</xref>; <xref rid="ref1" ref-type="bibr">Abdel-Glil et al., 2022</xref>).</p>
      <p>The current knowledge on the genetic diversity of <italic>B. abortus</italic> circulating in Kazakhstan is limited to MLVA-typing data. Interestingly, the MLVA genotyping investigations demonstrated low genetic diversity among strains circulating in Kazakhstan (<xref rid="ref55" ref-type="bibr">Shevtsov et al., 2015</xref>; <xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">Daugaliyeva et al., 2018</xref>). Inclusion in the analysis of strains deposited since 1935 made it possible to describe the predominance of <italic>B. abortus</italic> clade C1, with genetic proximity of the majority of strains to Russian, Chinese and European strains. A few strains were assigned to <italic>B. abortus</italic> clade C2 (<xref rid="ref56" ref-type="bibr">Shevtsova et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">Daugaliyeva et al., 2018</xref>). In order to better understand the population structure and origins of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in Kazakhstan, we selected 49 representative strains from Kazakhstan for whole-genome sequencing. We also selected 36 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains representing the genetic diversity previously uncovered by MLVA within the historical collection maintained by Inrae, Nouzilly, France (<xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>) to determine phylogenic relations among <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains and to evaluate the usefulness of genomic data in the epidemiological control of the infection in a highly endemic region, such as Kazakhstan.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="sec2">
      <label>2.</label>
      <title>Materials and methods</title>
      <sec id="sec3">
        <label>2.1.</label>
        <title><italic>Brucella abortus</italic> DNA and selection of strains for WGS</title>
        <p>DNA analyzed were selected among 213 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains isolated in Kazakhstan between 1943 and 2015 from bovine clinical material (aborted fetuses, lymph nodes, or whole blood). The strains were previously characterized by MLVA genotyping (<xref rid="ref55" ref-type="bibr">Shevtsov et al., 2015</xref>; <xref rid="ref56" ref-type="bibr">Shevtsova et al., 2016</xref>). The full MLVA assay (MLVA16) resolved 28 genotypes, 12 of which were represented by individual strains, the remaining genotypes were represented by up to 86 strains. The choice of strains for WGS was based on MLVA data, year and geographic origin. We selected 49 strains representing the 28 genotypes (up to eight strains per MLVA16 genotype). The selected strains originated from eight regions of Kazakhstan (<xref rid="SM5" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>; <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref>).</p>
        <p>Similarly, 212 <italic>B. abortus</italic> representative strains from the Inrae BCCN (Brucella Culture Collection Nouzilly) collected worldwide during the period 1976&#x2013;2006 were previously characterized by MLVA (<xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). Clade B and clade C represented 197 and 15 strains, respectively. A representative subset of 36 strains was selected for WGS (<xref rid="SM5" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec4">
        <label>2.2.</label>
        <title>Whole-genome sequencing</title>
        <p>Kazakhstan strain sequencing was performed on the MiSeq platform (Illumina) as recommended by the manufacturer (Illumina, CA, United States). Nextera XT DNA Library preparation Kit (Illumina, CA, United States) was used to prepare libraries with double barcoding. Paired-end libraries were sequenced using MiSeq Reagent Kit v3 (600&#x2009;cycles or 300&#x2009;bp read length). <italic>De novo</italic> assemblies were produced using SKESA version 2.3.0 (<xref rid="ref61" ref-type="bibr">Souvorov et al., 2018</xref>). The assemblies had an average of 49 contigs (range 33 to 88), an average N50 value of 171&#x2009;kb (range 75 to 260&#x2009;kb) and an average total assembly length of 3.25&#x2009;Mb (range 3.229&#x2013;3.256&#x2009;Mb).</p>
        <p>BCCN strains were sequenced by Genoscreen (Lille, France) using the MiSeq platform (Illumina). Read length was 250&#x2009;bp. Sequencing reads were assembled using SPAdes version 3.13 (<xref rid="ref4" ref-type="bibr">Bankevich et al., 2012</xref>). The assemblies had an average of 25 contigs (range 18 to 60), an average N50 value of 417&#x2009;kb (range 251 to 884&#x2009;kb) and an average total assembly length of 3.27&#x2009;Mb (range 3.260&#x2013;3.327&#x2009;Mb).</p>
        <p>Whole-genome MLST was run on genome assemblies using the BioNumerics &#x201C;MLST for WGS&#x201D; and &#x201C;WGS tools&#x201D; plugin and associated <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. scheme (Applied-Maths, Sint-Martens-Latem, Belgium). The plugins were also used to recover the MLST9 and MLST21 assignments (<xref rid="ref71" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2007</xref>, <xref rid="ref70" ref-type="bibr">2016</xref>) defined in the <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. database hosted by PubMLST (<xref rid="ref26" ref-type="bibr">Jolley et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="SM5" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>).</p>
        <p>Raw reads were deposited in the European Nucleotide Archive BioProject PRJNA892249 (Kazakhstan collection) and PRJNA901374 (BCCN collection, France). Individual sequence reads archives (SRA), bioproject and biosample accessions are indicated in <xref rid="SM5" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec5">
        <label>2.3.</label>
        <title>Whole-genome single-nucleotide polymorphism analysis</title>
        <p>All publicly available <italic>Brucella</italic> assemblies, sequence reads archive, and associated metadata, were downloaded from EBI ENA (read archives) or NCBI (assemblies) (last updated 2022-09-10). Sequence read archives were <italic>de novo</italic> assembled with SKESA. All assemblies were imported into BioNumerics version 8.1 (Applied-Maths, Sint-Martens-Latem, Belgium). The assemblies were used to produce artificial reads data sets (50&#x2009;bp long, 10x coverage) for SNP calling by read mapping using genome assembly GCA_000740155 (<italic>B. abortus</italic> strain Tulya) as reference genome (<xref rid="ref7" ref-type="bibr">Blouin et al., 2012</xref>; <xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). The BioNumerics parameters for reads mapping were 95% minimum sequence identity, minimum inter-SNP distance 12 base-pairs. Maximum parsimony trees were drawn within BioNumerics. The list of all public datasets evaluated is presented in <xref rid="SM6" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>. <xref rid="SM6" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref> includes comments facilitating the selection of strains, including &#x201C;duplicates&#x201D; (more than one dataset available for the same strain), &#x201C;redundant&#x201D; (coincident wgSNP genotype). Some datasets provided poor coverage or induced topological issues, due to sample mix or inappropriate assembly procedure.</p>
        <p>For input in BEAST version 1.10.4 (<xref rid="ref64" ref-type="bibr">Suchard et al., 2018</xref>), a table of SNP positions from selected strains was exported from BioNumerics and SNPs were concatenated as fasta files. BEAST was run under a general time-reversible model of nucleotide substitution with a gamma distribution between sites, a relaxed molecular clock, Bayesian skyline plot (BSP) demographic model, lognormal distribution for population sizes as previously described (<xref rid="ref27" ref-type="bibr">Kamath et al., 2016</xref>). The convergence of 20 independent runs with a chain length of 150 million was examined using Tracer v1.7 (<xref rid="ref46" ref-type="bibr">Rambaut et al., 2018</xref>). The selected runs were merged with a burning of 15 million using LogCombiner and TreeAnnotator (<xref rid="ref64" ref-type="bibr">Suchard et al., 2018</xref>). The resulting trees were visualized using FigTree v1.4.4 (<xref rid="ref45" ref-type="bibr">Rambaut, 2018</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec6">
        <label>2.4.</label>
        <title>Analysis of homoplasia in MLVA profiles</title>
        <p>The previously published MLVA16 profiles of 49 strains from Kazakhstan were used within BioNumerics for clustering based on categorical data distance measure and unweighted paired group with arithmetic means method (UPGMA). MLVA homoplasia was recognized when MLVA clustering was not congruent with the phylogeny deduced from wgSNP analysis.</p>

        <title>MLST assignment of the 49 <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> strains from Kazakhstan</title>
        <p>Four MLST9 STs were present, ST1 (two strains) ST2 (45 strains), ST5 (one strain), and ST119 (new MLST9 genotype, represented by one strain). ST2 and the closely related ST119 belong to subclade C1 whereas ST1 and ST5 belong to subclade C2 (<xref rid="ref71" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2007</xref>). Using the wgMLST analysis assay, alleles could be called at 3,312 up to 3,345 loci in the 46 clade C1 strains. Seven hundred and three loci were called in all 46 strains and were polymorphic. <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref> shows the clustering derived from the corresponding wgMLST character dataset of the 46 clade C1 strains.</p>

      <sec id="sec10">
        <label>3.3.</label>
        <title>Congruence between WGS phylogeny and MLVA clustering</title>
        <p>The wgSNP analysis grouped 31 strains from Kazakhstan into 8 clusters labeled WGS_1 to WGS_8 in <xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>; <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref>, which show a difference within each cluster of no more than 7 SNPs. Cluster WGS_1 comprised six &#x201C;historical&#x201D; strains lacking accompanying epizootological data, isolated in WKR, Akmola and Almaty regions between 1948 and 1970. The four strains identical by wgSNP from WGS_1 were also identical by MLVA16, while Kaz049 and Kaz046 differed at the highly variable Bruce07 locus. In spite of their very high wgSNP similarity, the strains were collected over a period of 13&#x2009;years in three regions of Kazakhstan. Previous reports described similar long term maintenance of highly similar wgSNP or wgMLST genotypes (<xref rid="ref20" ref-type="bibr">Garofolo et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="ref25" ref-type="bibr">Janowicz et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="ref3" ref-type="bibr">Allen et al., 2020</xref>; <xref rid="ref23" ref-type="bibr">Holzer et al., 2021</xref>). Cluster WGS_2 was formed by ten strains isolated from Kostanay region in the 2008&#x2013;2011 period and strain a_1 isolated in Almaty region in 2007. According to epidemiological data, nine out of ten strains from the Kostanay region represented four independent outbreaks. These strains represented three MLVA16 genotypes with a difference in the Bruce09 hypervariable locus. Cluster WGS_3 included two epidemiologically unrelated strains from EKR and Almaty regions, differing from each other by one SNPs, and by MLVA16 at the Bruce09 locus. Cluster WGS_4 was represented by two strains from the Kostanay region from two independent outbreaks which are identical in wgSNP and MLVA16. Cluster WGS_5 cluster was represented by two &#x201C;historical&#x201D; epidemiologically independent strains from WKR and Almaty regions identical in MLVA16 analysis and separated by two SNPs. Cluster WGS-6 combined two epidemiologically unrelated strains which are identical in their MLVA16 profiles and differ by three SNPs. Cluster WGS_7 combined two strains from the same outbreak in WKR, with an identical wgSNP genotype and differing in MLVA16 at locus Bruce07. Cluster WGS_8 combined four strains isolated in 2015 in the Akmola region, all from the same settlement and representing a single outbreak. The maximum difference between these strains is two SNPs, with all having an individual MLVA16 profile that differs in Bruce07 or Bruce09.</p>
        <p>According to clustering on the basis of MLVA data, 10 MLVA16 genotypes (out of 28 genotypes) combined two to eight strains (<xref rid="SM2" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S2</xref>). Homoplasia was suspected in five genotypes: MLVA_6 genotype includes two historical strains from Almaty (Kaz030 and Kaz041) and one recent strain from WKR (Kaz086). Kaz041 belongs to branch C, whereas Kaz086 and Kaz030 belong to E-III and E-IV, respectively, (<xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>; <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref>). Genotype MLVA_7 included two strains, Kaz052 and Kaz070 isolated from Akmola and WKR regions in 2015, and belonging to E-IV and E-II, respectively. Genotype MLVA_14 combined four historical strains isolated from WKR and Akmola part of cluster WGS_1 from branch C and strain a_112 isolated in Kostanay in 2011 from branch E-I. Genotype MLVA_16 combined seven strains from cluster WGS_2 in branch E-I isolated in Almaty and Kostanay between 2007 and 2011, and also historical strain Kaz046 isolated in Almaty in 1957, belonging to cluster WGS_1 branch C. The MLVA_19 genotype combined strain Kaz020 (branch E-I, isolated in 2015, EKR region) and historical strain Kaz029 from Atyrau region from branch E-III. These lack of congruence between MLVA16 typing and wgSNP phylogeny are due to minor variations in the most variable loci constituting panel2B, in agreement with previous observations regarding the instability of these loci (<xref rid="ref19" ref-type="bibr">Garcia-Yoldi et al., 2007</xref>; <xref rid="ref34" ref-type="bibr">Maquart et al., 2009</xref>; <xref rid="ref3" ref-type="bibr">Allen et al., 2020</xref>).</p>

        <label>3.7.</label>
        <title>Tentative dating of <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> introduction events in Kazakhstan</title>
        <p>As a preliminary attempt to date these introduction events, we applied BEAST to the previous selection of 236 <italic>B. abortus</italic> and to 193 <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains (<xref rid="SM6" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>). <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and <italic>B. abortus</italic> are closest relatives within genus <italic>Brucella</italic>. They share the same mutation inactivating the Entner&#x2013;Doudoroff pathway (EDP) for hexose catabolism, suggesting that their most recent common ancestor was already an obligate animal pathogen (<xref rid="ref33" ref-type="bibr">Machelart et al., 2020</xref>). The inclusion of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> allowed to take advantage of the WGS sequence data recovered from a well-dated Medieval sample (Sardinia, <italic>circa</italic> year 1,375&#x2009;CE), thus providing a key time point (<xref rid="ref28" ref-type="bibr">Kay et al., 2014</xref>). SNPs were called by mapping on genome accession GCA_000740155 from <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain Tulya (23,086 SNPs were identified). Representative dating estimates are included in <xref rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figures 3</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="fig5" ref-type="fig">5</xref>. The analysis suggested that currently circulating <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains in Kazakhstan originated from imports in the 19<sup>th</sup> and 20<sup>th</sup> century. The split toward the European sublineage in branch D would have occurred in year 1759&#x2013;1842. The Italian and Kazakhstan strains constituting branch A would share a most recent common ancestor <italic>circa</italic> year 1,641&#x2013;1762. The estimated molecular evolution rate is 0.3 SNP per year per genome (95% HPD&#x2014;highest probability density&#x2014;range 0.24&#x2013;0.45). A higher evolution rate of 0.46 SNP per year per genome (95% HPD range 0.30&#x2013;0.74) was previously proposed (<xref rid="ref27" ref-type="bibr">Kamath et al., 2016</xref>). The main differences in the two investigations were the collection of strains used (both <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in the present investigation versus North American <italic>B. abortus</italic> clade C2 only in Kamath et al.) and the inclusion in the present investigation of the Medieval sample.</p>

    <sec sec-type="discussions" id="sec15">
      <label>4.</label>
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>Brucellosis in cattle remains a major problem for cattle breeders in Kazakhstan. Genetic monitoring of genotypic dynamics is not included in the infection control strategy at the state level, but is implemented in the country through scientific grants and is mainly based on MLVA technology. This is the first study to describe the genetic diversity of <italic>B. abortus</italic> isolated between 1947 and 2015 in the territory of Kazakhstan using genome-wide sequence data that confirmed the presence of the two major subclades C1 and C2. Clades A and B were absent, in agreement with the previously published MLVA clustering analysis (<xref rid="ref56" ref-type="bibr">Shevtsova et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">Daugaliyeva et al., 2018</xref>) and well established very strong geographic association of clades A and B with East and West Africa, respectively, (<xref rid="ref70" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>).</p>
      <p>In this study, in five among the 10 MLVA16 genotypes represented by at least two strains, unrelated wgSNP genotypes were observed. The high percentage of homoplasia in the VNTR analysis is primarily due to the targeted selection of strains with identical MLVA16 from outbreaks which are unrelated by epidemiological data, and to the high level of homoplasia associated with the most variable VNTR loci. Four out of five cases of MLVA16 homoplasia involved combinations of the &#x201C;historical&#x201D; and currently circulating strains. Therefore, MLVA16 homoplasia seems higher in strains collected over an extended period of time. Eight wgSNP clusters were identical in MLVA16 or differed only in one or two hypervariable loci (Bruce07 or Bruce09), generally supporting the MLVA clustering. Previous studies of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains using whole-genome SNP analysis and MLVA demonstrated similar results in outbreak differentiation and detection of imported strains, with a conclusion of a need to use genome-wide SNPs for reliable phylogenetic inference (<xref rid="ref20" ref-type="bibr">Garofolo et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="ref3" ref-type="bibr">Allen et al., 2020</xref>; <xref rid="ref63" ref-type="bibr">Su&#xE1;rez-Esquivel et al., 2020</xref>; <xref rid="ref23" ref-type="bibr">Holzer et al., 2021</xref>). Comparable strain clustering for MLVA11 and wgSNP allows MLVA in combination with epidemiological data to be considered as a first choice of methods to select strains for WGS in highly endemic regions, but only WGS analyzes will provide sufficiently precise phylogenetic information and might progressively become the first choice methods if global sequencing costs keep decreasing.</p>
      <p>The identification of very closely related wgSNP genotypes in different regions with a difference of isolation by several years indicates a long-term circulation of <italic>B. abortus</italic> genotypes in Kazakhstan. Also, identification of the same wgSNP genotype cluster in unrelated outbreaks in the same region, such as Kostanay, indicates circulation of infected animals between farms. Thus, uncontrolled cattle trade and movement, as well as keeping animals from different farms on the same pastures, is postulated to be among main factors in the spread of the cattle brucellosis infection in Kazakhstan (<xref rid="ref65" ref-type="bibr">Syzdykov et al., 2014</xref>; <xref rid="ref11" ref-type="bibr">Charypkhan et al., 2019</xref>).</p>
      <p>The Bayesian phylodynamic approach suggested that <italic>B. abortus</italic> lineages currently circulating in Kazakhstan were introduced in the 19th-20th centuries from Europe, mainly from Russia (North Caucasia). It may be interesting to note that evolution of subclades C1 and C2 showed similarities in this respect. The import of clade C2 into the United States was dated to the end of the 17<sup>th</sup> century (<xref rid="ref27" ref-type="bibr">Kamath et al., 2016</xref>), with closest neighbor lineages corresponding to strains isolated in Western Europe.</p>
      <p>The introduction of <italic>B. abortus</italic> clade C1 in Kazakhstan might have happened during periods of human migration and because of the importation of numerous livestock for breeding with native breeds of cattle. Migration processes to Kazakhstan from the territory of the Russian Empire, and later the USSR, began with the accession of the northern territories of Kazakhstan in the Russian Empire in the 1730s and continued until the 1970s (<xref rid="ref44" ref-type="bibr">Qazaqstan_tarihy, 2018</xref>; <xref rid="ref43" ref-type="bibr">Presidential_Library_RF, 2022</xref>). The imperial period was characterized by mass migration of peasants, whose migration had an impact on the livestock breeding system of the nomadic people. Migrations had a wave-like character and were associated with the abolition of serfdom and the resettlement of free peasants from the European part of Russia since 1861. In 1889 the law on resettlement provided land plots and loans to peasant settlers. Crop failure and famine in European Russia and the Stolypin reforms of 1906&#x2013;1911 further stimulated these migrations (<xref rid="ref47" ref-type="bibr">Rather and Abdullah, 2018</xref>). In 1897 Russians made up 12 percent of the total population of Kazakhstan, i.e., 600,000 inhabitants. From the end of the 19th century to 1916, about 1,400,000 European Russians arrived in the Kazakh steppes, making up 40% of the population of the steppe regions of Kazakhstan (<xref rid="ref5" ref-type="bibr">Bell-Fialloff, 2016</xref>). There are no exact data on imported livestock for that period, however, there are some data indicating that people moved with their livestock, and original Kalmyk-cattle-breeds appeared in Kazakhstan along with immigrants from Voronezh, Stavropol, Astrakhan, and other provinces of southern Russia (<xref rid="ref6" ref-type="bibr">Belmont, 1960</xref>). During this period, many Kazakhs were forced to rebuild their traditional way of life with the transition to agriculture and a semi-nomadic lifestyle due to the seizure of land in favor of settlers for farming and taking rangelands to graze Russian riches&#x2019; cattle (<xref rid="ref48" ref-type="bibr">Sailaukyzy et al., 2018</xref>). In the structure of Kazakhs herds, the number of cattle had increased because of greater demand for cattle meat. At the end of the 19th and the beginning of the 20th century, selective transformation of aboriginal cattle began, for which a massive import of cattle from various regions of Russia was carried out. For example, to create the Alatau breed from 1904 to 1940, Swiss and Kostroma cattle were brought to Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan from the Smolensk, Sumy and Kostroma regions (<xref rid="ref59" ref-type="bibr">Soldatov, 2001</xref>). The Aulieatinskaya cattle breed has been developed since 1885 by crossing the Dutch Black Pied breed with aboriginal cattle and subsequent improvement in the 1930s of the 20th century by East Friesian bulls (<xref rid="ref14" ref-type="bibr">Dmitriev and Ernst, 1989</xref>). All these processes might have constituted opportunities for a wide distribution of European strains of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in Kazakhstan, but it is important to note that essentially subclade C1 strains contaminate Kazakhstan whereas Western Europe is associated with subclade C2. Subclade C1 is common in North Caucasus (<xref rid="ref31" ref-type="bibr">Kovalev et al., 2021</xref>). At the same time, the disastrous large-scale collectivization carried out in 1929&#x2013;1933 resulted in a four-fold reduction in the local cattle population (<xref rid="ref73" ref-type="bibr">Zhumasultanova, 2021</xref>), which could lead to a reduction in the historically circulating strains of <italic>B. abortus</italic>. The observed reduction in the genetic diversity of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains in Kazakhstan in the 21st century is possibly associated with the successful implementation of epidemic-control activities similar to methods implemented in the 1970&#x2013;1980s. During 4&#x2009;years, from 1981 to 1985, the incidence of brucellosis in cattle decreased from 3.5 to 2.2% and a reduction in the most affected areas was as high as 30% (<xref rid="ref54" ref-type="bibr">Shablov et al., 1988</xref>). Another factor for consideration is a two-fold reduction in the cattle population from 1991 to 2000.</p>
      <p>The isolation of two <italic>B. abortus</italic> subclade C2 strains Kaz025 and Kaz027 genetically closest to the <italic>B. abortus</italic> 104&#x2009;M vaccine strain is intriguing. We could not find records of the use of this vaccine in Kazakhstan. The <italic>B. abortus</italic> 104&#x2009;M strain was first isolated from an aborted fetus of cattle in the central European Russia in 1929, selected and proposed as a vaccine strain for human vaccination by Kh. S. Kotlyarova in 1950 (<xref rid="ref30" ref-type="bibr">Kotlyarova, 1950</xref>; <xref rid="ref57" ref-type="bibr">Shumilov et al., 1983</xref>). Despite proven immunogenicity, strain 104&#x2009;M did not find wide application in the USSR and was used in experimental vaccination of cattle and in limited production trials on small cattle. In this connection, the probability of importation of a vaccinated animal is low. We are more inclined toward the introduction of genetically close pathogenic strains. Expanding the results of genome-wide data on <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains isolated in the central European Russia will improve understanding of the origin of strains Kaz025 and Kaz027.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec16">
      <label>5.</label>
      <title>Concluding remarks</title>
      <p>The new data of various origins contributed in this report strengthen the strong association of clades A and B with East and West Africa, respectively. The topology of the observed phylogeny combined with human history is pointing to East Africa as current most parsimonious scenario for the origin of <italic>B. abortus</italic>. The WGS data analysis of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from Kazakhstan shows that currently circulating lineages were introduced only recently in Kazakhstan, most of them during the 19<sup>th</sup> or 20<sup>th</sup> century. The closest currently known lineages are present in Caucasia, in agreement with the history of recent population migrations. This recent introduction is reminiscent of the situation described in Costa Rica, in which WGS data analysis allowed to identify five independent introductions responsible for the current population structure of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in Costa Rica (<xref rid="ref63" ref-type="bibr">Su&#xE1;rez-Esquivel et al., 2020</xref>). Costa Rica was contaminated by <italic>B. abortus</italic> subclade C2 strains, imported from neighboring countries, whereas Kazakhstan was contaminated essentially by subclade C1 strains. This reflects the progressive spread of <italic>Brucella</italic> worldwide.</p>
