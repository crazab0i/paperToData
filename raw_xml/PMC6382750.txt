Following their uptake by the host cell, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replicate inside a membrane-bound compartment&#8212;the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole&#8212;whose acidification is essential for the survival of the pathogen. Therefore, identifying the genes that contribute to the survival of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in acidic environments will greatly assist our understanding of its molecular pathogenic mechanisms and of the attenuated virulence of the Rev.1 strain. Here, we conducted a comprehensive comparative transcriptome analysis of the Rev.1 vaccine strain against the virulent reference strain 16M in cultures grown under either normal or acidic conditions. We found 403 genes that respond differently to acidic conditions in the two strains (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8805; 2). These genes are involved in crucial cellular processes, including metabolic, biosynthetic, and transport processes. Among the highly enriched genes that were downregulated in Rev.1 under acidic conditions were acetyl-CoA synthetase, aldehyde dehydrogenase, cell division proteins, a cold-shock protein, GroEL, and VirB3. The downregulation of these genes may explain the attenuated virulence of Rev.1 and provide new insights into the virulence mechanisms of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> 16M</kwd><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1</kwd><kwd>acid toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are facultative intracellular bacteria that are responsible for brucellosis&#8212;a zoonotic infection that causes abortions and sterility in ruminants, pigs, dogs, and rodents, and a severely debilitating febrile illness in humans (<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">Ko and Splitter, 2003</xref>; <xref rid="B58" ref-type="bibr">von Bargen et al., 2012</xref>). One factor that crucially contributes to the virulence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is their ability to survive within various host cells, where they are inaccessible to the humoral immune response of the host (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Delrue et al., 2004</xref>). Following uptake by the host cells, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> create a unique, highly acidic intracellular niche&#8212;the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (BCV)&#8212;in which they reside and multiply (<xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">Celli, 2006</xref>; <xref rid="B54" ref-type="bibr">Starr et al., 2008</xref>). The acidification of the BCV is essential for inducing the major <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence determinant, the type-IV secretion system (T4SS; <xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">Porte et al., 1999</xref>; <xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">Boschiroli et al., 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">K&#246;hler et al., 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">Ke et al., 2015</xref>) which is encoded by the <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> locus in their chromosomes. As the T4SS system (and, especially, the proteins VirB3&#8211;6 and VirB8&#8211;11) plays a crucial role in inhibiting the host immune response and in the intracellular survival and replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> within the host cells (<xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">Comerci et al., 2001</xref>; <xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">den Hartigh et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">Ke et al., 2015</xref>; <xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">Smith et al., 2016</xref>), the ability of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to survive within the acidic conditions of the BCV is key to their pathogenesis and can be used to study the underlying mechanisms (<xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">Roop et al., 2009</xref>).</p><p><xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">Porte et al. (1999)</xref> reported that the pH in phagosomes containing live Brucella <italic toggle="yes">suis</italic> decreases to 4.0 within 1 h following infection, and that this value persists for at least 5 h. Thus, one can assume the existence of a complex, transcription-level regulation network, which responds to specific cellular signals that enable the bacteria to survive in the acidic BCV environment. Indeed, two recent comparative transcriptome analyses employed RNA-seq to determine the changes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> gene expression in cultures containing normal-pH media (namely, pH<sub>7.3</sub>) versus those containing low-pH media (pH<sub>4.4</sub>), thereby revealing novel molecular mechanisms leading to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> pathogenicity (<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">Liu et al., 2015</xref>, <xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">2016</xref>). Notably, one gene that was shown to play an important role in the resistance of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to low-pH conditions is BMEI1329, which encodes a two-component response regulator gene in the transcriptional regulation pathway of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> (<xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">Liu et al., 2016</xref>).</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic>, which infects goats and sheep mainly around the Mediterranean and the Persian Gulf, is the most pathogenic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species for humans (<xref rid="B43" ref-type="bibr">Poester et al., 2013</xref>). Among the brucellosis vaccines used in high-prevalence regions, a widely used one utilizes the live attenuated Rev.1 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strain (<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">Avila-Calder&#243;n et al., 2013</xref>). This strain, originally developed from the virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 6056 strain by Elberg and Herzberg in the mid-1950s, successfully protects and reduces abortions in small ruminants (<xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">Herzberg and Elberg, 1953</xref>; <xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>), but it remains infectious for humans and causes abortions in small ruminants vaccinated during the last trimester of gestation. To improve brucellosis vaccines, we need to better understand the mechanisms underlying the virulence attenuation of the Rev.1 vaccine strain (as compared with that of other, pathogenic strains), but these mechanisms are yet unclear.</p><p>In a recent study, we sequenced and annotated the whole genome of the original Elberg <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain (passage 101, 1970) and compared it to that of the virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M strain (<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">Salmon-Divon et al., 2018a</xref>,<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">b</xref>). We found that, as compared with 16M, Rev.1 contains non-synonymous and frameshift mutations in important virulence-related genes&#8212;including genes involved in lipid metabolism, stress response, regulation, amino acid metabolism, and cell-wall synthesis&#8212;which we assumed are related to the attenuated virulence of this strain. In this study, we aimed to extend these findings to elucidate the intracellular survival mechanisms of the virulent 16M strain versus the vaccine Rev.1 strain. To this end, and in light of the importance of the acidic BCV environment for the virulence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, we employed RNA-seq to comprehensively compare the transcriptome of the Rev.1 and 16M strains, each grown under either low- or normal-pH conditions, under the hypothesis that the gene expression patterns of the two strains will differ between the two conditions. Our analysis revealed several candidate genes that may be related to the attenuated virulence of Rev.1 and may, therefore, facilitate the design of improved brucellosis vaccines.</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="s1"><title>Materials and Methods</title><sec><title>Bacteria Strains and Culture Conditions</title><p>Bacterial strains used in the present study were <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M (INRA <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Culture Collection)&#8212;the commonly used, virulent, wild-type biotype 1 strain&#8212;and the original attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain (passage 101, 1970). For comparative assays, both the Rev.1 and 16M strains were cultured for 72 h on tryptic soy agar (TSA) plates at 37&#176;C under 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. The low-pH treatment assay was performed as reported previously (<xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">Liu et al., 2016</xref>). Briefly, bacteria were grown with shaking for 24 h in 10 ml of a tryptic soy broth (TSB; pH<sub>7.3</sub>) at 37&#176;C, with an initial density of 1 &#215; 10<sup>7</sup> CFU/ml. The final bacterial densities were adjusted to 5 &#215; 10<sup>8</sup> CFU/ml (OD<sub>600</sub> &#8773; 0.4) before the low-pH treatment, in which 1 ml of the culture was centrifuged at 7000 &#215;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, resuspended in a pH<sub>4.4</sub> TSB culture, and incubated for 4 h at 37&#176;C. In the control group, bacteria were cultured in a pH<sub>7.3</sub> TSB and incubated at 37&#176;C for 4 h. After incubation, cell cultures were collected and centrifuged at 7000 &#215;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, and then the supernatants were removed and an RNA Protect Reagent (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) was added to the pellets to prevent RNA degradation. Five different biological replicates were used for each strain under each type of condition (total 20 samples). All the work with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains was performed at a biosafety level 3 laboratory in the Kimron Veterinary Institute, Bet Dagan, Israel.</p></sec><sec><title>RNA Isolation</title><p>The total RNA of the 16M and Rev.1 strains was isolated using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen) with a DNase treatment (Qiagen). RNA was eluted from the column using RNase-free water. RNA quality was measured by Bioanalyzer (Agilent, Waldbronn, Germany). Libraries were prepared using the ScriptSeq RNA-Seq Library Preparation Kit (Illumina, Inc., San Diego, CA, United States). Library quantity and pooling were measured by Qubit [dsDNA high sensitivity (HS); Molecular Probes, Inc., Eugene, OR, United States]. The pool was size-selected by using a 4% agarose gel. Library quality was measured by TapeStation (HS; Agilent). For RNA-seq, the NextSeq 500 high output kit V2 was used (Illumina, Inc.). The reads were single end at the length of 75 bp (&#8764;10 million reads per sample). Sample denaturation and loading were conducted according to the manufacturer&#8217;s instructions. Library preparation and RNA-seq were conducted at the Center for Genomic Technologies at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, Jerusalem, Israel.</p></sec><sec><title>Reverse Transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR)</title><p>To confirm the RNA-seq results, five upregulated or downregulated genes from the RNA-seq analysis were selected and a RT-PCR was used to confirm the expression changes of these genes in both strains (16M and Rev.1) and conditions (low- and normal-pH). PCR primers were designed using Primer-BLAST (<xref rid="B62" ref-type="bibr">Ye et al., 2012</xref>) and are listed in <xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SM1">Supplementary Table S1</xref>. Complementary DNA (cDNA) was obtained by a reverse transcription of 850 ng total RNA at a final reaction volume of 20 &#956;l, containing 4 &#956;l qScript Reaction Mix, and 1 &#956;l qScript Reverse Transcriptase (Quantabio, Beverly, MA, United States). Quantitative RT-PCR assays were purchased from Biosearch Technologies (Petaluma, CA, United States) and used according to the manufacturer&#8217;s instructions. PCR reactions were conducted in a final reaction volume of 10 &#956;l containing 20 ng of cDNA template, 5 &#956;l of PerfeCTa SYBR Green FastMix, ROX (Quantabio), and 1 &#956;l of primer mix. All reactions were run in triplicate and the reference gene 16S rRNA was amplified in a parallel reaction for normalization.</p></sec><sec><title>RNA-Seq Analysis</title><p>Following quality control with FastQC<sup><xref ref-type="fn" rid="fn01">1</xref></sup>, the reads were processed to trim adaptors and low-quality bases by using Trim Galore software<sup><xref ref-type="fn" rid="fn02">2</xref></sup>. The EDGE-pro v1.3.1 software (<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">Magoc et al., 2013</xref>) was used with the default parameters to map reads to the <italic toggle="yes">B.</italic>
<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> 16M reference genome (GCF_000007125.1), filter out multi-aligned reads, and estimate the expression levels of each gene. To convert the EDGE-pro output to a count-table format, the &#8220;edgeToDeseq.perl&#8221; script (provided with the software) was used. Normalization and differential gene expression analysis were conducted with the edgeR (<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">Robinson et al., 2010</xref>) and Limma R packages (<xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">Smyth, 2005</xref>), using as input the count table generated by EDGE-pro. Briefly, genes that did not show more than 1 count per million (CPM) mapped reads in at least three samples were filtered out. Then, a TMM normalization (<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">Robinson and Oshlack, 2010</xref>) was applied, followed by voom transformation (<xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">Law et al., 2014</xref>). Linear models to assess differential gene expression were generated by fitting a model with a coefficient for all factor combinations (strain and low-pH treatment) and then extracting the comparisons of interest, which also included the interaction between strain and treatment effects. The aim of adding the interaction term in this experimental setup was to detect genes that respond differently to pH treatment in Rev.1 compared to 16M; we named these genes &#8220;interaction genes.&#8221; Only genes that demonstrated a fold change &#8805; 2 and an FDR &#8804; 0.05 were considered significant. Sequencing reads from this study were deposited in the NCBI SRA repository under the accession number <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank" xlink:href="PRJNA498082">PRJNA498082</ext-link>. The significantly upregulated or downregulated genes were subjected to a gene ontology enrichment analysis using ClusterProfiler (<xref rid="B63" ref-type="bibr">Yu et al., 2012</xref>) with a cutoff of FDR &lt; 0.05. To perform the gene ontology analysis, we first generated a database annotation package for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M using the &#8220;makeOrgPackage&#8221; command from the AnnotationForge R package (<xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">Carlson, 2018</xref>). As input, we used the GO annotation, downloaded from QuickGO (<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">Binns et al., 2009</xref>). Additional comparisons of the biological processes were performed with the Comparative GO web server (<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">Fruzangohar et al., 2013</xref>) using all the upregulated and downregulated genes. Multidimensional scaling analysis (MDS) was conducted using the &#8220;plotMDS&#8221; command within the edgeR (<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">Robinson et al., 2010</xref>) package. A heatmap of the 403 genes that respond differently to acidic conditions in the two strains was generated using the &#8220;heatmap3&#8221; R package (<xref rid="B64" ref-type="bibr">Zhao et al., 2014</xref>), employing 1-Pearson correlation as the distance measure and &#8220;complete&#8221; as the linkage method. Genes were categorized into five clusters based on the generated dendrogram, and genes within each cluster were characterized based on Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COGs) annotations. For this purpose, protein sequences of the clustered genes were searched against a local COG BLAST database, which was downloaded from NCBI using the reverse position-specific BLAST (RPS-BLAST) tool (<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">Marchler-Bauer et al., 2013</xref>). The expectation value (E) threshold was set to 0.01 and the BLAST output was parsed using an updated version of the cdd2cog.pl script<sup><xref ref-type="fn" rid="fn03">3</xref></sup> to obtain the assignment statistics of the COGs. The number of genes within each heatmap cluster belonging to each COG assignment was calculated, and the ontologies with the highest number of genes were indicated in the heatmap.</p></sec><sec><title>Cell Infection Test</title><p>JEG-3 (ATCC<sup>&#174;</sup> HTB-36<sup>TM</sup>) human trophoblasts were grown in Eagle&#8217;s Minimum Essential Medium (EMEM; ATCC<sup>&#174;</sup> 30-2003<sup>TM</sup>) with 10% fetal bovine serum. For intracellular replication experiments, 2 &#215; 10<sup>5</sup> cells were seeded in a 24-well plate and cultured overnight at 37&#176;C under 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. Monolayers of cells were infected with the 16M or Rev.1 strains at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 500 (100 &#956;l of bacterial suspension per well). To synchronize the infection, the infected plates were centrifuged at 400 g for 5 min at room temperature, followed by a 75 min incubation at 37 &#176;C in an atmosphere containing 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. The cells were then washed three times with PBS and re-incubated for another 60 min in a medium containing 50 &#956;g/ml gentamicin to eliminate extracellular bacteria, after which the number of internalized bacteria was measured (time zero of the culture). To assess the intracellular bacterial growth, the concentration of gentamicin was reduced to 5 &#956;g/ml. To monitor the intracellular survival of the bacteria at various times post-infection, the infected cells were lysed for 10 min with 0.1% Triton X-100 in water and serial dilutions of the lysates were plated on TSA plates to enumerate the colony-forming units. Three identical wells were evaluated at each time for each strain. Experiments were repeated three times, independently.</p></sec></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p>We used RNA-seq to conduct a comprehensive comparative transcriptomic analysis of the gene expression profiles of the Rev.1 (vaccine) and 16M (virulent) <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains, grown either under low-pH conditions that mimic the intracellular niche of the BCV (pH<sub>4.4</sub>; referred to here as the &#8220;low- pH&#8221; group) or normal-pH conditions (pH<sub>7.3</sub>; &#8220;normal-pH&#8221; group). The raw sequence outputs for each group are presented in <xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>. Therefore, we adopted a naive approach and detected the effect of the acidic environment on gene expression in each strain separately, then compared the final list of DE genes. Our next step was to identify the potential dependency between strain and environmental pH, i.e., we sought to detect genes that respond differently to acidic stress in Rev.1 versus 16M. To this end, we added the &#8220;interaction&#8221; term to our statistical model, which revealed 403 genes that can be referred to as &#8220;interaction genes&#8221; (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8805; 2; <xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SM1">Supplementary Table S7</xref>) and may potentially shed light on the attenuation mechanisms of Rev.1. Annotating these &#8220;interaction genes&#8221; revealed that the most enriched biological processes were related to metabolic, biosynthetic, and transport processes; the most enriched molecular functions were related to catalytic, hydrolase, nucleotide binding, oxidoreductase, and transporter activities; and the most enriched cellular compartments were related to integral components of the membrane (<xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SM1">Supplementary Tables S8&#8211;S10</xref>). To identify genes that are potentially involved in the attenuation and survival of Rev.1, we searched the interaction genes for those that are associated with bacterial virulence and survival within the host and found four highly downregulated genes involved in metabolism processes and mitigation of acidic and oxidative stresses (<xref rid="T4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>). The mRNA levels of all genes obtained by the RT-qPCR were in high accordance with those obtained by our RNA-seq analysis (<xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="SM1">Supplementary Table S1</xref>).</p></sec><sec><title>Differential Survival of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M and Rev.1 Within JEG-3 Human Trophoblastic Cells</title><p>As shown by <xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">Porte et al. (1999)</xref>, the acidic environment during the early phase of infection is necessary for the survival and multiplication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in host cells. Therefore, we investigated the ability of the virulent 16M and the attenuated Rev.1 strains to infect and replicate within the human trophoblastic cell line JEG-3. As expected, the number of bacteria that were replicated over time (4 and 24 h) was higher in 16M than in Rev.1 (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="F7">Figure 7</xref>).</p><fig id="F7" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>FIGURE 7</label><caption><p>Bacterial burden, measured as colony forming units (CFUs) over time. JEG-3 trophoblasts were infected with bacteria at a MOI of 500, and CFUs were determined 0, 4, and 24 h thereafter. <bold>(A)</bold> Boxplots representing the distribution of 16M (blue) and Rev.1 (yellow) bacterial count replicates, measured 0, 4, and 24 h following infection. <italic toggle="yes">P-</italic>values were calculated using two-sample Wilcoxon test. <bold>(B)</bold> Scatter plot indicating the change in bacterial counts over time (means &#177; SD) following infection. Three replicate wells were evaluated at each time for each strain.</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="fmicb-10-00250-g007.jpg"/></fig></sec></sec><sec><title>Discussion</title><p>To elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying the attenuation of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain, we conducted a comparative transcriptomic analysis between Rev.1 and its virulent counterpart, 16M, each grown under either normal- or low-pH conditions. When the two strains were grown under normal-pH conditions, Rev.1 showed a marked upregulation, as compared with 16M, of various genes that encode ABC transporters&#8212;a large and widespread family of proteins (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">Garmory and Titball, 2004</xref>). ABC transporters, which export solutes, antibiotics, and extracellular toxins, play a role in various cellular processes, such as translational regulation and DNA repair (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">Garmory and Titball, 2004</xref>), and the number of ABC systems appears to depend upon the bacterial adaptation to its environment (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">Garmory and Titball, 2004</xref>; <xref rid="B55" ref-type="bibr">Tanaka et al., 2018</xref>). The upregulation of ABC transporter-related genes in the attenuated Rev.1 strain should be considered in light of two other findings. First, Rev.1 showed an upregulated expression of BMEII0704, which encodes for bacterioferritin (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). Notably, iron plays an important role in the survival of pathogens within host cells (<xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">Collins, 2003</xref>), and during infection, macrophages actively export iron from the phagosome (which is the replicative niche of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>; <xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">Forbes and Gros, 2001</xref>); it was previously suggested that Rev.1 may have lost the ability to regulate bacterioferritin synthesis and degradation (<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">Eschenbrenner et al., 2002</xref>). Second, Rev.1 showed a downregulated expression of BMEI1759 (<xref rid="T3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>), which encodes for the vitamin B12-dependent methyltransferase, MetH (<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">Lestrate et al., 2000</xref>). As MetH is involved in methionine biosynthesis, its downregulation in Rev.1 may have impaired amino acid metabolism in this strain. Taken together, these finding suggest that an improper regulation of essential metabolic pathways, including iron and amino acid metabolism, may have affected the ABC transporter activity, leading to down/upregulation of specific transporters to compensate for the gain/loss of critical metabolites.</p><p>To further understand the molecular mechanisms underlying Rev.1 attenuation, we examined the &#8220;interaction genes,&#8221; i.e., the main genes that are influenced differently by the acidic treatment in Rev.1 and in 16M. These interaction genes are probably the key genes involved in the attenuation of Rev.1, and are, therefore, of particular interest. Among these interaction genes, we found that acetyl-coenzyme A (acetyl-CoA) synthetase was significantly downregulated in the low-pH group of Rev.1, as compared with the low-pH group of 16M. Acetyl-CoA is the molecule by which glycolytic pyruvate enters the tricarboxylic acid cycle, it is a crucial precursor of lipid synthesis, and it acts as a sole donor of the acetyl groups for acetylation (<xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">Pietrocola et al., 2015</xref>). Previous studies revealed a clear association between the metabolism of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and its persistence in its hosts (<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">Hong et al., 2000</xref>; <xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">Lestrate et al., 2000</xref>; <xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">Barbier et al., 2011</xref>). The significantly high downregulation of acetyl-CoA synthetase in Rev.1 may decrease the levels of acetyl-CoA production, thereby affecting crucial metabolic processes that may potentially have a major contribution to bacterial attenuation.</p><p>Pathogenic bacteria must deal with oxidative stress emanating from the host immune response during invasion and persistent infection (<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">Cabiscol et al., 2000</xref>; <xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">Singh et al., 2013</xref>). We found several key interaction genes that were significantly downregulated in Rev.1, as compared with 16M, and which encode proteins involved in oxidative stress: aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), the DNA starvation/stationary phase protection protein Dps, and a cold-shock protein (CSP). Prokaryotic and eukaryotic ALDHs metabolize endogenous and exogenous aldehydes to mitigate oxidative stress, (<xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">Singh et al., 2013</xref>) and an upregulation of bacterial ALDH was shown to occur following exposure to environmental or chemical stressors. It was suggested that such an upregulation is a critical element in the response of bacteria to oxidative stress (<xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">Singh et al., 2013</xref>). Dps was shown to protect <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> from oxidative stress, UV and gamma irradiation, iron and copper toxicity, thermal stress, and acid and base shocks (<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">Martinez and Kolter, 1997</xref>; <xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">Nair and Finkel, 2004</xref>; <xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">Karas et al., 2015</xref>). CSP-A activity was found to be associated with the ability of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> to resist acidic and H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> stresses, especially during the mid-log-phase (<xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">Wang et al., 2014</xref>), and it was suggested that the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> CSP highly contributes to its virulence, most likely by facilitating its adaptation to the harsh environmental circumstances within the host (<xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">Wang et al., 2014</xref>). Taken together, it is possible that the downregulation of these key interaction genes in Rev.1 results in its inability to cope with oxidative stress in the host, thus contributing to bacterial attenuation.</p><p>As compared with Rev.1, 16M demonstrated a downregulation (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8805; 2) of interaction genes that encode for four proteins of the SUF system and the heat-shock protein IbpA, which were shown to be involved in the resistance to heat and oxidative stress (<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">Kitagawa et al., 2000</xref>; <xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">Angelini et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">Outten, 2015</xref>). In <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>, the SUF pathway plays a major role in preserving Fe-S cluster biosynthesis under oxidative stress conditions (<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">Angelini et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">Outten, 2015</xref>). The small heat shock proteins (sHsps) IbpA and IbpB were previously suggested to be involved in the resistances to heat and oxidative stress, as overexpression of <italic toggle="yes">ibpA</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">ibpB</italic> in <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> increased the resistance to heat and superoxide stress (<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">Kitagawa et al., 2000</xref>). Our transcriptomic analysis revealed enhanced enrichment within the molecular function of oxidoreductase activity in the low-pH group of 16M, but not in the low-pH group of Rev.1. As oxidoreductase protects bacteria against oxidative stress (<xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">Lumppio et al., 2001</xref>), we assume that the enhanced expression of the SUF system and IbpA by Rev.1, as compared with 16M, compensates for its impaired oxidoreductase activity, thereby enabling survival within the harsh oxidative intracellular environment of the host.</p><p>As compared with the low-pH group of 16M, the low-pH group of Rev.1 showed a downregulation (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8764; 2) of five key interaction genes BME_RS02910, BMEI0584, BMEI0583, BME_RS13825, and BMEI1943 that encode for FtsZ, FtsA, FtsQ, FtsK, and DnaA respectively, all of which participate in critical stages of the cell cycle (<xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">Margolin, 2005</xref>; <xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">Sherratt et al., 2010</xref>; <xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">van den Ent et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">Bell and Kaguni, 2013</xref>; <xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">Loose and Mitchison, 2014</xref>). This finding may indicate that, in the acidic conditions of the BCV, the replication capabilities of Rev.1 are reduced, which lowers their intracellular survival compared to 16M. The significantly lower intracellular survival of Rev.1 in trophoblasts (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="F7">Figure 7</xref>) supports this conclusion.</p><p>Under low-pH conditions, Rev.1 also showed a downregulation of the interaction gene BMEII1048 that encodes the molecular chaperone, GroEL (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8764; 2). Molecular chaperons facilitate protein folding, preventing protein denaturation, and are involved in various cellular processes, including DNA replication, UV mutagenesis, bacterial growth, and RNA transcription (<xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">Maleki et al., 2016</xref>). Under acidic conditions, partially unfolded proteins may emerge (<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">Mendoza et al., 2017</xref>) and molecular chaperones may stabilize them to prevent their acid-induced aggregation. Indeed, the <italic toggle="yes">Helicobacter pylori</italic> GroEL homolog, HSP60, was shown to be induced upon acid stress (<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">Mendoza et al., 2017</xref>). Thus, the downregulation of the interaction gene encoding GroEL in Rev.1 under low-pH conditions may lead to the accumulation of partially unfolded and desaturated proteins, leading to bacterial attenuation.</p><p>Finally, the interaction gene BMEII0027, which encodes for the T4SS protein VirB3, was upregulated (FDR &lt; 0.05, fold change &#8764; 2) in the low-pH group of 16M, as compared with the low-pH group of Rev.1. This may have a harmful effect on the survival of Rev.1 within host cells, as VirB3 was shown to be essential for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence because, together with VirB4, VirB6, VirB8, and the N-terminus of VirB10, it comprises the inner membrane complex of the T4SS apparatus (<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">Ke et al., 2015</xref>).</p><p>Notably, the Rev.1 upregulated interaction gene BMEII1116 that encodes the HTH-type quorum sensing-dependent transcriptional regulator VjbR, was shown to contribute to the virulence and survival of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> by regulating the expression of various virulence factors (<xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">Delrue et al., 2005</xref>; <xref rid="B60" ref-type="bibr">Weeks et al., 2010</xref>). It is possible that this interaction gene somewhat compensates for the lower expression of highly important virulent genes, such as VirB3, in Rev.1.</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusion</title><p>Through a comparative transcriptomic analysis, we revealed DE key genes involved in various crucial pathways, which are either upregulated or downregulated under acidic conditions in Rev.1, as compared with 16M. We suggest that these genes&#8212;and, especially, those mentioned in <xref rid="T4" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>&#8212;are involved in the molecular mechanisms underlying Rev.1 attenuation, although further characterization through mutation and knockout experiments is required to conclusively determine the role of these genes in acid resistance and virulence attenuation of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain.</p></sec><sec><title>Author Contributions</title><p>MS-D and DK conceived and coordinated the study.
