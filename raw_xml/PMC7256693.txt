xlink:href="pjm-67-2-151.pdf"/><abstract><title>Abstract</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucellae</italic> are Gram-negative, small rods infecting mammals and capable of causing disease called brucellosis. The infection results in abortion and sterility in domestic animals (sheeps, pigs, rams etc). Especially dangerous for humans are: <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis, Brucella suis, Brucella abortus</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella canis</italic> that trigger unspecific symptoms (flu-like manifestation). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> rods are introduced via host cells, by inhalation, skin abrasions, ingestion or mucosal membranes. The most important feature of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is the ability to survive and multiply within both phagocytic and non-phagocytic cells. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> does not produce classical virulence factors: exotoxin, cytolisins, exoenzymes, plasmids, fimbria, and drug resistant forms. Major virulence factors are: lipopolysaccharide (LPS), T4SS secretion system and BvrR/BvrS system, which allow interaction with host cell surface, formation of an early, late BCV (<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Containing Vacuole) and interaction with endoplasmic reticulum (ER) when the bacteria multiply. The treatment of brucellosis is based on two-drug therapy, the most common combinations of antibiotics are: doxycycline with rifampicin or fluoroquinolones with rifampicin. Currently, also other methods are used to disrupt <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intracellular replication (tauroursodeoxycholic acid or ginseng saponin fraction A).</p></abstract><kwd-group><title>Key words:</title><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic></kwd><kwd>endoplasmic reticulum</kwd><kwd>macrophage</kwd><kwd>replication</kwd><kwd>virulence sec-type="introduction" id="sec1"><title>Introduction</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is a genus of bacteria belonging to the phylum Proteobacteria, class Alphaproteobacteria, order Rhizobiales, family Brucellaceae. Alphaproteobacteria is a very diverse group as to this class belongs both, the pathogens associated with plants: <italic toggle="yes">Agrobacterium</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Sinorhizobium</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Mesorhizobium</italic> spp. and the pathogens, which cause dangerous infections of animals, <italic toggle="yes">e.g. Ricketsia</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Bartonella</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. and many others (<xref rid="bib34" ref-type="bibr">Dwight and Bowman, 2011</xref>).</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genus is responsible for brucellosis, a severe febrile disease. Brucellosis is a worldwide problem, causing abortion and infertility in domestic and wild animals (<xref rid="bib58" ref-type="bibr">Lapaque <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). Infection factors are aerobic, small, Gram-negative rods. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, a genus discovered in 1887 by David Bruce, contains the following species: <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis, Brucella ovis, Brucella abortus, Brucella canis, Brucella melitensis, Brucella neotomae, Brucella ceti, Brucella pinnipedialis, Brucella microti, Brucella inopinata, Brucella papionis, Brucella vulpis</italic> and other strains without standing in nomenclature, that include environmental samples (<xref rid="bib40" ref-type="bibr">Gali&#324;ska and Zag&#243;rski, 2013</xref>; <xref rid="bib100" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>; <xref rid="bib87" ref-type="bibr">Scholz <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2016</xref>). Some species contain biovars, for example: <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> have five biovars, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> contain three and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> &#8211; nine biovars (<xref rid="bib66" ref-type="bibr">Mizak <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>). Most of these species infect mainly specific hosts. <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> causes disease in cattle and infections usually lead to abortion; whereas <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> is responsible for brucellosis in pigs, resulting in reproductive problems. Sheep are hosts for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>; infection causes impaired fertility. <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> is an etiological factor in sterility of rams (<xref rid="bib65" ref-type="bibr">Megid <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Currently, about 500 000 cases of human brucellosis have been reported worldwide annually (<xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Byndloss and Tsolis, 2016</xref>). Brucellosis is an endemic zoonosis with infection predominantly occurring in Middle East, Mediterranean rim (Portugal, Spain, Greece), Asia, Africa, South and Central America where the intake of dairy products is high, and protection of animal health is insufficient (<xref rid="bib83" ref-type="bibr">Rubach <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>). There are single cases reported in Poland, however connected with occupational exposure or with traveling to Mediterranean countries (<xref rid="bib40" ref-type="bibr">Gali&#324;ska and Zag&#243;rski, 2013</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> are isolated not only from livestock but also from different wildlife species (bears, buffalo, bison, caribu, camelids, elk, ferrets, deer, foxes, rodents, rabbits, wolves) and marine mammals (dolphins, dugongs, manatees, otters, sea porpoise) (<xref rid="bib30" ref-type="bibr">Coelho <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is rarely encountered in wildlife, nonetheless individual cases have been reported in ibex and in chamois in Alps. <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> have not been detected in wildlife in Europe up to date. <italic toggle="yes">B. pinnipedialis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic> cause the most common infections in marine mammals. Birds are resistant to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection, whereas fish seem to be susceptible to <italic toggle="yes">B. pinnipedialis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic> infections (<xref rid="bib44" ref-type="bibr">Godfroid <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>). Infection is transmitted through close contact and during a common pasture.</p><p>Brucellosis is transferred from animals to humans, frequently human to human transmission occurs (<xref rid="bib74" ref-type="bibr">Osman <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2016</xref>). Especially dangerous for humans are: <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis, B. suis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus, B. canis</italic>. Brucellosis in human presents with symptoms like influenza: undulating fever, depression, weight loss, hepatomegaly, and splenomegaly (<xref rid="bib15" ref-type="bibr">Bing&#246;l <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1999</xref>). Mainly human cases are connected to occupational risk or consumption of unprocessed dairy products (Boschiroli <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001; <xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figureido <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> rods can enter via host cells by inhalation, ingestion, skin abrasion, or mucosal membranes (<xref rid="bib38" ref-type="bibr">Franco <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>). After penetration into host, rods multiply in lymph nodes; afterward, they penetrate other organs (<xref rid="bib40" ref-type="bibr">Gali&#324;ska and Zag&#243;rski, 2013</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, can modify immune response in host cells; it has an affinity to the cells of specific tissues, <italic toggle="yes">e.g</italic>. placental trophoblast in fetal lung, pregnant females or reproductive system (<xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figureido <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). Brucellosis causes enlargement of lymph nodes, liver and spleen (<xref rid="bib76" ref-type="bibr">Perkins <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Pathogenicity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is dependent on their ability to multiply and survive within macrophages (<xref rid="bib85" ref-type="bibr">Sangari and Ag&#369;ero, 1996</xref>; <xref rid="bib29" ref-type="bibr">Christopher <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="Characteristics of Brucella" id="sec2"><title>Characteristics of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic></title><p>Species of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> belong to small coccobacilli, measuring about 0,6&#8211;1,5 &#956;m (<xref rid="bib4" ref-type="bibr">Alton and Forsyth, 1996</xref>). They occur in single forms; rarely they create pairs or chains (<xref rid="bib66" ref-type="bibr">Mizak <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are non-spore forming and non-motile Gram-negative cocobacilli (GNCB) (<xref rid="bib4" ref-type="bibr">Alton and Forsyth, 1996</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is an intracellular pathogen, during an infection it survives and multiplies in macrophages; the bacteria adapt to the acidic pH, low levels of oxygen, and low levels of nutrients (<xref rid="bib56" ref-type="bibr">K&#337;hler <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>).</p><p>Lipopolisaccharide (LPS) is an essential element of structure building in each Gram-negative bacterial cell. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is a genus that creates two forms of LPS. The smooth forms present complete LPS in the outer membrane, the rooth phenotype does not contain polisaccharide O-chain (<xref rid="bib58" ref-type="bibr">Lapaque <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>; <xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). These infectious agents are able to produce cytochrome oxidase, catalase, and most of them are able to hydrolyze urea (<xref rid="bib53" ref-type="bibr">Iowa State University, 2009</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> does not produce classical pathogenic factors, such as: exotoxin, cytolisins, exoensymes, exoproteins, capsules, plasmids, fimbria, and drug resistant forms (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>; <xref rid="bib9" ref-type="bibr">Baldi and Giambartolomei, 2013</xref>; <xref rid="bib97" ref-type="bibr">Tan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). Bacterial cells are able to survive for a prolonged time in water, aborted fetus, soil, dairy products, meat, dung, and dust (<xref rid="bib50" ref-type="bibr">Gwida <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). For isolation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. the enrichment and selective media such as Thayer-Martin&#8217;s medium or Farrell&#8217;s medium are commonly used. The colonies mature after four to six days of incubation at temperature of 37&#176;C. They can also grow at 28&#176;C, but poorly and slowly. Moreover, these bacteria can grow in both aerobic atmosphere and in 10% CO<sub>2</sub>; while, their growth is enhanced without additional CO<sub>2</sub> on a serum dextrose agar (<xref rid="bib53" ref-type="bibr">Iowa State University, 2009</xref>; <xref rid="bib100" ref-type="bibr">Whatmore <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>; <xref rid="bib48" ref-type="bibr">Gupte and Kaur, 2015</xref>).</p><p>A wide range of bacterial detection methods is available. The predominatingly used culture media are: Bacto Tryptose (Difco), Triptcase soy (BBL), Tryptone soya (Oxoid), Triptic soy (Gibco). For culture of blood or body fluid a biphase medium called Castaneda should be used. Castaneda consists of two phases: liquid and solid closed in bottle. Liquid medium contains 1&#8211;2% of sodium citrate. Sample (5&#8211;10 ml) is added to the medium and incubated in 37&#176;C in perpendiculary standing bottle in 10% carbon dioxide atmosphere (<xref rid="bib48" ref-type="bibr">Gupte and Kaur, 2015</xref>). Serological tests are used to detect infection by examination of a specific antibodies level in serum. In the first week of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection the titres of IgM are dominant, but in the second week IgG class is prevalent. After four weeks, both types of antibodies reach a peak; durable, high titres of IgG can evidence failure in treatment (<xref rid="bib2" ref-type="bibr">Al Dahouk <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>). Serum Agglutination Test (SAT) and Enzyme linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) are the most common serological tests used for diagnosis of brucellosis. SAT is based on a survey of agglutination titer of different serum dilution against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> cell suspension (<xref rid="bib3" ref-type="bibr">Alshaalan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>). ELISA depends on detection of antibodies against the antigen &#8211; smooth LPS in serum (<xref rid="bib43" ref-type="bibr">Gerasu and Kassa, 2016</xref>). The most effective methods for detection of brucellosis are molecular techniques (classical PCR, real-time PCR). The PCR method applies various pairs of primers to amplify different fragments of the genome. The examples of genes used to identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are: BCSP 31 (primers: B4/B5), sequence of 16S rRNA (primers: F4/R2), omp2 gene (primers: JPF/JPR) (<xref rid="bib8" ref-type="bibr">Baddour and Alkhalifa, 2008</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="Virulence factors" id="sec3"><title>Virulence factors</title><p><bold>Lipopolisaccharide.</bold> LPS is an essential virulence factor of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. LPS consists of lipid A, oligosaccharide core and O-antigen in Gram-negative bacteria. Lipopolisaccharide is different and non-classical in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> as compared to other Gram-negative bacteria, for example <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> (<xref rid="bib21" ref-type="bibr">Cardosos <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>; <xref rid="bib29" ref-type="bibr">Christopher <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Lipopolisaccharide from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains is less toxic and less active than the classical LPS isolated from <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>. Classical LPS causes a high pyrogenicity, while non-classical LPS shows low pyrogenicity, being a weak inducer of tumor necrosis factor (<xref rid="bib29" ref-type="bibr">Christopher <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Three features distinguishing it from other Gram-negatives characterize lipid A found in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>: i) the fundamental component is diaminoglucose instead glucosamine, ii) longer acyl groups, and iii) lipid A is connected to the core by amide bonds, instead ester and amide bounds (<xref rid="bib58" ref-type="bibr">Lapaque <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). In strains with smooth colonies, the smooth LPS, (S-LPS) contains: i) lipid A, that consists of two types of aminoglycose, and fatty acid besides &#946;-hydroxymiristic acid, ii) core comprises mannose, glucose, quinovosamine, and iii) O-chains are composed of 4-formamido-4,6-dideoxymannose. The structure of the R-LPS in strains with rough colonies is similar to the S-LPS, except for O-chains, which are reduced or absent (<xref rid="bib31" ref-type="bibr">Corbel, 1997</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> has S-LPS. The O-chain connects with lipid rafts on the macrophage surface and the bacteria enter the cell. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains with R-LPS, for example <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> do not connect with lipid rafts and rapidly connect with lysosomes (<xref rid="bib58" ref-type="bibr">Lapaque <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). The strains with S-LPS are able to restrain host cell apoptosis by the interaction of the O-chain with TNF-&#945; (tumor necrosis factor). Thus, dead cells do not release specific factors, therefore they do not activate the immune system and <italic toggle="yes">Brucellae</italic> are able to avoid host immune surveillance (<xref rid="bib36" ref-type="bibr">Fernandez-Prada <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>).</p><p><bold>Type IV secretion system (T4SS).</bold> T4SS is a multi-protein complex and participates in secretion of bacterial macromolecues (<xref rid="bib24" ref-type="bibr">Cascales and Christie, 2003</xref>). This system is typified by v<italic toggle="yes">irB</italic> operon encoding 12 proteins (11 860 bp) and exhibits in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. with a high degree of similarity to T4SSs found in rhizobia, for example in phytopatogenic <italic toggle="yes">Agrobacterium tumefaciens</italic> (<xref rid="bib20" ref-type="bibr">O&#8217;Callaghan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1999</xref>). Expression of the <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> operon is regulated by the regulator of quorum-sensing &#8211; VjbR (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). The wild strains of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are able to multiply only in the endoplasmic reticulum. <italic toggle="yes">VirB</italic> mutants of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are unable to multiply within the endoplasmic reticulum, it can result from the incapability to reach the ER, or multiply within (<xref rid="bib32" ref-type="bibr">Delure <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>). In macrophages, rods of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are localized in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (BCV); this organelle interacts with the ER and is responsible for formation of specialized brucellae-multiplication compartment (<xref rid="bib56" ref-type="bibr">K&#337;hler <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>). The acquisition of endoplasmic reticulum membrane depends on a functional <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> secretion system &#8211; T4SS (<xref rid="bib25" ref-type="bibr">Celli <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>).</p><p><bold>Superoxide dismutase and catalase.</bold> Macrophages with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> produce reactive oxygen intermediates (ROIs), this is a primary mechanism of destruction of the bacteria ingested, and it also prevents their intracellular replication (<xref rid="bib42" ref-type="bibr">Gee <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>; <xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). The following ROIs: O<sup>2&#8211;</sup> (superoxide), H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (hydrogen peroxide), OH<sup>&#8211;</sup> (hydroxyl radical) are very detrimental for cell structure. The production of enzymes is the main line of defense, counteracting reactive oxygen intermediates. These enzymes include superoxide dismutase (SOD) and catalase (<xref rid="bib42" ref-type="bibr">Gee <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). SOD (metalloenzyme) is encoded by <italic toggle="yes">sod</italic> sequence. An enzyme contains iron, magnesium, or zinc and copper at its active site (<xref rid="bib14" ref-type="bibr">Benov and Fridovich, 1994</xref>). SOD is responsible for dismutation of O<sup>2&#8211;</sup> (superoxide) to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (hydrogen peroxide) and O<sub>2</sub> (oxygen) &#8211; transfer from one molecule to another (2O<sup>2&#8211;</sup>+2H<sup>+</sup> &#8594;H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> +O<sub>2</sub>) (<xref rid="bib46" ref-type="bibr">Gopal and Elumalai, 2017</xref>). Some species possess two types of SOD (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus, B. melitensis, B. suis</italic>). The first is cytoplasmic &#8211; a Mn cofactor &#8211; SodA. SodA neutralizes endogenously generated O<sup>2&#8211;</sup> &#8211; product of aerobic metabolism. The second one, SodC is periplasmic Cu, Zn-SOD, an enzyme responsible for neutralizing exogenously generated O<sup>2&#8211;</sup> and protection from the respiratory burst within macrophages (<xref rid="bib12" ref-type="bibr">Beck <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1990</xref>, <xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>, <xref rid="bib63" ref-type="bibr">Martin <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2012</xref>).</p><p>Catalase decomposes hydrogen peroxide into oxygen and water. Catalase activity is limited to the periplasmic space, where together with Cu-Zn SOD leave external sources of ROI unchanged (Kim <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2000). Catalase is not necessary virulence factor, the other enzymes can compensate lack of this enzyme in catalase mutants, <italic toggle="yes">e.g</italic>. alkyl hydroperoxide reductase or enzymes involved in DNA repair mechanisms (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). Catalase is encoded by a sequence similar to <italic toggle="yes">katE</italic> gene of <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli. B. abortus, B. melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> catalase production is regulated by an increased external level of H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<xref rid="bib41" ref-type="bibr">Gee <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2004</xref>).</p><p><bold>Cyclic &#946;-1-2-glucans (C&#946;G).</bold>
<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> C&#946;G belongs to II OPGs (Osmoregulated periplasmic glucans) family (<xref rid="bib16" ref-type="bibr">Bohin, 2000</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> C&#946;G impacts intracellular trafficking by acting on lipid rafts on macrophage surface. These glucans participate in control of the phagosome-lysosome fusion. Mutants are destroyed in phagolysosome and they are not able to multiply. Even more, mutants treated by C&#946;G are able to control vacuole maturation and lysosome fusion, so they can reach to the ER and replicate there (<xref rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">Arellano-Reynoso <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>).</p><p><bold>Urease.</bold> In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> there are non-identical urease operons in two separate genomes. Urease is a metalloenzyme, that decomposes urea to carbonic acid and to ammonium form, and it results in pH increase. This feature enables its survival in acid environment (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). In I chromosome, there are two urea-operons: <italic toggle="yes">ure-1</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">ure-2</italic>, separated by 1 Mb of DNA. <italic toggle="yes">Ure-1</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">ure-2</italic> encode structural genes: <italic toggle="yes">ureA, ureB, ureC</italic> and accessory genes: <italic toggle="yes">ureD, ureE, ureF, ureG</italic> (Mobley <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1995). That urease may protect <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> during passage through the digestive tract (stomach), when the bacteria access their host through the oral route (<xref rid="bib10" ref-type="bibr">Bandara <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>). Urease is produced by all bacteria belonging to the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> but <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> (<xref rid="bib86" ref-type="bibr">Sangari <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>).</p><p><bold>Cytochrome oxidase.</bold> Cytochrome oxidase is an enzyme facilitating <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>&#8217;s survival inside the macrophages, where oxygen availability is limited. There are two operons in genome encoding two types of high oxygen-affinity oxidases: cytochrome cbb3-type and cytochrome bd (ubiquinol oxidases) oxidases. Cytochrome cbb3 oxidase is expressed <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic> and allows for colonization of anoxic tissues (maximal action in microaerobiosis). Cytochrome bd oxidase is expressed during intracellular multiplication and enables adjustment to the replicative niche (<xref rid="bib60" ref-type="bibr">Loiser-Meyer <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>), by restraining the creation of oxidative free radicals and detoxification of compartment inside the cell (<xref rid="bib35" ref-type="bibr">Endley <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>).</p><p><bold>Alkyl hydroperoxide reductase (AhpC, AhpD)</bold>. These enzymes attempt protection against oxygen radical and reactive nitrogen (<xref rid="bib28" ref-type="bibr">Chen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">AhpC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">ahpD</italic> are organized in an operon under one promoter control. <italic toggle="yes">AhpC</italic> mutants are more sensitive to peroxide killing and are vulnerable to spontaneous mutagenesis (<xref rid="bib33" ref-type="bibr">DelVecchio <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>).</p><p><bold>Nitric oxide reductase (NorD).</bold> Reduction of nitrate to dinitrogen gas is an essential process for bacteria in case of oxygen deficiency inside the cell; this process allows for respiration of nitrate (<xref rid="bib95" ref-type="bibr">Stevanin <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). The infected macrophages produce nitric oxide (NO), and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can use it for own purposes. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> NorD consists of four types of reductases: Nir &#8211; nitrite reductase, Nar &#8211; nitrate reductase, Nor &#8211; nitric oxide reductase and Nos &#8211; nitrous oxide reductase, called the nitrification island. Possibility concerning productions of this enzymes helps to protect <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> against low-oxygen conditions inside macrophages (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>).</p><p><bold><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence factor A (BvfA).</bold> Periplasmic protein that occurs only in genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>; there are no homologous sequences in Gen Bank. The <italic toggle="yes">bvfA</italic> expression is induced in macrophages, through phagosome acidification. Presumably this protein is involved in forming the replication intracellular niche. BvfA function is not precisely identified (<xref rid="bib59" ref-type="bibr">Lavigne <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>).</p><p><bold>Base excision repair (BER).</bold>
<italic toggle="yes">XthA</italic> gene encodes exonuclease III, which takes part in the base excision repair of DNA. Two different sequences of <italic toggle="yes">xthA</italic> occur in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genome: <italic toggle="yes">xthA-1</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">xthA-2. XthA-1</italic> mutants exhibit increased sensitivity to reactive oxygen species (ROS), so this enzyme is responsible for protection against oxidative destruction (<xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>).</p><p><bold>BvrR/BvrS system.</bold> The analysis of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomic library has confirmed an occurrence of two open reading frames: <italic toggle="yes">bvrR</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">bvrS</italic>. The <italic toggle="yes">bvrR</italic> encodes BvrR proteins (237 amino acid) and <italic toggle="yes">bvrS</italic> encodes BvrS (601 amino acid). There are two potential promoters (&#8211;10 and 35 seq. located 50 bp upstream ORF of <italic toggle="yes">bvrR</italic>), and ribosome-binding sequence (9 bp upstream of the first codon) (<xref rid="bib92" ref-type="bibr">Sola-Landa <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). BvrR exhibits resemblance to response regulators proteins, as N-terminal domain is composed of highly conserved aminoacids: aspartic (pos: 14, 15, 58) and lysine (pos: 107). C-terminal domain showed high similarity sequence to OmpR family; therefore, this protein can be included as part of this family (<xref rid="bib67" ref-type="bibr">Mizuno and Tanaka, 1997</xref>; <xref rid="bib64" ref-type="bibr">Mart&#237;nez-N&#363;&#241;ez <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). The protein is composed of three highly conserved domains: N-terminal sensing, periplasmic domain together with transmembrane component, cytoplasmic domain with distinctive histidine residue and C-terminal ATP-binding domain (<xref rid="bib101" ref-type="bibr">Viadas <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). BvrS includes four highly conserved regions on C-terminal domain: H, N, D/F, and G. This feature causes BvrS homologous to sensor proteins of the histidine protein kinase family (<xref rid="bib96" ref-type="bibr">Stock <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1995</xref>). BvrS is located in the cell membrane (<xref rid="bib64" ref-type="bibr">Mart&#237;nez-N&#363;&#241;ez <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Brucella BvrR/BvrS are the best characterized components of the virulence system; mutants are incapable of invasion, prevention phagosome-lysosome fusion and intracellular replication. BvrR/BvrS system is a regulator of expression of multiple genes (<xref rid="bib101" ref-type="bibr">Viadas <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). These proteins affect the transcription of the membrane proteins: Omp3b (Omp22) or Omp3a (Omp25a) and have the influence on other non-protein membrane molecules and hence on functional and structural membrane homeostasis (<xref rid="bib62" ref-type="bibr">Manterola <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">BvrR</italic>/<italic toggle="yes">bvrS</italic> mutants show structural changes in LPS, but O-chains seem to be undisturbed. These mutants are incapable of activation of GTPase (Cdc42) before entry into the cell, so they persist extracellularly and in consequence they do not infect the cell (<xref rid="bib49" ref-type="bibr">Guzm&#225;n-Verri <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>). BvrR/BvrS is also responsible for limited lysosome fusion and intracellular trafficking (<xref rid="bib61" ref-type="bibr">L&#243;pez-Go&#241;i <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>).</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> BvrR/BvrS regulatory system action activate sensor domain of the BvrS protein by environmental signals through kinase activity. Additionally, BvrS causes phosphorylation and activation of BvrR protein. BvrR activates transcription of <italic toggle="yes">omp3a, omp3b</italic> and other genes responsible for lipid A structure and perhaps core of LPS. In consequence, <italic toggle="yes">bvrS/bvrR</italic> mutants are more sensitive to cationic peptides and display increased permeability for surfactants (<xref rid="bib61" ref-type="bibr">L&#243;pez-Go&#241;i <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="bib88" ref-type="bibr">Seleem <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). The influence of Omp3a and Omp3b on virulence remains unexplained in details (<xref rid="bib62" ref-type="bibr">Manterola <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2007</xref>).</p><p>It has been proven that BvrR/BvrS two-component system regulate the expression of <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> by positive stimulation of <italic toggle="yes">vjbR</italic> transcription. <italic toggle="yes">VjbR</italic> transcriptional factor interacts with <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> promoter (Mart&#237;nez-N&#363;&#241;ez <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2002).</p><p><bold>Role of virulence factors in chronic persistence</bold>. Evading an immunological response to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigens depends on LPS structure. The appearance of elongated fatty acid on the lipid A (<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-C<sub>28</sub> compared to others Enterobacteriaceae C<sub>12</sub>&#8211;C<sub>16</sub>) leads to poor activation of TLR4 (Tool-like receptor 4) (<xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figueiredo <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>; <xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Byndloss and Tsolis, 2016</xref>). The other feature of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. LPS is core oligosaccharide glycosylation pattern that prevents a connection of the bacteria with TLR4 (co-receptor MD-2) (<xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Byndloss and Tsolis, 2016</xref>). Toll-like receptors, the transmembrane proteins, act as PRRs (Pattern recognition receptors) and initiate the innate immune responses. TLRs are responsible for the recognition of components of microorganism (<xref rid="bib98" ref-type="bibr">Uemattsu <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). The TLR protein is composed of two domains: extracellular domain that is rich in leucine repeats and it is responsible for recognition of microbial components, and cytoplasmic domain &#8211; TIR, involved in signal transmission, activation of intermediate proteins and finally an activation of NF-&#312;&#946; and cytokines (<xref rid="bib80" ref-type="bibr">Radhakrishnan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2009</xref>). TLR5 detects flagellin, but flagellin in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. can avoid interaction with TLR5 as it lacks the domain recognized by TLR. (<xref rid="bib5" ref-type="bibr">Andersen-Nissen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>; <xref rid="bib55" ref-type="bibr">Kim, 2015</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> encoded TcpB/BtpA protein that acts as a following virulence factor (TcpB &#8211; <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, Btp1/BtpA &#8211; <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>). Three pathogenic microorganism can produce similar proteins: <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (<xref rid="bib80" ref-type="bibr">Radhakrishnan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2009</xref>). These proteins contain TIR domain and show similarity to TIRAP (MAL) &#8211; a TLR adaptor protein. TcpB promotes a degradation of TIRAP and disrupts TLR4 signalling that result in inhibition of proinflammatory cytokines production and dendritic cell maturation (<xref rid="bib72" ref-type="bibr">Newman <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>; <xref rid="bib73" ref-type="bibr">Oliveira <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>; <xref rid="bib80" ref-type="bibr">Radhakrishnan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2009</xref>; <xref rid="bib89" ref-type="bibr">Sengupta <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>; <xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Byndloss and Tsolis, 2016</xref>). TIRAP triggers recruitment of MyD88 and hence mediates TLR4 and TLR2 signalling (<xref rid="bib54" ref-type="bibr">Kagan and Medzhitov, 2006</xref>). It seems to be likely that TcpB is able to interact with Death Domain of MyD88 and affect the signalling pathway (<xref rid="bib27" ref-type="bibr">Chaudhary <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2012</xref>). Another group of researchers has proven that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> encodes another TIR domain-containing protein, called BtpB (present in all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains). This protein reacts with MyD88, inhibits TLR signalling, and disrupts activation of dendritic cells BtpB, restraining TLR2, TLR4, TLR5 and TLR9 signalling; and together with BtpA affects DC maturation and inflects host inflammatory responses (<xref rid="bib84" ref-type="bibr">Salcedo <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>).</p><p><italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> has proline racemase and thus is able to produce anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10. This cytokine modulates macrophage activity during early phase of infection and leads to persistence and long-term survival of microorganism inside host cells (<xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Byndloss and Tsolis, 2016</xref>).</p><p><bold>Role of virulence factors in reproductive disease.</bold> The investigations performed on bovine placental explants have proven that in early infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. aborus</italic> the suppression of proinflammatory cytokines occured. This process is dependent on BtpB and T4SS proteins (use of mutants of the <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">btpB</italic> genes results in the reverse effect by enhancement of the proinflammatory cytokines production) (<xref rid="bib69" ref-type="bibr">Mol <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2014</xref>). In later phases, 12 h after infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, stimulation of proinflammatory cytokines and CXC chemokines production &#8211; CXCL6 (GCP-2) and CXCL8 (interleukin 8) takes place. CXCL6 and IL-8 known as neutrophil chemoattractants cause neutrophil influx and have been reported to cause necrotizing placentis after infecting a pregnant cow (Carvalho <xref rid="bib22" ref-type="bibr">Neta <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="Pathogenesis" id="sec4"><title>Pathogenesis</title><p><bold>Invasion to cell.</bold> The <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains survive and multiply within both phagocytic and non-phagocytic cells. The main targets for this bacterium are macrophages, dendritic cells and trophoblast cells. However, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can also multiply within other cells, for example epithelioid cell (HeLa) or murine fibroblast (NIH3T3) (<xref rid="bib77" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>; <xref rid="bib26" ref-type="bibr">Celli, 2006</xref>; <xref rid="bib102" ref-type="bibr">Xavier <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> translocates across the mucosal epithelial cells layer, where the professional phagocytes (macrophages and DC cells) engulf the bacteria. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> survives within non-phagocytic cells up to 72 hours after infection, overcomes the epithelial barrier and then penetrates the phagocytic cells. Approximately 10% of these bacteria survive this initial phase. In macrophages the pathogen avoids the host immune response; therefore, it can multiply and spread to other tissues using cellular tropism. The <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains penetrate the host cells through a zipper-like mechanism (<xref rid="bib47" ref-type="bibr">Gorvel and Moreno, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figureido <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). Bacteria can spread in a host through the lymph nodes and then translocate to the preferred tissues in reproductive tract (<xref rid="bib55" ref-type="bibr">Kim, 2015</xref>). There, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> induces acute or chronic infection of reproductive tract that leads to abortion or severe reproductive tract diseases. (<xref rid="bib51" ref-type="bibr">He, 2012</xref>).</p><p>The non-opsonized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> organisms are internalized through lectin or fibronectin receptors but opsonized by complement and Fc receptors. The opsonized bacteria are more prone to be destroyed within macrophages than non-opsonized ones. The pathogen binds to receptors containing of sulfated residues and sialic acid on surfaces of epithelial cells (<xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figureido <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>).</p><p>Penetration into the epithelial cell requires actin polymerization (<xref rid="bib55" ref-type="bibr">Kim, 2015</xref>). The adhesion of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> to the cell surface leads to activation of GTPases of Rho subfamily, <italic toggle="yes">e.g</italic>. Rho, Rac, and Cdc42 (<xref rid="bib49" ref-type="bibr">Guzm&#225;n-Verri <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>). These proteins are involved in cytoskeletal regulation and have an impact on parasitic bacterial internalization. Cdc42 is the only GTPase activated directly by <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> during the contact with a non-phagocytic cell. It seems that other GTP-ases (Rho or Rac) are activated indirectly, because their inhibition impede invasion into host cells (<xref rid="bib99" ref-type="bibr">Waterman-Storer <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1999</xref>). Other protein acting as second messengers, <italic toggle="yes">i.e</italic>. cGMP, PIP3-kinase, MAP-kinase and tyrosine kinase are involved in adhesion of bacteria to the host cell surface (<xref rid="bib49" ref-type="bibr">Guzm&#225;n-Verri <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>).</p><p>Adhesion to macrophage surface is also associated with small GTPases activation (<xref rid="bib49" ref-type="bibr">Guzm&#225;n-Verri <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001</xref>) and F-actin polymerization (transient and rapid F-actin accumulation). In the early stages of adhesion Annexin I is also involved, a protein that is implicated in membrane fusion (<xref rid="bib57" ref-type="bibr">Kusumawati <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>). Bacterial internalization occurs also by the lipid rafts &#8211; microdomains that occur in the macrophage cell membrane. These structure contribute to intracellular trafficking of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (Fig. <xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">1</xref>) (<xref rid="bib102" ref-type="bibr">Xavier <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). Non-opsonized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains internalize in human monocytes and murine macrophages by lipid rafts. This process requires activation of TLR4 and PI3-kinase. However, this process in human dendritic cells is only partly dependent on lipid rafts (<xref rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">von Bargen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2012</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains with lack of O-polysaccharides in LPS (R-LPS) do not penetrate eukaryotic cells by lipid rafts, and thus are exterminated by macrophages (<xref rid="bib79" ref-type="bibr">Porte <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2003</xref>; <xref rid="bib45" ref-type="bibr">Gomez <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>). Lipid rafts are rich in cholesterol, GPI (glycosylphosphatidylinositol) and GM1 (gangliosides) (<xref rid="bib18" ref-type="bibr">Brown and London, 1998</xref>). Lipid rafts-associated proteins: GPI and GM1 as well as cholesterol inosculate with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-contain macropinosomes and facilitate internalization with macrophage (<xref rid="bib71" ref-type="bibr">Naroeni and Porte, 2002</xref>).</p><fig id="fig1" orientation="portrait" position="float"><label>Fig. 1.</label><caption><p>Mammalian cell invasion and intracellular trafficking.</p><p>Smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> invasion into a cell by lipid rafts and acquisition of Rab5 and EEA1 markers &#8211; early BCV. &#946;-1,2-glucans present in mature BCV and modification of lipid rafts. Then, the transient BCV interacts with lysosomes, T4SS is activated and it regulates intracellular trafficking from autophagosome to endoplasmatic reticulum (BCV acquires LAMP1 and Sec61&#946; markers &#8211; late BCV &#8211; occurs only in epithelial cells). BCV acquires the endoplasmic reticulum markers (calnexin, calreticulin and Sec61-&#946;) and the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replicates. Rough <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> organisms do not penetrate cell by lipid rafts, and therefore is exterminated.</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="pjm-67-2-151-f001.jpg"/></fig><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can be recognized by TLRs, but owing to modifications its interaction with TLRs is 10-fold lesser than for Enterobacteria. Hence, the activation of NF-&#954;&#946; and production of inflammatory cytokines is weaker (<xref rid="bib37" ref-type="bibr">de Figureido <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>).</p><p><bold>Intracellular trafficking.</bold> It has been supposed that intracellular trafficking is not essentially different in professional and non-professional phagocytes (<xref rid="bib47" ref-type="bibr">Gorvel and Moreno, 2002</xref>). After several minutes of invasion, the bacteria interact with early endosomal network-related compartment &#8211; early BCV &#8211; <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Containing Vacuole. This compartment is characterized by Rab5 (GTP-binding protein) and EEA1 (early endosomal antigen 1) markers (<xref rid="bib78" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>; <xref rid="bib77" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2000</xref>). &#946;-1,2-glucans are necessary for the regulation of BCV maturation in macrophages as well as in epithelial cells. Additional function of &#946;-1,2-glucan is the modification of rich in cholesterol lipid rafts, which are located on BCV membrane surface (<xref rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">Arellano-Reynoso <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). The interaction with early endocytic network last about 10 minutes (<xref rid="bib78" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). At this stage, acidification of BCV takes place leading to changes on the bacterial genes expression and enabling intracellular survival (<xref rid="bib23" ref-type="bibr">Carvalho Neta <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2010</xref>). BCV does not interact with late endosome and it avoids fusion with lysosomes (by &#946;-glucans and LPS occurrence) (<xref rid="bib47" ref-type="bibr">Gorvel and Moreno, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="bib26" ref-type="bibr">Celli, 2006</xref>). However, early BCV is transformed to intermediate BCV that is LAMP1 and Rab-7 marked (late endosomal/lysosomal markers), indicating that interactions with late endosomal compartments and lysosomes become necessary. What is more, in this step BCV acquires also Rab-interacting lysosomal protein (RILP) that is Rab-7 effector (<xref rid="bib94" ref-type="bibr">Starr <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>; <xref rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">von Bargen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2012</xref>; <xref rid="bib45" ref-type="bibr">Gomez <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>). The interaction between BCV and late endosomes/lysosomes is transitional and controlled. This event allows for acidification of BCV and expression of acidic-contingent bacterial factors, <italic toggle="yes">e.g., virB</italic>; simultaneously, the cathepsin D action does not take place (Boschiroli <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2001; <xref rid="bib94" ref-type="bibr">Starr <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>; <xref rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">von Bargen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2012</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> type IV secretion (T4SS encoded by <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> operon) is responsible for regulation of intracellular trafficking from autophagosome to endoplasmatic reticulum (ER) (Fig. <xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">1</xref>) (<xref rid="bib47" ref-type="bibr">Gorvel and Moreno, 2002</xref>).</p><p>About 1 hour after internalization, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> organisms are located within multimembranous autophagosome with LAMP1 and Sec61&#946; (calreculin). This structure is also called as a late BCV and occurs only in epithelial cells (<xref rid="bib78" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). LAMP1 function is not accurately described; however, it presumably participates in the pathogen intracellular survival (<xref rid="bib47" ref-type="bibr">Gorvel and Moreno, 2002</xref>). The final step of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intercellular trafficking is an acquisition of the markers characteristic of the endoplasmic reticulum: calnexin, calreticulin and Sec61&#946;, although in this step BCV lose the LAMP-1 (<xref rid="bib78" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). However, this protein is constantly present in large vacuole only in human monocytes, in which opsonized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> multiply (<xref rid="bib13" ref-type="bibr">Bellaire <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2005</xref>). ER is the only compartment that is suitable for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication (<xref rid="bib78" ref-type="bibr">Pizarro-Cerd&#225; <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 1998</xref>). The mechanism of BCV-ER connection remains unclear. In this process there are involved small GTPases Rab2, GAPDH &#8211; glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase, the COPI complex (Coat Protein Complex I) and protein kinase C (PKC<sub>I</sub>). COPI and PKC<sub>I</sub> control vesicular trafficking to the ER from Golgi. GAPDH/COPI/Rab2/PKC<sub>I</sub> complex is responsible for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication within the ER (<xref rid="bib39" ref-type="bibr">Fugier <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2009</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="Treatment of brucellosis" id="sec5"><title>Treatment of brucellosis</title><p>Currently, there are no effective vaccines for human, although several <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines are accessible for livestock. Live, attenuated vaccines bereft of virulence factors, still present residual virulence (<italic toggle="yes">e.g.</italic>, Live <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain 19, Live <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain RB51, Live <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain Rev-1). Subunit vaccines are proven to be relatively safe and they raise less concerns compared to live vaccines. They do not cause infection, as they present purified proteins or DNA to stimulate immune response. Researchers are still working on the improvement in livestock vaccines and their application in preventing human infections (<xref rid="bib103" ref-type="bibr">Yang <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>).</p><p>To implement a successful treatment against brucellosis, antibiotics penetrating into macrophages as well as active in acidic environment are essential (<xref rid="bib81" ref-type="bibr">Ranjbar, 2015</xref>). Brucellosis is a disease, that rarely leads to death and responds well to diverse therapeutic strategies (<xref rid="bib93" ref-type="bibr">Sol&#237;s <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). However, single-antibiotics therapy is inadequate in brucellosis, as it leads to relapse of disease (<xref rid="bib75" ref-type="bibr">Pappas <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2006</xref>). Similarly, the therapy with single agent like: oxytetracycline, rifampin or doxycycline, causes high rate of relapses (9&#8211;25%) and prolongation of therapy does not provide satisfying effects. The treatment with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole or ciprofloxacin results in relapse in 30% and 83% of cases, respectively (<xref rid="bib81" ref-type="bibr">Ranjbar, 2015</xref>). Treatment should prevent relapse of disease, further complications (arthritis, spondylitis, sacroilitis <italic toggle="yes">etc</italic>.) and enable quick relief of symptoms. The combination of two antibiotics in therapy of infections caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is more effective than monotherapy. The WHO in 1986 recommended doxycycline with rifampicin for six weeks, replaced with tetracycline in combination with streptomycin. Currently, the combinations of other antibiotics or chemotherapeutics in therapy of brucellosis are used, such as fluoroquinolones or co-trimoxazole with rifampicin, doxycycline-streptomycin and doxycycline-rifampicin (<xref rid="bib90" ref-type="bibr">Skalsky <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2008</xref>). During the treatment of brucellosis with streptomycin and doxycycline (SD), a failure of treatment and relapse rates at 7.4% and 4.8%, respectively were noted. Almost similar results of therapy were observed during therapy with doxycycline and rifampin (DR) or streptomycin together with tetracycline (ST); however, their relapse rates were higher than in SD treatment. Another dual therapies of brucellosis are known, for example doxycycline and gentamicin (DG) with the average failure rate of 5.2% and the relapse rate of 5.9%, or cotrimoxazole and rifampicin (RCTM) used in children brucellosis with the failure of treatment and relapse rates at 0&#8211;16.4% and 3.1&#8211;10%, respectively. The treatment with ciprofloxacin or ofloxacin with doxycycline, cotrimoxazole, rifampicin brought about the relapse rate between 3.2 to 26% (average 11.4%) and the failure rate between 3.2% to 26% (12.2%) (<xref rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">Alavi and Alavi, 2013</xref>). There were three clinical trials that used triple-drug therapy with doxycycline, rifampicin and aminoglycoside. There is no clear evidence on the superiority of triple-drug therapy when compared to the two-drug therapy. Nevertheless, it seems that triple-drug therapy is more effective in preventing relapses, but less successful in short-term treatment than two-drug therapy (<xref rid="bib93" ref-type="bibr">Sol&#237;s <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>). The research by <xref rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">Alavi and Alavi (2013)</xref> suggested triple therapy for eight weeks in complicated cases (with spondylitis, or arthritis) due to lower treatment failure rates than two-drug therapy. Doxycycline and aminoglycoside therapy is recommended in uncomplicated chronic, or acute cases and in complicated cases without endocarditis, spondylitis, arthritis. In uncomplicated cases, streptomycin and doxycycline, or gentamicin are also advocated (<xref rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">Alavi and Alavi, 2013</xref>).</p><p><xref rid="bib91" ref-type="bibr">Smith and colleagues (2013)</xref> found a new strategy to treat brucellosis. The connection of BCV with the ER requires remodeling of endoplasmatic reticulum, which is necessary in modification of the ER structure during host stress response, which is called the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR). The disruption of UPR, through tauroursodeoxycholic acid drug can inhibit <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication. UPR can be a novel target in the of brucellosis (<xref rid="bib91" ref-type="bibr">Smith <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2013</xref>).</p><p>There are studies concerning the influence of ginseng saponin fraction A (RGSF-A) for combating brucellosis. Ginseng is a valued plant in Asia, considered a panacea to variety range of diseases. <xref rid="bib6" ref-type="bibr">Arayan <italic toggle="yes">et al</italic>. (2015)</xref> examined the influence of RGSF-A for eradication of bacterial infection in RAW 264.7 cells. In this study, the bacterial internalization and adhesion were reduced in the cells treated when compared to the control cells without treatment. RGSF-A takes part in down-regulation of MAPKs (mitogen-activated protein kinases) and hence, limits polymerization of F-actin and inhibits bacterial penetration into the cells. RGSF-A influences also intracellular trafficking of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and favors interaction of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>-containing phagosomes (BCPs) with LAMP-1. LAMP-1 is transmembrane protein, that is responsible for the fusion of lysosomes with phagosomes, enabling the connection of BCPs with lysosome and elimination of bacteria (<xref rid="bib6" ref-type="bibr">Arayan <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2015</xref>; <xref rid="bib82" ref-type="bibr">Reyes <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2016</xref>; <xref rid="bib52" ref-type="bibr">Huy <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2017</xref>). <xref rid="bib52" ref-type="bibr">Huy <italic toggle="yes">et al</italic>. (2017)</xref> have proven that ginsenoside Rg3 &#8211; panaxadiol saponin components of RGSF-A have been the major factor controlling brucellosis.</p><p>Undoubtedly, there are also other promising herbal plants <italic toggle="yes">e.g. Teucrium polium, Scophularia deserti, Alhagi, Eucalyptus</italic>, garlic and roots of barberry that contain bioactive ingredients (flavones, flavonoids, anthocyanins and tanins) that can be effecive in preventing or even combat brucellosis (<xref rid="bib70" ref-type="bibr">Naghadi <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic>, 2016</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="Conclusions" id="sec6"><title>Conclusions</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is an intracellular pathogen, especially dangerous for domestic animals, which causes massive infections and thus significant economic losses. Moreover, people who work with infected animals comprise a risk group, <italic toggle="yes">e.g.</italic>, farmers, veterinarians, or laboratorians and they are most endangered of being exposed to the pathogen. Brucellosis in human causes non-specific symptoms, therefore no plausible estimation can be managed to detect the number of infected people. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is an inquisitive etiological agent, as does not produce classical virulence factors. The process of infection is a complex one, and there are many unexplained issues associated with it. Therefore, further studies of infection mechanisms are required.</p></sec></body><back><ref-list content-type="none"><title>Literature</title><ref id="bib1"><mixed-citation publication-type="journal"><person-group person-group-type="author"><name name-style="western"><surname>Alavi</surname><given-names>S.M.</given-names></name> and <name name-style="western"><surname>Alavi</surname><given-names>L.</given-names></name></person-group><year>2013</year><article-title>Treatment of brucellosis: a systematic review of studies in recent twenty years</article-title>. <source>Caspian.
