2018</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="s1"><title>GENOME ANNOUNCEMENT</title><p>Brucellosis is the most common bacterial zoonotic disease worldwide, infecting over half a million people annually (<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>). <named-content content-type="genus-species">Brucella</named-content> species are intracellular Gram-negative bacteria which were first isolated in 1887 by Sir David Bruce from the spleens of soldiers with fatal cases of brucellosis (<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>). Among the six classically known <named-content content-type="genus-species">Brucella</named-content> species, <named-content content-type="genus-species">B. melitensis</named-content> is considered the most pathogenic species in humans (<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>). <named-content content-type="genus-species">Brucella</named-content> species cause abortions in the third trimester in their natural hosts (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>), and live attenuated vaccines are aimed at preventing these events in order to reduce the environmental bacterial load. <named-content content-type="genus-species">B. melitensis</named-content> vaccine strain Rev.1 was developed by Elberg and Herzberg in the mid-1950s (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>) and was shown to successfully protect and reduce abortions among small ruminants (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). Unlike <named-content content-type="genus-species">B. melitensis</named-content> type strains, Rev.1 is resistant to streptomycin and susceptible to penicillin G (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>, <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). In 1970, passage 101 was made available as a freeze-dried seed stock culture. The strain originating from this passage resembled the original parental seed material, making it compatible for prophylactic vaccination of small ruminants (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). One ampule of the lyophilized stock was sent to Menachem Banai (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>), at the Israeli National, OIE and FAO Reference Laboratory for Brucellosis, Kimron Veterinary Institute, Israel, following his personal correspondence with Sanford Elberg (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>). An ampule of the same Rev.1 seed material was sent simultaneously to Jean-Michel Verger, INRA, who further reestablished its innocuousness and deposited the strain in European Pharmacopoeia (<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>). Here, we report a whole-genome sequence of this original strain.</p><p>Genomic DNA was purified using the DNeasy blood and tissue kit (Qiagen) and was sent for sequencing in both PacBio and Illumina platforms (Institute for Genome Sciences, Baltimore, MD, and Crown Institute for Genomics, G-INCPM, Weizmann Institute of Science, Israel, respectively). The average read length of the PacBio raw data was &#8764;7&#8201;kb, with a maximum read length of about 42,000 bases (coverage, &#8764;496&#215;). Illumina paired-end sequencing generated 1.2 million 2 &#215; 250-bp reads (coverage, &#8764;182&#215;). Hybrid assembly of the genome using both short (Illumina) and long (PacBio) reads was done using the Unicycler pipeline (<xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>).</p><p>The genome assembly produced two large scaffolds of 2,121,368 and 1,177,802&#8201;bp in length, representing the two <named-content content-type="genus-species">B. melitensis</named-content> chromosomes. The sizes of the chromosomes were highly similar to those of the 16M reference strain (2,117,144 and 1,177,787&#8201;bp), generating an estimated total genome size of 3,299,170&#8201;bp. Genome annotation was carried out using the Rapid Annotations using Subsystems Technology (RAST) server (<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>). The total genome had a G+C content of 57.2% and contained 3,327 open reading frames (ORFs), 54 tRNAs, and 9 rRNA genes. Out of the 3,327 coding sequences, 1,741 were categorized into RAST subsystems. Within the 1,741 annotated coding sequences (CDSs), the majority were classified into subsystems of amino acids and derivatives (429 CDSs), carbohydrates (367 CDSs), cofactors, vitamins, prosthetic groups, pigments (265 CDSs), protein metabolism (246 CDSs), and membrane transport (161 CDSs). A further detailed analysis will be included in our future publication.</p><sec id="s1.1"><title>Accession number(s).</title><p>This whole-genome shotgun project has been deposited in DDBJ/EMBL/GenBank under the accession numbers <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/CP024715" assigning-authority="genbank">CP024715</ext-link> and <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/CP024716" assigning-authority="genbank">CP024716</ext-link>.</p></sec></sec></body><back><fn-group><fn fn-type="other"><p><bold>Citation</bold> Salmon-Divon M, Banai M, Bardenstein S, Blum SE, Kornspan D. 2018.
