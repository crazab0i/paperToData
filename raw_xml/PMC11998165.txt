However, there is currently no <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine available for humans. Although some attenuated live vaccines have been approved for animals, their protective efficacy is suboptimal. In previous studies, we utilized an epitope- and structure-based vaccinology platform to identify the immunodominant epitopes of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigens OMP19, OMP16, OMP25, and L7/L12, and constructed the multi-epitope vaccine MEV-Fc against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. MEV-Fc enhanced Th1 and Th2 immune responses and strongly induced the production of the pro-inflammatory cytokine IFN-&#947;. Furthermore, MEV-Fc protected mice against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection compared to control group (PBS). In conclusion, our results provide new insights and data support for the development of human <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines.</p><sec><title>Supplementary Information</title><p>The BioMed Central Ltd., part of Springer Nature 2025</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="Sec1"><title>Introduction</title><p id="Par2">Brucellosis is one of the most common zoonotic diseases worldwide, capable of being transmitting from animals to humans, causing severe infections in both animals and humans and affecting multiple body systems [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>]. The high incidence rates in humans and animals render this infection a significant public health concern in underdeveloped countries, while also imposing considerable economic losses on animal production [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>]. In livestock, this condition leads to abortion, stillbirth, infertility, higher mortality rates in young animals, prolonged calving intervals, reduced feed efficiency, weight loss, and decreased milk production [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>]. Humans can contract the disease through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of contaminated raw dairy products [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>]. Symptoms of human infection include fatigue, sweating, chills, fluctuating fever, myalgia, and arthralgia [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>]. Currently, vaccination stands as the most effective method for the prevention and control of this zoonotic disease. Live attenuated vaccines for veterinary use have been widely employed and play a critical role in controlling <italic toggle="yes">brucellosis</italic> epidemics; However, these live attenuated veterinary vaccines have several drawbacks, including human infection, spontaneous abortion in animals, antibiotic resistance, and insufficient protective efficacy [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. Currently, there is no approved vaccine for human infection caused by this pathogen. To safeguard the livelihood and health of populations at risk, especially slaughterhouse workers who face continuous exposure to infected animals, an urgent need exists to develop safe and effective alternatives to the existing live attenuated vaccines against this zoonotic disease.</p><p id="Par3">Studies have shown that subunit vaccines exhibit high safety and specificity, with fewer side effects following vaccination [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>]. However, due to limited antigenic epitopes, their protective efficacy is lower compared to traditional vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>]. Over the past few decades, a variety of antigens, such as OMP19, OMP25, L7/L12, and OMP16, have been studied from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>]. They are considered promising candidate proteins due to their high immunogenicity and ability to induce in vivo immune protection. OMP16 and OMP19 are outer membrane lipoproteins located on the surface of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, both exposed to the bacterial surface and commonly present across all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains. OMP25, a <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> outer membrane protein, plays a critical role in maintaining membrane integrity due to its high conservation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">14</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">15</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> ribosomal protein L7/L12, a conserved protein with immunogenic properties, plays an auxiliary role in immune protection. These antigens have demonstrated the ability to prevent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections by reducing bacterial loads in organs of mice. However, subunit vaccines using known antigens cannot achieve the protection levels conferred by live attenuated vaccines. Further studies are required to identify novel antigens to enhance vaccine efficacy [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>]. Multiepitope vaccines have emerged as an attractive alternative to traditional live attenuated and inactivated vaccines due to their favorable safety profiles and ability to elicit targeted immune responses against specific epitopes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>].</p><p id="Par4">Bioinformatics techniques have shown significant advantages in modern vaccine design and development, including ease of operation, cost efficiency, and higher success rates [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>]. Using this technology allows for efficient evaluation of the immunogenicity of candidate subunit vaccines. Particularly in multiepitope vaccine design, bioinformatics employs reliable in silico tools to predict MHC binding sites, T cell, and B cell epitopes, thereby significantly shortening development timelines and reducing risk and cost [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>]. Compared to traditional laboratory methods, bioinformatics not only enhances prediction accuracy but also facilitates the design of more effective vaccines tailored for the protection of various common hosts against different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">21</xref>].</p><p id="Par5">In this study, an epitope- and structure-based vaccinology platform was applied to develop a multiepitope candidate vaccine, MEV-Fc, composed of CTL, HTL, and B cell epitopes from OMP16, OMP19, L7/L12, and OMP25, along with molecular adjuvants and an Fc fragment. The antigenicity, physicochemical, and structural properties of the candidate vaccine were analyzed, and the rOMP16, rOMP19, rL7/L12, rOMP25, and rMEV-Fc proteins from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> were expressed and purified. Subsequently, their immunogenicity and protective efficacy were evaluated and compared in a <italic toggle="yes">brucellosis</italic> model using BALB/c mice.</p></sec><sec id="Sec2"><title>Materials and methods</title><sec id="Sec3"><title>Animals and ethics statement</title><p id="Par6">All animal experiments were approved by the Bioethics Committee of Shihezi University and conducted in accordance with the Committee&#8217;s recommendations (A2024-436). The animal breeding was carried out at the Experimental Animal Center of the College of Animal Science and Technology, Shihezi University. The environmental temperature was controlled at (23&#8201;&#177;&#8201;3)&#8451; and the humidity at (55%&#177;5%). Before use, materials such as cages, water bottles, and bedding were sterilized. Food, bedding, and water were replaced every four days.</p></sec><sec id="Sec4"><title>Acquisition and construction of candidate vaccine sequences</title><p id="Par7">The amino acid sequences of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> major antigens OMP19 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="AAB06277.1">AAB06277.1</ext-link>), OMP16 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="AEF59023.1">AEF59023.1</ext-link>), OMP25 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="AFJ79953.1">AFJ79953.1</ext-link>) and L7/L12 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="AAL51929.1">AAL51929.1</ext-link>) were obtained from the NCBI database. Based on our previous research [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>], the dominant CTL and HTL epitopes and B-cell epitopes of the antigens OMP16, OMP19, L7/L12, and OMP25 from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> were linked using GPGPG linkers to form Multi-epitope Vaccines (MEV). The human beta-defensins 3 (hBD-3) sequence and a pan HLA-DR-binding epitope (PADRE) sequence were linked to the N-terminal of the MEV through EAAAK linkers. Moreover, the mouse IgG Fc fragment (P01868-1) sequence was linked to the C-terminal of the MEV via KK linkers, resulting in the complete vaccine construct sequence, named MEV-Fc. Finally, the DNA sequences for OMP16, OMP19, L7/L12, OMP25, and MEV-Fc were obtained through reverse translation and codon optimization of their amino acid sequences (Supplementary Table <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="Sec5"><title>Evaluation of physicochemical properties of candidate vaccines</title><p id="Par8">The characteristics of the candidate vaccine were evaluated using bioinformatics analysis software. First, the ProtParam server (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://www.expasy.org/resources/protparam">https://www.expasy.org/resources/protparam</ext-link>) was used to analyze its physicochemical properties [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>]. Antigenicity and immunogenicity analyses were conducted using the VaxiJen v2.0 server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">24</xref>] and the IEDB Class I Immunogenicity server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">25</xref>]. Toxicity was assessed using ToxinPred, with the threshold value set at 0.6 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">26</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="Sec6"><title>Secondary and tertiary structure prediction</title><p id="Par9">The secondary structure of MEV-Fc was predicted using the SOPMA secondary structure prediction tool (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://npsa-pbil.ibcp.fr/cgibin/npsa_automat.pl?page=/NPSA/npsa_sopma">http://npsa-pbil.ibcp.fr/cgibin/npsa_automat.pl?page=/NPSA/npsa_sopma</ext-link>. html) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>]. The tertiary structure of MEV-Fc was predicted using the Robetta server (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://robetta.bakerlab.org/">http://robetta.bakerlab.org/</ext-link>) and further assessed through Swiss-model Structure Assessment and Ramachandran Plot (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://swissmodel.expasy.org/assess">https://swissmodel.expasy.org/assess</ext-link>). The quality of the constructed model was evaluated. The Prosa web server (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://prosa.services.came.sbg.ac.at/prosa.php/">https://prosa.services.came.sbg.ac.at/prosa.php/</ext-link>) was used to further assess uncertainties in the tertiary structure [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="Sec7"><title>Expression purification of recombinant proteins</title><p id="Par10">After optimizing the synthesis of DNA sequences for OMP19, OMP16, L7/L12, OMP25, and MEV-Fc (Generalbiol, China), the sequences were inserted into the expression vector pET-22b (Ampicillin) and then transfected into <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> (BL21, DE3). Induction was performed overnight at 15 &#8451; using 0.2 mM IPTG. The products were purified using high-affinity Ni-NTA resin (ClonTech, China). The concentration of the purified recombinant proteins was determined using a BCA protein assay kit (Thermo). Additionally, the proteins were analyzed using SDS-PAGE and western blot. </p></sec></sec><sec id="Sec24"><title>Discussion</title><p id="Par37">Due to the reemergence of brucellosis worldwide with increasing incidences of human infection, vaccination is considered to be the most effective prevention method [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">33</xref>]. The peptide-based vaccine approach provides an innovative path for vaccine development as it targets the most immunogenic peptide fragments to elicit an immune response, making it an attractive alternative in vaccine research [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>]. Yin et al., developed a multi-epitope subunit vaccine from OMP16, OMP2b, OMP31, and BP26 that conferred high levels of protection in mice against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">35</xref>]. The Fc-fusion protein technique is currently one of the most effective strategies for extending the half-life of protein- and peptide-based drugs. Antigen-Fc fusion proteins can specifically target Fc receptors on the surface of antigen-presenting cells, thereby improving their immunogenicity. Fusion of Fc fragments with specific antigens has emerged as a novel vaccination strategy for preventing infectious diseases [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>]. Therefore, in our previous study [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>], we designed an Fc-fusion multi-epitope vaccine (MEV-Fc) based on the immunodominant epitopes of OMP16, OMP19, L7/L12 and OMP25 to prevent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection.</p><p id="Par38">In this study, we first analyzed the physicochemical properties, secondary structure, and tertiary structure of the candidate vaccine. The results demonstrated that the candidate vaccine exhibited excellent antigenicity, stability, hydrophilicity, solubility, and non-toxicity. Protein regions with native unfolding and &#945;-helices are commonly recognized as typical structural antigens, while &#946;-turns and loosely coiled random structures are more favorable for forming antigenic epitopes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>]. Therefore, candidate vaccines with abundant such structures provide a robust structural foundation for vaccine development. Considering Robetta&#8217;s outstanding performance in protein tertiary structure prediction, we utilized it to predict the high-quality tertiary structures of the candidate vaccine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>]. Based on the Ramachandran plot, over 98% of residues were located in favorable and allowed regions, indicating the protein structures were accurately predicted. These findings suggest that the candidate vaccine possesses a strong structural basis for vaccine development.</p><p id="Par39">The <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> system offers unique advantages for large-scale production of safe, effective, and cost-efficient subunit vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>]. We employed the <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> expression system to obtain recombinant protein vaccines and peptide vaccines and evaluated their immunogenicity and protective efficacy in BALB/c mice. For intracellular pathogens, ensuring effective defense mechanisms requires a synergistic interplay of cell-mediated and humoral immune responses [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">40</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">41</xref>]. IELISA results showed a significant increase in IgG, IgG1 and IgG2a levels in the sera of mice immunized with all candidate vaccines. In the BALB/c mouse model, IgG1 and IgG2a antibodies serve as indicators of Th2 and Th1 polarization, respectively [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">42</xref>]. The mice immunized in the MEV-Fc group induced a balanced Th1 and Th2 response (the IgG2a/IgG1 ratio was approximately 1.0). Numerous studies claim that the protective immune response against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is associated with high-level Th1 and Th2 responses. This implies that candidate vaccines with great potential likely need to simultaneously trigger robust Th1 and Th2 responses to more effectively counter <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">43</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">44</xref>].</p><p id="Par40">The secretion of IFN-&#947; activates macrophages, enhances Th1 cell activity, and promotes cellular immune responses, playing a crucial role in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-specific cellular immunity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">46</xref>]. We found that when spleen lymphocytes were stimulated with the candidate vaccine, the MEV-Fc, S2, and rOMP25 groups exhibited significantly higher numbers of IFN-&#947;-secreting T cells compared to the PBS group. Additionally, IFN-&#947; secretion levels in mouse sera were detected using iELISA, with rL7/L12, MEV-Fc, and S2 groups showing significantly higher IFN-&#947; levels than the PBS control group. Interestingly, MEV-Fc and S2 exhibited significantly higher IFN-&#947; secretion levels in both lymphocyte stimulation and serum compared to the control group, whereas other groups displayed varying trends. One possible explanation is that ELISPOT reflects a localized response, mainly showing the activation of antigen-specific T cells in lymph nodes or the spleen, while serum IFN-&#947; represents a systemic immune response, which may be influenced by factors such as inflammation levels and reactions of other immune cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">46</xref>].</p><p id="Par41">CD4<sup>+</sup> and CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells play pivotal roles in the protection offered during <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection, with resistance to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> attributed to their synergistic effects [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR47">47</xref>]. Several previous studies have shown that both CD4<sup>+</sup> and CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells can play important roles in controlling <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR48">48</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">49</xref>]. In our results, the CD4<sup>+</sup>/CD8<sup>+</sup> T-cell ratio and the numbers of CD4<sup>+</sup>and CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells in the MEV-Fc group increased significantly. Although the importance of CD4<sup>+</sup> and CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells in the immunity against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection has been controversial [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">50</xref>], both of these T-cell populations are likely to be important [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>].</p><p id="Par42">Although mice are not the natural hosts of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, they are the most commonly used experimental models for studying the in-vivo virulence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>]. Therefore, in this study, BALB/c model animals were used to evaluate the protective effect of candidate vaccines. We found that the live vaccine (S2) had the highest protective effect against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M28 infection. This might be because it can effectively infect host cells and generate a variety of endogenous antigens in antigen-presenting cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">53</xref>], thereby eliciting a broader and more effective immune response to a real-life infection, which is higher than that of other candidate vaccines. Compared with the OMP16 group, OMP19 group, OMP25 group, and L7/L12 group, the MEV - Fc group had a significant protective effect, which might be attributed to a significant increase in the cellular immune response. These data are exciting because a polypeptide vaccine can provide effective protection in a mouse model of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. Subsequently, we will further explore the mechanism by which it protects mice from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection.</p><p id="Par43">Despite the promising nature of the current study, this study also has certain limitations: The synergistic effect between serum IgG and secretory IgA remains unclear. Our lack of knowledge of this mechanism may affect the comprehensive understanding of the immune response and the further optimization of vaccines. The route of immunization plays a crucial role in the immunogenicity of vaccines. In this study, the route of administration and the immunization schedule still need to be optimized. In addition, this study mainly conducted experimental evaluations using BALB/c mouse models. There are certain differences in physiology and immune responses between mouse models and humans, so they cannot fully and accurately reflect the efficacy and safety of vaccines in humans.</p></sec><sec id="Sec25"><title>Conclusions</title><p id="Par44">MEV-Fc successfully induced robust humoral and cellular immune responses by targeting multiple <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigens. This study aimed to develop a polypeptide vaccine against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. As a result, a novel peptide vaccine candidate, MEV-Fc, was designed, primarily targeting <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins OMP16, OMP19, L7/L12, and OMP25. By comparing the peptide vaccine containing antigen fragments of all four proteins with single recombinant proteins, we found that the peptide vaccine demonstrated superiority in multiple aspects. The polypeptide vaccine rMEV-Fc significantly stimulated the immune system, eliciting high levels of specific IgG and inducing both Th1 and Th2 responses. Moreover, it provided high protection efficiency in mice following bacterial challenge. Nevertheless, further experiments and clinical trials are needed to comprehensively assess the efficacy of this peptide vaccine under different conditions.</p></sec><sec id="Sec26" sec-type="supplementary-material"><title>Electronic supplementary material</title><p>Below is the link to the electronic supplementary material.</p><p>
