Molecular typing was used to characterize <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates.</p></sec><sec id="sec003"><title>Results</title><p>Ten percent of farmworkers tested seropositive and while <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> biovar 1 was isolated from the blood of three individuals, an isolate identical to the <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> S19 vaccine strain was isolated from a fourth person. All four bacteremic individuals were employed as milkers and did not have obvious disease.</p></sec><sec id="sec004"><title>Conclusions</title><p>The isolation of the highly infectious pathogen <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> from seropositive workers is consistent with the notion that the pathogen may persist in the blood without causing overt disease. While vaccination with strain S19 is essential for the control of bovine brucellosis the vaccine strain may be transmitted to the human population and protective measures remain important to prevent exposure also in view of the presence of <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>. To create awareness for this potentially severe disease more information on the prevalence of the pathogen in different risk groups and in livestock in the Sudan is Availability</title><p>All relevant data are within the paper.</p></notes></front><body><sec sec-type="intro" id="sec005"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is an highly infectious and contagious zoonotic disease feared for its debilitating and incapacitating character [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref001" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. Brucellosis is one of the most important zoonoses and is common in countries and communities where a large proportion of the inhabitants is involved in livestock farming activities, people live in close contact with their animals or consume raw milk and other dairy products prepared from fresh milk. Until recently the disease received very little attention as a cause of illness in African countries [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref002" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref003" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. Brucellosis is caused by infection with slow-growing, small, Gram negative, cocco-bacilla bacteria of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref004" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>]. Infection of livestock is associated with infertility, late term abortions, birth of weak calf, reduced milk production, and in males with orchitis and epididymitis [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref005" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref006" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. Human brucellosis is a febrile disease capable of masquerading as a myriad of entities, both infectious and non-infectious [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref007" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>]. The disease may affect any organ system and has a tendency towards chronicity and persistence. Brucellosis is difficult to diagnose requiring laboratory confirmation and treatment demands prolonged use of a combination of antibiotics with in some severe cases surgery [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref008" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>].</p><p>On the basis of pathogenicity, host preference and phenotypic characteristics, eleven <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species are recognized of which four, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">suis and</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">more rarely</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">canis</italic>, cause disease in human beings [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref009" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>]. Each of these species have different livestock species as their preferred host which for <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> is cattle, for <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> goat and sheep, for <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">suis</italic> swine and <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">canis</italic> infects dogs. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species are further sub-divided in subtypes or biovars [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref010" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>]. Although <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> is more infectious and more often causes disease in human beings compared to <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> no difference in disease presentation and severity is observed when comparing patients infected with the two pathogens [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref007" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>].</p><p>The first report on the presence of brucellosis in the Sudan dates back from 1908. Since then a series of studies have provided serological evidence for infection in cattle, goat, sheep and camels in different parts of Sudan [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref011" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref012" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>]. Much fewer studies have addressed the presence of brucellosis in the human population of the Sudan. A low seroprevalence rate (1%) was reported for occupational contacts including butchers, slaughterhouse workers, milkers and cow attendants in Kassala State in Central Sudan [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref013" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>]. In a more recent study a seroprevalence of 9% was reported for abattoir workers and the seroprevalence in camel nomads was as high as 60% [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref014" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>]. The identification of risk groups and knowledge of the species that is causing disease in the human population is important for the development of a control policy, surveillance strategy and implementation of preventive measures [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref015" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. Vaccination of livestock is the cornerstone for the control and prevention of brucellosis and effective and cheap life attenuated vaccines are available including S19 for cattle, and Rev-1 for goat and sheep [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref016" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. In this study we investigate the presence of brucellosis among farmworkers employed at two cattle farms in Khartoum state and applied multiple-locus variable-number tandem repeat analysis (MLVA-16) for the identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates cultured from the blood of seropositive individuals [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref017" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>].</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="sec006"><title>Materials and Methods</title><sec id="sec007"><title>Ethics Statement</title><p>The study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Institutional Review Board the University of Alzaiem Alazhari. An oral consent procedure approved by the Ethics Committee because of the limited impact of the data and sample collection procedure and illiteracy of part of the participants. The purpose of the study was explained to each participant and oral consent was recorded on the patient data sheet in the presence of one witness. Record sheets were kept in a secured place with access by the principal investigators only. Individuals who tested positive were referred for further medical examination.</p></sec><sec id="sec008"><title>Inclusion of Participants</title><p>Between April&#8212;May 2010 twenty farmworkers working at a cattle farm in Omdurman and eighty farmworkers employed at a cattle farm in Khartoum North Sharg Alneel in Khartoum state were asked to voluntarily donate a blood sample for investigation for brucellosis. A brief questionnaire was completed for all participants. Farmworkers were randomly selected and all 100 workers agreed to participate.</p></sec><sec id="sec009"><title>Laboratory Testing</title><p>Serological testing in the Rose Bengal test and in the Serum Agglutination Test (SAT) with BA and BM antigen was performed according to routine diagnostic procedures [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref018" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref019" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> IgM/IgG lateral flow assay (LFA) which consists of two cassettes, one for the detection of specific IgM antibodies and the other for the detection of specific IgG antibodies was performed as described [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref020" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. Briefly, 5 &#956;L serum was placed into the sample port of the assay device followed by the addition of 130 &#956;L running buffer supplied with the test. Test results are read after 10&#8211;15 min by visual inspection for a stained <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigen test line in the viewing port of the assay device. LFAs were scored positive when staining at the test and control lines was observed and scored negative when the test line remained unstained.</p><p>For blood culture 7.5 ml freshly collected venipuncture blood was mixed with 1.5 ml sodium citrate and 20 ml distilled water, centrifuged at 2,000 x g for 30 min and the pellet was cultured on both tryptose agar and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> agar (Hi medium) with supplements in 10% CO<sub>2</sub> at 37&#176;C for up to 2 weeks before considering negative for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref021" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>]. Blood culture was performed for seropositive individuals.</p></sec><sec id="sec010"><title>DNA Extraction and Multiple Locus Variable Number Tandem Repeat Analysis</title><p>Culture isolates were inactivated by suspending one loop of a solid bacterial culture in 200 &#956;l DNA storage and extraction buffer consisting of 5.25 M GuSCN, 20 mM EDTA, 1.3% (wt/vol) Triton X-100 and 1 mg/ml alpha-casein in 50 mM Tris.HCl (pH = 6.4). DNA extraction was performed as described by Boom and coworkers [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref022" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. PCR based on multiple locus variable number tandem repeat analysis (MLVA) genotyping of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates was performed with MLVA-16 panel 1 (bruce06, -08, -11, -12, -42, -43, -45 and -55) primer sets for species identification and MLVA-16 panels 2A (bruce18, -19 and -21) and 2B (bruce04, -07, -09, -16 and -30) primer sets for further subspecies differentiation [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref017" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. PCR products were separated by electrophoresis on 2% (panel 1) or 3% (panel 2) agarose gels stained with ethidium bromide and viewed by UV illumination. The length of the PCR product was deduced in dependence of the expected tandem repeat unit by comparison with a 100 bp or a 20 bp molecular marker ladder. For each run, DNA control from two reference strains was carried along. MLVA-16 patterns were compared with isolates in the public database <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> 2010 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://mlva.u-psud.fr">http://mlva.u-psud.fr</ext-link>; accessed May 2012) using cluster analysis performed by unweighted pair group method with arithmetic mean (UPGMA) algorithm [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref023" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>]. The distance between two genotypes is defined as the minimum number of changes in the number of repeats of any locus that converts one genotype to the other.</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="results" id="sec011"><title>Results</title><p>Blood samples collected from one hundred laborers working at two farms in the Khartoum area were investigated. The sera from 10 (10%) individuals tested seropositive for brucellosis and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> was successfully isolated from the blood of four of them (<xref rid="pone.0123374.t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). The four culture positive individuals were males between 26 and 38 years old employed as cattle milkers of which one also milked goat. The six culture negative seropositive individuals included four milkers, one veterinarian and one veterinarian technician. All participants denied feeling ill and to have physical complaints and none of them had been diagnosed with brucellosis before.</p><table-wrap id="pone.0123374.t001" orientation="portrait" The three other isolates all were identified as <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> biovar 1 with a MLVA-16 pattern identical to strain BCCN96_22 isolated from a sheep in Israel in 1996 [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref017" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. This strain is closely related (distance = 1) to strains that were isolated from sheep in South Africa and Spain between 1986 and 2000 [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref017" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>] and differs from the <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev1 vaccine strain by the number of repeats at panel 2B locus 16 (distance = 5) and from the 16M reference strain by the number of repeats at the panel 2A locus 18 and the panel 2B loci 07, 09 and 16 (distance = 11).</p></sec><sec sec-type="conclusions" id="sec012"><title>Discussion</title><p>All ten seropositive farmworkers employed at two farms in Khartoum state identified in this study including the four milkers with a positive blood culture were at work and did not present with obvious disease at the time the sample was collected. While brucellosis is feared because of its tendency to become chronic and the risk of severe disabling disease, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are able to survive by replication within macrophages, has a multiple mechanisms to evade the immune system and may persist in their host causing no or mild disease only. The finding of infected individuals without clear disease is not unusual and asymptomatic culture positive individuals have been previously identified by screening household members of brucellosis patients. Spink and Anderson [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref024" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>] described one case identified in 1950. Two asymptomatic household members of patients with a <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> biovar 3 infection one of whom was a breast feeding mother of a child with brucellosis and from whom the pathogen was isolated from breast milk were reported in a study performed in Turkey [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref025" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. Two other asymptomatic <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> blood culture positive household members from patients with brucellosis were described in a study from Peru [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref026" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>]. Evidence for the presence of the pathogen in the blood of occupational exposed asymptomatic individuals was obtained previously in studies applying the polymerase chain reaction for the detection of the DNA of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref027" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref028" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. Individuals with a latent subclinical infection are at risk of developing severe disease and should be treated. Therefore, the finding of culture positive farmworkers is worrisome given the limited awareness of clinicians in the Sudan to diagnose the disease and of the ignorance of the general population to protect from exposure. Brucellosis is rarely diagnosed in Sudan and must be confirmed by laboratory testing even symptomatic patients likely do not receive appropriate treatment. While the culture positive individuals did not report a history of brucellosis the possibility exists that brucellosis was misdiagnosed during a previous disease episode and evolved without severe sequellea or that an early disease episode was mild and medical care was not sought. Symptoms and signs of brucellosis are highly diverse and brucellosis is impossible to diagnose without laboratory conformation [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref001" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref007" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>].</p><p>The detection of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> culture positive individuals by screening occupational exposed farmworkers indicates that brucellosis is a common but underdiagnosed infection in the Sudan. In this study we applied a very simple and rapid field test, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> IgM/IgG lateral flow point-of-care test, for the serodiagnosis of brucellosis [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref001" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. The test is applied on a drop of blood collected by finger prick, does not requires specific equipment or training to perform and provides a very quick result [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref019" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. The test could be very useful for the confirmation of brucellosis in hospitals that do not have laboratory facilities and to provide medical services in the field for patients who do not have easy access to medical services [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref026" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref029" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>].</p><p>Close contact with infected livestock is a major risk factor for attracting brucellosis and milk collected from infected animals may contain high concentrations of the pathogen. Two of the culture positive individuals reported consumption of raw milk and one ate raw meat. Thus the farmworkers could have been exposed by different routes, including close contact with infected animals, exposure to contaminated milk and ingestion of raw foodstuff and the provision of information to farmworkers in Sudan on the use of protective measures will be important.</p><p>Cattle at the farms included in this study had been vaccinated with the S19 vaccine by teams of the by the Ministry of Animal Resources. The S19 vaccine is a live attenuated vaccine that is contagious for human and cases of clinical disease has been observed upon accidental exposure to the vaccine in veterinarians and vaccine plant workers [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref030" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref032" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>]. In a small proportion of vaccinated animals that aborted the vaccine strain can be isolated from abortion material [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref033" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>]. S19 also may be isolated from the milk of vaccinated cows. Therefore, farmers and other workers who are involved in vaccination or handle vaccinated cattle and consumers who have consumed contaminated milk from vaccinated cows that has not been pasteurized have a slight risk of infection and attracting the disease. It is not known whether cattle was positive for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> at the time of vaccination.</p><p>The finding of <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> in three farmworkers working at cattle farms is noteworthy. One of these farmworkers also milked goat which could have been the source of infection for that person. While the source of infection has not been investigated infection of cows with <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> has been reported [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref034" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref037" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>]. Transmission to cattle may occur when infected goats are kept together with cattle and mixed herding is an important risk factor in the transmission of <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> from goat to cattle. If indeed <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> is present in cattle in Sudan this will complicate the control of bovine brucellosis and may require an approach that also considers goats and sheep. The S19 vaccine does not protect cattle from infection with <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> and the <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev-1 vaccine for the vaccination of goat and sheep has not been validated for use in cattle [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref037" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>].</p><p>
<italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> biovar 1 was not identified in Sudan before. Studies employing culture have demonstrated the presence in Sudan of <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> biovar 3 in sheep and goat, <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> biovar 6 in cattle and <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> biovar 3 and biovar 6 in camel [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref038" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref039" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>].</p><p>In conclusion, isolation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> from 4% of milkers presents an unacceptable health risks and efforts should be undertaken to provide health education and to implement preventive measures. The isolation of <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> from cattle milkers suggests that the species is transmitted to cattle. The isolation of the <italic toggle="yes">B</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">abortus</italic> S19 vaccine strain from the blood of one farm employee raises concern about the safe use of the vaccine. Further studies are warranted to investigate the presence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in different livestock species in the Khartoum area and to determine the prevalence of brucellosis in different risk groups and hospitalize patients. Information on the prevalence of brucellosis and importance of the disease as a public health problem in Sudan is essential to create awareness and to improve early case detection. The use of a rapid and simple point-of-care test such as the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> IgM/IgG lateral flow assay could be very useful to provide medical services to hard-to-reach populations and to improve early case detection [<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref025" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>,<xref rid="pone.0123374.ref029" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>].</p></sec></body><back><ack><p>The authors would like to express their gratitude to their colleagues and technicians at the faculty of Medical Laboratory Science of the Alzaiem Alazhari University for their valuable support and would like to thank the head and staff at Department of Brucellosis of the Central Veterinary Research Laboratories in Soba, Khartoum for their kind assistance with this work.</p></ack><ref-list><title>References</title><ref id="pone.0123374.ref001"><label>1</label><mixed-citation publication-type="journal"><name name-style="western"><surname>Franco</surname><given-names>MP</given-names></name>, <name name-style="western"><surname>Mulder</surname><given-names>M</given-names></name>, <name name-style="western"><surname>Gilman</surname><given-names>RH</given-names></name>, <name name-style="western"><surname>Smits</surname><given-names>HL</given-names></name>. <article-title>Human brucellosis</article-title>. <source>Lancet Infect Dis</source>. <year>2007</year>;<volume>7</volume>: <fpage>775</fpage>&#8211;<lpage>786</lpage>.
