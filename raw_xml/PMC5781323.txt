Immunisation with attenuated vaccine is an effective method of prevention, but it can interfere with diagnosis. Live, attenuated <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> strain 104M has been used for the prevention of human brucellosis in China since 1965. However, at present, no fast and reliable method exists that can distinguish this strain from field strains. Single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP)-based assays offer a new approach for such discrimination. SNP-based minor groove binder (MGB) and Cycleave assays have been used for rapid identification of four <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine strains (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strains S19, A19 and RB51, and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev1). The main objective of this study was to develop a PCR assay for rapid and specific detection of strain 104M.</p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p id="Par2">We developed a SNP-based MGB PCR assay that could successfully distinguish strain 104M from 18 representative strains of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> biovars 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 and 9, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovars 1, 2 and 3, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovars 1, 2, 3 and 4, <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B. neotomae</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>), four <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine strains (A19, S19, S2, M5), and 55 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> clinical field strains. The assay gave a negative reaction with four non-<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species (<italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Pasteurella multocida</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus suis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>). The minimum sensitivity of the assay, evaluated using 10-fold dilutions of chromosomal DNA, was 220&#160;fg for the 104M strain and 76&#160;fg for the single non-104M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strain tested (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> A19). The assay was also reproducible (intra- and inter-assay coefficients of variation&#8201;=&#8201;0.006&#8211;0.022 and 0.012&#8211;0.044, respectively).</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusions</title><p id="Par3">A SNP-based MGB PCR assay was developed that could straightforwardly and unambiguously distinguish <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain 104M from non-104M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains. Compared to the classical isolation and identification approaches of bacteriology, this real-time PCR assay has substantial advantages in terms of simplicity and speed, and also reduces potential exposure to live <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. The assay developed is therefore a simple, rapid, sensitive, and specific tool for brucellosis diagnosis and control.</p></sec></abstract><kwd-group The Author(s) 2018</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="Sec1"><title>Background</title><p id="Par14">Brucellosis is a widespread zoonotic disease caused by various Gram-negative <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> bacterial species that damages human health and results in considerable economic losses. Annually, more than 500,000 new human brucellosis cases are reported worldwide [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>], and cases have increased rapidly over the last decade in all provinces in China [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>]. Human brucellosis is transmitted by eating contaminated food products of animal origin and via direct animal contact [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>], and although rarely fatal, it can be severely debilitating and disabling [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>].</p><p id="Par15">For over a century, vaccination and the culling of animals has been performed to control this disease [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>]. In China, live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains are widely used for the prevention and control of brucellosis, including <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> strain A19 in cattle, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> S2 used in swine, and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M5 in sheep and goat. In addition, live, attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104M has been adopted as a vaccine for use in humans since 1965. This strain, which was first isolated from the foetus of an aborted cow in the former Soviet republic in 1950, exhibits low virulence, high stability and high immuno-antigenicity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>].</p><p id="Par16">However, since it is a live attenuated strain, vaccination with 104M may cause vaccine-related cases of brucellosis, and it may be difficult to differentiate between a vaccine response and a natural infection, which complicates diagnosis. At present, a rapid and reliable method for distinguishing 104M from field strains is not available. The main objective of this study was to develop a PCR assay for rapid and specific detection of 104M.</p></sec><sec id="Sec2"><title>Methods</title><sec id="Sec3"><title>Strains and DNA extraction</title><p id="Par17"><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains used in the present study are listed in Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>. In addition, four non-target organisms (<italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> K99, <italic toggle="yes">Pasteurella multocida</italic> C48&#8211;1, <italic toggle="yes">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic> DI-1, and <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus suis</italic> ST171) were included. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains were cultured on tryptose agar at 37&#160;&#176;C with 5&#8211;10% CO<sub>2</sub> when required for 48&#8211;72&#160;h in a biosafety level 3-equipped laboratory. Bacteria were then washed with normal saline containing 0.5% formaldehyde, and inactivated at 37&#160;&#176;C for 24&#160;h. The four non-<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species were cultivated as described previously [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>], and harvested and inactivated as described above. The assay was reproducible, with intra-assay CVs ranging from 0.006 to 0.022, and inter-assay CVs of 0.012 to 0.044. The efficiency of the assay was 88.1% for FAM fluorescence and 69.9% for VIC fluorescence. These figures were used to determine the threshold for detection.</p></sec><sec id="Sec11"><title>Detection of clinical field strains</title><p id="Par29">The results demonstrated strong VIC fluorescence and weak FAM fluorescence for all 55 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>. field isolates tested (Table <xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>), indicating that none were the 104M strain.</p></sec></sec><sec id="Sec12"><title>Discussion</title><p id="Par30">The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain 104M is a stable antigenic structure with low virulence and high immunogenicity, hence it has been used in China since 1965 to vaccinate cattle and humans against brucellosis. Because using a live vaccine may lead to severe pathogenic injury associated with allergy, the 104M strain was only recommended for high-risk populations in China [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>], such as those at high risk due to their occupation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>]. The scratch vaccination method was used to introduce five billion bacteria, which achieved 90% protection for a 12&#160;month duration [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. In some areas in China, vaccination intervention in humans had an obvious effect; the reported cases of brucellosis in the Arong Banner declined sharply by 84.17% from 2005 to 2006 following vaccination, and the morbidity rate of brucellosis declined from 34,732 per 100,000 to 5454 per 100,000 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>].</p><p id="Par31">However, due to lack of serological differentiation, it is difficult to distinguish 104M by serological assay alone. The recent development of SNP-based real-time assays offers a new approach for overcoming this hurdle. SNP-based MGB and Cycleave assays have been used for rapid identification of four <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine strains (<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strains S19, A19 and RB51, and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev1) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">12</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>].</p><p id="Par32">In the present study, we developed a new MBG PCR assay that can successfully distinguish 104M strains from other bacterial strains, with a sensitivity of 220&#160;fg, equating to around 60 cells. Furthermore, our MGB PCR assay can detect non-104&#160;M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains in a single reaction with a sensitivity of 76&#160;fg, equating to around 30 cells. This assay allows accurate and reliable discrimination of 104M and non-104&#160;M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains from common species and biovars of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines, and other bacterial strains. Our assay therefore provides a simple, rapid, sensitive, and specific tool for use in the control of brucellosis.</p></sec><sec id="Sec13"><title>Conclusions</title><p id="Par33">A SNP-based MGB PCR assay was developed that could straightforwardly and unambiguously distinguish <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain 104M. Results of our study indicate the assay allows accurate and reliable discrimination of 104M and non-104&#160;M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains from common species and biovars of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines, and other bacterial strains. The minimum detection limit of the assay was 220&#160;fg for strain 104M and 76&#160;fg for the single non-104&#160;M <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strain tested. Compared to the classical isolation and identification approaches of bacteriology, this real-time PCR assay has substantial advantages in terms of simplicity and speed, and also reduces the potential for exposure to live <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. As real-time PCR instruments become more widely used in China, the approach will become widely applicable in routine diagnostics. The assay developed is therefore a simple, rapid, sensitive, and specific tool for brucellosis diagnosis and control.</p></sec></body><back><glossary><title>Abbreviations</title><def-list><def-item><term><italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic></term><def><p id="Par4">
