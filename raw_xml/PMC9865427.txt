      <abstract>
        <p>Brucellosis is a common zoonotic disease in Iran. Antimicrobial-resistant (AMR) <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates have been reported from different developing countries, posing an imminent health hazard. The objective of this study was to evaluate AMR and virulence-associated factors in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates recovered from humans and animals in different regions of Iran using classical phenotyping and next generation sequencing (NGS) technology. Our findings revealed that <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is the most common species in bovines, small ruminants and camels. <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> was isolated only from one human case. Probable intermediate or resistant phenotype patterns for rifampicin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, ampicillin-sulbactam and colistin were found. Whole genome sequencing (WGS) identified <italic toggle="yes">mprF</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepG</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepF</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepE</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">bepD</italic> in all isolates but failed to determine other classical AMR genes. Forty-three genes associated with five virulence factors were identified in the genomes of all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates, and no difference in the distribution of virulence-associated genes was found. Of them, 27 genes were associated with lipopolysaccharide (LPS), 12 genes were related to a type IV secretion system (<italic toggle="yes">virB1-B12</italic>), two were associated with the toll-interleukin-1 receptor (TIR) domain-containing proteins (<italic toggle="yes">btpA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">btpB</italic>), one gene encoded the Rab2 interacting conserved protein A (<italic toggle="yes">ricA</italic>) and one was associated with the production of cyclic &#x3B2;-1,2 glucans (<italic toggle="yes">cgs</italic>). This is the first investigation reporting the molecular-based AMR and virulence factors in brucellae isolated from different animal hosts and humans in Iran. Iranian <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates are still in vitro susceptible to the majority of antibiotics used for the treatment of human brucellosis. WGS failed to determine classical AMR genes and no difference was found in the distribution of virulence-associated genes in all isolates. Still, the absence of classical AMR genes in genomes of resistant strains is puzzling, and investigation of phenotypic resistance mechanisms at the proteomic and transcriptomic levels is needed. </p>
      </abstract>

    <sec sec-type="intro" id="sec1-pathogens-12-00082">
      <title>1. Introduction</title>
      <p>Brucellosis is a zoonotic bacterial infection causing significant economic losses in livestock populations and severely debilitating disease in humans worldwide [<xref rid="B1-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>,<xref rid="B2-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. Brucellosis can be transmitted from infected animals to humans mainly by consuming unpasteurized dairy and undercooked meat/meat products, or through close contact with an infected animal [<xref rid="B3-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. Four species of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (<italic toggle="yes">B</italic>.) are pathogenic for humans: <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> (main hosts: sheep and goat), <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (swidae), <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (bovidae), and <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> (canidae) [<xref rid="B4-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>]. However, the cross-species infection of hosts with different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. has also been documented [<xref rid="B5-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>]. The clinical symptoms of human brucellosis are non-specific and variable, ranging from severe acute or irregular febrile illness, headache, anorexia, fatigue, weight loss, generalized aching and arthralgia to chronic disease with severe complications [<xref rid="B6-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. The treatment of brucellosis demands prolonged and appropriate antimicrobial therapy. The antibiotic treatment of intracellular bacteria such as <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is hampered by handicapped intracellular diffusion of antibiotics as well as the development of resistance to antibiotics [<xref rid="B7-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="B9-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>]. Different mechanisms can induce antibiotic resistance, e.g., efflux pumps, enzymatic inactivation, horizontal gene transfer and modification of drug targets [<xref rid="B10-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>]. In several studies, antibiotic resistance to commonly used antimicrobial drugs, including trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole and rifampicin, has been reported [<xref rid="B8-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="B11-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>,<xref rid="B12-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>]. Therefore, susceptibility testing and detection of genetic resistance determinants have been strongly proposed as effective methods to monitor the efficiency of antibiotics for the advised treatment of human brucellosis [<xref rid="B13-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>,<xref rid="B14-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>,<xref rid="B15-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. However, antimicrobial susceptibility testing is not carried out routinely due to the traditional approach to therapy and technical challenges. It has to be noted that laboratory infections with aerosolized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. often occur; thus, a specialized BSL3 laboratory needs to be established [<xref rid="B16-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. In Iran, several studies on brucellosis showed that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> were the most isolated <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species in livestock and humans [<xref rid="B17-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>,<xref rid="B18-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>]. Most human brucellosis cases have been caused by <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, and a few by <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> [<xref rid="B19-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. Different studies have reported a relapse rate of 13&#x2013;18% in Iranian patients as one of the most critical complications, even following an appropriate treatment [<xref rid="B20-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>,<xref rid="B21-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>,<xref rid="B22-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. Several studies reported AMR in a few isolates [<xref rid="B12-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>,<xref rid="B23-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>] and an increasing trend in the number of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates with a resistant phenotype was noted [<xref rid="B24-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>,<xref rid="B25-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. However, it is unclear whether this is due to the development of intrinsic or acquired resistance against antibiotic compounds, or whether the results are a consequence of the intracellular nature of brucellae and thus the inability of antimicrobial compounds to penetrate the infected site, e.g., bone tissue or reticuloendothelial cells. Furthermore, identification and characterization of virulent genes are essential to implementing efficient disease control and prevention approaches, and evaluating the pathogenicity of brucellae [<xref rid="B26-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>]. However, very few investigations have been performed applying whole-genome sequencing (WGS) to evaluate the antimicrobial resistance and investigate virulence genes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> worldwide [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="B28-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. No data on the resistance genes of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates based on WGS exist from Iran. The current study aimed to examine the sensitivity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates recovered from humans and animals in Iran against most of the antibiotics used for brucellosis treatment in human patients to verify the adequacy of current treatment guidelines. Moreover, NGS technology was applied to investigate AMR and virulence-associated genes.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec2-pathogens-12-00082">
      <title>2. Materials and Methods</title>
      <sec id="sec2dot1-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>2.1. Ethics Committee</title>
        <p>This survey was part of the national surveillance plan for brucellosis, 2015&#x2013;2020, and all the activities follow the ethics requirement of the plan. This study was approved by the ethics committee of the Razi Vaccine and Serum Research Institute, Karaj, Iran (IR.RVSRI.REC.2015.001) in 2015, confirming that all experiments were performed following relevant guidelines and regulations. The patients gave informed consent for sampling and for participating in the survey/questionnaire.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2dot2-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>2.2. Brucella Isolates</title>
        <p>Forty <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates (23 of human origin and 17 of animal origin) from culture-positive human and animal cases of brucellosis collected at the Razi Vaccine and Serum Research Institute, Karaj, Iran from 2015 to 2020 were analyzed. For this study, we selected the exanimated isolates according to the various species, various biovars and different geographical locations that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. isolated in Iran. The isolates were recovered from different specimens. Human isolates (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 23) were recovered from blood samples (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 22) and one cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) sample. Animal isolates (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 17) were recovered from milk (six cows, one camel, and one sheep), four lymph nodes (three cows and one camel), and five aborted fetuses (four sheep and one goat). All camels were apparently healthy and had not received any <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine. Goats, cows, and sheep had an abortion history on the farm. Animal samples from aborted fetuses (abomasum content, liver, spleen, and kidneys) and milk were gathered in a sterile falcon tube and kept at &#x2212;20 &#xB0;C until further evaluation. Human cases were patients referred with clinical complaints of brucellosis to Razi laboratory with positive Rose Bengal, Wright, and 2ME tests. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2dot3-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>2.3. Brucella Isolation, Biotyping, and Molecular Confirmation</title>
        <p>For bacterial isolation, milk samples were centrifuged for 15 min at 3000&#xD7; <italic toggle="yes">g</italic>. Subsequently, the sediment and the creamy upper layer of samples were spread on cultivation media. Primary cultivation of all samples was carried out by streaking of 10 &#x3BC;L samples on a <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> selective agar [<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> agar (Himedia, Mumbai, India) supplemented with 5% inactivated horse serum, nystatin (50,000 IU), nalidixic acid (2.5 mg), vancomycin (10 mg), bacitracin (12,500 IU), polymyxin B (2500 IU), and cycloheximide (50 mg) (Oxoid, Basingstoke, UK)]. Plates were kept for 14 days at 37 &#xB0;C under 10% CO<sub>2</sub>. Suspected colonies, i.e., round, pinpoint, translucent, and pearly white colonies were selected for further analysis. A panel of classical biotyping tests was performed, i.e., H2S production, dependence on carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), lysis by specific phages, agglutination by acriflavine, growth characteristics on dye-agar media containing thionin and basic fuchsin and agglutination with specific monospecific <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antisera of A and M [<xref rid="B29-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>]. The interpretation of results was performed according to the WOAH (OIE) manual (<uri xlink:href="http://www.oie.int/en/animal-health-in-the-world/animal-diseases/Brucellosis/">http://www.oie.int/en/animal-health-in-the-world/animal-diseases/Brucellosis/</uri> (accessed on 15 October 2022)). Extraction of genomic DNA was carried out using the Exgene Cell SV kit (GeneAll, Seoul, Republic of Korea) according to the manufacturers protocol. Nanodrop (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) was used to evaluate DNA concentration. Furthermore, the DNA integrity was analyzed with 1% agarose gel. Samples were stored at &#x2212;20 &#xB0;C for later analysis. AMOS-PCR and Bruce-Ladder PCR were done as previously described [<xref rid="B30-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>,<xref rid="B31-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. 1% agarose gel electrophoresis was used to resolve the PCR products.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2dot4-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>2.4. Antibiotic Susceptibility Testing (AST)</title>
        <p>All identified isolates were subjected to AST using disk diffusion susceptibility tests and minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) tests for nine antibiotics. Disk diffusion susceptibility tests were performed for the antibiotics gentamicin (10 &#x3BC;g), streptomycin (10 &#x3BC;g), rifampin (5&#x3BC;g), doxycycline (30 &#x3BC;g), ceftriaxone (30 &#x3BC;g), ampicillin-sulbactam (10 + 10 &#x3BC;g) and trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (1.25/23.75 &#x3BC;g). The criteria for choosing these nine antibiotics was based on WHO guidelines on treatment of human brucellosis and the recommendation of used antibiotics in different studies [<xref rid="B25-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. The results of all antimicrobial tests were read after 48 h. The thresholds of antibiotic tests were set using the guidelines for <italic toggle="yes">Haemophilus</italic> spp. [<xref rid="B8-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="B32-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>,<xref rid="B33-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>]. The MICs were evaluated by E-test. Briefly, a solution (0.5 McFarland standard) was prepared and spread onto the Muller-Hinton agar plates enriched with 5% sheep&#x2019;s blood. The bacterial plates were kept under 10% CO<sub>2</sub> at 37 &#xB0;C, and AST was recorded after 48 h. MICs of bacterial isolates for rifampin (0.016&#x2013;256 &#x3BC;g/mL), gentamicin (0.064&#x2013;1024 &#x3BC;g/mL), doxycycline (0.016&#x2013;256 &#x3BC;g/mL), ceftriaxone (0.016&#x2013;256 &#x3BC;g/mL), streptomycin (0.064&#x2013;1024 &#x3BC;g/mL), trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (0.002&#x2013;32 &#x3BC;g/mL), imipenem (0.002&#x2013;32 &#x3BC;g/mL), ampicillin (0.016&#x2013;256 &#x3BC;g/mL), and colistin (0.016&#x2013;256 &#x3BC;g/mL) were evaluated according to the manufacturer&#x2019;s protocol (liofilchem/Italy) and the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) guidelines (2020) [<xref rid="B34-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. Reference strains, <italic toggle="yes">Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic> (ATCC 27853), <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> (ATCC 25922), <italic toggle="yes">Staphylococcus aureus</italic> (ATCC 29213), <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus pneumonia</italic> (ATCC 49619), and <italic toggle="yes">Enterococcus faecalis</italic> (ATCC 29212) were used to confirm the AST results. All ASTs were performed in duplicates for all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates [<xref rid="B35-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>,<xref rid="B36-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec2dot5-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>2.5. WGS and in Silico Detection of AMR and Virulence-Associated Genes</title>
        <p>After DNA extraction through the Exgene Cell SV kit (GeneAll, South Korea), the sequencing library was prepared. The samples were sequenced on an Illumina MiSeq (Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA) using paired-end sequencing. The analysis and assembly of raw sequencing data were carried out as previously described [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. Detection of the gene and protein sequences for AMR-associated genes was carried out through several databases, including the Resistance Gene Identifier (RGI) according to the ResFinder database [<xref rid="B37-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>], the Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database (CARD) [<xref rid="B38-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], and the NCBI AMR Finder Plus (<uri xlink:href="https://github.com/ncbi/amr/wiki/Running-AMRFinderPlus">https://github.com/ncbi/amr/wiki/Running-AMRFinderPlus</uri>, accessed on 15 October 2022) [<xref rid="B39-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. Potential virulence-associated genes were identified via the virulence factor database using the core dataset &#x201C;VFDB, <uri xlink:href="http://www.mgc.ac.cn/VFs/">http://www.mgc.ac.cn/VFs/</uri> (accessed on 22 June 2022)&#x201D; [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="B40-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="results" id="sec3-pathogens-12-00082">
      <title>3. Results</title>
      <sec id="sec3dot1-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>3.1. Brucella Identification and Characterization</title>
        <p>In the current study, 40 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates (3 <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and 37 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>) strains were characterized by cultivation-dependent and molecular methods (Bruce-Ladder PCR, AMOS PCR, and WGS). The results of Bruce-Ladder PCR and AMOS PCR were consistent with the results of WGS. <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> was detected in human blood (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 21), and human CSF (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 1), bovine milk (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 4), bovine lymph nodes (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 3), camel milk (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 1), camel lymph nodes (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 1), and ovine aborted fetus (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 4), ovine milk (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 1) and caprine aborted fetus (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 1) samples. These isolates showed identical results in the Bruce-Ladder PCR and AMOS PCR, and were also identified as <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> through WGS. One isolate from a human patient and two isolates from milk of seropositive cows were confirmed as <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> by PCR and WGS using the Kraken program (<xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot2-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>3.2. Phenotypic AMR Profiles of Brucella Strains</title>
        <p>The obtained MIC and disk diffusion values for all tested antibiotics are shown in <xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref> and <xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t003" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>. According to MIC measurements, all tested <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates were susceptible to doxycycline (MIC90 = 0.094 &#x3BC;g/mL), rifampicin (MIC90 = 0.5 &#x3BC;g/mL), gentamycin (MIC90 = 0.75 &#x3BC;g/mL), imipenem (MIC90 = 4 &#x3BC;g/mL), ceftriaxone (MIC90 = 0.75 &#x3BC;g/mL), streptomycin (MIC90 = 0.5 &#x3BC;g/mL) and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (MIC90 = 0.064 &#x3BC;g/mL). Non-susceptible isolates (resistant and intermediate) were observed only for colistin and ampicillin-sulbactam. All <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 40) showed resistance to colistin, while most <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 23) had intermediate resistance for ampicillin-sulbactam (MIC90 = 2 &#x3BC;g/mL) (<xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). In disk diffusion assays, 12 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates (32.5%) showed an inhibition zone of 19&#x2013;17 mm for discs with 5 &#x3BC;g rifampicin. They were classified as intermediate rifampin-resistant according to the slow-growing bacteria standards of CLSI. In contrast, all <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> isolates (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 3) and 17 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates exhibited an inhibition zone of &#x2265;20 mm (20 and 33 mm, respectively) and were considered susceptible. Resistance to rifampicin was seen in 8 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> (21.6%) isolates by disk diffusion values of &#x2264;16 &#x3BC;g/mL for incubation at 10% CO<sub>2</sub>. In contrast, only 13 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> (35%) isolates showed resistance to ampicillin-sulbactam according to disk diffusion values and breakpoints of between 13 and 19 mm (&#x2264;19 mm) (<xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t003" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot3-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>3.3. Whole-Genome Sequencing and Data Availability</title>
        <p>Sequencing of 40 Iranian <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains yielded an average of 1,645,251 reads per isolate (range 1,217,718&#x2013;2,835,032) with an average length of 275 bp. The mean coverage of genome sequences was 105.9, ranging from 99 to 202. Moreover, the Kraken2 software was applied to classify each read and assembled contig to evaluate the accurate species identification and detection of potential contaminations [<xref rid="B41-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. The first match for all isolates at the genus level was &#x201C;<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>&#x201D;, on average 99.7% of the reads (minimum 99.5%, maximum 99.8%). At the level of species detection, the first match for 37 isolates was &#x201C;<italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>&#x201D;, and three isolates were confirmed as &#x201C;<italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>&#x201D;. From these reads, genomes were assembled for all isolates with an average genome size of 3288,126 bp, minimum of 3243,150 bp and a mean N50 of 336.295 bp (range 251,030&#x2013;462,204 bp). The GC ratio was on average 57.24% (<xref rid="app1-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="app">Table S1</xref>). All study data are included in the article and supporting information (<xref rid="app1-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="app">Table S2</xref>). The data have also been submitted to the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA). The project accession number is PRJEB50179.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot4-pathogens-12-00082">
        <title>3.4. In Silico Identification of AMR and Virulence-Associated Genes</title>
        <p>The in silico analysis of AMR genes in 40 genomes of Iranian <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates using several databases yielded only the multiple peptide resistance factors (<italic toggle="yes">Brucella_suis</italic>_mprF) protein and efflux-related genes <italic toggle="yes">bepC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepD</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepE</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepF</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bepG</italic> by the CARD and AMRFinderPlus, respectively (<xref rid="app1-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="app">Table S2</xref>). Those genes were found in all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomes, either susceptible or resistant, except one strain lacked <italic toggle="yes">bepG</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">bepF</italic>. The virulence factor database (VFDB) revealed the presence of forty-three virulence and pathogenicity factors in all tested <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains (<xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t004" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>). The majority of these were associated with the LPS (lipopolysaccharide) operon (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 27), followed by genes encoding the type IV secretion system (<italic toggle="yes">virB1-B12</italic>). Furthermore, two genes (<italic toggle="yes">btpA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">btpB</italic>) code for TIR domain-containing proteins that inhibit dendritic cell maturation and proinflammatory cytokines&#x2019; production, increasing immune evasion. One gene, <italic toggle="yes">ricA</italic>, encoded the Rab2 interacting conserved protein A that specifically interacts with the GDP-bound form of Rab2 and may play an influential role in the maturation of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole, as it could slow down intracellular replication and thus increase evasion from the innate immune system. Finally, one gene (<italic toggle="yes">cgs</italic>) is associated with the production of cyclic &#x3B2;-1,2-glucans that increase intracellular survival (<xref rid="pathogens-12-00082-t004" ref-type="table">Table 4</xref>). It is significant to highlight that Iranian <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates showed no difference in the distribution of virulence-associated genes, even from different hosts.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="discussion" id="sec4-pathogens-12-00082">
      <title>4. Discussion</title>
      <p>Brucellosis remains a notorious zoonotic infection causing significant damage to the farming industry and public health. The disease is prevalent in Mediterranean and Middle Eastern countries including Iran [<xref rid="B42-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>,<xref rid="B43-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>,<xref rid="B44-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>]. According to the WHO, the brucellosis burden specifically on developing and low-income countries substantiates its classification as a serious zoonotic disease. In Iran, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> are the dominant <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species [<xref rid="B19-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. Our study confirmed the accurate diagnosis of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. isolated from animals and humans through classical and molecular typing. The current study used classical biotyping and bacterial culture as the gold standard combined with DNA-based tools (multiplex PCR and WGS) to identify <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains isolated from various hosts. The diagnosis of the different methods used in this study was consistent at the genus and species levels. Multiplex PCR tests and WGS appeared to be a reliable and rapid approach for the accurate classification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="B31-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>] with the potential to replace classical biotyping. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> identification by molecular methods has been reported as a fast and precise test in diagnostic laboratories. However, for improving in silico <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> identification, global reference databases are required to identify different species accurately. In this way, WGS provides a powerful method for accurate typing of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. because of the evaluation of the entire bacterial genome, thus improving discriminatory power [<xref rid="B45-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>,<xref rid="B46-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>] and replacing or overcoming the classical approach. Although WGS enables detailed strain typing, PCR should still be considered an essential method. It quickly provides information on genus and species identity and thus helps to take appropriate safety measures to decrease the risk of laboratory-acquired infections. These results improve our knowledge on the current species of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in ruminant and human reservoirs of Iran and confirm a significant burden of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>. In livestock, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is common in bovines, camels, and small ruminants [<xref rid="B44-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>]. The increasing number of cases of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> in cattle are also reported from other Middle East and African countries [<xref rid="B47-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>,<xref rid="B48-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>,<xref rid="B49-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>,<xref rid="B50-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>] and Iran [<xref rid="B51-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>]. The results of this study confirm that human brucellosis in Iran is mainly caused by <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, which is the predominant species causing human disease globally [<xref rid="B52-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>,<xref rid="B53-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>,<xref rid="B54-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>].</p>
      <p>In Iran, brucellosis treatment follows the World Health Organization (WHO) guidelines [<xref rid="B6-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. The WHO recommends a combination of streptomycin and doxycycline or gentamicin and doxycycline for patients younger than 60 years, and rifampicin and doxycycline for patients older than 60 years and children [<xref rid="B55-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>,<xref rid="B56-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>]. For pregnant women, trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole (cotrimoxazole) plus rifampin has been used [<xref rid="B57-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>]. For pregnant women &lt;36 weeks gestation, the treatment depends on a combination of trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole and rifampicin, and after 36 weeks gestation, rifampicin is administered as a monotherapy [<xref rid="B58-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>]. Our results show that all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates were susceptible to ceftriaxone, imipenem, doxycycline and gentamicin. The findings that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates were susceptible to tetracycline, doxycycline, streptomycin, ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, levofloxacin and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole are in accordance with reports from Egypt [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>], Turkey [<xref rid="B59-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>], Saudi Arabia [<xref rid="B60-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>], China [<xref rid="B61-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>] and Norway [<xref rid="B28-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. Intermediate or resistant phenotypes for rifampicin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, ampicillin-sulbactam and colistin were, however, found. Indeed, antimicrobial resistance is frequently observed in brucellae [<xref rid="B62-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>]. This increase in AMR may be responsible for the increasing number of relapses which was reported over the last few years in different studies [<xref rid="B20-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>,<xref rid="B35-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>,<xref rid="B61-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>]. However, AST is often not practised before the start of the treatment due to the notorious serological diagnosis and the lack of suitable samples. A considerable number of brucellae were found resistant to rifampin in Iran [<xref rid="B23-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>,<xref rid="B63-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>] and several countries in the Middle East, such as Turkey [<xref rid="B9-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>,<xref rid="B15-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>], Saudi Arabia [<xref rid="B64-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>], Qatar [<xref rid="B65-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>] and Egypt [<xref rid="B27-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. However, it is still an essential antibiotic in the treatment regimens of brucellosis. Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole was found to be an effective antimicrobial compound in the treatment of human brucellosis [<xref rid="B66-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>]. However, a decreased susceptibility was reported in Iran previously [<xref rid="B32-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>,<xref rid="B63-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>,<xref rid="B67-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>,<xref rid="B68-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>].</p>
      <p>The analysis of WGS data of 40 Iranian isolates revealed two AMR genes, multiple peptide resistance factors (<italic toggle="yes">mpr</italic>F) and the outer membrane efflux protein <italic toggle="yes">bep</italic> G, F, C, E and D in all strains. It is known that <italic toggle="yes">mpr</italic>F plays an essential role in resistance to cationic antibiotics such as gentamycin, moenomycin and vancomycin [<xref rid="B69-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>]. However, the results from the disk diffusion assays in this study showed no resistance to gentamycin. It is known that <italic toggle="yes">bep</italic> proteins increase resistance to some antibiotic compounds such as tetracycline, doxycycline, chloramphenicol and ciprofloxacin in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> [<xref rid="B70-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">70</xref>]. Hence, none of these <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates showed resistance to those antibiotics. The inability to detect classical resistance genes in the genome of brucellae is puzzling. This finding might be explained by the presence of other factors, like mutations in housekeeping genes or regulatory mechanisms [<xref rid="B71-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>], or till now unknown AMR genes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, which are not yet registered in public AMR databases. Furthermore, the intracellular lifestyle of brucellae that prohibits the penetration of different antimicrobials into the cells may play a role in the resistance development of these bacteria. There are only a few molecular-based investigations on the genetic determinants of antibiotic resistance in brucellae [<xref rid="B28-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>,<xref rid="B61-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>,<xref rid="B72-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>,<xref rid="B73-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>].</p>
      <p>In the present study, we have also analyzed the virulence genes of different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates in silico. Brucellae, as facultative intracellular bacteria, do not use &#x201C;classic&#x201D; virulence factors such as proteases, cytolysins, exotoxins, capsules, exoenzymes, virulence plasmids and pili or fimbriae [<xref rid="B74-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>]. In the current study, most identified pathogenicity-associated genes are involved in LPS production and type IV secretion systems. Until now, there exist few reports on the detection of virulence-associated genes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains isolated from humans and livestock from Iran [<xref rid="B75-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>,<xref rid="B76-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">76</xref>]. Examination of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains isolated from animal and human hosts in Iran revealed the presence of <italic toggle="yes">virB5</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">btpA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">btpB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">vceC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bpe275</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">bspB</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">virB2</italic> genes in all strains, while <italic toggle="yes">betB</italic> was found in 97% and <italic toggle="yes">prpA</italic> in 86% of the strains [<xref rid="B75-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. Another study also reported the presence of <italic toggle="yes">ure</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">wbkA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">omp19</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">manA, mviN</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">perA</italic> genes in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> using multiplex-PCR tests [<xref rid="B77-pathogens-12-00082" ref-type="bibr">77</xref>]. Although several PCR methods have been reported to identify virulence- and resistance-associated genes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates, these methods are limited by the species-specificity of used primers.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="conclusions" id="sec5-pathogens-12-00082">
      <title>5. Conclusions</title>
      <p>The implementation of high-throughput WGS allowed for more comprehensive detection of virulence- and resistance-associated genes. No clear difference in the distribution of the AMR and virulence genes among both resistant and sensitive <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains was found, even for those recovered from different hosts. Therefore, further investigations of antibiotic susceptibility have to be continued on <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates. Although the study of resistance and virulence mechanisms based on the genome was helpful and provided a comprehensive explanation in several microorganisms, it is of little value in the case of brucellae. Thus, resistance and virulence mechanisms at the proteomic and transcriptomic levels have to be considered in brucellae in future research.</p>
    </sec>
