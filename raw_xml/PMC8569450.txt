For successful surveillance and control programs, rapid identification and characterization of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates and reliable differentiation of vaccinated and naturally infected animals are essential prerequisites. Although MALDI-TOF MS is increasingly applied in clinical microbiology laboratories for the diagnosis of brucellosis, species or even strain differentiation by this method remains a challenge. To detect biomarkers, which enable to distinguish the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates, we initially searched for unique marker proteins by <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> comparison of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and 16M proteomes. We found 113 protein sequences of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M that revealed a homologous sequence in the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 annotation and 17 of these sequences yielded potential biomarker pairs. MALDI-TOF MS spectra of 18 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine and 183 Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates were subsequently analyzed to validate the identified marker candidates. This approach detected two genus-wide unique biomarkers with properties most similar to the ribosomal proteins L24 and S12. These two proteins clearly discriminated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 from the closely related <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M and the Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates. In addition, we verified their discriminatory power using a set of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains from various origins and of different MLVA types. Based on our results, we propose MALDI-TOF MS profiling as a rapid, cost-effective alternative to the traditional, time-consuming approach to differentiate certain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates on strain level.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic></kwd><kwd>MALDI-TOF sec-type="intro" id="S1"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is a global zoonotic disease affecting domestic and wild animals as well as humans (<xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">Pappas et al., 2006</xref>). Three out of twelve currently known <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species are responsible for most of the reported human brucellosis cases, namely <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, primarily transmitted from sheep and goats, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> from cattle, and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> from swine (<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">Hull and Schumaker, 2018</xref>). Ovine and caprine brucellosis are endemic throughout the Middle East as well as in many countries of Africa, Asia, and Latin America (<xref rid="B55" ref-type="bibr">Rossetti et al., 2017</xref>). In these regions, efforts are undertaken to control brucellosis by the vaccination of sheep and goats.</p><p>The most common vaccination policy for small ruminant livestock against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> infections is the application of the live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain to female animals aged between 2 and 6 months (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>). This procedure has proven to be protective and to reduce abortions in treated animals, but may be contraindicated in females that are vaccinated during their last trimester of pregnancy (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>). Although attenuated, the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain is still capable to infect humans and cause disease, either through the consumption of contaminated milk from vaccinated animals or by accidental exposure during the vaccination procedures (<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">Arapovic et al., 2020</xref>). Hence, laboratory methods that can easily distinguish <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field strains from the vaccine strain are relevant for (i) effective brucellosis control programs, (ii) for epidemiological surveillance, and (iii) for outbreak clarification.</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1 possesses several characteristics, including streptomycin resistance and a distinct dye sensitivity pattern, which enables its discrimination from field strains using bacteriological tests (<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">Elberg and Meyer, 1958</xref>). However, atypical <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 field and Rev.1 vaccine isolates have been described, which may lead to misinterpretations (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">Banai et al., 1990</xref>; <xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">Lucero et al., 2006</xref>). Moreover, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 induces like other <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains the production of antibodies directed against its smooth lipopolysaccharide (LPS). This property interferes with serological testing for brucellosis due to cross-reactivity between the smooth LPS of the vaccine strain and other smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species or the LPS of widespread Gram-negative pathogens such as <italic toggle="yes">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella</italic> spp. (<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">Corbell, 1975</xref>). Hence, more robust molecular markers are needed, and a molecular screening method has been developed based on the <italic toggle="yes">Pst</italic>I site polymorphism in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella omp</italic>2 gene of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain, which allows its differentiation from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">Bardenstein et al., 2002</xref>). In addition, <italic toggle="yes">rpsL</italic>-directed PCR-RFLP and multiplex PCR assays have been established to discriminate <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 wild-type strains from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 (<xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">Cloeckaert et al., 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">Garcia-Yoldi et al., 2006</xref>).</p><p>Similar to PCR-based microbial diagnostics, matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization&#8211;time of flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS) profiling has emerged as a rapid and cost-effective laboratory method to identify bacteria in recent years (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">Croxatto et al., 2012</xref>). Rigorous preprocessing and generous peak binning during spectra creation for MALDI-TOF MS platforms, like Bruker Biotyper, Vitek MS or Andromas lead to robust identification performances for many bacteria at genus and even at species level (<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">Clark et al., 2013</xref>). However, strain identification relies on discriminating strain-specific differences in the proteome under the constraints of MALDI-TOF MS. These subtle differences are caused by genomic alterations, i.e., indels, frameshifts, non-synonymous single nucleotide polymorphisms or pseudogenization that manifest as a change in abundance or result in an altered amino acid sequence and post-translational modifications of a protein. While pseudogenization or changes in steady-state levels of proteins may be detected by modified peak intensities, changes in the molecular composition of a protein may lead to a unique mass shift of its corresponding peak, oftentimes referred to as a biomarker. Species- and even strain-specific biomarkers have been reported for the MALDI-TOF MS-based identification and differentiation of various bacterial pathogens, like <italic toggle="yes">Haemophilus</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Helicobacter pylori</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Campylobacter</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic> or methicillin-resistant <italic toggle="yes">Staphylococcus aureus</italic> (<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">Sandrin et al., 2013</xref>). MALDI-TOF MS has also been applied for the identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. However, classical <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species are highly homologous (<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">Hoyer and Mccullough, 1968</xref>), which is why commercially available reference libraries have shown shortcomings in the reliable classification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> beyond genus level (<xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">Ferreira et al., 2010</xref>; <xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">Cunningham and Patel, 2013</xref>; <xref rid="B66" ref-type="bibr">Tracz et al., 2016</xref>) or the differentiation of closely related <italic toggle="yes">Ochrobactrum</italic> species (<xref rid="B54" ref-type="bibr">Poonawala et al., 2018</xref>), recently reclassified as <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp., for example <italic toggle="yes">B. anthropi</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. intermedium</italic> (<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">Hordt et al., 2020</xref>). However, the generation of in-house reference libraries of MALDI-TOF MS spectra may allow for correct identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species and some of their respective biovars (<xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">Lista et al., 2011</xref>; <xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">Karger et al., 2013</xref>; <xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">Mesureur et al., 2018</xref>; <xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">Da Silva et al., 2020</xref>).</p><p>Our study aimed to establish a novel MALDI-TOF MS-based diagnostic approach that facilitates the rapid differentiation of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates. We took advantage of a comparative <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> proteomics analysis and a comprehensive in-house library of MALDI-TOF MS spectra to identify specific protein biomarkers for the resolution of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates on sub-species level.</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="S2"><title>Materials and Methods</title><sec id="S2.SS1"><title><italic toggle="yes">In silico</italic> Proteome Comparison</title><p>Complete genomes of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 strain 16M, GCF_000007125.1_ASM712v1 (<xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">Delvecchio et al., 2002</xref>) and GCF_000740415.1_ASM74041v1 (<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">Minogue et al., 2014</xref>), as well as <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 strain Rev.1, GCF_002953595.1_ASM295359v1 (<xref rid="B56" ref-type="bibr">Salmon-Divon et al., 2018</xref>), were retrieved from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI<sup><xref rid="footnote1" ref-type="fn">1</xref></sup>). All <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains were obtained from the collection of the Kimron Veterinary Institute (KVI) in Bet Dagan, Israel, and were cultured for 48&#8211;72 h on tryptic soy agar (TSA) plates at 37&#176;C under 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. All bacteria were characterized using standard methods: growth on TSA plates with penicillin G and streptomycin, dye sensitivity (thionine, fuchsine), H<sub>2</sub>S production, urease activity, and agglutination with mono-specific anti-M and anti-A serum. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1 isolates were verified by <italic toggle="yes">omp</italic>2 PCR and <italic toggle="yes">Pst</italic>I digestion of the amplicon (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">Bardenstein et al., 2002</xref>). We prepared biological triplicates of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine isolates but only produced single preparations of the Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates. Bacteria were grown with shaking in 10 ml of a tryptic soy broth (TSB pH 7.3) at 37&#176;C for 24 h (OD<sub>600</sub>&#8764;0.3&#8211;0.4), centrifuged at 7,000 &#215; <italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, washed with PBS and resuspended in 300 &#956;l PBS and 900 &#956;l 100% ethanol. The bacterial solutions were left at room temperature for 48 h before 100 &#956;l were plated on TSA plates for sterility testing. The work on live agents was performed at the KVI biosafety level 3 facility. Inactivated <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> samples were split into aliquots and stored at &#8722;20&#176;C for on-site use or shipping on dry ice to the German Federal Institute for Risk Assessment (BfR) in Berlin, Germany.</p></sec><sec id="S2.SS3"><title>MALDI-TOF</title><sec id="S2.SS3.SSS1"><title>Spectra Acquisition</title><p>At both institutes, samples were independently prepared for mass spectrometry by ethanol-formic acid extraction according to manufacturer&#8217;s instructions before being spotted on a 96-spot steel plate target and covered with alpha-cyano-4-hydroxy-cinnamic acid (HCCA) matrix solution (Bruker Daltonik GmbH, Bremen, Germany). Mass spectra were measured using a microflex LT MALDI-TOF MS system (Bruker Daltonik) operated by the Biotyper automation software flexControl (v3.4.135.0, Bruker Daltonik). To increase data robustness, twelve technical replicate spectra were acquired from four different target spots using the recommended instrument settings for bacterial identification (linear positive ion detection mode, 60 Hz laser frequency, 20 kV acceleration voltage, 18.1&#8211;18.2 kV IS2 voltage). Spectra were initially analyzed at BfR using the Bruker Biotyper software (v3.1) with MSP library version MBT_7311 (7311 entries), the Security-Relevant (SR) Database (104 entries) and a customized in-house database to confirm identification as <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>.</p></sec><sec id="S2.SS3.SSS2"><title>Data Analysis</title><p>Raw spectra data from the Bruker microflex MS instruments were imported into the statistical computing environment &#8220;R&#8221; (v3.6.3) and analyzed with the R packages MALDIquantForeign (v0.12) and MALDIquant (v1.19.3) (<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">Gibb and Strimmer, 2012</xref>). Raw spectra were preprocessed using default functions and parameters, i.e., square root transformation, smoothing, baseline removal and normalization. Spectra were combined by the function <italic toggle="yes">averageMassSpectra</italic>, first by averaging technical replicate spectra into a &#8220;sample spectrum&#8221; and second by averaging sample spectra of the same group into a &#8220;group spectrum.&#8221; The groups were defined by bacteriological classification of a sample as &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221; or the field isolate outgroup &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; as well as by the institute where protein extracts were subjected to MALDI-TOF MS, namely &#8220;BfR&#8221; or &#8220;KVI.&#8221; Two intermediary spectra alignment steps were applied in the preprocessing: technical replicate spectra were aligned against each other whereas sample spectra were aligned against 37 reference peaks derived from all available sample spectra (method = &#8220;strict,&#8221; minFrequency = 0.9, tolerance = 0.002) of this study (<xref rid="TS4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 4</xref>). The reference peaks with a relative frequency of &#8805; 90% were distributed in a mass interval between 3,100 and 11,500 Da. All graphical spectra representations (gel, spectra and peaks) were drawn with customized ggplot2 functions from the R package Tidyverse v1.3.0 (<xref rid="B70" ref-type="bibr">Wickham et al., 2019</xref>). A comparison of all sample spectra is shown in <xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 1</xref>.</p></sec><sec id="S2.SS3.SSS3"><title>Biomarker Validation</title><p>Sample spectra from the groups &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221; and &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; were manually screened for peaks corresponding to exact mass value pairs of short-listed proteins (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) from the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and 16M proteomes, respectively. Potential mass shifts in the mass value pairs caused by protein-specific PTMs were also considered. Biomarker mass intervals and decision boundaries for Rev.1 classification were determined by visual inspection of the peak distribution in the vicinity of accurate masses.</p></sec></sec><sec id="S2.SS4"><title><italic toggle="yes">In silico</italic> Genotyping and Pan Proteome Sequence Analysis</title><p>The NCBI database was queried<sup><xref rid="footnote3" ref-type="fn">3</xref></sup> for all RefSeq annotated assemblies of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (accession date 2020-11-05). Since MALDI-TOF MS for bacterial identification focusses on a narrow mass-over-charge (<italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic>) ratio ranging from 2,000 to 20,000, we first assessed a sequence-based <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> approach to find biomarkers. This method is feasible with respect to the limited number of expected divergent proteins useful for the discrimination of genetically closely related strains, such as <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and the reference strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M, both members of the same MLVA (Multiple-Locus Variable-number of tandem repeats Analysis) group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Americas&#8221; (<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>). To this end, we compared the <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> translated open-reading frames (ORFs) of two different <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M complete genomes (<xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">Delvecchio et al., 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">Minogue et al., 2014</xref>) against a complete <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 genome (<xref rid="B56" ref-type="bibr">Salmon-Divon et al., 2018</xref>). To ease the matching of respective ORFs, all genomes were subjected to re-annotation by the RASTtk pipeline at PATRIC (<xref rid="TS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 1</xref>) and to a set analysis of their protein coding sequences. The resulting <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> core proteome shared by all three <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> genome assemblies consisted of 2,962 translated coding sequences with identical amino acid sequences (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). Hence, about 90% of the protein sequences derived from the three <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> genome sequences were not considered for our analysis. We further excluded 240 (191 + 49) proteins that differed between the two <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M annotations or were unique to either one and additional 196 (159 + 37) proteins identical between <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and only one of the two <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M annotations (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). Applying these filters, 113 out of 127 sequences identical in both <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> proteomes matched with homologous sequences in the group of 141 annotated proteins specific for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1. These 113 proteins harbor minor amino acid exchanges as a result of SNPs in their respective genes. Only few proteins matched the second filter criteria of bearing a mass within the working range of MALDI-TOF MS and a small but discernable mass difference.</p><p>The resulting short-list of 17 potential biomarker pairs (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) is comprised of three hypothetical proteins, proteins with housekeeping enzymatic properties and five proteins (L24, L6, S12, S8, S7) of the 50S and 30S ribosomal subunits. Ribosomal proteins, highly abundant in bacterial cells, are well-known biomarkers for the identification and classification of bacteria by MALDI-TOF MS (<xref rid="B62" ref-type="bibr">Suarez et al., 2013</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="S3.SS2"><title>Ribosomal Proteins L24 and S12 Are Potential Discriminatory Marker Proteins to Identify the Vaccine Strain <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1</title><p>As a proof-of-concept for our bioinformatics approach, we screened an initial set of MALDI-TOF MS spectra from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 for the presence of the 17 group-specific <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> peak pairs and potentially post-translationally modified (PTM) variants thereof. For PTMs, we considered N-terminal methionine excision (NME, average &#916;mass = &#8722;131.2 Da), methylation (&#916;mass = 14.0 Da), acetylation (&#916;mass = 42.0 Da), and modifications noted in the UniProt database annotation of the respective proteins. Out of the 17 predicted biomarkers, a distinct peak pair in the sample spectra resembled the ribosomal protein L24 with averaged accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,178 in the vaccine strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221; and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,208 in the reference strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> 16M&#8221; (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). The exact mass difference of &#8722;30.1 Da between the ion [M<sub><italic toggle="yes">Thr28</italic></sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis Rev.1</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,178.8 and the ion [M<sub>Met28</sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis 16M</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,207.9 corresponded well to the observed peak shift. A further noticeable peak pair comprised the ribosomal protein S12 with averaged accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,785 in the field strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> 16M&#8221; and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,798 in the vaccine strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1.&#8221; The exact mass difference of +16.0 Da between the ion [M<sub>Pro91</sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis 16M</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,871.2 and the ion [M<sub>Leu91</sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis Rev.1</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,887.2 matched well to the observed peak shift (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p><p>In summary, these results supported the feasibility of our combined <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> and proteomic-based approach to identify new biomarkers for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> diagnostics that enable the differentiation between a <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> reference strain and a closely related vaccine strain.</p></sec><sec id="S3.SS3"><title>Differentiation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Field Isolates From the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1 Vaccine Strain</title><p>Subsequently, we assessed the robustness of the discrimination between <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> wild-type isolates and the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain based on the ribosomal proteins L24 and S12. For this purpose, we analyzed <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates collected from humans, goats, sheep and cattle in Israel, where ovine and caprine brucellosis is known to be endemic and a routine vaccination program for livestock with <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 has been implemented during the last decades (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>). Moreover, the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains endemic in Israel mostly belong to the MLVA group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> East-Mediterranean,&#8221; whereas <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 is part of the MLVA group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Americas&#8221; (<xref rid="B68" ref-type="bibr">Vergnaud et al., 2018</xref>).</p><p>The MALDI-TOF MS spectra of 18 <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strains and 183 Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates were compared, and we further evaluated the impact of spectra acquisition by performing MALDI-TOF MS measurements at the two institutes BfR and KVI (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 1</xref>). As seen before in the comparison between <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>), we detected a distinct peak pair in the sample spectra for the ribosomal protein L24 with averaged accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,178 in the vaccine strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221; and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 11,208 in the field strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; (<xref rid="F3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>). In the mass range for double charged ions of the ribosomal protein L24, we detected accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 5,590 (group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221;) and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 5,605 (group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis&#8221;</italic>) (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 2</xref>) corresponding to the ions [M<sub><italic toggle="yes">Thr28</italic></sub>+2H]<sup>2+</sup> and [M<sub>Met28</sub>+2H]<sup>2+</sup>, respectively. In a subset of &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; field strains samples, this peak pair is interspersed with a second signal at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 5,595, which is not present in the single charged state (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 2B</xref>, &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; groups).</p><p>Furthermore, the peak pair for the ribosomal protein S12 was found in the sample spectra with averaged accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,784 in the field strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,798 in the vaccine strain group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221; (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 3</xref>). The exact mass difference of +16.0 Da between the ion [M<sub>Pro91</sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,871.2 and the ion [M<sub>Leu91</sub>+H]<sup>+</sup><italic toggle="yes"><sub><italic toggle="yes">melitensis Rev.1</italic></sub></italic> with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,887.2 corresponded well to the observed peak shift. However, for peaks that were not within the mass window of 3,000 to 11,500 Da (defined by reference peaks with at least 90% frequency in all measurements, see <xref rid="TS4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 4</xref>), the alignment by a locally weighted scatterplot smoothing function (LOWESS) was less accurate, leading to a higher mass scattering. Ribosomal protein S12 ion variants show respective peaks in the &#8220;BfR&#8221; subgroups with the strongest intensities for ion [M-Met+&#946;MeS+H]<sup>+</sup>, the beta-methylthiolated ribosomal protein S12 ion lacking the N-terminal methionine (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 3</xref>). In the mass range for double charged ions of probable ribosomal protein S12, we detected accurate masses at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 6,894 (group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221;) and <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 6,902 (group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Rev.1&#8221;) (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 4</xref>) corresponding to the ions [M<sub>Pro91</sub>-Met+&#946;MeS+2H]<sup>2+</sup> and [M<sub>Leu91</sub>-Met+&#946;MeS+2H]<sup>2+</sup>, respectively.</p><p>Based on our peak observations, we determined mass windows with a decision boundary to distinguish between ribosomal protein L24 and S12 variants, and tested whether these thresholds facilitate the differentiation between the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates and the Rev.1 vaccine strain (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). The alignment procedure against a reference spectrum decreased the standard deviation considerably (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>), e.g., for &#8220;BfR&#8221; peaks within the ribosomal protein L24 (z = 1) decision mass window (11,197.5:11,235) from mean<sub>non&#8211;aligned</sub> = 11,206.9 with SD<sub>non&#8211;aligned</sub> = 2.9 for technical replicate spectra to mean<sub>aligned</sub> = 11,208.1 with SD<sub>aligned</sub> = 0.4 for sample spectra (<xref rid="F3" ref-type="fig">Figures 3D&#8211;F</xref>). Peaks from the KVI measurement scattered more widely (technical replicate spectra mean<sub>non&#8211;aligned</sub> = 11,213.3 with SD<sub>non&#8211;aligned</sub> = 4.7 versus sample spectra mean<sub>aligned</sub> = 11,211.4 with SD<sub>aligned</sub> = 4.5), due to a recalibration of the KVI instrument between measurements.</p><p>Recently, a study has been published that also aimed to detect biomarkers enabling the discrimination of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates and the Rev.1 vaccine strain by MALDI-TOF MS (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al., 2020</xref>). For this purpose, 73 clinical and veterinary <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> isolates from Greece were analyzed. Initially, a cluster analysis on a subset of 17 field strains that represented the three <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovars against three commercial <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strains had been performed. Two discriminating peaks were described in this study: Peak <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 3,528 could be detected in all tested Greek <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates but not in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1, whereas peak <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 7,328 was unique for the vaccine strain (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al., 2020</xref>). Strikingly, these biomarker peaks were not identified in our study, thus we compared the data published by <xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al. (2020)</xref> with our data. The first described biomarker with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 3,528, supposed to be only present in the spectra of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Greek field isolates, could be found in the spectra of all Israeli field isolates, but also in the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M reference strain and the Rev.1 strain (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4A</xref>). The second biomarker with <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 7,328 neither occurred in the Greek <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al., 2020</xref>) nor in the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates from Israel (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4A</xref>), but in the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain of both studies. However, this peak does not reflect a true biomarker for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 against the closely related <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M from the same MLVA group &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic> Americas,&#8221; which also harbors this causative protein (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4A</xref>).</p><p>In contrast, our here reported ribosomal protein biomarkers L24 and S12 distinguish the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain not only from the Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates but also from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4B</xref>). <xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al. (2020)</xref> probably did not detect these ribosomal biomarkers since their forward analysis tolerance parameter, which was not stated, may have excluded the detection of close peaks with the Mass-Up default settings or due to low mass intensities in <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> ranges above 10 kDa. In order to prove the universal character of our biomarkers and to exclude clonal and regional effects, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains from 17 different MLVA groups (<xref rid="TS6" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 6</xref>) were subjected to MALDI-TOF MS analysis and the spectra were searched for the presence of the protein biomarkers L24 and S12. While the biomarker peaks described by <xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al. (2020)</xref> for the differentiation of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 could also be found in the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field strains of this diversity set (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4C</xref>), the analysis confirmed the discriminatory power of the L24 and S12 biomarkers, which distinguish the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain from naturally occurring <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains in general (<xref rid="F4" ref-type="fig">Figure 4D</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="S3.SS4"><title>Discriminatory Power of the L24 and S12 Signals for the Strain-Level Identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> Rev.1 by MALDI-TOF MS</title><p>We further evaluated the uniqueness of the ribosomal proteins L24 and S12 as marker proteins for the unambiguous identification of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 by comparing their amino acid sequences with the L24 and S12 protein entries in the NCBI protein database originating from 802 sequenced <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates.</p><p>Unexpectedly, a blastP-based comparison did not identify a protein sequence identical to the L24-M28T variant of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 in any other isolate of the NCBI genome dataset (<xref rid="F5" ref-type="fig">Figure 5</xref> and <xref rid="TS7" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 7</xref>). Instead, the amino acid sequences of the ribosomal L24 proteins from most isolates of the analyzed <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species were identical to the L24 protein sequence of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. However, few isolates of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> clade C, of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> East-Mediterranean clade and of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> bv. 2 harbor altered L24 protein sequences with the amino acid exchanges K90R, N67S and G55D, respectively (<xref rid="F6" ref-type="fig">Figure 6</xref>). The L24 protein variant of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> East-Mediterranean isolate with the N67S amino acid exchange exhibits a &#8722;27 Da mass difference to the wild-type L24 protein. Hence, its spectrum peak cannot be distinguished by MALDI-TOF MS from the &#8722;30 Da spectrum peak of L24 variant from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 (<xref rid="TS7" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 7</xref>). Interestingly, all <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> isolates encode for a L24 protein with the unique modification V81I resulting in a +14 Da mass difference compared to the common L24 proteins (<xref rid="F5" ref-type="fig">Figure 5</xref> and <xref rid="TS7" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 7</xref>).</p><p>For ribosomal protein S12, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 exclusively carried the P91L mutation with its +16 Da mass difference compared to the most frequent S12 variant in the NCBI dataset (<xref rid="F5" ref-type="fig">Figure 5</xref> and <xref rid="TS8" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 8</xref>). However, the ribosomal S12 proteins of all analyzed <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic> clade B isolates harbor the amino acid exchange V55I that leads to a mass difference of +14 Da. Consequently, this S12 protein variant is indistinguishable from the corresponding S12 protein peak of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 in MALDI-TOF MS analysis. Moreover, few strains of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> clade C encode for a ribosomal S12 protein with a K43R exchange and a limited number of isolates of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> West-Mediterranean clade express S12 protein variants with the amino acid changes K43R or K88R (<xref rid="F6" ref-type="fig">Figure 6</xref> and <xref rid="TS8" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 8</xref>). Mutations at corresponding codon positions have been identified in streptomycin-resistant strains of other bacterial species (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 5</xref>) like <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella</italic> Typhimurium, <italic toggle="yes">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Helicobacter pylori</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Klebsiella pneumoniae</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Erwinia carotovora</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">Thermus thermophiles</italic> (<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">Finken et al., 1993</xref>; <xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">Bjorkman et al., 1999</xref>; <xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">Gregory et al., 2001</xref>; <xref rid="B65" ref-type="bibr">Torii et al., 2003</xref>; <xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">Chumpolkulwong et al., 2004</xref>; <xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">Barnard et al., 2010</xref>; <xref rid="B67" ref-type="bibr">Tsai et al., 2014</xref>).</p><p>Taken together, our bioinformatics analysis suggests that the ribosomal protein variants of L24 and S12 identified here as biomarkers for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 have extremely high discriminatory properties allowing the direct differentiation of this vaccine strain from other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates even without performing a preceding classification as <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>.</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="discussion" id="S4"><title>Discussion</title><p>The worldwide emerging zoonotic disease brucellosis affects wildlife and livestock, especially cattle, goats and sheep (<xref rid="B58" ref-type="bibr">Seleem et al., 2010</xref>; <xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">Godfroid, 2017</xref>). In countries with endemic brucellosis, vaccination programs have been undertaken to combat <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections in farm animals (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">Banai, 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">Olsen and Stoffregen, 2005</xref>). However, these measures require efficient diagnostic tools to distinguish vaccine strains from naturally occurring wild-type <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains in the livestock herds.</p><p>Here, we propose MALDI-TOF MS profiling as a cost-effective alternative to the currently applied methods of classical microbiological testing (<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">Elberg and Meyer, 1958</xref>) and molecular PCR-techniques (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">Bardenstein et al., 2002</xref>; <xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">Lopez-Goni et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">Alvarez et al., 2017</xref>; <xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al., 2018</xref>) for the differentiation of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates. This approach will be of benefit for reference laboratories and large healthcare facilities that have already implemented MALDI-TOF MS diagnostics for the identification of bacterial pathogens. Moreover, due to the robustness of mass spectrometry, this rapid identification method is increasingly used in tropical countries, where it complements smaller point-of-care laboratories (<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">Fall et al., 2015</xref>; <xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">Chabriere et al., 2018</xref>).</p><p>Whole genome comparison has previously identified various mutations as genetic markers that distinguish <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 from reference strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M (<xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">Issa and Ashhab, 2016</xref>). However, besides a non-synonymous mutation affecting the ribosomal protein S12, most of the 32 listed genome-specific markers were not identified by our <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> analysis or did not translate into changes of MALDI-TOF MS peaks. This might be the consequence of (i) our filter criteria that restricted potential marker proteins on masses between 2,000 and 20,000 Da, (ii) mutations that do not translate into amino acid exchanges captured by MALDI-TOF MS, (iii) insufficient <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic> expression of the marker genes, or (iv) MALDI-TOF MS signal suppression effects leading to undetectable amounts of protein.</p><p>Our comparative <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> analysis of the translated open reading frames from the genomes of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M and Rev.1 predicted 17 potential MALDI-TOF MS biomarkers for the differentiation of both strains. Subsequent whole-cell MALDI-TOF MS spectra comparison of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M and Rev.1 confirmed that two marker peak pairs, resembling the ribosomal proteins L24 and S12 in their single and double charged states, have the discriminatory power to distinguish between these closely related <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains. Our observation is in agreement with previous studies, demonstrating that the sequence variabilities and abundance of ribosomal proteins in microbial cells allow their usage as robust biomarkers for the identification of pathogenic and non-pathogenic bacteria at species and strain level by whole-cell MALDI-TOF MS (<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">Sandrin et al., 2013</xref>).</p><p>The ribosomal protein L24 is encoded by the <italic toggle="yes">rplX</italic> gene, for which spontaneous missense mutations have been described before (<xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">Nishi et al., 1987</xref>; <xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">Sharp et al., 1992</xref>). Accordingly, our comparative bioinformatics analysis of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> pan proteome not only identified the altered L24 protein in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 but also five additional variants. These mutated L24 proteins exhibit mass differences from about &#8722;30 to +69 Da compared to the wild-type L24 protein of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and may represent additional biomarkers, especially for the identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella ovis</italic> and for the improved differentiation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and closely related former <italic toggle="yes">Ochrobactrum</italic> species. Likewise, MALDI-TOF MS studies of various other bacteria have identified peak differences of L24 protein variants. Consequently, the ribosomal protein L24 has served as species-specific biomarker for <italic toggle="yes">Flavobacterium psychrophilum</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Bacillus</italic> spp., <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus thermophilus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Listeria monocytogenes</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Francisella tularensis</italic> as well (<xref rid="B64" ref-type="bibr">Teramoto et al., 2007</xref>; <xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">Hotta et al., 2011</xref>; <xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">Durighello et al., 2014</xref>; <xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">Ojima-Kato et al., 2016</xref>; <xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">Fernandez-Alvarez et al., 2018</xref>).</p><p>The streptomycin resistance is a distinct characteristic of vaccine strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 that was introduced during vaccine derivation by <xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">Elberg and Faunce (1957)</xref>. It is mediated through a spontaneous <italic toggle="yes">rpsL</italic> mutation that leads to an altered ribosomal protein S12 with the amino acid exchange Pro to Leu at codon position 91 (P91L) (<xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">Cloeckaert et al., 2002</xref>). We showed here that this altered protein sequence resulted in a distinct shift of the MALDI-TOF MS peak <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 13,784, which can be used as a biomarker for the identification of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1.</p><p>Two post-translational modifications of ribosomal protein S12 have been observed in other bacterial species (<xref rid="B43" ref-type="bibr">Kowalak and Walsh, 1996</xref>; <xref rid="B63" ref-type="bibr">Suh et al., 2005</xref>) or were inferred from sequence similarity: NME (average mass change: &#8722;131.2 Da) and beta-methylthiolation of Asp89 (average mass change: +46.1 Da, see UniProt annotation of RS12_BRUME). We also found this mutation in the <italic toggle="yes">rpsL</italic> genes of streptomycin resistant <italic toggle="yes">T. thermophilus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">K. pneumoniae</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> isolates, further illustrating that certain antibiotic resistances in bacteria correlate with ribosomal protein changes that can be detected by MALDI-TOF MS (<xref rid="B71" ref-type="bibr">Wilcox et al., 2001</xref>; <xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">Carr et al., 2005</xref>).</p><p>The usage of the ribosomal proteins S12 and L24 signals as MALDI-TOF MS biomarkers enabled the unequivocal discrimination between <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 and the analyzed Israeli <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates, as well as the closely related type strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. Comparable results were obtained from MALDI-TOF MS measurements performed at two different institutes, emphasizing the high quality, accuracy and reproducibility of the established method.</p><p>Moreover, our bioinformatics analysis of hitherto published <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteomes did not identify any other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolate that encodes for S12 and L24 proteins with identical amino acid sequences, further illustrating their unique character. Our here identified biomarkers for the identification of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain had not been identified by a recent MALDI-TOF MS based study pursuing the same aim (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al., 2020</xref>). The two alternative biomarkers described in the work by <xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">Christoforidou et al. (2020)</xref> for the discrimination between <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates and the Rev.1 vaccine strain may only be of benefit for local applications in Greece. According to our analysis, the first proposed biomarker (<italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 3,528) was not only present in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M, but also in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1, whereas the second putative biomarker (<italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 7,328) was not exclusive for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 as indicated, but was also found in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. Two analytical restraints in their study could have contributed to these different findings. First, the mass trimming to a window of interest has omitted the detection of the single charged variants of probable ribosomal proteins L24 and S12. Second, mathematical binning of continuous sample peak <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> values for subsequent cluster analysis utilized a tolerance threshold that limited its discriminatory power. The double charged ribosomal protein L24 variants in our study were detectable at a mass resolution of 400 whereas double charged ribosomal protein S12 variants required a higher analytical mass resolution of 860. Christoforidou and colleagues used the MALDIquant default tolerance of 0.002 for peak binning, i.e., a mass resolution limit of 250, and therefore, were not able to detect any of the double charged variants of ribosomal protein L24 or S12. Furthermore, if spectra within a group display diversity, as seen in the ribosomal protein L24 subset of &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">melitensis</italic>&#8221; field isolates at <italic toggle="yes">m/z</italic> 5,595 (<xref rid="DS1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 2</xref>), biomarker significance in statistical tests will diminish. Applying these analytical considerations, our MALDI-TOF MS approach identified two genus-wide unique biomarkers that unambiguously discriminated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates and the closely related type strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M without any misidentification.</p><p>Several manufacturers around the world produce the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain from different seed stocks. Hence, a comprehensive characterization of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 is required for quality controls in vaccine production facilities, since Rev.1 strains of different production sites may differ significantly from the original Elberg strain (<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">Bosseray, 1991</xref>). To avoid alterations in the attenuated virulence and to assure strain stability during the vaccine production process, the gene expression profile of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain should be checked on a regular basis. In this context, our MALDI-TOF MS based analysis may serve as an additional standardization tool in the commercial production of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine.</p></sec><sec sec-type="conclusion" id="S5"><title>Conclusion</title><p>The here described ribosomal marker proteins for distinguishing the vaccine strain <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field strains by MALDI-TOF MS will improve the differential diagnosis necessary for brucellosis control efforts within vaccination programs and subsequently the successful eradication of the zoonoses from small ruminants. Natural streptomycin resistance in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is rare, but <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species exhibit variable streptomycin susceptibilities and the development of spontaneous resistance has been seen <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic> (<xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">Hall and Manion, 1970</xref>; <xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">Hall, 1990</xref>). This observation is supported by our identification of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates that encode for ribosomal protein S12 variants with amino acid sequences of streptomycin resistant alleles. Our approach does not detect streptomycin resistance mediated by mutations of the 16S rRNA (<xref rid="B61" ref-type="bibr">Springer et al., 2001</xref>). However, the improved MALDI-TOF MS based screening for the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain and other streptomycin resistant <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> field isolates with ribosomal protein S12 variants may reduce the risk of an inadequate first line therapy in human brucellosis, since streptomycin is commonly used in the antibiotic regimen applied for patients infected with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (<xref rid="B60" ref-type="bibr">Skalsky et al., 2008</xref>; <xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">Meng et al., 2018</xref>).</p></sec><sec sec-type="data-availability" id="S6"><title>Data Availability Statement</title><p>Most of the datasets presented in this study can be found in online repositories.
