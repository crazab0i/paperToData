    <sec id="sec1-microorganisms-12-00866">
      <title>1. Brucellosis and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Reproductive Pathogenesis</title>
      <p>Brucellosis is one of the most common and widespread bacterial zoonoses and is caused by bacteria belonging to the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. This Gram-negative bacterial genus belongs to the Class &#x3B1;-2 of the Phylum <italic toggle="yes">Proteobacteria</italic> and includes a variety of species that exhibit a wide diversity of preferential hosts and zoonotic potentials. Classical brucellae, the most epidemiologically relevant <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, infect domestic ruminants (<italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, sheep and goats; <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, cattle; and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>, sheep), swine (<italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovars 1-3), and dogs (<italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>) [<xref rid="B1-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. Nonetheless, the already mentioned host preference of the different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. does not mean host restriction: <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> can also infect cattle and other ruminants; <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> infects buffaloes, camels, deer, dogs, horses, goats, and sheep, whereas <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> infects cattle, horses, rabbits, and dogs [<xref rid="B2-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. These species are the main causative agents of human brucellosis (except for the non- or low-zoonotic <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovar 2, and <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>); <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is the most important species worldwide.</p>
      <p>The pathology in the natural host features a wide range of reproductive symptoms [<xref rid="B2-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="B3-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. Infection may result in infertility, reproductive failure with abortions/stillbirths, or the birth of weak offspring, all related to the development of placentitis [<xref rid="B4-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>,<xref rid="B5-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>] and reduced milk production due to infection and inflammation of the mammary glands [<xref rid="B5-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="B7-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. </p>
      <p>In this context, human infection is acquired through contact with infected animals and the consumption of unpasteurised milk products. Therefore, brucellosis is an occupational risk for veterinarians, slaughterhouse workers, butchers, and livestock farmers and a public health threat in populations consuming raw milk or unpasteurised dairy products [<xref rid="B9-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>]. Although under-reporting is common and official records are of questionable value in resource-limited countries, the available evidence suggests that brucellosis is emerging or re-emerging in many regions as a result of both the increased demand for animal products, leading to intensification of livestock production, and poor understanding of the disease and control measures [<xref rid="B10-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>,<xref rid="B11-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>,<xref rid="B12-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>,<xref rid="B13-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>,<xref rid="B14-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>,<xref rid="B15-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>,<xref rid="B16-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B17-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. A recent worldwide study has provided an empirical estimate of between 1.6 and 2.1 million new cases of human brucellosis annually, most of them in Africa and Asia, but also occurring areas within the Americas and Europe [<xref rid="B18-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>]. The economic impact of brucellosis depends on the prevalence, species affected, management, sociopolitical decisions, trade restrictions, and the extent of human disease and is considered to be very important in low-income countries around the world [<xref rid="B10-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>,<xref rid="B15-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>,<xref rid="B19-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>,<xref rid="B20-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. Human-to-human transmission is negligible, and control of the disease relies on animal vaccination. Currently, the only effective vaccines are live attenuated strains, which have several drawbacks such as an abortifacient effect when administered to pregnant animals, similar to that of wild-type strains [<xref rid="B21-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>], imposing a great barrier to the application of mass vaccination campaigns. For this reason, there is a clear need for research into the development of reproductively safe <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines. </p>
      <p>Transmission in animals usually occurs through the head mucosae by contact with contaminated placentae, genital secretions, or aborted foetuses or ingestion of contaminated milk [<xref rid="B5-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="B7-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B22-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B23-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>]. Thus, the principal port of entry for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are the mucosal barriers, mainly the nasal and oral mucosae [<xref rid="B24-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>] (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). Here, brucellae are internalised by cells of the mononuclear phagocyte system, where they avoid intracellular killing and multiply in high numbers in a compartment derived from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) [<xref rid="B25-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>,<xref rid="B26-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>,<xref rid="B27-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>,<xref rid="B28-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>]. From the lymph nodes, brucellae disseminate systematically to other lymphoid tissues, such as the spleen [<xref rid="B29-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>], and to reproductive organs such as the gravid uterus and the placenta [<xref rid="B30-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>] (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>).</p>
      <p>As mentioned above, brucellae have a predilection for the placenta in ruminants, leading to placentitis, which impairs nutrient delivery to the foetus and induces foetal stress, a phenomenon that has been recognised for decades [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. This, along with foetal infection, has been hypothesised as the cause of abortions and perinatal deaths [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>,<xref rid="B32-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>,<xref rid="B33-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>,<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. In pregnant ruminants, around 85% of brucellae are found at high numbers in placental structures, especially at the cotyledons, with approximately 10<sup>13</sup> CFU/g in bovine or 10<sup>8</sup> CFU/g in caprine species [<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>,<xref rid="B35-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>]. Such high numbers of brucellae in reproductive and aborted tissues ensure transmission via aerosols, ingestion, or sexual intercourse [<xref rid="B30-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>]. Therefore, this abundant multiplication in the reproductive tract of the natural hosts is crucial in the biology of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, a very efficient strategy for bacteria to spread to new hosts [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>].</p>
      <p>Placentitis is considered a consequence of the inflammation and necrosis caused by the death of infected placental trophoblasts, in which <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are found intracellularly [<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>,<xref rid="B36-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>,<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. This trophoblast necrosis in infected placentae is different from the behaviour of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in the mononuclear phagocyte system, whose cells appear apparently unaware of the presence of the parasite [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>,<xref rid="B42-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>,<xref rid="B43-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>,<xref rid="B44-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>,<xref rid="B45-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>]. In fact, classical <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains inhibit the death of these phagocytic cells [<xref rid="B43-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>,<xref rid="B46-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>].</p>
      <p>In a <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> intravenously (IV)-infected pregnant goat model, the access to the placenta was shown to occur at the level of the erythrophagocytic trophoblasts [<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>], with subsequent spreading to the cells of the chorioallantoic membrane, preferentially trophoblasts. Similarly, Payne reported that brucellae were carried to the uterus by the blood, initially infecting the endometrium and subsequently spreading to the placenta and foetus [<xref rid="B47-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> were detected in phagosomes and the rough ER (RER) of chorioallantoic trophoblasts [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B22-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>] and replicated within the RER causing hypertrophy of this organelle [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B22-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. Infection of the chorionic trophoblast may happen through cell-to-cell lateral transfer from the infected erythrophagocytic trophoblasts [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. Then, progression of infection may occur by new cell-to-cell transfer between trophoblasts or by trophoblastic necrosis and rupture, releasing brucellae to the extracellular spaces and further endocytosis by adjacent trophoblasts [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B48-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>]. Macrophages and neutrophils are recruited to these ulcerated and necrotised areas [<xref rid="B5-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>]. There, macrophages become infected and brucellae replicate at high numbers within them and, together with neutrophils, induce an inflammatory reaction in the placenta [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B22-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. Although the role of host immunity on the development of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced placentitis has not been explored in the natural host, as has been performed for other reproductive pathogens such as <italic toggle="yes">Chlamydia abortus</italic> [<xref rid="B49-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>], a recent publication has reviewed the immune responses that are possibly involved in trophoblast necrosis and the derived gestational complications of brucellosis [<xref rid="B50-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>]. Trophoblast necrosis leads to chorioallantoic ulceration and the release of brucellae into the uterine lumen [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>,<xref rid="B47-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>]. The proximity of placental capillaries to these ulcerated areas allows brucellae to access foetal circulation, spreading through the placentome structure and into the foetal tissues [<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. For all these reasons, trophoblasts have been suggested as the primary cell type involved in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis leading to abortion [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B34-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. In the end, the resulting lesions, placentitis, and vasculitis, in both maternal and foetal tissues, may weaken the unions between foetal trophoblasts and the maternal epithelium, contributing to the death of the foetus and consequent abortion [<xref rid="B47-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>,<xref rid="B51-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>,<xref rid="B52-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>,<xref rid="B53-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>].</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec2-microorganisms-12-00866">
      <title>2. Placentation in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Natural Hosts and Mouse</title>
      <p>The study of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella&#x2019;s</italic> systemic and reproductive pathogenesis in the natural host is greatly restricted due to their size, containment infrastructure, maintenance costs, and ethical aspects. Although mice are not natural hosts for relevant <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp., this laboratory model is widely utilised in brucellosis research [<xref rid="B54-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>]. Replication profiles in the mouse spleen are valuable for determining variations in virulence among <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains and live vaccines. The mouse model is also instrumental in assessing the potential protective efficacy of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine candidates. To a lesser extent, the pregnant mouse model (the focus of the present review) has been employed to study the abortifacient effect of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, mainly <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, with only one study on <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic>, one on <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis,</italic> and none on <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>.</p>
      <p>To understand both the strengths and limitations of this murine model, it is crucial to comprehend the placental structures of the diversity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> natural hosts and mice. The placenta is an organ that forms by the apposition of maternal and foetal (trophoblast; from the Greek <italic toggle="yes">trephein</italic>, to feed, and <italic toggle="yes">blastos</italic>, germinator) tissues whose function is to maximise the exchange of molecules (e.g., nutrients, debris, signalling factors, etc.) and to minimise immunological rejection by the maternal immune system while the foetus develops. To accomplish these key functions, a wide diversity of placentation types has arisen through mammalian evolution [<xref rid="B55-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>], which can be classified micro- and macroscopically according to their histological structures and shapes (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p>
      <p>Suidae exhibit an epitheliochorial placentation characterised by the apposition of the foetal trophoblast (-<italic toggle="yes">chorial</italic>) to the maternal uterine epithelium (<italic toggle="yes">epithelio</italic>-), with a mean gestation period of 115 days [<xref rid="B56-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>,<xref rid="B57-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>]. In this placentation, there is minimal invasion of the uterine lining and no cell/tissue layers are removed (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). This way, the uterine epithelium comes into contact with the trophoblast and both layers interdigitate through microvilli, greatly increasing the exchange surface, which is further increased by the development of villi over the entire surface of the placenta in a diffuse pattern (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p>
      <p>Ruminants (e.g., sheep, goats, and cows) exhibit a cotyledonary (with 20&#x2013;150 placentome units) and synepitheliochorial placentation in which there is a fusion (<italic toggle="yes">syn</italic>-) of maternal epithelium (-<italic toggle="yes">epithelio</italic>-) and foetal trophoblasts (-<italic toggle="yes">chorial</italic>) (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>) [<xref rid="B56-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>,<xref rid="B57-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>], with mean gestation periods of 147, 150, and 283 days for sheep, goats, and cows, respectively. Microscopically, the uterine epithelium (the caruncle) and the trophoblasts (the cotyledon) establish close contacts by the formation of interdigitations through microvilli that greatly increase the exchange surface. A population of mononuclear trophoblastic cells fuse together into binucleated cells that migrate and further fuse with the uterine caruncular epithelium forming foetomaternal trinucleated cells or syncytial plaques of bigger sizes (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>), helping to transfer effector molecules towards the maternal side [<xref rid="B58-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>]. Macroscopically, this foetomaternal structure folds (further increasing exchange surface) into a delimitated structure, the placentome, the total number of which depends on the species.</p>
      <p>Dogs (as do other carnivores) exhibit an endotheliochorial placentation in which trophoblastic cells (-<italic toggle="yes">chorial</italic>) form a syncytium that completely erodes the uterine epithelium, directly apposing to an originated amorphous interstitial layer around the persisting maternal endothelium (<italic toggle="yes">endothelio</italic>-) (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>) [<xref rid="B56-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>,<xref rid="B57-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>]. This layer is not continuous in all its surface and some trophoblast&#x2013;endometrium apposition can still be found in peripheral regions of the placenta. In this placentation type, the foetomaternal structure also develops villi and folding in order to increase the exchange surface and acquires a zonary distribution around the conceptus (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p>
      <p>Rodents exhibit a discoidal labyrinthine haemotrichorial in which maternal blood (<italic toggle="yes">haemo</italic>-) bathes a three-cell-layered (-<italic toggle="yes">tri</italic>-) foetal trophoblast (-<italic toggle="yes">chorial</italic>) placentation (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>) [<xref rid="B56-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>,<xref rid="B57-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>], with a mean gestational duration of 19&#x2013;21 days. Microscopically, the uterine epithelium is completely eroded upon implantation and maternal blood directly bathes the foetal trophoblast formed by a syncytiotrophoblast population that establishes two cell layers surrounding the foetal capillaries (syncytiotrophoblast I and II) and a mononuclear trophoblast population (cytotrophoblasts) that lines the maternal blood sinuses (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>) [<xref rid="B59-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>]. Macroscopically, this foetomaternal exchange structure folds into an amorphous network [<xref rid="B60-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>], called the labyrinth, that is structurally supported by the spongiotrophoblast and limits with the decidua (i.e., the maternal side of the placenta) through a lining of trophoblastic giant cells (TGC) known as parietal TGCs, a region known as the junctional zone. The decidua is another specific trait of haemochorial placentation; it is formed by the transformation of the endometrium prior to trophoblast invasion and during placenta formation and helps to anchor the placenta in order to channel maternal blood vessels into the placenta and to protect the developing embryo from external aggressions.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec3-microorganisms-12-00866">
      <title>3. The Pregnant Mouse Model in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Research</title>
      <p>The first steps into the evaluation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced reproductive pathogenesis in pregnant mice were carried out in the early 80s by Nicole Bosseray using <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref> and <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). These studies focused on characterising the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 strain 544 infection profile in pregnant CD-1 mice as a tool to study <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>,<xref rid="B62-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>], the processes involved in vertical transmission [<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>], and the role of vaccination in the prevention of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced abortion [<xref rid="B64-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>,<xref rid="B65-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>]. Bosseray&#x2019;s studies were followed by Tobias et al. (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>), who characterised the pregnancy outcome of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 strain 2308-infected mice from the histopathological point of view and then compared it with the outcome of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>-based S19 and RB51 vaccine strains [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>].</p>
      <p>Studies in the pregnant mouse model were not resumed until 2005 (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>), when Kim et al. characterised the abortifacient effect of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 at different inoculation times during pregnancy and compared it with the effect of the S19 vaccine [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>], in a similar way to that of Bosseray and Tobias et al. in the previous decades (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). However, it appears that these studies were not considered by Kim et al. (&#x201C;(sic)&#x201D;: &#x201C;no studies on the induction of abortion by Brucella have been conducted in the pregnant mouse&#x201D;) [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>,<xref rid="B62-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>,<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>,<xref rid="B64-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>,<xref rid="B65-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>]. This way, Kim et al.&#x2019;s work was the main reference in the following decades for subsequent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> studies in the pregnant mouse model that focused on different aspects of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis during pregnancy [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>,<xref rid="B67-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>,<xref rid="B68-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>,<xref rid="B69-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>,<xref rid="B70-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">70</xref>,<xref rid="B71-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>,<xref rid="B72-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>]. Later (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>), Wang et al. also characterised the dose-dependent abortifacient effect of a <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 3 field strain (NI strain) (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>].</p>
      <p>Up until 2022 (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>), few studies had assayed the abortifacient potential of live brucellosis vaccines, and the ones that did so focused only on <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>-based vaccines such as the S19 and/or RB51 (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B64-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>]. In 2022, Poveda-Urkixo et al. characterised the abortion induction of several <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains, the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 strain 16M and the Rev1 live attenuated vaccine strain, and the <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovar 2 strain CITA198 (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Then, these authors used their model to evaluate the reproductive safety of two vaccine candidates on the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M- and Rev1-backgrounds [<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. Coincidentally to these studies, the pregnant mouse was also used to study the abortifacient effect of the <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> strain ATCC<sup>&#xAE;</sup> 25840 as well as a <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>-based vaccine candidate (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) [<xref rid="B76-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">76</xref>].</p>
      <p>This review of the literature indicates that the variety of publications that have investigated different aspects of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis in the pregnant mouse have employed a plethora of experimental designs: mouse strains, doses, routes of inoculation, infection at different times post-conception (p.c.), abortion/stillbirth readouts, etc., (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). Thus, herein we compare the different experimental designs employed for the pregnant mouse model of reproductive brucellosis, focusing on <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced adverse gestational events and the use of the model for vaccine safety evaluation.</p>
      <sec id="sec3dot1-microorganisms-12-00866">
        <title>3.1. Methodological Aspects of the Model</title>
        <sec id="sec3dot1dot1-microorganisms-12-00866">
          <title>3.1.1. Mouse Pregnancy Outcome and Abortion Readout</title>
          <p>In ruminants, abortions at mid&#x2013;late gestation are the main clinical manifestation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in the field [<xref rid="B77-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">77</xref>]. Abortions at this stage have been linked to hormonal and nutritional factors [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>,<xref rid="B78-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">78</xref>], but the underlying mechanisms are poorly understood, and it is possible that foetal deaths during the first trimester of pregnancy may go unnoticed [<xref rid="B79-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">79</xref>]. This way, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced abortions are mainly noticed at stillbirth [<xref rid="B31-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>], as happens with other abortifacient pathogens such as <italic toggle="yes">C. abortus</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">Toxoplasma gondii</italic> [<xref rid="B80-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">80</xref>,<xref rid="B81-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">81</xref>]. In the pregnant mouse model, the effects of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection may result in two different outcomes depending on the time of pregnancy at which abortion happens. On the one hand, early adverse pregnancy events may result in abortion and resorption of the still small conceptuses, resulting in a decreased litter size. On the other hand, late events may result in foetal death with no resorption, as the foetuses are mostly developed and of a considerable size, resulting in a decreased pup viability (with observable aborted foetuses/stillbirths).</p>
          <p>In the literature, publications have typically expressed adverse pregnancy events in the pregnant mouse model only as a decrease in pup viability. Possible explanations for this may be that it was the only parameter investigated [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B67-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>,<xref rid="B71-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>,<xref rid="B72-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>,<xref rid="B82-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">82</xref>,<xref rid="B83-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">83</xref>], that authors did not find a remarkable effect on litter sizes [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>], or that reductions in litter sizes were unnoticed by the authors despite obtaining them in their results [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. In the latter study, Poveda-Urkixo et al. noted that 2/5 pregnant mice inoculated with 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M at 4 days p.c. (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) experienced complete abortions as foetal resorptions [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. However, these two mice were not included in the determination of the litter size, which was therefore averaged over the remaining three mice, resulting in a litter size of 15.0 &#xB1; 3.6. This way, the calculation result is the highest litter size from all the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains studied, even higher than the PBS control (13.3 &#xB1; 0.6 pups/litter). If the calculation had included the two complete-abortion events, the litter size result would be 9.0 pups/litter, a value more coherent with the expected abortifacient effect of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. The phenomenon of litter size reduction was indeed noticed in a study by Hashino et al., who showed that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 congenitally infected mice (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) exhibited a reduction in litter sizes from 14.92 to 7.65 pups on average [<xref rid="B68-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>].</p>

        <sec id="sec3dot1dot2-microorganisms-12-00866">
          <title>3.1.2. Inoculation Day</title>
          <p>Regarding the inoculation time during pregnancy, although there is a current consensus on noting the morning after the overnight mating of the mice (at which the vaginal plug is noticed) as day 0.5 [<xref rid="B84-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">84</xref>,<xref rid="B85-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">85</xref>], there is some diversity in the enumeration of mouse gestation in the reviewed literature (beginning with Bosseray&#x2019;s work in the 1980s), as well as in the mating protocols followed. Hence, in order to avoid misunderstandings in the comments and comparisons made in this review, the specific enumerations and mating conditions used in the literature are also summarised (when provided) in <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>.</p>
          <p>Bosseray initially screened several time points (3-, 7-, 11-, and 15-days p.c.) with a <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 challenge in CD-1 pregnant mice (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). After assessing the splenic and placental bacterial loads on day 18 p.c., she identified a temporal window from 7 to 11 days p.c. when the pregnant mouse placentae were more prone to colonisation by brucellae [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>]. However, these different degrees of placental colonisation did not correlate with any abortion event, with only 4 perinatal deaths out of 330 pups born from infected dams [<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>]. Inoculation within Bosseray&#x2019;s 7&#x2013;11-days-p.c. temporal window was also executed in later studies by Tobias et al. [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. In these papers, the raw abortion numbers resulting from intraperitoneal (IP) infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 of 9-day-pregnant mice (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) are difficult to interpret; however, the authors describe cases of autolysed or inviable foetuses. The ambiguous abortifacient effect exerted by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in Tobias et al.&#x2019;s studies may be related to the use of Balb/c mice that, as previously mentioned, yield lower litter sizes that could hinder the manifestation of adverse pregnancy outcomes. These mouse strains differ widely in their reproductive performance, with CD-1 mice yielding an average of 13.5 pups/litter and Balb/c mice yielding 5.8 pups/litter (JanvierLabs). The smaller litter sizes of Balb/c mice may limit the possibility of observing significant effects on the reproductive outcome of pregnant mice as models of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis, given the narrower range of adverse pregnancy events to be tracked. Nonetheless, Bosseray&#x2019;s and Tobias et al.&#x2019;s results are surprising, not only because they do not correlate with the abortion outcome of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> in bovines but also because a later publication by Kim et al. showed that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 infection on day-9 p.c. (i.e., within Bosseray&#x2019;s temporal window) did induce abortions in an ICR (equivalent to CD-1) pregnant mouse model [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. In this study, several infection days (3, 4, 6, 9, and 14 days p.c.) were screened in ICR pregnant mice IP infected with 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). While Bosseray found no abortion events regardless of the time of infection [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>], Kim et al. found that a dose of 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse induced the abortion of a considerable proportion of foetuses (28.1&#x2013;37.3%) when infecting at very early (i.e., day 3) or intermediate pregnancy time-points (i.e., days 6 and 9), an almost complete foetal abortion (98.4%) at the early 4 days p.c. and no abortion when infecting in late pregnancy (i.e., 14 days p.c.) [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. In this regard, it is intriguing how such a high level of abortion could occur at 4 days p.c. but not at 3 or 6 days p.c. Furthermore, such an early adverse effect on mice pregnancy should result in early-stage abortions that, due to their reduced dimension, would be more prone to be reabsorbed resulting in a decreased litter size; however, this does not seem to be the case, as authors recorded 15.75 pups/litter in the group inoculated at 4 days p.c. that showed a 98% abortion rate.</p>
          <p>Subsequent studies that, as previously mentioned, took Kim et al.&#x2019;s findings [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>] as reference, supported the results that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> induces adverse gestational events in pregnant mice infected on day-4 p.c. First, the same research group that conducted the Kim et al., 2005 study (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>) showed that IP infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 at 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse on day-4 p.c. (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) reduced the number of live foetuses from 12&#x2013;13 to 2&#x2013;3 on average<xref rid="fn001-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">1</xref> [<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>,<xref rid="B67-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>]. Subsequently, another research group (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>) showed that infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 on day-5 p.c. (via IP at 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) reduced foetal viability from &gt;90% in the control mice to 0&#x2013;7.04%<xref rid="fn002-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">2</xref> [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B71-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>,<xref rid="B72-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>], findings also supported by a publication by Ren et al., who showed a reduction in pup viability to 2.91% when infecting mice on day-5 p.c. [<xref rid="B83-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">83</xref>]. Similar to <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains have also been described as reducing foetal viability, completely in the case of the 16M virulent strain, to a lesser extent (i.e., 35.4 &#xB1; 33.0%; nonetheless, this group exhibited a remarkable deviation of viability rates among individuals) for the Rev1 vaccine when inoculation (6&#x2013;7 &#xD7; 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse via IP; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) is performed on day 4.5 &#xB1; 1 [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>], or to an even lesser extent for a <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> non-reference field strain at the same inoculation day [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>].</p>
          <p>To summarise, despite the 7&#x2013;11 days p.c. window identified by Bosseray when the placentae were more prone to colonisation by <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 (but not for the induction of abortions), earlier inoculation days (4&#x2013;5 days p.c.) have been adopted in subsequent publications based on the results obtained by Kim et al. [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>].</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec3dot1dot3-microorganisms-12-00866">
          <title>3.1.3. Route of Administration</title>
          <p>In most studies, the inoculation was performed through the IP route, except for Bosseray, who compared the suitability of the IP, intravenous (IV), and subcutaneous (SC) routes [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>,<xref rid="B62-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>]. Bosseray evaluated the impact of the inoculation route on infection outcome and showed that the dose needed to infect 50% of the placentae increased from the IP (<italic toggle="yes">ca.</italic> 10<sup>2</sup> CFU/mouse), to the IV (<italic toggle="yes">ca</italic>. 10<sup>3</sup> CFU/mouse), to the SC routes (<italic toggle="yes">ca</italic>. 10<sup>4.5</sup> CFU/mouse), the latter needing the highest dose [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>]. Interestingly, the progression of infection in placenta and spleen resolved differently. The spleen was rapidly and heavily colonised in the early minutes after IV inoculation, as a reflection of the blood clearing function of this organ. However, the increase in bacterial burden eventually slowed down from the early hours p.i. onwards, indicating an active killing by phagocytes and the multiplication of the surviving bacteria. In contrast, although few brucellae localised in the placenta early in the infection, the organ rapidly became the main focus of bacterial replication, evidencing the remarkable tropism of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> for this organ [<xref rid="B62-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>]. This aspect of an asymmetrical placenta/spleen infection was also highlighted in later publications after IP inoculation of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>,<xref rid="B67-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>], <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> NI [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>], <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 strain 16M and Rev1 [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>,<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>], and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> [<xref rid="B76-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">76</xref>]. Interestingly, the higher degree of placental colonisation did not result in an equally high foetal infection [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>], evidencing the invasion-limiting barrier function of the placenta [<xref rid="B86-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">86</xref>].</p>
          <p>Although the IP route has been the most widely used in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> mouse pregnant models, probably due to the ease of administration, further comparative research is needed to assess whether IP grants more reliable results in terms of reproductive effects than the SC, IV, or other routes of infection.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="sec3dot1dot4-microorganisms-12-00866">
          <title>3.1.4. Dose</title>
          <p>The dose-dependent effect on placental infection was also evaluated in Bosseray&#x2019;s pioneering studies [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>]. It was demonstrated that increasing doses of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 resulted in increased levels of placental colonisation, whether through the IP, IV, or SC route of administration, ranging from 0.41 to 6.00 log (10<sup>1</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse), 0.81 to 5.02 log (10<sup>1</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse), and 0.99 to 5.92 log (10<sup>2</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>8</sup> CFU/mouse,<xref rid="fn003-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">3</xref> respectively (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). However, as previously mentioned for the inoculation day, these increasing levels of bacterial placental colonisation did not correlate with any abortion event [<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>].</p>
          <p>The dose dependence of bacterial burden and abortion was later studied for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> in 2014 by Wang et al., who screened several infection doses (10<sup>3</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse) of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> biovar 3 strain NI (a field strain of bovine origin) in ICR pregnant mice infected at day-4 p.c. [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>] (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). Here, a dose-dependent effect was found not only on bacterial burden in spleens, placentae, and foetuses, as found by Bosseray [<xref rid="B61-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>], but also on the number of pregnant mice that experienced abortions/stillbirths. While a dose of 10<sup>3</sup> CFU/mouse was insufficient to induce any abortion, doses ranges of 10<sup>4</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse caused stillbirth cases in 50% of pregnant mice and the dose of 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse caused stillbirths in the entirety of the mice population [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>]. The highest dose of 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse was defined as the minimum dose needed to produce consistent severe placental lesions in pregnant mice, an aspect also described previously by Tobias et al. for <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. Yet, as already noted by Wang et al., comparing their abortion results with those of Kim et al.&#x2019;s [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>] reveals noticeable differences. Contrary to what would be expected for these two <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp., while in Wang et al. the inoculation of 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> NI resulted in 50% of the dams experiencing adverse reproductive events but only 0.04% of foetal death [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>], the inoculation of the same dose of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 at the same stage of pregnancy by Kim et al. induced abortion events in 100% of the pregnant mice, with 98.4% of foetuses aborted [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. In the face of this dichotomy, another publication from Kim et al.&#x2019;s group using the same experimental design evidenced that inoculation of mice at 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 (via IP on day 4 p.c.) resulted in 80.86% aborted foetuses<xref rid="fn004-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">4</xref> [<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>]. Further studies using an increased dose of 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse (via IP on day 5 p.c.) also showed foetal abortion rates between 90 and 100%<xref rid="fn005-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">5</xref> for <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B71-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>,<xref rid="B72-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>,<xref rid="B83-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">83</xref>] or <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>].</p>
          <p>Overall, doses of 10<sup>4</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse (depending on the infective species and/or strain) are needed to obtain significant rates of abortions in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-infected mice. It has also been shown that vaccine strains require higher doses to cause comparable reproductive problems.</p>
        </sec>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot2-microorganisms-12-00866">
        <title>3.2. Reproductive Pathogenesis in the Pregnant Mouse</title>
        <p>Tobias et al. carried out a comprehensive histopathological characterisation by infecting pregnant Balb/c mice with 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 via the IP route at day-9 p.c. and studying the pathology over the course of mouse pregnancy (12, 14, 16, and 18 days p.c.; i.e., 3, 5, 7, and 9 days p.i.) (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. Macroscopically, placentae from infected mice showed a reduced weight from day 14 until the end of gestation. According to the authors, placentae associated with viable foetuses were dark red and firm (as in non-infected mice) but with a yellow rim of material at the periphery, while placentae supporting unviable (edematous or autolysed) foetuses were pale and shrunken. Microscopically, it was noted that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 (as well as the S19 and RB51 vaccine strains), localised within TGCs at the placental decidua already at 3 days p.i. (i.e., 12 days p.c.) [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>,<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], as also happens within trophoblasts in the natural host [<xref rid="B6-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B22-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. This TGC targeting by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in the mouse placenta has been widely described in later publications [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>,<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Within TGCs, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> localised and replicated surrounding the nucleus [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>] inside membrane-bound cisternae with ribosomes that were continuous with normal RER and the perinuclear envelope [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. The regions where brucellae were localised within TGCs presented no inflammatory infiltrate yet (i.e., at 3 days p.i.), although bacteria could be also found within neutrophils; within them, brucellae were likely present inside phagosomes rather than ER cisternae [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. At 5 days p.i. (14 days p.c.), brucellae extensively covered the decidua basalis (i.e., proximal) and reached the spongiotrophoblast layer, where more infected TGCs could be found together with small foci of necrosis [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. At 7 days p.i. (i.e., 16 days p.c.), the whole decidua exhibited massive bacterial colonisation in the extracellular environment, with occasional infected TGCs together with necrotic regions filled with phagocytising neutrophils [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. Such massive bacterial growth in the decidua was also shown by fluorescence in term placentae of C57BL/6 pregnant mice IP infected with 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 at day-6 p.c. [<xref rid="B69-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>]. At the innermost layers, the spongiotrophoblast also exhibited multiple foci of moderate&#x2013;severe levels of necrosis with a lower degree of neutrophil infiltration [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. At 9 days p.i. (i.e., 18 days p.c.), the extension of the pathology described for the previous time-point increased, involving all placental layers, with extensive neutrophil infiltration, a common finding in other publications [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>,<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>], and lack of TGCs in severely affected placentae [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. Brucellae were also found to cause infarction at different levels: inducing thrombosis of the uterine vessels [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], micro-thrombi, or larger infarcted areas of the labyrinth blood vessels [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Such infarction phenomena could account for the foci of coagulative necrosis described in the spongiotrophoblast [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>,<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Noticeably, the progressive lack of TGCs in severely affected areas of infected placentae found by Tobias et al. could be due to progressive apoptosis of this cell population as a result of brucellae replication [<xref rid="B38-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], as noted in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> NI- [<xref rid="B40-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>] or <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308-infected placentae [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. This apoptosis of infected TGCs was shown to be mediated by the ER-stress-response cascade in a virulence-factor-dependent manner [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>], resulting in the induction of TNF&#x3B1; in the placenta, which plays a key role in the development of placental pathology and abortion induction but not in the ability of bacteria to replicate within the placentae [<xref rid="B72-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>].</p>
        <p>The dependence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathogenesis on known virulence factors has been shown throughout the literature. One of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella&#x2019;s</italic> main virulence factors is the VirB-T4SS (<underline>t</underline>ype <underline>4&#xA0;s</underline>ecretion <underline>s</underline>ystem), which translocates bacterial effectors to the cytoplasm of host cells and is essential for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella&#x2019;s</italic> intracellular survival, replication, and pathogenesis [<xref rid="B25-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>,<xref rid="B87-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">87</xref>,<xref rid="B88-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">88</xref>,<xref rid="B89-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">89</xref>,<xref rid="B90-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">90</xref>,<xref rid="B91-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">91</xref>]. Due to the key role of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> T4SS, mutants in this system are widely used as an attenuation control in virulence studies. Kim et al. and Watanabe et al. noticed that inoculation with 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse of a <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 &#x394;<italic toggle="yes">virB4</italic> mutant at day-4 p.c. (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) resulted in no abortion phenomena, while the same dose of the wild-type strain resulted in &gt;80% aborted foetuses, despite bacterial colonisation being similar between the two strain groups [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B66-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>]. Accordingly, a study by Byndloss et al. showed ca. 8% and 97% aborted foetuses for <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> &#x394;<italic toggle="yes">virB2</italic> and the corresponding wild-type strain, respectively [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. However, in contrast to the mentioned previous studies, a 2-log drop in bacterial placental recovery for the <italic toggle="yes">virB2</italic> mutant with regards to the wild-type strain was reported [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. Byndloss et al. also demonstrated that the T4SS-dependent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> effector molecule VceC (<underline>V</underline>irB-<underline>c</underline>oregulated <underline>e</underline>ffector <underline>C</underline>) is involved in the induction of the ER-stress cascade leading to the apoptosis of infected trophoblasts and abortion [<xref rid="B41-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. Infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 &#x394;<italic toggle="yes">vceC</italic> (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) resulted in a reduction in aborted foetuses (48.22&#x2013;61.63% abortions<xref rid="fn006-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">6</xref>), but with no significant drop in bacterial recovery from the placenta as happened for the VirB mutant.</p>
        <p>Likewise, the specific nutrient microenvironment found at the placenta, and thus the unique <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> metabolism, has also been proposed as an important driver of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive organ tropism, placental replication, and abortion induction. Regarding this, a study by Barbier et al. showed that depletion of erythritol catabolism (a sugar alcohol proposed as a preferential carbon source for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> at the placenta) in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 (administered at 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse via IP on day 6 and 14 p.c.; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) resulted in reduced bacterial burdens in the placentae and foetuses, although no data on abortion induction were shown [<xref rid="B69-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>].</p>
        <p>In the light of these studies, the reproductive pathogenic differences observed between virulent and attenuated strains open the possibility of using pregnant mice as a predictive model for the potential abortifacient effect of brucellosis vaccine candidates (<xref rid="sec3dot4-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="sec">Section 3.4</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot3-microorganisms-12-00866">
        <title>3.3. Vertical Transmission</title>
        <p>Bosseray found that in 60% of cases the pups of an infected dam were infected, and the infection persisted throughout the experiments (30 days) without progressing to cure or superinfection [<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>]. However, as pointed out by the author, infection clearance might have been expected, as it is observed both in the natural host [<xref rid="B92-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">92</xref>,<xref rid="B93-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">93</xref>] and in infected adult mice after at least 16 weeks [<xref rid="B94-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">94</xref>]. Infected newborns showed no signs of illness compared to non-infected pups, with no differences in the weight gain during the first 30 days of life. However, a later publication by Hashino et al. (from the same research group as Kim et al.) contradicts Bosseray regarding the health consequences on the offspring of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection [<xref rid="B63-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>]. In this publication, the growth and reproductive ability of the offspring born from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544-infected mice were investigated using a similar experimental design: IP infection of ICR mice with 10<sup>4</sup> CFU/mouse at 6.5 days p.c. [<xref rid="B68-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>] (<xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). In this study, mice born from infected mice exhibited a significantly lower weight gain over the 8 weeks of study and had reduced fertility. This congenitally infected generation gave birth to an also weakened and low-numbered second-generation offspring but did not transfer the infection, despite the first generation being actively infected at the moment of mating. Supporting these results, a more recent study by Adetunji et al. demonstrated that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 (10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse via IP; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) 8-week chronically infected non-pregnant mice yielded less successful pregnancies (i.e., 30.18 &#xB1; 21.05% success vs. 83.51 &#xB1; 4.56% success for the PBS control<xref rid="fn007-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="fn">7</xref>), despite not affecting the number of alive pups in the successful gestations [<xref rid="B82-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">82</xref>]. Brucellosis transmission from infected dams to offspring, either in utero or perinatally, is a common event in the natural host. Moreover, latent infections acquired through vertical transmission, when infected animals appear healthy and remain seronegative (thus becoming a dangerous source of brucellosis transmission), have been reported in up to 10% of the offspring from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-infected cattle or small ruminants [<xref rid="B93-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">93</xref>,<xref rid="B95-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">95</xref>,<xref rid="B96-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">96</xref>,<xref rid="B97-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">97</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec3dot4-microorganisms-12-00866">
        <title>3.4. Vaccine Safety Screening</title>
        <p>The assessment of brucellosis live vaccine candidates requires costly and cumbersome assays in the natural host (i.e., ovine, bovine, or porcine models) in order to evaluate, among other things, the vaccine safety in pregnant animals. However, experiments in the natural host are restricted due to animal size, the need for special facilities, high costs, and ethical aspects, thus laboratory models are imperative for an initial screening of vaccine candidates, and different mouse models are routinely used for this purpose [<xref rid="B54-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>]. In this context, the use of the pregnant mouse model for the assessment of the abortifacient effect of the live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S19 or <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 vaccine strains is also present in the literature, although these studies are much scarcer than for other wild-type <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species and strains.</p>
        <p>In the initial studies on the pregnant mouse model, Tobias et al. showed that a dose of 10<sup>7.5</sup> CFU/mouse of the S19 vaccine strain (via the IP route on day-9 p.c.; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) was needed to cause lesions or splenic and placental colonisations similar to those obtained with the inoculation of 10<sup>5.7</sup> CFU/mouse of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 [<xref rid="B37-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>]. Also, Kim et al. noted that the inoculation of pregnant mice with the S19 vaccine (via the IP route on day-4 p.c.; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) reduced the foetal abortion rate to 0.01%, when <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 544 induced a 98.4% of abortion in the same conditions, despite not finding remarkable differences in terms of bacterial placental colonisation [<xref rid="B39-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>].</p>
        <p>Regarding Rev1, the infection kinetics of this vaccine were evaluated upon inoculation of 4 &#xB1; 1-day-pregnant mice (via IP with 6&#x2013;7 &#xD7; 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse; <xref rid="microorganisms-12-00866-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>) in a recent publication by Poveda-Urkixo et al. [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Rev1 infection resulted in approximately 35% pup viability (although individual data ranged from 0 to 75%), while infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M led to complete abortion, in line with the expected attenuation of the vaccine strain. Likewise, although a relevant mention was not detected in the article, Rev1 infection also resulted in a reduced litter size of 9 pups/litter when compared to the 13.3 pups/litter in the PBS controls. This litter size reduction was similar on average to that of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M strain but less aggressive in the sense that two mice from the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M-infected mice experienced complete abortions with no pups present at term (see <xref rid="sec3dot1dot1-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="sec">Section 3.1.1</xref>), while the events in the Rev1-infected mice, in the two worst cases, resulted in litter sizes of four or nine pups [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. The results in this study also evidence the differential progression of bacterial colonisation of the spleen and placentae originally mentioned by Bosseray, with the spleen being heavily colonised at early times p.i. and then decreasing to stable values and the placentae being colonised at lower levels but experiencing a steady increase in bacterial loads [<xref rid="B62-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>]. At term (i.e., 14 days p.i. or 18 days p.c.), the Rev1 bacterial burden in the placentae was in line with previous findings for other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. and strains, with values between 6.43 and 8.44 log (CFU/g) [<xref rid="B73-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>,<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. The characterisation of both the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 and 16M strains allowed the authors to further evaluate a vaccine candidate with a deleted <italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic>, which is involved in the synthesis of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> lipopolysaccharide [<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. In both backgrounds, the <italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic> mutant showed similar splenic colonisation values (slightly reduced for the <italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic> candidates) than the respective parental strains but, surprisingly, a complete absence of placental colonisation; the gestational outcome of the infected pregnant mice in terms of pup viability or litter size were not evidenced in this study. In these two publications, the behaviour of the corresponding vaccine candidates was further assessed in the ovine natural host, whereas the Rev1&#x394;<italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic> candidate showed similar safety results in terms of absence of abortifacient effect and bacterial recovery from infected dams [<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>], the 16M&#x394;<italic toggle="yes">wzm</italic> candidate was recovered from delivered placentae from infected ewes as opposed to the previous findings in the pregnant mouse model in this publication [<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>].</p>
        <p>Finally, another publication evaluated the safety and abortion protection of a <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> ATCC<sup>&#xAE;</sup> 25840 mutant of an ABC transporter required for the intracellular survival and in vivo pathogenesis of <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> in mice and the natural host [<xref rid="B76-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">76</xref>]. Although this study did not assess the safety of the candidate itself in pregnant animals, as no placental colonisation or abortion induction were tracked or demonstrated, it did show that the candidate induced reduced histopathological lesions in the placentae and uteruses of pregnant mice.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="sec4-microorganisms-12-00866">
      <title>4. Concluding Remarks</title>
      <p>In the light of this review, it is important to remark on the discrepancies regarding the presence/absence of abortions or litter reductions even for very similar experimental designs and even with the same <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. and/or strains. In addition, emphasis should be placed on the use of homogeneous mating conditions and the reporting of adverse pregnancy outcomes for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection, not only in terms of reduced pup viability but also including the effect on litter size or other informative gestational indicators. Nonetheless, there is sufficient evidence in the literature to support the notion that the pregnant mouse model is useful to study <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-induced placentitis and abortions, as shown by the histopathological progression of placental infection and the involvement of known <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence factors (e.g., VirB-T4SS or VceC).</p>
      <p>The review on the experimental conditions employed through the literature shows that the IP inoculation of large-litter-size mice (such as ICR/CD-1) in the first half of gestation with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> induces abortion in pregnant mice, allowing the study of their reproductive pathogenesis. Regarding the dose, while 10<sup>4</sup>&#x2013;10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse has been established as the optimal dose for <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> biovar 1, the scarcity of publications, together with the use of different strains (from different biovars), makes it difficult to draw conclusions for <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> and suggests that a dose close to 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse may be necessary to achieve foetal abortion rates comparable to those of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>. Therefore, work with the pregnant mouse model may need to be standardised on a species- and strain-specific basis.</p>
      <p>Concerning the establishment of the pregnant mouse model as a preliminary screening for vaccine safety improvement, the different protocols employed, together with the conflicting results obtained in mice and ewes, question the utility of the current mouse model and call for its optimisation. In this context, the study of brucellosis vaccines may greatly benefit from the retrospective or parallel evaluation of new vaccine candidates both in the mouse and the natural host (e.g., ovine), as recently performed by Mena-Bueno et al. and Zabalza-Barangu&#xE1; et al. [<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>,<xref rid="B75-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. Nevertheless, as noted by these authors, the pregnant mouse model failed to replicate the reproductive safety outcome observed for at least one of the vaccine candidates in the ovine natural host [<xref rid="B74-microorganisms-12-00866" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>], leading to the conclusion that work is still needed for a further refinement of the model as a tool for vaccine safety screening.</p>
      <p>Finally, considering the diverse placentation types of the different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. hosts, the standardisation of the pregnant mouse model for species other than <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, such as zoonotic <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>, will broaden the understanding of the pathogenicity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucellaceae</italic>. This may also contribute to the development of reproductively safe vaccines for different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> hosts.</p>
