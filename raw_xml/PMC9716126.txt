      <abstract id="Abs1">
        <sec>
          <title>Abstract</title>
          <p id="Par1"><italic>Brucella suis</italic> mediates the transmission of brucellosis in humans and animals and a significant facultative zoonotic pathogen found in livestock. It has the capacity to survive and multiply in a phagocytic environment and to acquire resistance under hostile conditions thus becoming a threat globally. Antibiotic resistance is posing a substantial public health threat, hence there is an unmet and urgent clinical need for immune-based non-antibiotic methods to treat brucellosis. Hence, we aimed to explore the whole proteome of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> to predict antigenic proteins as a vaccine target and designed a novel chimeric vaccine (multi-epitope vaccine) through subtractive genomics-based reverse vaccinology approaches. The applied subsequent hierarchical shortlisting resulted in the identification of Multidrug efflux Resistance-nodulation-division (RND) transporter outer membrane subunit (gene BepC) that may act as a potential vaccine target. T-cell and B-cell epitopes have been predicted from target proteins using a number of immunoinformatic methods. Six MHC I, ten MHC II, and four B-cell epitopes were used to create a 324-amino-acid MEV construct, which was coupled with appropriate linkers and adjuvant. To boost the immunological response to the vaccine, the vaccine was combined with the TLR4 agonist HBHA protein. The MEV structure predicted was found to be highly antigenic, non-toxic, non-allergenic, flexible, stable, and soluble. To confirm the interactions with the receptors, a molecular docking simulation of the MEV was done using the human TLR4 (toll-like receptor 4) and HLAs. The stability and binding of the MEV-docked complexes with TLR4 were assessed using molecular dynamics (MD) simulation. Finally, MEV was reverse translated, its cDNA structure was evaluated, and then, in silico cloning into an <italic>E. coli</italic> expression host was conducted to promote maximum vaccine protein production with appropriate post-translational modifications. These comprehensive computer calculations backed up the efficacy of the suggested MEV in protecting against <italic>B. suis</italic> infections. However, more experimental validations are needed to adequately assess the vaccine candidate&#x2019;s potential.</p>

    <sec id="Sec1">
      <title>Introduction
</title>
      <p id="Par6">Brucellosis is an endemic disease, also known as Malta fever, Mediterranean fever, undulant fever, and Bang&#x2019;s disease caused by <italic>Brucella</italic> genus belong to a family of <italic>Brucellaceae</italic>, class <italic>Alphaproteobacteria</italic> infecting both animals and humans [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>]. Species of this genus are Gram-negative intracellular facultative pathogens. Based on specific phenotypes with the host and environmental adaptation, till now more than ten known species of <italic>Brucella</italic> are identified [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>]. Brucellosis is characterized as acute fever illness [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>], associated with various symptoms in human such as liver and spleen disorders, reproductive abnormalities, neurological problems, heart-related problems, and also have been classified as a potential bioterrorism agent [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>]. Brucellosis remains endemic in various emerging countries in Asia, Africa, Middle East, and South America, where screening of livestock and vaccination fails to control and exterminate the disease [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>].</p>
      <p id="Par7">The World Health Organization (WHO), Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), and World Organization for Animal Health (OIE) consider brucellosis as one of the most contagious zoonotic disease worldwide [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>], yet human brucellosis remains the commonest zoonotic disease around the world [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. Brucellosis is transmitted to humans via several routes including, eating raw dairy products from infected animals, aerosol inhalation of an infected animal in clinical lab and slaughterhouse, handling, and exposure to tissue and body fluid of infected animals without Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>]. According to Hull [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>], there are 500,000 cases of human brucellosis reported per year around the globe due to their ability to survive and multiply within the host phagocytotic and non-phagocytotic cell. Surprisingly, <italic>Brucella</italic> did not show classical virulence mechanisms such as producing cytolysin, plasmids, fimbria, exotoxin, exoenzymes, and drug resistant forms. However, <italic>Brucella</italic> is having major virulence factors such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>], &#x3B2;-cyclic glucan [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>], outer membrane proteins (Omps) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">12</xref>], MucR [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>], T4SS secretion system, and BvrR/BvrS system which permit the <italic>Brucella</italic> to interact with the host cell [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">14</xref>]. Similarly, T4SS-based VirB proteins complex their 5 effectors are an essential part of <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogenesis that regulate the host cell inflammation response and vesicle trafficking [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">15</xref>]. Due to the limited knowledge regarding the genomics of <italic>Brucella suis</italic>, a new species-specific therapeutic compound and vaccine candidates are difficult to design experimentally [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>]. The immune system prophylactic and designing of new therapeutic approaches are of significant interest to combat the antimicrobial resistance. The peptide-based chimeric vaccine, expressed by the pathogenic strain of <italic>Brucella</italic>, would be an appropriate alternative to its resistance.</p>
      <p id="Par8">Nevertheless, evaluation of thousands of macro-molecules and their subsequent in vivo assays in the wet lab need a lot of time and cost for vaccine design. On the other hand, development in computational biology and various other bioinformatics fields have made wonderful progress leading to a great reduction in consumption of time and associated expenses [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>&#x2013;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>]. Bioinformatics analysis typically employs alternative approaches for finding novel drug targets, identifying vaccine or drug candidates, elucidating the host&#x2013;pathogen interactions, designing structure-based drugs, allowing genome-based comparative study, and so on. It is thereby reducing the conventional laboratory-based experimental practice [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>&#x2013;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>]. The subtractive genomic and reverse vaccinology are one of the most used computational approaches to evaluate the suitability of the vaccine target among the previously known drug target based on selectivity and specificity. Numerous studies had widely reported the use of subtractive genomics and reverse vaccinology approaches against various pathogenic strains for the identification of novel species-specific therapeutic targets [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>&#x2013;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">25</xref>]. Hence, in the current study, we used the subtractive genomic analysis and reverse vaccinology approach against the whole proteome of the <italic>Brucella suis</italic> strain to shortlist the vital proteins as vaccine targets. The results of this study suggest that our resultant proteins may be considered the best vaccine targets along with identified multi-epitopes chimeric vaccine that can be used for establishing a universal vaccine, which may provide a basic pipeline against Brucellosis.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec2">
      <title>Material and methods</title>
      <p id="Par9">The current study utilized a subtractive genomic and reverse vaccinology approach for the identification of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> specific vaccine targets [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">26</xref>].</p>
      <sec id="Sec3">
        <title>Data retrieval</title>
        <p id="Par10">The complete proteome of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> was retrieved from the National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) database [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>] along with the complete proteome of Human from UNIPROT database (Table <xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>]. The Database of Essential Gene (DEG) was used to identify the essentiality of proteins, whereas KEGG sever was used to retrieve human and Brucella metabolic pathways.<table-wrap id="Tab1"><label>Table 1</label><caption><p>Complete proteome of human-host and <italic>Brucella suis</italic></p></caption><table frame="hsides" rules="groups"><thead><tr><th align="left">Strain ID</th><th align="left">Strain name</th><th align="left">Proteins</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td align="left">GCF_0000007505.1</td><td align="left">1330</td><td align="left">2974</td></tr><tr><td align="left">9606</td><td align="left">Human</td><td align="left">&#x2009;~&#x2009;20,0000</td></tr></tbody></table></table-wrap></p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec4">
        <title>Prioritization of pathogen-specific metabolic pathways</title>
        <p id="Par11">The KEGG [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">29</xref>] and Automatic Annotation Server (KAAS) metabolic pathway database were used for the analysis of human-host and <italic>Brucella suis</italic> metabolic pathways. We retrieved the metabolic pathway ID with related information from the KEGG database. Those pathways which were present in both considered a common metabolic pathway, while the remaining considered unique metabolic pathways. We retrieved the FASTA sequence of proteins involved in unique metabolic pathways of the <italic>Brucella suis</italic> from the NCBI database.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec5">
        <title>Non-homologous protein identification</title>
        <p id="Par12">Unique metabolic pathways proteins were subjected to evaluate through a BLASTp against the whole proteome of a human using the cut-off value of 0.0001 (<italic>E</italic>-value 10<sup>&#x2013;3</sup>). Those proteins which had a high sequence similarity (&gt;&#x2009;80%) with human proteome were excluded and the remaining proteins having no similarities were retrieved and further analyzed in the next step of subtractive genomic analysis.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec6">
        <title>Prioritization of essential proteins among the whole proteome of Brucella suis</title>
        <p id="Par13">In order to prioritize the essential proteins of the <italic>Brucella suis</italic> the proteins were analyzed via BLASTp with a threshold value of 10<sup>&#x2013;5</sup>, against the Database of Essential Gene (DEG) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">30</xref>] consisted of proteins responsible for the survival of the organisms. Those proteins which had sequence similarity with essential protein of DEG were further analyzed and the non-essential proteins were excluded. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec7">
        <title>Determination of virulence factor (proteins)</title>
        <p id="Par14">The virulence of proteins helps bacteria to destroy the host-immune system with the help of colonization and invading the host immune cell and as a result, the disease caused. For the determination of virulence of proteins, VFDB (virulence factor of pathogenic bacteria) online database [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">31</xref>] was used. The shortlisted proteins of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> were subjected to BLAST against the VFDB.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec8">
        <title>Identification of resistance proteins</title>
        <p id="Par15">The Antibiotic Resistance Gene-ANNOTation V6 (ARG-ANNOT V6) tool was used for the prediction of novel resistance protein sequences from whole-genome and proteome of a pathogen. All the resistant proteins data were collected and analyzed from different experimental published work, different online sources, and protein sequences were retrieved from NCBI database. The shortlisted proteins FASTA sequence were then subjected to BLAST against the resistance proteins of the ARG-ANNOT V6 database with a threshold of 10<sup>&#x2013;5</sup> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">32</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec9">
        <title>Subcellular localization of shortlisted proteins</title>
        <p id="Par16">All the shortlisted proteins were then subjected to PSORTb version 3.0.2 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">33</xref>] and Cello v.2.5 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>] online tool in order to identify subcellular localization. The main principle of subcellular localization (SCI BLAST) is to BLAST all the shortlisted proteins against the PSORTb and Cello v.2.5 online servers. The subcellular localization consists of cytoplasm, cytoplasmic membrane, periplasmic membrane, and extracellular space, and some are unknown.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec10">
        <title>Non-homolog gut protein identification</title>
        <p id="Par17">Nonhomologous essential proteins selected as vaccine candidates and novel drug targets were further subjected to standalone BLASTp with an E value cutoff score of 0.001 against the data set present in the Human Microbiome Project server (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://www.hmpdacc.org/hmp/">https://www.hmpdacc.org/hmp/</ext-link>) &#x201C;28,331&#x201D; (BioProject) retrieved as gastrointestinal tract from HMP [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">35</xref>] i.e., (<ext-link ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://hmpdacc.org/hmp/catalog/grid.php?dataset=genomic&amp;hmp_isolation_body_site=gastrointestinal_tract">https://hmpdacc.org/hmp/catalog/grid.php?dataset=genomic&amp;hmp_isolation_body_site=gastrointestinal_tract</ext-link>) having 26,295 proteins (accessed on 1<sup>st</sup> Sep, 2022). The HMP sampled microbes from a healthy adult population of 239 people, taking samples from 18 different sites around the body (the mouth, skin, nose, intestines, and genitourinary tract). This yielded&#x2009;~&#x2009;823 unique gut microbial species compositions [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>]. Novel therapeutic targets and vaccine candidates were found for proteins with a no similarity threshold. Based on these findings, precautions may be taken to ensure that human microflora proteins are not inadvertently inhibited or blocked.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec11">
        <title>Prediction of antigenic protein</title>
        <p id="Par18">Outer membrane protein shortlisted from the above subtractive genomic analysis was selected that contain the potency for vaccine development. A bioinformatics approach was applied to that protein to identify the epitopes to boost the immune response of the host. For the prediction of antigenic outer membrane protein VaxiJen webserver [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>] was used with a default parameter of 0.5 considered potent antigenicity. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec12">
        <title>Prediction of MHC class I T-cell epitope</title>
        <p id="Par19">The MHC I epitopes for the selected protein was predicted by NetCTL server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>]. The predicted epitopes were chosen based on a high score. The default parameter of 0.75 was used for the prediction of potent T cell MHC I epitopes. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec13">
        <title>Prediction of MHC I epitope immunogenicity</title>
        <p id="Par20">A bioinformatics tool IEDB server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>] was used for MHC I antigenicity prediction. The MHC I epitopes should have the strength to evoke a host immune response. A default parameter was used to predict the immunogenicity of MHC I epitopes. Those epitopes having positive antigenic values were selected for further analysis. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec14">
        <title>Antigenicity, conservancy, and toxicity analysis</title>
        <p id="Par21">The immunogenic epitopes obtained from the IEDB server were analyzed for the antigenic capability using Vaxijen version 2.0 server with a threshold value of 0.5. The IEDB conservancy analysis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">40</xref>] tool was used for the assessment of the conserved sequence among all the genotypes of MHC I epitopes. The conserved sequence identity parameter was set as a default. This analysis showed the conserved epitopes within the given protein sequence [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">41</xref>]. The assessment of toxicity level was predicted by an online tool called ToxinPred. The parameter was set on default. The toxicity level confirms that the specific host immune response will only target the bacteria rather than host cell itself [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">42</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec15">
        <title>MHC II epitope prediction</title>
        <p id="Par22">The IEDB-AR server was used for the identification of MHC class 2 epitopes. It applies the consensus-based prediction approach of both the average relative binding matrix method and stabilization matrix alignment method [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">43</xref>].</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec16">
        <title>MHC I- and II-restricted allele cluster analysis</title>
        <p id="Par23">To confirm the predicted T-cell epitopes, the MHCcluster v2.0 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">44</xref>] was used to find the cluster of MHC-restricted alleles with their appropriate peptides. This server crosschecks the MHC-restricted allele analysis from the IEDB analysis resources. It results in the static heat map and phylogenetic tree analyzing the functional relation between peptides and HLAs. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec17">
        <title>Prediction of B-cell epitopes</title>
        <p id="Par24">The B-cell epitope prediction was performed by online bioinformatics server BCPred [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>] and FBCpred server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>]. The BCPred works on five different kernel methods whereas, FBCpred is based on consequent kernel methods. The cut-off score of 0.8 was used for B-cell epitopes identification via BCPred [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">46</xref>]. The IEDB B-cell epitope prediction server was used for biochemical properties analysis such as hydrophobicity, surface accessibility, hydrophilicity, amino acid composition, and secondary structure to predict the linear B-cell epitopes.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec18">
        <title>Construction of model vaccine</title>
        <p id="Par25">Different combinations of the shortlisted T-cell and B-cell epitopes were conjugated sequentially to model a vaccine construct with low toxicity, allergenicity, and high immunogenicity. During the vaccine construction, four different epitopes sequence were added in different combinations. Different amino acid linkers such as GGGS were used between these sequences. To enhance the antigenicity of the vaccine, PADRE (Pan HLA-DR reactive epitope) along with four different adjuvants such as HBHA, HBHA conserved, ribosomal, and beta-defensin were used in each vaccine construct, respectively. The PADRE sequence induced CD4&#x2009;+&#x2009;T-cells that improve efficacy and potency of peptide vaccine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR47">47</xref>]. The adjuvant HBHA and ribosomal adjuvants sequence are agonists of the toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) while beta-defensin adjuvant is an agonist of the TLR1, TLR2, and TLR4. The sequence-fused vaccine construct was used for further analysis. </p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec19">
        <title>Evaluation of allergenicity, antigenicity, and solubility for vaccine construct</title>
        <p id="Par26">The allergenicity was determined by online tool AlgPred program (Saha &amp; Raghava, 2006). The AlgPred tool uses all these parameters (IgE epitope&#x2009;+&#x2009;MAST&#x2009;+&#x2009;ARPs BLAST&#x2009;+&#x2009;SVM) combined to predict the allergenicity of vaccine construct with a threshold value of&#x2009;&#x2212;&#x2009;0.40 prediction score. The ANTIGENpro program [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR48">48</xref>] was used for the determination of vaccine antigenicity while SOLpro was used for the solubility of a vaccine.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec20">
        <title>Physicochemical properties of vaccine construct</title>
        <p id="Par27">The physicochemical properties of vaccine sequence were observed by the Expasy ProtParam online server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">49</xref>]. This tool is used for the identification of different physicochemical properties such as molecular weight, number of amino acids, PI values, hydropathicity GRAVY values, aliphatic index, instability index, and estimated half-life of the protein of a generated protein model. This software can analyze various physicochemical properties based on pKa values of different amino acids sequence of a vaccine. The instability index of protein forecasts whether our vaccine is stable or unstable. The aliphatic index of a protein referred to the volume occupied by the aliphatic side chains of amino acid.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec21">
        <title>Secondary structure modeling</title>
        <p id="Par28">The modeling of vaccine construct was performed by the online tool Swiss modeler [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">50</xref>]. The homology models are constructed using the automated SWISS-MODEL homology modeling server pipeline, and experimental resolve crystal structures of proteins are mapped between the PDB database and UniProtKB using SIFTS [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]. The PSIPRED and PROCHECK were used for the model structure evaluation. The Psi-BLAST selects the relating sequence which had similarity&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;80.6% with reported proteins. The best model for each protein was selected and subjected to further analysis.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec22">
        <title>Molecular docking and molecular dynamic simulation</title>
        <p id="Par29">The molecular docking of V1 with 6 different human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles was performed via online tool PatchDock [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>] to show the interaction of V1 with HLA alleles. Six different HLA alleles were downloaded from the PDB database with its PDB ID 2FSE (HLA-DRB1*01:01), 3C5J (HLA-DR B3*02:02), 1H15 (HLA-DR B5*01:01), 2Q6W (HLA-DR B3*01:01), 2SEB (HLA-DRB1*04:01), and 1A6A (HLA-DR B1*03:01). The FireDock (Fast Interaction Refinement in Molecular Docking) was used for further validation and more refinement of interactions [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>]. Similarly, docking of vaccine (V1) with TLR4 was performed by the GRAMMX [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">53</xref>]. Finally, molecular dynamic simulation of vaccine and TLR4 complex was performed using GROMACS [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR54">54</xref>]. The final vaccine solvation was executed with an SPC water model in a cubic box with energy minimization using the steepest algorithm. Here, the system was firstly equilibrated using NVT ensemble followed by NPT ensemble. Finally, the vaccine&#x2019;s molecular dynamic simulation was performed for 10&#xA0;ns. Furthermore, molecular dynamic simulation of docked complex (vaccine with TLR4) was performed via iMODs server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR55">55</xref>]. The iMODs define and calculate the flexibility of protein complex and can be accessed freely. The server asses the direction and extent of the immanent motions of the complex in terms of B-factors, deformability, covariance, and eigenvalue.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec23">
        <title>Optimization and in silico cloning of final vaccine model</title>
        <p id="Par30">The amino acid sequence of vaccine was reversed translated into DNA sequence to optimized and enhance the expression of chimeric vaccine proteins in the <italic>E. coli</italic> system through JCAT (Java Codon Adaptation Tool) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR56">56</xref>]. The JCAT tool resulted in the prediction of GC content and codon Adaption Index score (CAI) for DNA sequences while avoiding the cleavage site for restriction enzymes Rho-independent termination of transcription and prokaryotic ribosome binding sites. Snapgene tool was used for the insertion of an optimized amino acid sequence of vaccine construct in <italic>E. coli</italic> pET-28a(&#x2009;+) expression system.</p>

      <title>Results</title>
      <sec id="Sec25">
        <title>Unique metabolic pathway analysis</title>
        <p id="Par31">Complete metabolic pathways of both <italic>Brucella suis</italic> (109 pathways) and human host (330 pathways) were downloaded from the KEGG (Kyoto encyclopedia of gene and genome) server. We compared both the human and <italic>Brucella suis</italic> metabolic pathways manually to find both the common and unique metabolic pathways. The results showed that 71 pathways were common (Table <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">S1</xref>) both in humans and <italic>Brucella suis</italic> and the 38 pathways (Table <xref rid="Tab2" ref-type="table">2</xref>) were unique to <italic>Brucella suis</italic>. Among these 38 pathways, two-component system, quorum sensing, ribosome, flagellar assembly, and bacterial secretion pathways was observed as highly unique pathways to brucella with 64, 114, 55, 35, and 27 unique proteins. Two-component system (TCS) and quorum sensing regulate the expression of virulence genes, enable the pathogen to detect changes in its external environment and adapt its gene expression appropriately [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR57">57</xref>]. For example, the quorum-sensing (QS) regulator VjbR, which triggers virB expression, is encoded by the gene encoding the two-component regulator BvrRS, which enables the brucellae to sense the acidic pH and food deprivation they face in the eBCV [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR57">57</xref>]. In <italic>Brucella</italic>, a non-motile bacterium, the flagellum, is involved in virulence, infectivity, cell proliferation, and biofilm formation. <italic>Brucella</italic>&#x2019;s flagellar proteins are expressed, providing evidence for an evolutionary scenario in which a free-living bacterium acquired flagellar genes from environmental microorganisms, conferring on it the ability to reach other hosts (mammals), and, under selective pressure from the environment, can express these genes, helping it evade the immune response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR58">58</xref>]. Likewise, the bacterial secretion pathways and ribosomal pathways help <italic>brucella</italic> to evade certain non-favorable environment by the secretion of various toxins and proteins. Additionally, Methane metabolism, Lysine biosynthesis, Lipopolysaccharide biosynthesis, O-Antigen nucleotide sugar biosynthesis, Peptidoglycan biosynthesis, Benzoate degradation, Beta-Lactam resistance, and Cationic antimicrobial peptide (CAMP) resistance pathways were consisting of 10&#x2013;20 unique proteins. These metabolic pathways enable brucella to modulate the cell wall composition to evade the antibiotic environment.       <sec id="Sec26">
        <title>Prioritization of non-homologous proteins</title>
        <p id="Par32">We subjected 503 unique metabolic pathways of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> to run a BLASTp with a cutoff value 10<sup>&#x2013;3</sup> against the whole proteome of humans to identify only non-homologous proteins for novel drug targets prioritization. We selected only non-homologous proteins to avoid the undesirable side effects of the drug. The BLASTp results showed 82 proteins were homologous to human host with high similarity with human proteome and were excluded. The remaining 421 non-homologous proteins were analyzed in next step.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec27">
        <title>Identification of the essential proteins</title>
        <p id="Par33">The Database of Essential Gene (DEG) provides complete information of the essentiality of proteins of the bacteria determined from the experimental analysis. Those proteins having high similarity with proteins of DEG were selected as essential proteins. We ran a BLASTp of non-homologous 421 proteins against the DEG database with a default parameter 60% sequence identity. The results showed that 350 proteins were essential for the survival of <italic>Brucella suis</italic>.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec28">
        <title>Predicting the virulence factor (proteins) of B. suis</title>
        <p id="Par34">The VFDB database comes up with complete information of protein virulence. The VFDB explored the importance of virulent proteins in disease progression. The VFDB results revealed that 45 proteins out of 350 (shortlisted proteins after the analysis of metabolic pathways associated proteins) were correlated with virulence of <italic>B. suis</italic>. However, these 45 proteins can be used as a novel and potent drug target against <italic>B. suis</italic> strain 1330.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec29">
        <title>Identification of the resistance proteins</title>
        <p id="Par35">The pathogens resistant to drugs are more hassle to treat the disease, which requires a higher dose that shows a diverse effect in patients. The pathogen acquires with a drug resistance is due to the continuous exposure to drug or the drug used in a higher dose. We subjected the FASTA sequence of shortlisted virulent proteins to ARG-ANNOT V6 online tool. The results showed that 42 out of 45 proteins were correlated with the resistivity of a pathogen. These 42 proteins are mostly involved in the degradation and efflux of numerous drugs. However, these 45 proteins can be used as a potential drug target.</p>
      </sec>

The cytoplasmic proteins may act as drug target and outer membrane proteins may act as vaccine target. The results of current study showed&#x2009;~&#x2009;23 cytoplasmic,&#x2009;~&#x2009;14 periplasmic,&#x2009;~&#x2009;6 outer membrane, and&#x2009;~&#x2009;4 inner membrane proteins.       <sec id="Sec38">
        <title>B-cell epitope prediction in BepC</title>
        <p id="Par44">In order to inflict humoral immunity, apart from the MHC-I and MHC-II epitopes (cellular immunity), B-cell epitopes were also identified using BepC protein sequences. For the elimination of pathogen, humoral immunity is also needed along with cellular immunity. The B-cell epitope prediction and classification plays a vital role in vaccine designing, antibody production, and immunodiagnostic tests. Identifying the B-cell epitopes experimentally is an expensive and time-consuming process while computational methods are highly desirable to predict the B-cell epitopes. Hence, the prediction of B-cell epitopes was performed using BCPreds, FBCpred, and ABCpred tools. The results showed that five epitopes were generated via BCPred server, twelve epitopes were generated through the FBCpred tool and twenty-eight epitopes were predicted via ABCpred server (Table <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">S3</xref>).</p>

      <sec id="Sec40">
        <title>Construction of final vaccine model</title>
        <p id="Par46">An adjuvant and PADRE sequence (AKFVAAWTLKAAA) are the most significant component of a multi-epitope vaccine that inflict strong immune response in the host body [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR61">61</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR62">62</xref>]. The PADRE sequences were chosen for the construction of vaccine in order to examine the adjuvants effects on the antigenicity and allergenicity and to combat the polymorphism complications of HLA-DR molecules caused in the global population. A total of sixteen vaccine constructs were joined with the EAAAK linker and respective adjuvants (HBHA, HBHA conserved, ribosomal, and beta defensin). The MHC Class-I, MHC Class-II, and B-cell epitopes were linked with GGGS, HEYGAEALERAG, and PADRE sequence. A study reported that all the epitopes were joined together with these two linkers do not change the conformation of designed vaccine construct [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR63">63</xref>] (Table <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">S4</xref>).</p>

      <sec id="Sec41">
        <title>Prediction of antigenicity, allergenicity, and solubility for vaccine models</title>
        <p id="Par47">The prediction of non-allergic vaccine constructs was performed by AlgPred online server. The constructed vaccine should not be allergenic, because allergic vaccines can induce a cross-reaction in the body. The allergenicity of all the sixteen vaccine constructs was checked and only those vaccines were selected which showed a value&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;0.7. Similarly, the antigenicity and solubility of the designed multi-epitope vaccines were also determined by ANTIGENpro and SOLpro, respectively. The solubility of all the vaccine constructs was&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;0.8. Finally, we selected only one vaccine to construct on the basis of allergenicity, antigenicity, and solubility for further analysis (Table <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">S5</xref>).</p>

    <sec id="Sec47">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p id="Par55">Understanding the proteome of a pathogen is important as it facilitates further comprehensive analysis of proteins in various biochemical and pathological pathways that help in the identification of novel drug targets. The development of a novel therapeutic target and vaccination is a significant scientific challenge to combat <italic>Brucella suis</italic>-related brucellosis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR64">64</xref>]. Peptide-based vaccines are now possible to design due to the advancement of sequence-based technologies, computational analysis, and the abundance of genomes and proteomics data for many diseases [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR61">61</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR62">62</xref>]. The subtractive genomics along with reverse vaccinology can aid in arraying the vast information regarding genomics and proteomics of various pathogens providing acceleration in drug and vaccines designing and pharmacogenomics in the treatments of bacterial infection. The &#x201C;vaccinomics/Reverse Vaccinology&#x201D; [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR65">65</xref>] approach has been proved to be promising approach widely used against Meningococcus B (MenB) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR66">66</xref>], antibiotic-resistant <italic>Staphylococcus aureus</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR67">67</xref>]<italic>, </italic><italic>Chlamydia</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR68">68</xref>], group A <italic>Streptococcus</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR69">69</xref>], and <italic>Streptococcus pneumonia</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR70">70</xref>].</p>
      <p id="Par56">Brucellosis is characterized as acute fever illness associated with various symptoms in human such as liver and spleen disorders, reproductive abnormalities, neurological problems, heart-related problems, and also have been classified as a potential bioterrorism agent. There are 500,000 cases of human brucellosis reported per year around the globe due to their ability to survive and multiply within the host phagocytotic and non-phagocytotic cell. Due to the limited knowledge regarding the genomics of <italic>Brucella suis</italic>, new species-specific therapeutic compound and vaccine candidates are difficult to design experimentally. Therefore, the development of new vaccines model against <italic>Brucella suis</italic> is necessary.</p>
      <p id="Par57">In the present study, we have applied reverse vaccinology and subtractive genomics based computational scheme to screen whole proteome of <italic>Brucella Suis</italic> for the identification of novel drug target and multi-epitope vaccine construction. We selected unique metabolic pathways composed of proteins unique to <italic>Brucella suis</italic> and excluded the common metabolic pathways present both in <italic>brucella</italic> and human-host since unique proteins are of interest. Total unique metabolic pathways consisted of 503 proteins followed by the foretelling of non-homologous protein from the complete proteome of <italic>Brucella Suis</italic>. Similarly, the essential proteins from the complete proteome of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> were determine which can be used as a potential drug target. The DEG analysis led to the identification of essential protein required for the survival of <italic>Brucella suis</italic> followed by the determination of virulence protein which are responsible for infection in human. Likewise, resistance protein identification was also performed. Resistance proteins are those which are responsible for the efflux of various antibiotic from the bacterial cell to counteract the drug action [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR71">71</xref>]. The subcellular localization of shortlisted proteins was also predicted for the identification of cytoplasmic protein for the drug targets and cell membrane protein for the construction of multi-epitope vaccine [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR72">72</xref>]. We shortlisted one protein namely <italic>Multidrug Efflux RND Transporter Outer Membrane Subunit BepC</italic> (involve in LPS metabolic pathway) for vaccine construction<italic>.</italic> Since this metabolic pathway is essential for bacteria but is absent in humans, BepC is being investigated as a possible vaccine candidate [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR73">73</xref>]. Surprisingly, <italic>Brucella</italic> did not show classical virulence mechanisms such as producing cytolysins, plasmids, fimbria, exotoxin, exoenzymes, and drug resistant forms. However, <italic>Brucella</italic> is having major virulence factors such as, lipopolysaccharide (LPS) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>]. This research employed a combination of prediction techniques to identify putative B- and T-cell epitopes that might trigger humoral or cell-mediated responses. Antibodies, also called immunoglobulins, are produced in large part by a kind of cell called a B-lymphocyte. Each of these epitopes takes on a linear or conformational shape [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR74">74</xref>]. Only in the protein&#x2019;s basic structure can the linear B-cell fold be found in its entirety. It is protein folding that brings together discontinuous or conformational B-cell epitopes. This is an essential factor while developing vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR75">75</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR76">76</xref>]. As a result of their ability to recognize and interact with MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex) linked to antigen-presenting cells, T cells are classified as CD4&#x2009;+&#x2009;and CD8&#x2009;+&#x2009;cells, respectively (APCs). In summary, these APCs have surface antigens that are recognized by T-cell receptors [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR73">73</xref>]. The predicted peptide in present study were combined with the help of different linkers and adjuvant to construct chimeric based vaccine against <italic>Brucella suis</italic>. The addition of used adjuvant increased the immunogenicity of the vaccine formulation. Because of its immunomodulatory characteristics, this adjuvant is increasingly being employed in multiepitope vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR72">72</xref>]. Eventually, 16 vaccine constructs were designed against <italic>Brucella suis</italic> which were comprehensively investigated for toxicity profile, allergenicity pattern immunogenicity, and conservancy analysis resultant in only one final vaccine construct. The interaction of modeled vaccine with human leukocyte antigen (HLA) allele to interpret effective immune response was examine using molecular docking simulation studies with GRAMAXX and GROMACS tools. Protein&#x2013;protein docking is frequently used in reverse vaccinology to identify the most promising vaccine design [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR72">72</xref>]. However, the results of docking analysis showed the binding affinity of promiscuous epitopes with different HLA alleles. An important TLR in mammals, TLR2/4 can identify lipoproteins from bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR77">77</xref>]. Therefore, the shortlisted vaccine construct was used for the binding affinity and stability estimation of the of the vaccine and TLR4 complex. The JCAT tool was used for codon optimization. The JCAT calculated the CAI score which was 1 and the GC content was 70.78% indicating the higher level of expression. The pET28a vector was used for its heterologous cloning and expression in <italic>E. coli</italic> by the Snapgene tool. In order to confirm the findings of this research, it is recommended that the vaccine candidate be expressed in bacteria for further investigation. The inquiry would next be guided by pre-clinical studies, including tissue-culture or cell-culture systems, and animal experimentation.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec48">
      <title>Conclusion</title>
      <p id="Par58">The current study applied the subtractive genomics and reverse vaccinology approach for the prioritization of potent vaccine targets against <italic>Brucella suis</italic> 1300 strain. It smears multiple essential analyses at different stages i.e., non-homologs, essential, and unique to pathogens proteins, and sub cellular localization. In this study numbers of proteins along with Multidrug efflux RND transporter outer membrane subunit BepC was shortlisted as a novel vaccine target against <italic>Brucella suis</italic>. Consequently, the shortlisted essential proteins may be further study and used as a therapeutic vaccine candidate for <italic>Brucella Suis</italic>. Furthermore, immunogenicity prediction, allergenicity identification, and tertiary structure analysis of vaccine proposed it as a potent chimeric vaccine against <italic>Brucella suis</italic>. The molecular docking simulation and codon optimization were also found as satisfactory hence giving confidence to this study. It was found that the designed MEV in the present study can make stable interactions with human immune receptors and able to stimulate an efficient host immune system response. However, experimental validation with computational approaches is required for further analysis to improve the efficacy of predicted MEV.</p>
