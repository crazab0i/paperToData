<italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp.</italic> are Gram-negative, facultative intracellular pathogens that cause brucellosis in both humans and animals. The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain 104&#160;M is the only vaccine available in China for the prevention of brucellosis in humans. Although the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome has been fully sequenced, the current genome annotations are not yet complete. In addition, the main mechanisms underpinning its residual toxicity and vaccine-induced immune protection have yet to be elucidated. Mapping the proteome of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M will help to improve genome annotation quality, thereby facilitating a greater understanding of its biology.</p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p>In this study, we utilized a proteogenomic approach that combined subcellular fractionation and peptide fractionation to perform a whole-proteome analysis and genome reannotation of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M using high-resolution mass spectrometry. In total, 1,729 proteins (56.3% of 3,072) including 218 hypothetical proteins were identified using the culture conditions that were employed this study. The annotations of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome were also refined following identification and validation by reverse transcription-PCR. In addition, 14 pivotal virulence factors and 17 known protective antigens known to be involved in residual toxicity and immune protection were confirmed at the protein level following induction by the 104&#160;M vaccine. Moreover, a further insight into the cell biology of multichromosomal bacteria was obtained following the elucidation of differences in protein expression levels between the small and large chromosomes.</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusions</title><p>The work presented in this report used a proteogenomic approach to perform whole-proteome analysis and genome reannotation in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M; this work helped to improve genome annotation quality. Our analysis of virulence factors, protective antigens and other protein effectors provided the basis for further research to elucidate the mechanisms of residual toxicity and immune protection induced by the 104&#160;M vaccine. Finally, the potential link between replication dynamics, gene function, and protein expression levels in this multichromosomal bacterium was detailed.</p></sec><sec><title>Electronic supplementary The Author(s) 2017</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="Sec1"><title>Background</title><p>
<italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>. are Gram-negative, intracellular bacterial pathogens that can cause brucellosis in both humans and animals [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>]. As one of the most common zoonotic diseases, brucellosis is a significant economic and public health problem worldwide with more than 500,000 new cases reported annually [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> are the most pathogenic strains in humans and have been identified as potential bio-terrorism agents [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>]. At present, vaccination is the most effective approach for preventing and controlling brucellosis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M vaccine strain is the only vaccine that has been widely used in China for the control and prevention of human brucellosis since its approval by the Chinese Food and Drug Administration in 1965 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>]. This vaccine strain was isolated from the placenta of a sick cow in a former Soviet republic. The strain exhibits strong immunogenicity with low and stable residual toxicity in experimental animals [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>].</p><p>Although the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome has been fully sequenced (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide-range" xlink:href="CP009625:CP009626">CP009625-CP009626</ext-link>), it was annotated using in silico methods with many unannotated genes and errors. Furthermore, a large number of hypothetical proteins (620 of 3,072) have yet to be annotated, thereby limiting our understanding of the biological processes pertaining to this strain [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. Proteogenomics is an important tool for integrating protein-level information into the genome annotation process, thereby greatly improving genome annotation quality [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>]. Therefore, a proteogenomics study is required to both validate the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome annotation and re-annotate mis-annotated novel genes.</p><p>The underlying molecular and physiological mechanisms that cause possible residual toxicity and immune protection following 104&#160;M vaccine induction remain to be elucidated. In our preliminary work, we have determined the whole-genome sequence of 104&#160;M and conducted a comparative analysis against its homologous virulent strain A13334 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. It revealed highly similar genome structures but a set of genes missing between 104&#160;M and A13334 that related to virulence alteration. This observation suggests that the remaining virulence associated genes in the 104&#160;M genome may lead to the residual toxicity associated with the 104&#160;M vaccine. We also observed a number of protective antigens that can promote both humoral and cellular immunity in the 104&#160;M genome. The occurrence of these antigens is likely to result in the enhancement of host defense mechanisms pertaining to bacterial infections. The identification of these pivotal virulence factors and protective antigens at the protein level is likely to be beneficial in the elucidation of mechanisms that underpin residual toxicity and immune protection following induction by the 104&#160;M vaccine.</p><p>To this end, we used a proteogenomics approach that combined subcellular fractionation and peptide fractionation to perform whole-proteome analysis and genome reannotation in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M. Virulence factors, known protective antigens and other protein effectors that are critical for bacterial virulence and vaccine protection were identified at the protein level. Moreover, differences in protein expression levels between the small and large chromosomes in this multichromosomal bacterium were analyzed. This study presents a comprehensive <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M proteome analysis while also providing a platform to aid in the understanding of the molecular mechanisms pertaining to the 104&#160;M vaccine.</p></sec><sec id="Sec2" sec-type="discussion"><title>Results and discussion</title><sec id="Sec3"><title>Proteogenomic strategy for the analysis of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M</title><p>This study incorporated a genome-wide protein identification survey of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M using in vitro culture conditions, thereby facilitating refinement of previous genome annotations. Traditional approaches to proteomics analyses of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> based on two dimensional gel electrophoresis (2-DE) and high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) coupled with mass spectrometry (MS) have proved successful; however, hydrophobic and low abundance proteins are under-represented using this approach [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">14</xref>]. To enhance coverage of the expressed genome, different strategies for subcellular and peptide fractionation were combined with subsequent LC-tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) analysis to resolve <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> proteins for global analysis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">15</xref>]. In the present study, we performed subcellular fractionation prior to protein extraction to reduce interference. Whole cell protein and membrane protein extracts were prepared for strain 104&#160;M. Peptide fractionation prior to analysis is an alternative method that achieves higher proteome coverage. Complexity reduction was achieved by SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) and strong cation exchange (SCX) HPLC separation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>].</p><p>In total, 90 samples were generated and subjected to LC-MS/MS analysis using a linear trap quadrupole (LTQ) Obitrap Velos mass spectrometer. The workflow of this study is summarized in Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig1" ref-type="fig">1</xref>. The raw MS data were searched against two databases: (i) a UniProt protein database (UP000064067) and (ii) a 6&#160;F translated genome database of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M (Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S1) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>]. The search was conducted using SearchGUI and required four different search algorithms (X!Tandem, MS-GF+, Comet and PeptideShaker). Peptides and proteins were inferred from the spectrum identification results using PeptideShaker. Peptide spectrum matches (PSMs), peptides and proteins were validated at a 1.0% false discovery rate (FDR) threshold estimated using the decoy hit distribution (Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S2-S4). The distribution of precursor mass deviations and the identified peptides that were observed following MS analysis are presented in Additional file <xref rid="MOESM2" ref-type="media">2</xref>: Figure S1.<fig id="Fig1" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Fig. 1</label><caption><p>Experimental identification and bioinformatics analysis workflow of the proteogenomics study. Whole cell protein and membrane protein extracts were prepared from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M cultures grown to exponential phase. Complexity reduction was achieved by SDS-PAGE and SCX HPLC separation. After protein extraction and pre-fractionation, enzymatically digested proteins or peptides were analyzed by LC-MS/MS. All spectra were searched against the six-frame (6&#160;F) database and the UniProt database by SearchGUI. Identified peptides were mapped to the 104&#160;M genome and annotation was refined at three levels: (i) confirmation of the existing open reading frame (ORF) models; (ii) refinement of the existing ORF models; and (iii) identification of novel ORF models. Subsequently, the protein physical\chemical property and function were analyzed</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" id="MO1" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="12864_2017_3800_Fig1_HTML.jpg"/></fig>
</p></sec><sec id="Sec4"><title>Analysis of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M protein expression</title><p>We mapped the unique peptides identified in this study to the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M UniProt database (3,072 proteins), and only proteins that were identified by at least two unique peptides were confirmed. A total of 1,729 proteins (1,224 proteins encoded by chromosome 1 and 505 encoded by chromosome 2) were identified, covering approximately 56.3% of the predicted proteome (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig2" ref-type="fig">2</xref>, Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S2). Approximately 85.1% (2.79&#160;Mb) of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genome is protein-coding with annotations in the 104&#160;M database, and the expressed genome corresponded to 63.8% (1.78&#160;Mb) of the annotated protein-coding regions. Each identified protein was represented with an average sequence coverage of 34.9% (Additional file <xref rid="MOESM2" ref-type="media">2</xref>: Figure S1).<fig id="Fig2" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Fig. 2</label><caption><p>Overview of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M protein expression. <bold>a</bold> Overview of the proteomic evidence for predicted protein-coding genes in the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome. <bold>b</bold> Venn diagram of proteins identified by four different fractionation methods: whole SDS-PAGE (whole cell protein with SDS-PAGE fractionation), whole SCX (whole cell protein digestion with SCX fractionation), membrane SDS-PAGE (membrane protein with SDS-PAGE fractionation) and membrane SCX (membrane protein digestion with SCX fractionation). <bold>c</bold> Proteome landscape of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M. </p><p>The proteins were identified following in-gel fractionation (SDS-PAGE), in-solution fractionation (SCX), whole cell protein fractionation and membrane protein fractionation (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig2" ref-type="fig">2b</xref>). Many of the proteins that were uniquely identified in the membrane fractions had relatively extreme grand average of hydropathy (GRAVY) scores (Additional file <xref rid="MOESM2" ref-type="media">2</xref>: Figure S2). Large proteins were more likely to be identified after SCX separation than by SDS-PAGE and approximately all unique proteins with molecular weights (MWs) greater than 90&#160;kDa were identified by SCX (Additional file <xref rid="MOESM2" ref-type="media">2</xref>: Figure S2). This further emphasizes the advantage of membrane fractionation and SCX for protein separation. Lamontagne et al. identified 621 proteins in preliminary work using three protein separation approaches: SDS-PAGE, isoelectric focusing, and off-line 2-D peptide chromatography [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>]. In this study, a combination of subcellular fractionation and peptide fractionation increased the number of membrane and cytoplasmic proteins identifiable by mass spectrometry; This approach provided a significantly higher degree of discrimination and enhanced coverage of the expressed genome.</p></sec><sec id="Sec5"><title>Physical and chemical property distributions of identified proteins</title><p>The proteins identified in this study covered a wide range of MW and isoelectric point (pI) values (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3a</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>]. The theoretical MW distribution for the identified proteins ranged from 4.7&#160;kDa (Entericidin EcnAB protein) to 320.4&#160;kDa (NdvB protein). Moreover, approximately 85.0% (1,469 of 1,729) of all the identified proteins had MWs in the range 10&#8211;60&#160;kDa (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3b</xref>). The pI values of the identified proteins ranged from 3.67 to 11.88. These values are similar to those observed for the total proteome (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3c</xref>). Approximately 67.0% of the proteins identified in the 104&#160;M database had pI values between 6 and 8. Eleven proteins with high pI scores (&#8805;11) and 20 with high MW (&#8805;120&#160;kDa) were beyond the general separation limits of 2-DE.<fig id="Fig3" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Fig. 3</label><caption><p>Distribution of the predicted and identified proteins in the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome according to their molecular function. <bold>a</bold> Distribution of pI values and MW ranges. Distribution of identified protein <bold>b</bold> MW, <bold>c</bold> pI, <bold>d</bold> GRAVY, <bold>e</bold> TMH, <bold>f</bold> subcellular localization and <bold>g</bold> functional classification of proteins identified according to clusters of orthologous groups (COGs). Annotated and identified proteins are shown in <italic toggle="yes">blue</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">red</italic>, respectively</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" id="MO3" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="12864_2017_3800_Fig3_HTML.jpg"/></fig>
</p><p>The GRAVY values for all of the identifications (average &#8722;0.012) were used to evaluate protein hydrophilicity and hydrophobicity (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3d</xref>). A total of 58 proteins were identified with high GRAVY scores (&#8805;0.6); such proteins are so hydrophobic that they are rarely detected by 2-DE due to their susceptibility to precipitation during isoelectric focusing. A total of 301 potential membrane proteins with at least one transmembrane helix (TMH) were identified (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3e</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>]. Among these, the number of predicted TMHs ranged from one to 25. The subcellular localization of identified proteins was predicted using Cello2.5 (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3f</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>]. Two hundred and eighty-three proteins (16.4%) were predicted to be localized to the cytoplasmic membrane and 1,007 (58.2%) of the proteins were identified as cytoplasmic, which is consistent with findings from previous studies [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>]. Each identified protein was classified based on clusters of orthologous groups (COG) and associated with a functional category (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3g</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">21</xref>]. Among the 1,729 identified proteins, 1,570 were annotated in the COG database. Many of the identified proteins were involved in amino acid transport and metabolism, translation, ribosomal structure and biogenesis, energy production and energy conversion. All of these categories contain key functional proteins that are important for cell growth and reproduction.</p></sec><sec id="Sec6"><title>Identification of virulence factors and function analysis</title><p>
<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is unusual because it does not harbor typical virulence factors. Instead, its virulence appears to be an integrated aspect of its physiology, exhibiting a tendency to invade and persist in the human host through inhibition of programmed cell death [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">23</xref>]. As previously stated, the genomic analysis of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M revealed the presence of some virulence associated genes in the 104&#160;M genome which may be related to the vaccine residual toxicity. In this study, we confirmed the presence of 104&#160;M-specific virulence factors of 104&#160;M at the protein level following cell culture. The majority of these factors were observed to be involved in intracellular survival, two-component regulatory systems, and the VirB secretion or transport system (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">24</xref>]. For example, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> cyclic &#946;-1,2-glucan (C&#946;G) is a key virulence factor that interferes with the maturation of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole and consequently, prevents its fusion with lysosomes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">25</xref>]. The enzyme responsible for the synthesis of C&#946;G (Cyclic &#946;-1,2-glucan synthase protein, Cgs) was represented by 54 peptides. Similarly, the WbkA and WbkC proteins, which were represented by six and 14 peptides, respectively, are involved in the synthesis of the O-polysaccharide and its translocation to the periplasm [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">26</xref>]. In addition, the BvrR/BvrS sensory-regulatory system, which plays an important role in the stealth program, was represented by 23 and eight peptides, respectively [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>]. Moreover, T4SS, which is encoded by the Virb operon (Virb8, represented by three peptides), is also a vitally important factor used for the translocation of virulence factors into mammalian cells and is required for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> trafficking diversion [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>]. Finally, in total 14 pivotal virulence factors in 104&#160;M were identified at the protein level following cell culture. </p></sec><sec id="Sec7"><title>Identification of known protective antigens and function analysis</title><p>The efficacy of the 104&#160;M vaccine against brucellosis has been largely confirmed following its widespread application in China; however, the predominant protective mechanisms associated with the vaccine remain to be elucidated. Interestingly, almost all of the widely reported putative <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> protective antigens listed in the Protegen database were observed to harbor high peptide numbers (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">29</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">30</xref>]. For example, 110 peptides derived from the molecular chaperone, DnaK, were identified. This is a member of the highly conserved 70-kDa heat-shock protein (hsp70) family, which assists in folding nascent polypeptide chains and is a known protective antigen [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">31</xref>]. In addition, 37 peptides were identified to be derived from the L7/L12 ribosomal protein, which is a major constituent of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> nucleoprotein fraction used in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> skin tests as a vaccine candidate [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">32</xref>]. Similarly, 54 peptides derived from the antigen periplasmic binding protein P39 were reported. The latter has been reported to be a T-cell immunodominant <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigen that induces a Th1-type immune response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>]. These antigens could promote both humoral and cellular immunity, resulting in the enhancement of host defense mechanisms pertaining to subsequent bacterial infections. According to our results, the average identified peptide number and PSM number was higher for Chr I compared with Chr II (peptide number 14.03&#8201;&gt;&#8201;11.81, Wilcoxon-Rank-Sum test <italic toggle="yes">p</italic>-value&#8201;&lt;&#8201;0.01; PSM number 128.9&#8201;&gt;&#8201;101.6, Wilcoxon-Rank-Sum test <italic toggle="yes">p</italic>-value&#8201;&lt;&#8201;0.01, Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig6" ref-type="fig">6a</xref>).<fig id="Fig6" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Fig. 6</label><caption><p>Large Chromosome and Small Chromosome of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M exhibit differences in protein expression. <bold>a</bold> Comparison of protein abundance (peptide numbers and PSM numbers) between the small and large chromosomes of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M. <bold>b</bold> Range of protein abundance expressed by the small and large chromosomes listed in NSAF order (highest level at the top of the list). <bold>c</bold> Model of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> cell cycle. Replication of Chr I is initiated before Chr II. <bold>d</bold> Distribution of COGs within two different chromosomes of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" id="MO6" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="12864_2017_3800_Fig6_HTML.jpg"/></fig>
</p><p>Since larger proteins would be expected to generate more peptides and, therefore, more spectral counts than smaller proteins, we used normalized spectral abundance factors (NSAF) for comparison of relative quantitation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR48">48</xref>]. The NSAF values for each detected protein are presented in Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S2. After comparing protein abundance between the two chromosomes using the NSAF values, we observed that the relative abundance of proteins pertaining to Chr I was generally higher compared with Chr II (6.364&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>&#8722;4</sup>&#8201;&gt;&#8201;4.38&#8201;&#215;&#8201;10<sup>&#8722;4</sup>, Wilcoxon-Rank-Sum test <italic toggle="yes">p</italic>-value&#8201;&lt;&#8201;0.01). The differential protein abundance between the two chromosomes in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M was considered to be statistically significant, which may reflect the differential protein expression between the two bacterial chromosomes.</p><p>Proteins were ranked from highest to lowest according to the NSAF values (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig6" ref-type="fig">6b</xref>). If the expression levels of proteins located on the large and small chromosomes are randomly distributed, then the expected ratio of Chr II proteins would be 1,071/3,072 (or 34.9%) in any arbitrarily selected range. However, according to our results, the actual proportions of Chr II proteins (505/1729, 29.2%) were lower than expected for the upper range of expression levels, except for those proteins with the highest expression levels. For example, of the proteins with expression levels ranging from 1 to 500, only 95 proteins (19%) were located on Chr II. The protein expression levels pertaining to Chr I were generally higher compared with Chr II.</p><p>It is well known that the replication of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> genome occurs concomitantly with cell growth and division. The origin and terminator regions of multichromosomes in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> have distinct dynamics and localization patterns, whereby the large chromosome is replicated prior to the small chromosome (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig6" ref-type="fig">6c</xref>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>]. In addition, the replication of chromosomes results in an increase in the copy number of genes located near origins of replication compared with genes located near termini. This may be a potential reason for the differential protein expression between the large and small chromosome. Moreover, an analysis conducted to elucidate the COG categories of the 500 most highly expressed genes in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M suggests that the proteins associated with rapid cell growth are expressed at relatively high levels. The genes encoding for these proteins were over-represented on the large chromosome and concurrently under-represented on the small chromosome (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig6" ref-type="fig">6d</xref>).</p><p>We also analyzed the GC composition and codon usage between the two chromosomes in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> that may affect translation or protein expression. The results showed that there was no significant difference in GC-content (57.2%/57.3%) and codon usage between the two chromosomes (date shown in Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S8) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. The potential reason for differential protein expression may be related to the replication dynamics and gene function differences of the two chromosomes in this multichromosomal bacterium. This study is the first to report differences in protein expression levels in two chromosomes in this multichromosomal bacterium and is in accordance with a similar pattern reported for <italic toggle="yes">Vibrio cholera</italic> following transcriptome analysis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">49</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">50</xref>].</p></sec></sec><sec id="Sec11"><title>Conclusions</title><p>Brucellosis is a significant economic and public health problem worldwide. The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M vaccine strain has played an important role in the prevention and control of brucellosis in China. In this study, we used a proteogenomics approach that combined subcellular fractionation and peptide fractionation to perform whole-proteome analysis and genome reannotation in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M. In total, 1,729 proteins (56.3% of 3072), including 218 hypothetical proteins, were identified under the culture conditions given in this study. As part of this analysis, 14 pivotal virulence factors, 17 known protective antigens and other important protein effectors that are related to bacterial virulence and vaccine protection were confirmed. These data represents a dataset of proteins identified following growth of 104&#160;M under conventional culture conditions. It is possible that some coding sequences did not express under the conditions chosen in this study. Analysis of differential proteome expression patterns of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> in other culture conditions can serve as a starting point in the discovery of protein determinants associated with pathogen adaptation and pathogenesis. These differentially expressed genes potentially promote pathogen adaptation to more complex in vivo environments and, therefore, require further study.</p><p>In this study, we also validated the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome annotation and re-annotated mis-annotated ORFs; validation improved the quality of the genome annotation. Six novel ORFs and three modified existing ORFs were also identified as part of this study. We confirmed the annotated TIS for 29 genes through N-terminal acetylation analysis. Furthermore, after comparing protein abundance using NSAF values, we observed differential protein expression between the two bacterial chromosomes in in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M. The potential reason for this differential expression may be related to differences in the replication dynamics and gene function pertaining to the two chromosomes; further work is required to verify this hypothesis. In conclusion, this study represents a comprehensive proteogenomics analysis of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M and is likely to improve our understanding of the mechanisms that underpin residual toxicity and vaccine-induced immune protection.</p></sec><sec id="Sec12"><title>Methods</title><sec id="Sec13"><title>Cell culture and protein extraction</title><p>The 104&#160;M strain was grown in tryptic soy broth (TSB) and harvested in the early exponential phase. Whole cell protein samples were prepared as described previously with some modifications [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>]. Briefly, cells harvested by centrifugation (7,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, 15&#160;min at 4&#160;&#176;C) were washed followed by resuspension in lysis buffer and disruption by ultra-sonication (25% amplitude, 15&#160;min at 0&#160;&#176;C). The resultant suspension was centrifuged (40,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, 30&#160;min at 18&#160;&#176;C) and the protein concentrations in the collected supernatants were measured using a BCA protein assay kit (Thermo Fischer Scientific, Waltham, USA).</p><p>Total membrane protein samples were also prepared using Triton X-114 phase-separation as described previously [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]. In brief, harvested cells were lysed by bead beating and then centrifuged (7,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, 15&#160;min at 4&#160;&#176;C) to remove insoluble material. Triton X-114 (2%&#160;v/v concentration) was added to the supernatant and then stirred (10&#160;min at 4&#160;&#176;C) to obtain the protein extract in a single phase. This mixture was then centrifuged (15,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, 10&#160;min at 4&#160;&#176;C) to remove residual insoluble matter and incubated at 37&#160;&#176;C for 10&#160;min. Subsequently, the solution was separated into upper (aqueous) and lower (detergent) phases. The detergent phase was collected and proteins were precipitated by acetone. Protein samples were stored at &#8722;80&#160;&#176;C for further analysis.</p></sec><sec id="Sec14"><title>Protein pre-fractionation and in-gel proteolytic digestion</title><p>Whole cell protein and membrane protein extracts were resolved by 12% SDS polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, stained with Coomassie blue R250 and excised into gel slices, which were then subjected to an in-gel tryptic digestion protocol [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]. In brief, protein-containing lanes were excised from the gels based on molecular weight and local protein amount. Each band was placed into an Eppendorf tube and destained with 30% acetonitrile and 35&#160;mM ammonium bicarbonate until colorless. Gel pieces were treated with pure acetonitrile and dried under vacuum. Sufficient ammonium bicarbonate (at a final concentration of 50&#160;mM) was added to each tube to cover the gel pieces. Trypsin was subsequently added and the tubes were placed on ice for 40&#160;min. All tubes were then incubated at 37&#160;&#176;C for 12&#160;h and centrifuged (12,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;<italic toggle="yes">g</italic>, 1&#160;min). Peptides were extracted from each supernatant as follows: 50&#160;&#956;l extraction buffer (5% formic acid, 50% acetonitrile) was added and the resultant mixture was centrifuged (12,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;g, 1&#160;min). Following incubation for 4&#160;min at room temperature, all supernatants were collected into new tubes. Acetonitrile (100%) was added to each tube and these mixtures were mixed by vortexing for 20&#160;min. Following centrifugation (12,000&#8201;&#215;&#8201;g, 1&#160;min), the resultant mixtures were incubated for 4&#160;min at room temperature; the supernatants were subsequently collected. The resultant peptides were dried under vacuum and stored at &#8722;80&#160;&#176;C until further use.</p></sec><sec id="Sec15"><title>In-solution proteolytic digestion and peptide pre-fractionation</title><p>Whole cell protein and membrane protein extracts were reduced (20&#160;min at 25&#160;&#176;C) in 1&#160;mM dithiothreitol and then alkylated in 5.5&#160;mM iodoacetamide (15&#160;min at 25&#160;&#176;C in the dark) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>]. Trypsin was added (1:50, w/w) and proteins were digested in solution overnight before samples were separated by SCX performed on a Dionex UltiMate 3000 (Thermo Fischer Scientific). Tryptic peptides were loaded onto a PolySULFOETHYL A (PolyLC, Columbia, MD, USA) column and eluted with a linear gradient using ACN/potassium phosphate buffers. After the elution, flow-through fractions were collected into fresh Eppendorf tubes. Adjacent fractions were desalted by C18 reversed-phase spin columns, separated into aliquots containing 150&#160;&#956;g peptides, dried under vacuum and stored at &#8722;80&#160;&#176;C until further use.</p></sec><sec id="Sec16"><title>LC-MS/MS analysis</title><p>The nanoAcquity Ultra Performance LC system (Waters Corporation, Milford, USA) was used for peptide separation equipped with a C18 reversed-phase microcapillary trapping (Maisch, Ammerbuch-Entringen, Germany) as described previously [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">53</xref>]. A total of 90 samples (30 samples conducted in triplicate) were loaded and eluted for 40&#160;min using a 5&#8211;40% CAN fraction-optimized nonlinear gradient in 0.1% formic acid. Eluted peptides were analyzed using a LTQ Obitrap Velos mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) as previously described [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]. The eluted peptides were electrosprayed with a distally applied spray voltage of 2.0&#160;kV. The mass spectrometry analysis was carried out in a data-dependent manner. Survey scans were performed at a resolution of 30,000 at target values of 1,000,000 ions in the Orbitrap analyzer with maximum allowed fill times of 150&#160;ms over a mass range of 300&#8211;1,600&#160;m/z. Finally, the 20 most intense precursor ions were chosen for MS/MS fragmentation by collision-induced fragmentation. The normalized collision energy for the MS/MS was set to 30%, and the transfer tube temperature was maintained at 220&#160;&#176;C. Exclusion of precursor ion masses over a time window of 30&#160;s was used to suppress repeated fragmentation of peaks.</p></sec><sec id="Sec17"><title>Database construction and data processing</title><p>The protein database used for MS/MS searches was constructed from the UniProt entries for <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M and comprised of 3,072 proteins. A six reading frame (6&#160;F) database was constructed based on all six possible ORFs in the 104&#160;M genome using the &#8220;getorf&#8221; program in EMBOSS, with all sequences between two stop codons regarded as a protein in this database [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR54">54</xref>]. The 6&#160;F database comprised 115,420 candidate coding sequences for all of the possible proteins (longer than 20 amino acids) encoded by the genome. Peak lists obtained from MS/MS spectra were identified using X! Tandem, MS-GF+, Comet and PeptideShaker. The identification was conducted using SearchGUI [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR55">55</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR57">57</xref>]. The decoy sequences were created by reversing the 104&#160;M UniProt database. The Enzyme specificity was set to trypsin and a maximum of two missed cleavages were allowed. The initial maximal was set to 20.0&#160;ppm as MS1 and 0.5&#160;Da as MS2 tolerances. The fixed modifications carbamidomethylation of C, variable modifications oxidation of M and acetylation of protein N-term were used. Protein identification was conducted against a concatenated target-decoy version of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M complement of the UniProt database. PeptideShaker provides statistical confidence estimates for each peptide and protein, taking into account protein inference issues. PSMs, peptides, and proteins were validated at a 1.0% FDR using the decoy hit distribution. All of the raw mass spectra files along with the identification results have been deposited into the ProteomeXchange Consortium via the PRIDE partner repository with the dataset identifier PXD005403 and are now available [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR58">58</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR59">59</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="Sec18"><title>Bioinformatics tools for the prediction of proteins</title><p>The analysis of unique proteins identified by different proteomics strategies was conducted using Patternlab4.0 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR60">60</xref>]. The venn diagram and circular map of the genome were drawn using jvenn and Circos [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR61">61</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR62">62</xref>]. The theoretical molecular mass and pI, and GRAVY values were predicted using the ExPASy-ProtParam tool [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>]. Protein transmembrane helices were predicted using TMHMM 2.0 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>]. The subcellular localization was predicted using the Cello2.5 program [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>]. The COGs classification system was employed to examine the distribution of different gene categories [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">21</xref>]. The protective antigens GO analyses were performed using the Blast2Go online tool [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>]. The protective antigens probability scores were predicted using the VaxiJen2.0 server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>]. Antigens were specified as pathogenic or nonpathogenic by the MP3 server [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>]. The conservation of new virulence factors and, protective antigens were assessed at both nucleotide and predicted amino acid levels between 18 different <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strains, using ClustalOmega [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR63">63</xref>]. The pfam protein family database was used to generate a complete and accurate classification of protein families and domains [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">42</xref>]. All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism (GraphPad Software, La Jolla, CA, USA).</p></sec><sec id="Sec19"><title>Proteogenomic workflow and bioinformatics analysis</title><p>All spectra were searched against the 6&#160;F database [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>]. The detected peptides (1% FDR) and the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M genome with associated annotations were imported into the VESPA proteogenomic program [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>]. Peptides were mapped to the 104&#160;M genome sequence and a set of unique peptides that did not match the annotated proteins of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 104&#160;M (orphan peptides) were identified and then mapped to the unique locations on the 104&#160;M genome. After the analysis, the annotations of existing ORF models were modified and novel ORFs were identified. All annotation identifications and corrections were assigned on the basis of at least two unique orphan peptides.</p></sec><sec id="Sec20"><title>RT-PCR validation</title><p>The novel ORF models and errors identified in this study were validated at the transcriptional level by RT-PCR as previously described [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR64">64</xref>]. The total RNA was extracted using the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Valencia, CA) and then RNase-free DNase was added to remove any contaminating genomic DNA. The cDNA synthesis was performed using the QuantiTect Reverse Transcription Kit (Qiagen). For the PCR reaction, the novel ORF models and errors cDNA was used as a template, while RNA and <italic toggle="yes">groEL</italic> cDNA were used as negative and positive controls, respectively. The gene-specific primers in this study are listed in Additional file <xref rid="MOESM1" ref-type="media">1</xref>: Table S9.</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="supplementary-material"><title>Additional files</title><sec id="Sec21"><p>
