      <abstract id="ABS1">
        <p id="P2">The BCG can elicit enhanced innate immune responses against a wide range of infections, known as trained immunity. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> is the causative agent of brucellosis, a debilitating disease that affects humans and animals. In this study, we demonstrate that C57BL/6 mouse bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) under BCG training enhance inflammatory responses against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus.</italic> BCG-trained macrophages showed increased MHC-II and CD40 expression on the cell surface and higher IL-6, IL-12 and IL-1&#x3B2; production. The increase in IL-1&#x3B2; secretion was accompanied by enhanced activation of canonical and non-canonical inflammasome platforms. We observed elevated caspase-11 expression and caspase-1 processing in BCG-trained macrophages in response to <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> compared to untrained cells. In addition, these BCG-trained cells showed higher NLRP3 expression after <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> infection. From a metabolic point of view, signaling through the Akt/mTOR/S6K pathway was also enhanced. Additionally, BCG training resulted in higher iNOS expression and nitrite production, culminating in an improved macrophage-killing capacity against intracellular <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>. <italic toggle="yes">In vivo</italic>, we monitored a significant reduction in the bacterial burden in organs from BCG-trained C57BL/6 mice when compared to the untrained group. Additionally, previous BCG-immunization of RAG<sup>&#x2212;/&#x2212;</sup> mice partially protects against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection, suggesting the important role of the innate immune compartment in this scenario. Furthermore, naive recipient mice that received BM transfer (BMT) from BCG-trained donors showed greater resistance to <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> when compared to their untrained counterparts. These results demonstrate that BCG-induced trained immunity in mice results in better control of intracellular <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus in vivo</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic>.</p>
