In these natural hosts, brucellae display a tropism towards the reproductive organs, such as the placenta, replicating in high numbers and leading to placentitis and abortion, an ability also exerted by the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> live-attenuated Rev1 strain, the only vaccine available for ovine brucellosis. It is broadly accepted that this tropism is mediated, at least in part, by the presence of certain preferred nutrients in the placenta, particularly erythritol, a polyol that is ultimately incorporated into the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> central carbon metabolism via two reactions dependent on transaldolase (Tal) or fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba). In the light of these remarks, we propose that blocking the incorporation of erythritol into the central carbon metabolism of Rev1 by deleting the genes encoding Tal and Fba may impair the ability of the vaccine to proliferate massively in the placenta. Therefore, a Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> double mutant was generated and confirmed to be unable to use erythritol. This mutant exhibited a reduced intracellular fitness both in BeWo trophoblasts and THP-1 macrophages. In the murine model, Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> provided comparable protection to the Rev1 reference vaccine while inducing fewer adverse reproductive events in pregnant animals. Altogether, these results postulate the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> mutant as a reproductively safer Rev1-derived vaccine candidate to be studied in the natural host.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic></kwd><kwd>Rev1</kwd><kwd>vaccine</kwd><kwd>erythritol</kwd><kwd>Fba</kwd><kwd>Tal</kwd><kwd>trophoblasts</kwd><kwd>pregnancy safety</kwd><kwd>abortion</kwd><kwd>placenta</kwd></kwd-group><funding-group><award-group><funding-source>MCIN/AEI/10.1303910.13039/501100011033</funding-source><award-id>PID2023-146797OB-C31</award-id><award-id>PID2023-146797OB-C32</award-id><award-id>PID2019-107601RB-C31</award-id><award-id>PID2019-107601RA-C32</award-id></award-group><award-group><funding-source>postdoctoral contract under the REPRODIVAC project</funding-source><award-id>10106081</award-id></award-group><award-group><funding-source>Arag&#243;n Government</funding-source><award-id>Grupo de Investigaci&#243;n A21_23R</award-id></award-group><funding-statement>This research was carried out in the frame of projects PID2023-146797OB-C31, PID2023-146797OB-C32, PID2019-107601RB-C31 and PID2019-107601RA-C32 financed by MCIN/AEI/10.1303910.13039/501100011033. Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is a widespread zoonosis caused by bacteria belonging to the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, a group of facultative intracellular bacteria that includes several species with different host-preferences. Among them, the most relevant species, the so-called classical <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp., infect domestic ruminants (<italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>, sheep and goats; <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, cattle; and <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic>, sheep), swine (<italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovars 1&#8211;3), and dogs (<italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>) [<xref rid="B1-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>,<xref rid="B2-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="B3-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>].</p><p>In these natural hosts, the pathology features diverse reproductive symptoms related to the development of placentitis: reproductive failure with abortions/stillbirths and birth of weak offspring; infertility [<xref rid="B4-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>,<xref rid="B5-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="B6-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="B7-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]; and reduced milk production due to infection and inflammation of mammary glands [<xref rid="B7-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B9-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>,<xref rid="B10-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>]. In this epizootiological context, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is the most relevant species, as it is the most common cause of human infection, which can be acquired through direct contact with infected animals or consumption of unpasteurized milk products. This way, brucellosis not only represents a public health problem but also leads to important economic losses due to the reduction of livestock productivity, contributing to poverty in resource-limited regions [<xref rid="B11-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>,<xref rid="B12-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>]. Nonetheless, human-to-human transmission is anecdotal, and control of the disease relies on animal vaccination, for which the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> live-attenuated Rev1 strain is the only vaccine recommended by the World Organization for Animal Health for small ruminants [<xref rid="B13-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>,<xref rid="B14-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>]. Unfortunately, the Rev1 vaccine exerts several drawbacks, the most notable of which is its abortifacient effect when administered to pregnant animals. In endemic regions, such as parts of Africa and Asia, mass vaccination is the only viable strategy for controlling brucellosis [<xref rid="B13-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>]. However, in these areas, where sheep and goats breed all year round rather than seasonally (impairing the application of the vaccine in non-gestating periods), the use of Rev1 could lead to substantial economic losses due to abortion-related reproductive failures. There is therefore a clear need for improved reproductively safe <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines that will help to control the disease in endemic areas.</p><p>The development of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reproductive pathology is caused by the pronounced tropism of brucellae for the genital organs, among them the placenta, where brucellae replicate in exceedingly high numbers [<xref rid="B15-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>,<xref rid="B16-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B17-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>,<xref rid="B18-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>,<xref rid="B19-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. This active replication requires a very efficient use of the substrates available at the placental microenvironment. Among them, erythritol is thought to be one of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-preferred carbon sources during infection [<xref rid="B20-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>,<xref rid="B21-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>,<xref rid="B22-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B23-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>,<xref rid="B24-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S19 vaccine, the other vaccine recommended by the World Organization for Animal Health but for cattle [<xref rid="B13-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>,<xref rid="B14-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>], is an attenuated strain that carries, among other modifications, a deletion in the erythritol catabolic operon affecting both <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> [<xref rid="B25-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. This deletion renders the S19 strain unable to use erythritol, an aspect that was initially related to the lower abortion induction of the vaccine [<xref rid="B24-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>,<xref rid="B26-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>]. However, further studies that restored both <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> showed that this did not restore the virulence of the strain [<xref rid="B27-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. This phenomenon may be explained by the other unknown genetic modifications of the S19 strain or the existence of a more complex metabolic scenario where erythritol is just one of several relevant substrates at the reproductive niche, as it is thought in the case of glycerol, lactate, and glutamate [<xref rid="B28-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>].</p><p>In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, erythritol is converted into erythrose-4-phosphate through a five-step pathway and introduced into the Pentose Phosphate pathway via the transaldolase- (Tal) and two transketolase (Tkt)-catalyzed reactions [<xref rid="B29-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>] (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). In this erythritol catabolic pathway, the first enzyme (the kinase EryA) carries an ATP-dependent phosphorylation that is futile when the downstream pathway is disrupted (i.e., mutations in <italic toggle="yes">eryB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">eryH</italic>, or <italic toggle="yes">eryI</italic>), resulting in growth inhibition caused by ATP depletion [<xref rid="B30-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>]. In addition, in a recent publication, we described that the fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba) exerts sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate phosphatase activity in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513 (biovar 5), allowing a more efficient use of the abundant erythritol in the placenta and other reproductive organs [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>].</p><p>Bearing the above in mind, we propose that blocking the incorporation of erythritol-derived erythrose-4-phosphate into the central carbon metabolism of Rev1 by deleting the genes encoding the transaldolase (Tal) and the fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba) may impair the ability of the vaccine to proliferate massively in the placenta, thereby preventing the induction of abortion in pregnant ewes [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. Therefore, in this work, we evaluated the impact of deleting <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> in the Rev1 vaccine background to develop a safer Rev1 vaccine.</p></sec><sec sec-type="results" id="sec2-ijms-25-11230"><title>2. Results and Discussion</title><sec id="sec2dot1-ijms-25-11230"><title>2.1. Dysfunction of Fba and Tal Abrogates Rev1 Growth on Erythritol</title><p>The analysis of the Rev1 genome showed a high conservation of <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> (99.7% identity in both cases) when compared to <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513, the strain where the Fba-bypass on erythritol catabolism was reported [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. At the protein level, these sequence differences translated into minor amino acidic changes, namely [V156A] in Fba and [Y199D] in Tal between Rev1 and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513, that do not extend to the predicted functional regions of Fba or the described functional regions of Tal, according to the UniProt database [<xref rid="B33-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>]. Hence, we employed the genetic tools generated in our previous work [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>] to construct the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>, Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic>, and Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> mutants.</p><p>Growth studies of the mutant strains in rich medium showed no effect of single <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> mutation on the growth of Rev1, consistent with previous findings in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513 [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>], and an impaired bacterial growth in the case of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> and Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). Intriguingly, the <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> single mutant exhibited shorter generation times and lower growth yields than the double mutant Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal.</italic> While the <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> mutant phenotype is likely due to Rev1&#8217;s reliance on the Pentose Phosphate pathway as the only route for hexose metabolism [<xref rid="B34-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>], the reduced doubling time of the <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>-<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> mutant compared to Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> may be due to metabolic rearrangements caused by the absence of two key enzymes in the central carbon metabolism. In the case of growth on erythritol as a carbon source, only the double mutation on <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> completely ablated the growth of Rev1 (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>), with the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> growth curves showing a longer lag phase not detected for the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> mutant, a phenomenon also observed for <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513 [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>]. These findings confirm the indispensability of both <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> for the growth of Rev1 on erythritol and support the existence of the newly described erythritol Fba-bypass [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>] in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, although further studies on other model <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are required to reach more robust conclusions on the genus.</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot2-ijms-25-11230"><title>2.2. Rev1&#916;fba&#916;tal Is Attenuated in Trophoblastic and Macrophagic Cellular Models</title><p>Once we validated the incapability of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> to grow on erythritol, we continued with the characterization of this double mutant as a possible vaccine candidate. As a first approach to assess the residual virulence of the strain, we carried out cell infections in BeWo human trophoblasts and THP-1 human macrophage-like cells, representative cell types of brucellae-preferential host cells upon infection [<xref rid="B8-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="B35-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>,<xref rid="B36-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>,<xref rid="B37-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>].</p><p>In BeWo, Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> showed virtually no increase in the number of intracellular CFUs during the first 24 h post-infection (p.i.) but a doubling time similar to that of Rev1 between the 24&#8211;48 h p.i. time-point, although reaching lower total CFU-values (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>, left panel). Similar results were observed in THP-1, with Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> showing a faintly increased intracellular killing compared to Rev1 at 24 h p.i. and slightly lower replication levels than the Rev1 during the 24&#8211;48 h p.i. period. (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>, right panel).</p><p>These in vitro findings are in line with previous publications that found attenuation of erythritol catabolic mutants in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in human and murine cell lines. Barbier et al. showed that mutation of EryI, downstream of the first EryA kinase, resulted in attenuation of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 in BeWo and THP-1 cells [<xref rid="B38-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], probably due to the previously described EryA-mediated ATP depletion toxicity [<xref rid="B30-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>], a hypothesis that would also explain the attenuation profiles observed for <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 1330 <italic toggle="yes">eryB</italic>- and <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic>-mutants in THP-1 cells or J774A.1 murine macrophages in studies prior to that of Barbier et al. [<xref rid="B39-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>]. Similarly, a <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 strain mutated in the erythritol operon promoter showed a mild attenuation in RAW264.7 mouse macrophages [<xref rid="B41-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. In this case, the results may not be as clear-cut since the deleted region could also contain the promoter of a series of genes encoded on the opposite strand. Additionally, the attenuation observed may be explained by its sensitivity to erythritol. Several years ago, erythritol was not detected in measurable quantities in the placentas of humans or mice [<xref rid="B22-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. However, Barbier et al. later found that, while this polyol is present, it is not essential for the multiplication of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> in human and murine trophoblastic and macrophage-like cells or in the spleens and conceptuses of mice [<xref rid="B38-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>], which may account for a more complex metabolic scenario in which other preferred nutrients for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, such as glycerol, lactate, or glutamate, also play a role in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> pathogenesis [<xref rid="B28-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot3-ijms-25-11230"><title>2.3. Rev1&#916;fba&#916;tal Protects at a Level Comparable to the Rev1 Vaccine in the Murine Model</title><p>After the <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic> screening in cell cultures, we studied the attenuation of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> in the murine virulence model. For this purpose, we intraperitoneally infected BALB/c mice with 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> or Rev1 and determined the splenic bacterial loads at one and five weeks p.i. (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f004" ref-type="fig">Figure 4</xref>). Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> showed a slightly lower splenic bacterial recovery when compared to Rev1 at 1 week p.i., while the splenic CFU counts of the mutant at 5 weeks p.i. were similar to those of the Rev1 control (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f004" ref-type="fig">Figure 4</xref>).</p><p>The results regarding the effect of mutations in erythritol catabolic enzymes are diverse. While <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 1330 &#916;<italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> or <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 &#916;<italic toggle="yes">eryA</italic> and &#916;<italic toggle="yes">eryH</italic> mutants were attenuated at early days of infection in mice, only the <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> mutant was attenuated at 56 days p.i. in BALB/C mice [<xref rid="B40-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>], with the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 mutants showing no attenuation in C57BL/6 mice at 30 days p.i. [<xref rid="B38-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. On top of this, the study carried out by Zhang et al. found that mutation of the erythritol operon promoter greatly impacted the virulence in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 in BALB/c mice in both acute and chronic phases of infection [<xref rid="B41-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>], although as mentioned, other possible effects due to the position of the deleted region need to be considered.</p><p>Nonetheless, despite this issue regarding solely the effect of erythritol use on virulence, it is still intriguing to find such an absence of attenuation for a mutant in enzymes also involved in key reactions of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> central carbon metabolism, suggesting potential compensating metabolic rearrangements able to restore bacterial fitness in vivo or the existence of a complex in vivo metabolic microenvironment in the spleen that renders the absence of these enzymes negligible. An example of these issues can be found in a recent work that describes an alternative pathway for the catabolism of erythritol in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 via erythronate, an oxidative product of erythritol in the host [<xref rid="B42-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>]. In this work, the erythronate metabolic pathway was shown to cooperate with erythritol catabolism, playing an important role in pathogenesis. This erythritol derivate is catabolized to dihydroxyacetone-phosphate by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and then fueled into the central carbon metabolism via Fba. In this regard, our Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> vaccine candidate would exert an impact on the ability of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to utilize erythritol through not only the classical erythritol catabolic pathway but also through the recently described <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> erythronate pathway.</p><p>Once we evaluated the virulence of the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> vaccine candidate in the spleen of mice, we were encouraged to investigate its protective capacity. Hence, we vaccinated mice subcutaneously with 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/mouse of our candidate or the Rev1 control and, four weeks after vaccination, challenged the mice with a virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> H38 strain. We observed that the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> vaccine candidate protected mice from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> H38 infection at the same level than the Rev1 vaccine and showed a reduced residual virulence when compared to Rev1 (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>).</p><p>Regarding protection, the study by Zhang et al. showed that BALB/c mice IP-vaccinated with 10<sup>6</sup> CFU/mouse of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 erythritol promoter mutant exhibited a higher protective efficacy than the S19 vaccine control at two and four weeks post-challenge [<xref rid="B41-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. Although this was not the case in our study, our Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> vaccine candidate protected mice against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> H38 infection, prompting the study of the reproductive safety of the vaccine candidate.</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot4-ijms-25-11230"><title>2.4. Rev1&#916;fba&#916;tal Is Reproductively Safer than the Rev1 Vaccine Strain in the Murine Model</title><p>Taking into account that <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> deletion did not affect Rev1 protection capacity and bearing in mind that the final goal of this work was to obtain a reproductively safe vaccine, we investigated the behavior of the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> mutant in pregnant mice. To this end, we evaluated the abortifacient effect of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> in pregnant mice, a model recently reviewed [<xref rid="B43-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>]. Briefly, we infected 8-day-pregnant CD-1 mice with 10<sup>7</sup> CFU/mouse of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> or the Rev1 control vaccine and evaluated the pregnancy outcome.</p><p>In the mentioned review, we highlighted the need of a more informative reporting of adverse pregnancy outcomes in the pregnant mouse model of reproductive brucellosis, making emphasis not only on fetal viability but also on the effect of infection on other parameters, such as litter sizes [<xref rid="B43-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>]. In accordance, in this experiment, we found not only a differential effect on fetal viability but also on the litter sizes of mice infected with Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> or the Rev1 vaccine control. To report this second effect on mouse pregnancy, we defined the &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">Pregnancy Index</italic>&#8221; as the proportion of fetuses that reach pregnancy term, whether viable or not, with respect to the PBS group (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). Then, to integrate both effects on mouse pregnancy into a single gestational indicator, we also defined the &#8220;<italic toggle="yes">Gestational Success Index</italic>&#8221; as the proportion of fetuses that reach pregnancy term in a viable status with respect to the PBS group (i.e., the combined probability of the <italic toggle="yes">Pregnancy Index</italic> and the <italic toggle="yes">Fetal Viability</italic>) (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>), which may be a more complete indicator to describe the effects of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection on mouse pregnancy. Using the previously described parameters, we observed that the pups from the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> group showed an improved <italic toggle="yes">Fetal Viability</italic> when compared to the Rev1 control (89% vs. 6%, respectively; <xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). Likewise, the dams infected with the mutant strain delivered larger litter sizes, resulting in an improved <italic toggle="yes">Pregnancy Index</italic> (72% vs. 57%, respectively; <xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). This way, the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> effect on the pregnancy outcome led to an improved <italic toggle="yes">Gestational Success Index</italic> of 66%, while the Rev1-infected group yielded a <italic toggle="yes">Gestational Success</italic> of 6% (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-t002" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>).</p><p>Taken together, the results obtained both in vitro and in vivo show that Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> is a vaccine candidate with a comparable level of protection to that of the Rev1 reference vaccine but with a significantly decreased abortifacient effect, highlighting the role of placental erythritol in the development of placentitis and abortion. The approach of reducing erythritol catabolism has been recently proven effective with a Rev1 mutant strain carrying the same genomic deletion found in the S19 vaccine, displaying a reduced capacity to colonize and induce damage to the reproductive system of male goats [<xref rid="B44-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>]. As previously mentioned, the main novelty of this approach lies in the fact that the mutation of both <italic toggle="yes">fba</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> avoids alternative erythritol utilization pathways, such as the erythronate one [<xref rid="B42-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>]. Likewise, the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> would also carry an impaired ability to used gluconeogenic substrates [<xref rid="B31-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>], such as glycerol, lactate, or glutamate (<xref rid="ijms-25-11230-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>), the three of them also present preferentially in reproductive organs and proposed as <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-preferred carbon sources together with erythritol [<xref rid="B28-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>].</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="conclusions" id="sec3-ijms-25-11230"><title>3. Conclusions</title><p>In summary, a Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> double mutant was generated lacking the fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba) and transaldolase (Tal) enzymes responsible of the final incorporation of erythritol through erythrose-4-P into the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> central carbon metabolism. Thus, Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> was unable to use erythritol as the sole carbon source. The vaccine candidate showed a reduced intracellular fitness in BeWo trophoblasts and THP-1 macrophages when compared to the Rev1 control, while in the murine model, persisted and provided comparable protection to the Rev1 reference vaccine while inducing fewer adverse reproductive events in pregnant mice, giving the Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> vaccine candidate significant improvements over the current Rev1 vaccine.</p><p>Altogether, this work provides a new basis for the development of safer <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines by suppressing their ability to massively replicate in the placenta, which could also be applied to other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. The final validation of Rev1&#916;<italic toggle="yes">fba</italic>&#916;<italic toggle="yes">tal</italic> in the natural host, in terms of residual virulence, protection, and reproductive safety, will help to verify this Rev1-derived candidate as a new improved vaccine against brucellosis with a lower abortifacient potential, which may facilitate the successful pursuing of mass vaccination campaigns in endemic areas.</p></sec><sec id="sec4-ijms-25-11230"><title>4. Materials and Methods</title><sec id="sec4dot1-ijms-25-11230"><title>4.1. Bacterial Strains and Plasmids</title><p>The bacterial strains and plasmids used in this work are listed in <xref rid="app1-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="app">Tables S1 and S2</xref> [<xref rid="B45-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>,<xref rid="B46-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>,<xref rid="B47-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>,<xref rid="B48-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>,<xref rid="B49-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>,<xref rid="B50-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>], respectively. All brucellae were handled under BSL-3 containment.</p></sec><sec id="sec4dot2-ijms-25-11230"><title>4.2. Culture Conditions</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains were routinely grown on solid or filtration-de-agarized BAB2 (Blood Agar Base No. 2; Oxoid, CM0271B) and <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> strains on agar-supplemented TSB (i.e., TSA; Tryptic Soy Broth, Scharlau; European Bacteriological Agar, Condalab) at 37 &#176;C. DNA Manipulations</title><p>Genomic sequences of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 513 and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 were obtained from the databases National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG). Searches for DNA and protein homologies were carried out using NCBI BLAST [<xref rid="B52-ijms-25-11230" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>]. Plasmid and chromosomal DNA were extracted with QIAprep<sup>&#174;</sup> Spin Miniprep and QIAamp<sup>&#174;</sup> DNA Mini Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany), respectively.</p></sec><sec id="sec4dot4-ijms-25-11230"><title>4.4.
