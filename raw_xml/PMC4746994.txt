However, the precise effects of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> on trophoblast cells remain unclear. Here, we describe the infection and replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> vaccine strain <italic toggle="yes">2</italic> (<italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>) in goat trophoblast cells (GTCs) and the cellular and molecular responses induced <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic>. Our studies demonstrated that <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> was able to infect and proliferate to high titers, hamper the proliferation of GTCs and induce apoptosis due to ER stress. Tunicamycin (Tm), a pharmacological chaperone that strongly mounts ER stress-induced apoptosis, inhibited <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> replication in GTCs. In addition, 4 phenyl butyric acid (4-PBA), a pharmacological chaperone that alleviates ER stress-induced apoptosis, significantly enhanced <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> replication in GTCs. The Unfolded Protein Response (UPR) chaperone molecule GRP78 also promoted <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> proliferation in GTCs by inhibiting ER stress-induced apoptosis. We also discovered that the IRE1 pathway, but not the PERK or ATF6 pathway, was activated in the process. However, decreasing the expression of phosphoIRE1&#945; and IRE1&#945; proteins with Irestatin 9389 (IRE1 antagonist) in GTCs did not affect the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>. Although GTC implantation was not affected upon <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection, progesterone secretion was suppressed, and prolactin and estrogen secretion increased; these effects were accompanied by changes in the expression of genes encoding key steroidogenic enzymes. This study systematically explored the mechanisms of abortion in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection from the viewpoint of pathogen invasion, ER stress and reproductive endocrinology. Our findings may provide new insight for understanding the mechanisms involved in goat abortions caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd>goat sec-type="intro" id="s1"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis, which is caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, is one of the most common zoonoses worldwide (Schurig et al., <xref rid="B43" ref-type="bibr">2002</xref>). Infection with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> results in a significant economic and health burden due to its high infectivity and chronic nature, as well as the difficulties in vaccine production (Taguchi et al., <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). Abortion and infertility in adult animals are the main characteristics of brucellosis. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccination is an important approach in the prevention and control of brucellosis (Nicoletti, <xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">1990</xref>), and understanding the molecular mechanisms of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> pathogenesis and host response, such as intracellular trafficking and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication, is critical for vaccination production and curbing brucellosis. The bacterium resides within a <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (BCV) after phagocytic uptake and entry into phagocytes or non-professional phagocytes (Celli et al., <xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>). Along the endocytic pathway, BCVs undergo maturation and become endosomal BCVs (eBCVs) (Starr et al., <xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">2012</xref>), and acidification of BCVs ensures the intracellular expression of genes that encode the VirB type IV secretion system (T4SS; Comerci et al., <xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">2001</xref>; Celli et al., <xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>, <xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">2005</xref>). Some VirB effectors, including VecC, BspA, BspB, BspC, BspD, and BspF, have been used to modulate secretory pathway functions (de Barsy et al., <xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Marchesini et al., <xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Myeni et al., <xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). Host factors such as Sar1 (Kuge et al., <xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">1994</xref>), Rab2 (de Barsy et al., <xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>), glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (Fugier et al., <xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>), and Yip1A (Taguchi et al., <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>) are required for the intracellular replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella.</italic></p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can modulate phagosome interaction with the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), a large membrane-bound organelle involved in protein biosynthesis as well as lipid, carbohydrate and protein transport. Indeed, the ER plays an important role in cellular homeostasis by manipulating the processing and folding of membrane and secretory proteins (Pluquet et al., <xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). However, when protein processing and folding requirements exceed the capacity of the ER, unfolded proteins accumulate, evoking ER stress and inducing the unfolded protein response (UPR), which involves inositol-requiring enzyme 1 (IRE1), double-stranded RNA-dependent protein kinase P (PKR)-like ER kinase (PERK) and activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6) (Schr&#246;der and Kaufman, <xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">2005</xref>). Many studies have confirmed that induction of the UPR pathway following <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> infection promotes the intracellular growth of these bacteria (Qin et al., <xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>; Smith et al., <xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>; Taguchi et al., <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>), especially the IRE1&#945; pathway (Qin et al., <xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>; Taguchi et al., <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). However, when UPR fails to manage misfolded and unfolded proteins, cellular apoptosis is induced due to persistent or excessive ER stress (Walter and Ron, <xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>). Survival and replication inside macrophages is critical for the establishment of chronic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. Virulent smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains, such as <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 2308 and smooth <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (Gross et al., <xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">2000</xref>; Tolomeo et al., <xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>; He et al., <xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>), inhibit programmed macrophage cell death and replicate inside macrophages. In contrast, rough <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strains, such as RB51 and RA1 (Chen and He, <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>), induce macrophage cell death through a caspase2-dependent pathway. Two death signal pathways control cell apoptosis: the mitochondria C pathway and the death receptor pathway. Caspases are crucial components in the execution of apoptosis. Caspase-8 is the initiator caspase in the death receptor pathway, whereas caspase-9 is the initiator caspase in the mitochondrial pathway; caspase-3 is the key executioner caspase in all apoptosis pathways (Li and Yuan, <xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>; Kantari and Walczak, <xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>). ER stress-mediated apoptosis is a new apoptosis signaling pathway (Gorman et al., <xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">2012</xref>), and the specific mechanism of ER stress apoptosis in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections is attracting much research interest.</p><p>Trophoblasts are cellular targets where <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> efficiently replicates in association with the ER. Recent studies have confirmed that ER stress is closely related to placental functions, such as placental development, fetal growth restriction (Yang et al., <xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>), progesterone secretion, and steroidogenic enzyme expression (Park et al., <xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">2014</xref>). Altering the features of placental trophoblast cells will result in a variety of complications during pregnancy. Under normal circumstances, goat pregnancy is typically maintained by a high concentration of progesterone, whereas the concentration of estrogen is very low. Before delivery, progesterone secretion decreases, and estrogen secretion increases to occupy a dominant position. Such an increase in estrogen or a reduced progesterone to estrogen ratio promotes the secretion of prolactin and initiates lactation (Cole and Cupps, <xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">1969</xref>). In addition, estrogen promotes the expression of PR; however, progesterone inhibits the expression of PR (Devillers et al., <xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>), resulting in the maximum receptivity capacity of the endometrium during embryo implantation. Finally, the decrease in PR stimulates endometrial function differentiation and the production of secretory proteins (Devillers et al., <xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains can infect and proliferate within placental trophoblasts during pregnancy in an infected host (Samartino and Enright, <xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">1993</xref>), which has significant pathological consequences, such as abortion and infertility (Kurdoglu et al., <xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">2010</xref>). It is known that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> replication occurs in the rough endoplasmic reticulum in infected caprine trophoblasts (Anderson and Cheville, <xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1986</xref>); however, the specific mechanism underlying abortion in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections remains unknown.</p><p>Here, we investigate the mechanisms of ER stress induced by <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection and replication in GTCs. Our results showed that GTC apoptosis and growth retardation were induced by <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> replication under ER stress. Changes in ER stress in GTCs influenced the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>, and both the endocrine balance of trophoblast cells and endometrial receptivity were defective during <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection. Our findings may provide new insight for understanding the mechanisms involved in goat abortions caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections.</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials and methods" id="s2"><title>Materials and methods</title><sec><title>Bacterial strain preparation and cultivation</title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> vaccine strain <italic toggle="yes">2</italic> (<italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>) cells were cultured in 50 ml of tryptic soy broth (TSB) medium with constant agitation at 37&#176;C. The bacteria were collected by centrifugation at 6000 &#215; g for 20 min at 4&#176;C and washed three times with 15 ml of phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). The numbers of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> cells were counted by plating on tryptic soy agar (TSA). pFPV-mCherry was a gift from Olivia Steele-Mortimer (Addgene plasmid # 20956), and the plasmid was transformed into <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> to obtain <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>-mCherry (red). All procedures involving live <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> cells were performed in a biosafety level 3 facility.</p></sec><sec><title><italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection assays</title><p>Goat trophoblast cells (GTCs) were immortalized by transfection with human telomerase reverse transcriptase (hTERT; Dong et al., <xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>); these cells were provided by professor Dewen Tong (Northwest A&amp;F University, Yangling, Shaanxi, China). A standard gentamicin protection assay was carried out to determine the number of intracellular <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> bacteria. GTCs were seeded in six-well plates (5 &#215; 10<sup>5</sup> cells per well) and infected with <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> at 100:1 MOI. Multiwell plates were placed at 37&#176;C in 5% CO<sub>2</sub>. After 4 h of incubation, the GTCs were washed three times with PBS and further cultured with cell culture medium containing 50 &#956;g/ml gentamicin to eliminate <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> cells adhering to the GTCs and in the culture medium. After 1 h, the GTCs were washed three times with PBS and further cultured with cell culture medium containing 25 &#956;g/ml gentamicin. This time was considered to be the beginning of the intracellular growth of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> within GTCs and the time point of treatment with Tm (ER stress activator), 4-PBA (ER stress antagonist), Irestatin9389 (IRE1&#945; antagonist), or chloroquine (autophagy antagonist). The cells and supernatants were collected, and relevant experiments were performed at specific times (&#8722;1, 12, 24, or 48 h). A schematic of the <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection assays is presented in Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1A</xref>. The process of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>-mCherry infection of GTCs was observed under a Nikon A1R si confocal microscope system, and the numbers of invasive <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> bacteria were determined. GTCs were treated with 0.1 ml of 0.1% Triton X-100 in PBS for 5 min at 37&#176;C, and the lysates were diluted in PBS and plated onto TSA to determine the colony-forming units (CFU; Posadas et al., <xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">2012</xref>).</p><fig id="F1" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Figure 1</label><caption><p><bold>Infection and proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> in GTCs. Changes in UPR in GTCs affect the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> in these cells.</p><p><italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> can infect and replicate in GTCs; we observed an infection rate of ~99%, with growth accelerating at 12 h up to a maximum bacterial load after 24 h. This is consistent with a previous report that over 95% of macrophages became infected with RB51 at any tested MOI (Chen and He, <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>). Our results may explain why the vaccine based on this <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strain cannot be used in pregnant domestic animals.</p><p>Bacterial survival and replication inside host cells are critical for the establishment of chronic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. Macrophages infected with rough <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains undergo apoptotic cell death, whereas macrophages infected with smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> show inhibition of spontaneously occurring apoptosis (Gross et al., <xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">2000</xref>; Tolomeo et al., <xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>; He et al., <xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>). The mechanisms by which smooth virulent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> inhibits apoptosis and promotes proliferation have been well demonstrated. Smooth virulent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains control or impact mitochondrial functions such as mitochondrial membrane transition and cytochrome c release (Chen and He, <xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>), though these smooth strains do not induce caspase-2-dependent cell death. Caspase-2 also acts in concert with the mitochondrial and death receptor pathways of apoptosis (Sidi et al., <xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>). In contrast, programmed cell death in macrophages infected with rough <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains is partially due to a transition in mitochondrial permeability and caspase-2 activation. Recent studies have confirmed that smooth <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can dissociate into rough mutants that are cytotoxic to macrophages and conducive to the spread of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> (Pei et al., <xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">2014</xref>). Therefore, we speculated that the inhibition and promotion of apoptosis occurs during the entire process of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. In our studies, <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection induced caspase-8, -9, and CHOP protein expression for apoptosis in GTCs. Although the activation of caspase-8 and-9 was observed, inhibition of these caspases only slightly blocked <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>-induced PMN cell death (Barquero-Calvo et al., <xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). When ER stress is excessive and prolonged, CHOP levels increase to mediate ER stress-induced apoptosis (Groenendyk et al., <xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">2010</xref>; Miyazaki et al., <xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>). Therefore, we believe that ER stress-induced apoptosis plays an important role in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections.</p><p>Host UPR plays an absolutely critical role in supporting <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication. GRP78 play essential roles in the biology and life cycles of viruses, bacteria, protozoa, and yeast cells, as well as higher eukaryotic cells. To guarantee persistent infection, Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV) and Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) prevent virus-induced apoptosis through constant up-regulation of GRP78 expression (Jiang et al., <xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">2014</xref>; Lyoo et al., <xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> takes advantage of VceC to interact with the ER chaperone GRP78 and localizes to the ER in HeLa cells (de Jong et al., <xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Streptomyces</italic> inhibits GRP78 protein expression and induces cell death under ER stress (Hayakawa et al., <xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">2014</xref>). Currently, Nexavar/Stivarga/Votrient is used to target GRP78 in the treatment of human malignancies, viral infections and bacterial diseases (Roberts et al., <xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection was recently suggested to induce UPR (Qin et al., <xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>; de Jong et al., <xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>; Smith et al., <xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>), and the apparent effect of TUDCA in inducing GRP78 and CHOP expression is due to the greatly diminished numbers of bacteria (Smith et al., <xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). This finding is consistent with our result that inhibiting CHOP protein expression with 4-PBA increased the number of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> CFUs in GTCs. In addition, enhancing CHOP protein expression with Tm inhibited the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> in GTCs. Enhancing expression of GRP78 and decreasing that of CHOP can also promote the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> in stably-transfected GRP78 over-expression GTC lines. Similarly, inhibiting GRP78 protein expression and increasing that of CHOP reduces <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> proliferation in stably-transfected GRP78 interference GTC lines. Thus, our study provides new evidence that increasing GRP78 expression promotes the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> in GTCs by inhibiting the apoptosis of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>-infected GTCs.</p><p>Using insect cells and murine embryonic fibroblasts with IRE1 knockdown, Qin et al. (<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>) demonstrated that bacterial replication is suppressed after UPR activation during <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. de Jong et al. (<xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>) and Yuki Taguchi et al. (<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>) suggested that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> infection activated the IRE1 pathway in HeLa cells, whereas Smith et al. (<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>) showed that all three UPR pathways were induced after infection of murine macrophages with <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>. In our study, we confirmed that <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection activated the IRE1 pathway and not the PERK and ATF6 pathways in GTCs. However, decreasing phosphoIRE1&#945; and IRE1&#945; expression with Irestatin 9389 in GTCs did not affect the number of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> bacteria at 24 h post-infection. Our results are consistent with the report that IRE1 knockdown in BMDM did not affect the numbers of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> cells at 24 h post-infection. However, host factor Yip1A mediated-IRE1 activation can be exploited by <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> to promote its replication in HeLa cells (Taguchi et al., <xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). This might reflect a difference between macrophages and epithelial cells with regard to different species of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. IRE1 can regulate autophagic events independently from other ER-associated signaling molecules. Our results confirmed that autophagy was induced in <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic>-infected GTCs, and decreasing LC3 protein expression with chloroquine decreased the number of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> bacteria at 24 h post-infection. Taken together, ER stress induced in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection is a widespread phenomenon, and such changes in ER stress in either phagocytic or non-phagocytic cells can affect the proliferation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in different types of bacteria. However, our research confirmed that ER stress-apoptosis and ER-phagy signaling pathways, and not the ER stress pathway, play a decisive role in the process of <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection.</p><p>According to our results, GTC growth was retarded following <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> replication, but the implantation, migration, and invasion abilities of GTCs were not affected. These results are consistent with previous reports that only <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> can interfere with trophoblast migration and invasion functions (Salcedo et al., <xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). Our study also proved that <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection simultaneously evoked ER stress and reduced the secretion of progesterone, though the secretion of estrogen and prolactin significantly increased. Our results are consistent with the report that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> bacteria preferentially replicate in placental trophoblasts during the middle and late stages of gestation after these cells actively secrete steroids (Gorvel and Moreno, <xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">2002</xref>). In pregnancy, decreased progesterone or an increased estrogen to progesterone ratio lead to premature delivery, and progesterone increase and estrogen decrease are key events for triggering implantation in the secretory stage (Tabibzadeh, <xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">1998</xref>). In addition, <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection disturbed PR and E&#945;R protein expression under progesterone stimulation in EECs. Ultimately, not only the endocrine balance of trophoblast cells under normal growth conditions but also endometrial receptivity was defective, which may be a reason for the abortion and premature delivery induced by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection.</p><p>In this paper, we reveal the mechanism by which <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> affects the host response and completes its proliferation. In addition, we also explore the mechanisms underlying the abortion induced by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection (Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F8">8</xref>). Our findings may provide new insight for understanding the mechanisms involved in goat abortion caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections. Our work provides a starting point for exploring the function of ER stress and adds a new dimension to our understanding of the mechanism of abortion induced by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. Our findings will also assist in the exploration of novel diagnosis and therapeutic strategies.</p><fig id="F8" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Figure 8</label><caption><p><bold>Updated model of the response of GTCs to <italic toggle="yes">B.suis.S2</italic> infection.
