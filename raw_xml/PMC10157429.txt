      <abstract abstract-type="key-points">
        <title>Key Points</title>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-1">
          <title>Question</title>
          <p>Do the protein-based SARS-CoV-2 vaccines (FINLAY-FR-2 and FINLAY-FR-1A) provide safe and effective protection against SARS-CoV-2?</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-2">
          <title>Findings</title>
          <p>In this randomized clinical trial including 23&#x2009;959 individuals, vaccine efficacy for prevention of polymerase chain reaction (PCR)&#x2013;positive symptomatic COVID-19 was 49.7%; severe disease, 76.8%; and hospitalization, 77.7% after the 2-dose regimen of FINLAY-FR-2, which improved to 64.9% for prevention of PCR-positive symptomatic COVID-19, 96.6% for prevention of severe disease, and 96.6% for prevention of hospitalization in recipients of a 2-dose regimen of FINLAY-FR-2 and a third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A. The incidence of serious adverse events was lower than 0.1%.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-3">
          <title>Meaning</title>
          <p>The findings of this trial suggest that FINLAY-FR-2, in combination with a third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A, is a safe vaccine inducing a potent immune response against COVID-19.</p>
        </sec>
      </abstract>
      <abstract>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-4">
          <title>Importance</title>
          <p>The protein-based SARS-CoV-2 vaccines FINLAY-FR-2 (Soberana 02) and FINLAY-FR-1A (Soberana Plus) showed good safety and immunogenicity in phase 1 and 2 trials, but the clinical efficacy of the vaccine remains unknown.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-5">
          <title>Objective</title>
          <p>To evaluate the efficacy and safety of a 2-dose regimen of FINLAY-FR-2 (cohort 1) and a 3-dose regimen of FINLAY-FR-2 with FINLAY-FR-1A (cohort 2) in Iranian adults.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-6">
          <title>Design, Setting, and Participants</title>
          <p>A multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 3 trial was conducted at 6 cities in cohort 1 and 2 cities in cohort 2. Participants included individuals aged 18 to 80 years without uncontrolled comorbidities, coagulation disorders, pregnancy or breastfeeding, recent immunoglobulin or immunosuppressive therapy, and clinical presentation or laboratory-confirmed COVID-19 on enrollment. The study was conducted from April 26 to September 25, 2021.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-7">
          <title>Interventions</title>
          <p>In cohort 1, 2 doses of FINLAY-FR-2 (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;13&#x2009;857) or placebo (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3462) were administered 28 days apart. In cohort 2, 2 doses of FINLAY-FR-2&#x2009;plus&#x2009;1 dose of FINLAY-FR-1A (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;4340) or 3 placebo doses (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1081) were administered 28 days apart. Vaccinations were administered via intramuscular injection.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-8">
          <title>Main Outcomes and Measures</title>
          <p>The primary outcome was polymerase chain reaction&#x2013;confirmed symptomatic COVID-19 infection at least 14 days after vaccination completion. Other outcomes were adverse events and severe COVID-19. Intention-to-treat analysis was performed.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-9">
          <title>Results</title>
          <p>In cohort 1 a total 17&#x2009;319 individuals received 2 doses and in cohort 2 5521 received 3 doses of the vaccine or placebo. Cohort 1 comprised 60.1% men in the vaccine group and 59.1% men in the placebo group; cohort 2 included 59.8% men in the vaccine group and 59.9% in the placebo group. The mean (SD) age was 39.3 (11.9) years in cohort 1 and 39.7 (12.0) years in cohort 2, with no significant difference between the vaccine and placebo groups. The median follow-up time in cohort 1 was 100 (IQR, 96-106) days and, in cohort 2, 142 (137-148) days. In cohort 1, 461 (3.2%) cases of COVID-19 occurred in the vaccine group and 221 (6.1%) in the placebo group (vaccine efficacy: 49.7%; 95% CI, 40.8%-57.3%) vs 75 (1.6%) and 51 (4.3%) in cohort 2 (vaccine efficacy: 64.9%; 95% CI, 49.7%-59.5%). The incidence of serious adverse events was lower than 0.1%, with no vaccine-related deaths.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-10">
          <title>Conclusions and Relevance</title>
          <p>In this multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 3 trial of the efficacy and safety of FINLAY-FR-2 and FINLAY-FR-1A, 2 doses of FINLAY-FR-2 plus the third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A showed acceptable vaccine efficacy against symptomatic COVID-19 as well as COVID-19&#x2013;related severe infections. Vaccination was generally safe and well tolerated. Therefore, Soberana may have utility as an option for mass vaccination of the population, especially in resource-limited settings, because of its storage condition and affordable price.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="ab-zoi230329-11">
          <title>Trial Registration</title>
          <p>isrctn.org Identifier: <ext-link xlink:href="https://www.irct.ir/trial/54833" ext-link-type="uri">IRCT20210303050558N1</ext-link></p>
        </sec>

    <sec id="H1-1-ZOI230329">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>Vaccines have been considered one of the most important interventions for curbing the COVID-19 pandemic. COVID-19 vaccine studies have been conducted based on different approaches including the first-generation platforms (eg, attenuated and inactivated virus vaccines), the second-generation vaccines, such as replicating vector vaccines and protein-based vaccines (eg, subunit and viral vectors), and the third-generation vaccines, such as nanoparticle and genetic vaccines (eg, DNA/RNA vaccines).<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref></sup> Several COVID-19 vaccines are developed from the SARS-CoV-2 spike glycoprotein receptor-binding domain (RBD) to prevent the virus attachment to human cell angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 receptors.</p>
      <p>FINLAY-FR-2 (Soberana 02) is a protein subunit vaccine conjugated to the tetanus toxoid carrier protein and has shown potential in preclinical studies<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref></sup> and phase 1, 2, and 3 trials.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref></sup> FINLAY-FR-1A (Soberana Plus), which is an RBD dimer without conjugation, has enhanced neutralization response in individuals recovering from COVID-19<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup> and, when used as the third dose to FINLAY-FR-2, induced neutralizing anti-RBD immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibodies (eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup> FINLAY-FR-2 and FINLAY-FR-1A have received permission for emergency use in Belarus, Cuba, Iran, Mexico, Nicaragua, and Venezuela. FINLAY-FR-2 and FINLAY-FR-1A vaccines are developed and produced at the Finlay Vaccine Institute of Cuba and branded as Pastocovac and Pastocovac Plus, and are manufactured by the Pasteur Institute of Iran after a successful technology transfer.</p>
      <p>On approval of the first sets of COVID-19 vaccines, governments all over the world rushed to place orders for supplies, based on the assumed effectiveness and relevance of the vaccine for their populations. While the recent launch of several safe and efficient COVID-19 vaccines has offered hope to tens of millions of people, vaccine supply is limited in low-income countries where the governments face supply challenges posed by vaccine cost as well as storage and transportation conditions.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> The potential advantage of RBD-based vaccines is their rapid and affordable production, ease of scalability, and stability at 2 to 8 &#xB0;C,<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref></sup> while the presence of multiple T- and B-cell epitopes of tetanus toxoid carriers, as part of a conjugate vaccine, potentiates cellular immune response.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref></sup></p>
      <p>We report a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled phase 3 vaccine trial to assess the efficacy, safety, and immunogenicity of FINLAY-FR-2 with the third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A, 50 &#x3BC;g, in Iran, as a country that has not reported on many trials.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="H1-2-ZOI230329">
      <title>Methods</title>
      <sec id="H2-1-ZOI230329">
        <title>Study Design and Participants</title>
        <p>A multicenter, randomized, double-blind, parallel-group, placebo-controlled phase 3 clinical trial was conducted to evaluate the efficacy, safety, and immunogenicity of a 2-dose regimen of conjugated protein-based FINLAY-FR-2, 25 &#x3BC;g, in one cohort (cohort 1) and a 2-dose regimen of FINLAY-FR-2 with a third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A, 50 &#x3BC;g, in a second cohort (cohort 2) in Iran. The study was conducted from April 26 to September 25, 2021. The trial protocol was reviewed and approved by Iran&#x2019;s Food and Drug Administration and the National Committee for Ethics in Biomedical Research. The study followed the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (<ext-link xlink:href="https://www.equator-network.org/reporting-guidelines/consort/" ext-link-type="uri">CONSORT</ext-link>) reporting guideline.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref></sup></p>
        <p>Participants were recruited from 8 geographically dispersed cities between April 26 and May 23, 2021 (eFigure 1 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>) on signing written informed consent. Participants received financial compensation. Cohort 1 included 17&#x2009;319 volunteers (resided in 6 cities), and cohort 2 included 5521 volunteers (lived in 2 cities) (eAppendix in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>
        <p>Volunteers aged 18 to 80 years without a current clinical presentation or laboratory-confirmed COVID-19 or history of COVID-19 vaccination, congenital or uncontrolled type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease, hypertension, and chronic liver disease could be included. The details of eligibility criteria are provided in the trial protocol (<xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 1</xref>).</p>
        <p>During the study, Iran initiated an immediate COVID-19 vaccination program, prioritizing older adults (&#x2265;65 years). To comply with the ethical principles,<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref></sup> and following the recommendation of the National Committee for Ethics in Biomedical Research, blinding for participants aged 65 years or older was discontinued (aged &#x2265;70 years: May 25, 2021; aged 65-70 years, July 13, 2021). These people were given priority in the national vaccination program to receive an approved vaccine and were excluded from analysis.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="H2-2-ZOI230329">
        <title>Randomization</title>
        <p>Randomization was conducted using a stratified balanced block randomization approach (block size: 25, stratum variable: city) on day 0, at a 4:1 ratio. Participants, study staff, and investigators were blinded for group allocation. Allocations were concealed through the central assignment. The vaccine and placebo vials were identical and indistinguishable in appearance.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="H2-3-ZOI230329">
        <title>Procedures</title>
        <p>FINLAY-FR-2 was administered via intramuscular injection, 28 days apart. FINLAY-FR-1A was used as a third dose in cohort 2 on day 56. Vaccine composition is presented in the eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>.</p>
        <p>QuantiVac ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay IgG) kit (Euroimmun, Germany) was used to determine the titration of anti-S1 IgG (including RBD) antibodies. To evaluate vaccine immunogenicity, a representative sample of participants (30% of the total) was selected from randomly selected cities on days 0 and 56 in cohort 1 (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;5905) and days 0 and 84 in cohort 2 (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;1975). Microneutralization assay<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref></sup> was also performed in a proportion of anti-S1 IgG ELISA-positive participants on days 56 (cohort 1; n&#x2009;=&#x2009;54) and 84 (cohort 2; n&#x2009;=&#x2009;58) using SARS-CoV-2 (hCoV-19/Iran/AK-SARS-A7/2020). Interferon-&#x3B3; was assessed on day 56 using the SARS-CoV-2 interferon gamma release assay (Euroimmun). To rule out the possibility of natural infection interference with the interferon-&#x3B3; release assay and neutralization tests, samples were collected from participants who had IgG antibodies on day 0 or those who tested positive in real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) testing until the immunogenicity sampling times were excluded.</p>
        <p>Participants with suspected COVID-19 (based on symptoms) were tested using a 1-step RT-PCR kit (Pishtaz Teb Diagnostics), with the findings confirmed with the 2019-nCoV Nucleic Acid Diagnostic Kit (Sansure Biotech). Participants with RT-PCR&#x2013;positive testing with an N gene cycle threshold value less than 25 underwent an S gene sequence analysis.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref></sup> SARS-CoV-2 variant determination was conducted using the Nextclade application.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref></sup> All sequences were submitted to the Global Initiative on Sharing Avian Influenza Data (eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="H2-4-ZOI230329">
        <title>Outcomes</title>
        <p>Consistent with other vaccine trials, the primary outcome was symptomatic COVID-19, confirmed by RT-PCR 14 days after the second and third doses. Secondary outcomes were severe COVID-19, COVID-19&#x2013;related death, adverse events (AEs), and humoral and cellular immune responses (eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>
        <p>Participants recorded solicited AEs (ie, set of symptoms or events that study participants are explicitly requested to document) using an online platform within 3 days after receiving each injection. Unsolicited AEs were followed up 28 days after each dose. Within 28 days after the injection of each dose, participants were followed up for any type of AEs (mildly or moderately severe, AEs with medically attended visits, or life-threatening AEs) on day 3 and then weekly through telephone calls. From day 28 until the end of the follow-up period, telephone follow-ups were performed every 2 weeks. Also, participants were supported with a continuously operating call center and medical visits. Medically attended AEs and serious AEs were monitored 6 months after the first dose in cohort 1 and 7 months after the first dose in cohort 2.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="H2-5-ZOI230329">
        <title>Statistical Analysis</title>
        <p>The sample size was calculated to be approximately 18&#x2009;000 participants in cohort 1 (vaccine: 14&#x2009;400, placebo: 3600) and 6000 in cohort 2 (vaccine: 4800, placebo: 1200) (eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). Intention-to-treat analysis was performed. Vaccine efficacy was defined as the reduction in the hazard ratio for the symptomatic COVID-19 (vaccine vs placebo), using a Cox proportional hazards regression model stratified by city. It was calculated as a 1 &#x2013; <italic>exp(&#x3B2;)</italic> of vaccine group vs the placebo group. The geometric mean titer ratio was used to calculate 95% CIs with the nonparametric percentile bootstrap method with 1000 repetitions. A bootstrap <italic>t</italic> test was used to compare log-transformed titers between the vaccine and placebo groups.</p>
        <p>Seroconversion was defined as a 4-fold increase in anti&#x2013;SARS-CoV-2 S1 IgG titer compared with the baseline. Adverse events were compared between the vaccine and placebo groups using the &#x3C7;<sup>2</sup> or Fisher exact tests. Risk ratios for any AEs are reported. For safety analysis, AEs of cohort 1 and cohort 2 in the first and second doses were combined. All statistical analyses were conducted in R, version 4.1.0 software (R Foundation for Statistical Computing). The testing for significance was 2-sided, with a threshold of .05. The comparison between the vaccine and placebo groups and the analysis of AEs were unpaired, while the analysis of the difference in antibody titer between day 0 and subsequent days was paired. Further details on statistical analysis are provided in the eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="H1-3-ZOI230329">
      <title>Results</title>
      <sec id="H2-6-ZOI230329">
        <title>Participants</title>
        <p>Between April 26 and May 23, 2021, of 24&#x2009;126 volunteers screened, 18&#x2009;000 were randomized to placebo or vaccine in cohort 1 and 6000 were randomized to placebo or vaccine in cohort 2. A total of 23&#x2009;959 participants (cohort 1 [n&#x2009;=&#x2009;17&#x2009;972] and cohort 2 [n&#x2009;=&#x2009;5987]) were randomly assigned to receive the vaccine (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;19&#x2009;165) or placebo (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;4794) (<xref rid="zoi230329f1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). Overall, the mean (SD) age was 39.3 (11.9) years in cohort 1 and 39.7 (12.0) years in cohort 2, with no significant difference between the vaccine and placebo groups. In cohort 1, participants&#x2019; mean (SD) age was 39.4 (11.9) years in the vaccine group (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;14 375; 8637 [60.1%] men, 5738 [39.9%] women) and 39.1 (11.7) years in the placebo group (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3597; 2127 [59.1%] men, 1470 [40.9%] women). In cohort 2, the mean (SD) age was 39.6 (12.1) years in the vaccine group (2866 [59.8%] men, 1924 [40.2%] women) and 39.9 (11.7) years in the placebo group (717 [59.9%] men, 480 [40.1%] women). At least 1 underlying disease was reported in 5208 (29.0%) participants in cohort 1 and 1798 (30.0%) in cohort 2.</p>

      <sec id="H2-7-ZOI230329">
        <title>Immunologic Response</title>
        <p>In the vaccine group, the seroconversion rates of anti&#x2013;SARS-CoV-2 S1 IgG increased 4 weeks after the second (81.1% [95% CI, 79.7%-82.2%]) and third (92.9% [95% CI, 91.4%-94.2%]) doses (<italic>P</italic>&#x2009;&lt;&#x2009;.001) (eTable 4 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). For neutralizing antibodies, the rates were 69.8% (95% CI, 53.8%-82.8%) after the second dose and 100% after the third dose (eFigure 3 and eMethods in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>
        <p>A specific IFN-&#x3B3; response to SARS-CoV-2 S1 was analyzed in 363 participants 4 weeks after the second dose (295 from the vaccine group and 68 from the placebo group). The geometric mean titer ratio of IFN-&#x3B3; between the vaccine and placebo groups was 5.3 (95% CI, 3.1-9.2; <italic>P</italic>&#x2009;=&#x2009;.001).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="H2-8-ZOI230329">
        <title>Efficacy</title>
        <p>The median follow-up time for cohort 1 was 100 days (IQR, 96-106 days), and for cohort 2, 142 days (IQR, 137-148 days). In cohort 1, 461 (3.2%) symptomatic cases in the vaccine group and 221 (6.1%) in the placebo group were observed, which yielded a vaccine efficacy (VE) of 49.7% (95% CI, 40.8%-57.3%). In cohort 2, 75 (1.6%) symptomatic cases occurred in the vaccine group and 51 (4.3%) in the placebo group, corresponding to a VE of 64.9% (95% CI, 49.7%-59.5%) (<xref rid="zoi230329f2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>; <xref rid="zoi230329t2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>; eFigure 2 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). Among participants who tested negative for anti&#x2013;SARS-CoV-2 S1 IgG on day 0, VE was relatively comparable to the VE in the total sample (cohort 1: 54.7%; 95% CI, 35.0%-68.5% and cohort 2: 64.1%; 95% CI, 34.0%-80.5%) (eResults in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). Vaccine efficacy for the prevention of severe cases in cohort 1 was 76.8% (95% CI, 61.7%-86.0%) and for prevention of COVID-19&#x2013;related hospitalization was 77.7% (95% CI, 60.9%-87.4%). Vaccine efficacy for the prevention of severe cases after the third dose was 96.6% (95% CI, 72.2-99.6) and for prevention of hospitalization was 96.6% (95% 5 CI, 72.2%-99.6%) (<xref rid="zoi230329t2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). During the follow-up period, only 1 COVID-19&#x2013;related death was detected in the placebo group; therefore, estimation of the VE for the prevention of COVID-19&#x2013;related death was not feasible. The VE estimates based on logistic regression model are provided in eTable 2 and eTable 3 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>. The VEs in different subgroups on the prevention of various outcomes based on age, sex, and underlying diseases are presented in eTable 1 of <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>. The number of participants needed to vaccinate in cohort 1 was 32.3 and in cohort 2 was 29.4 (eTable 9 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>

        <title>Safety</title>
        <p>Solicited AEs at the injection site were reported more frequently in the vaccine group than the placebo group after the first dose (7269 [38.2%] vs 737 [15.5%]), second dose (6470 [35.7%] vs 649 [14.4%]), and third dose (1042 [25.0%] vs 98 [9.3%]) (<xref rid="zoi230329f3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>; eTable 5 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). Local pain at the injection site was the most common AE, reported in 35.1% of the participants in the vaccine group and 13.9% in the placebo group (<xref rid="zoi230329f3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>; eTable 6 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>

        <p>Systemic solicited AEs were more common in the vaccine group than in the placebo group after the first dose (5585 [29.3%] vs 1211 [25.5%]), second dose (4427 [24.4%]) vs 977 [21.7%]), and third dose (739 [17.7%] vs 148 [14.1%]). Fatigue was the most common systemic AE in both the vaccine (dose 1: 2380 [12.5%], dose 2: 1971 [10.9%], and dose 3: 299 [7.2%]) and placebo (dose 1: 492 [10.4%], dose 2: 411 [9.1%], and dose 3: 62 [5.9%]) groups (<xref rid="zoi230329f3" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>; eTable 6 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>).</p>
        <p>Unsolicited AEs within 3 days after the injection were comparable among participants in the vaccine and placebo groups after the first (1696 [8.9%] vs 458 [9.6%]) and second (1324 [7.3%] vs 334 [7.4%]) doses. Four (&lt;0.1%) deaths were reported in the vaccine group (1 sudden cardiac death, 1 brain death after ventricular arrhythmias and cardiac arrest, 1 death of unknown cause, and 1 road accident) and 2 (&lt;0.1%) in the placebo group (1 from ischemic heart disease and 1 from cardiorespiratory arrest with underlying stroke and hypertension), none of which were related to the vaccine (eTable 8 in <xref rid="note-ZOI230329-1-s" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplement 2</xref>). Moreover, 2 COVID-19&#x2013;related deaths were reported in the placebo group of cohort 1, 1 of which occurred during the follow-up period for VE (57 days after the second dose), and 1 happened 22 days after unblinding (98 days after the second dose). One COVID-19&#x2013;related death occurred in the vaccine group of cohort 1 10 days after the end of the follow-up period for VE and unblinding (78 days after the second dose).</p>

    <sec id="H1-4-ZOI230329">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>Our analysis showed that the VE of 2 doses of FINLAY-FR-2 (cohort 1) in preventing symptomatic infections was 49.7% of the participants, severe cases in 76.8%, and COVID-19&#x2013;related hospitalizations in 77.7%. Vaccine efficacy was higher in recipients of the third dose (cohort 2), at 64.9%, for preventing symptomatic infections and 96.6% for preventing severe cases and hospitalizations.</p>
      <p>Subunit protein vaccines are one of the most viable options for resource-limited settings due to their possible storage in refrigerators and easier distribution.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref></sup> These vaccines have shown potential utility in preclinical animal studies and controlled trials.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref></sup> The preclinical results with the vaccine highlighted the advantages of immunization with the recombinant RBD&#x2013;tetanus toxoid conjugates and showed that it could trigger a robust immune response.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref></sup></p>
      <p>Findings of a phase 3 randomized clinical trial on FINLAY-FR-2 in 44&#x2009;031 participants in Cuba reported VE against PCR-confirmed COVID-19 cases to be 69.7% (95% CI, 56.5%-78.9%) for the 2-dose and 92.0% (95% CI, 80.4%-96.7%) for the 3-dose regimen.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref></sup> Our trial&#x2019;s VE against symptomatic disease has been lower, particularly for the 3-dose regimen. The observed differences between VE estimates against symptomatic disease could be partly explained by the differences in the force of infection, presence of comorbidities in the studied populations, and circulating SARS-CoV-2 variants before and during the trials in both countries. Although the case definitions of symptomatic disease in both trials have been equivalent, the cultural representation of the various signs and symptoms may vary between the 2 settings, resulting in patients presenting with a different clinical picture being selected for PCR confirmation.</p>
      <p>The VE assessed in this study should be interpreted in the context of the dominance of the immune-evasive Delta variant during the follow-up period.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>,<xref rid="zoi230329r27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref></sup> A study in England suggested that the effectiveness of the BNT162b2 vaccine decreased from 93.7% (95% CI, 91.6%-95.3%) among people with the Alpha variant to 88.0% (95% CI, 85.3%-90.1%) in the Delta variant.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref></sup> Studies have highlighted lowered VE of BNT162b2 vaccine against new infections in Qatar (64.2% [95% CI, 38.1%-80.1%]),<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref></sup> Israel (39% [95% CI, 9.0%-59.0%]),<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref></sup> and the US (42% [95% CI, 13.0%-62.0%]).<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref></sup> The VE of mRNA-1273 vaccine against the Delta variant has also shown a modest reduction (2.1-fold) in neutralizing titers.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref></sup> Moreover, the VE of ChAdOx1 nCoV-19 vaccine appears to be reduced against the Delta (67.0% [95% CI, 61.3%-71.8%] vs Alpha (74.5% [95% CI, 68.4%-79.4%]) variant.<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref></sup> Although our understanding of the adverse effects of vaccine-escaping variants on the existing VEs continues to increase, the reduced acquired immunity could partly explain the decreased VE estimates over time.</p>
      <p>This trial benefited from a large sample size and a cohort of geographically diverse volunteers across the country. FINLAY-FR-2 is one of the only vaccines that noted the need for a third dose from its early design phases and could serve as an accessible, scalable, and affordable option for resource-limited settings that have faced challenges in accessing an equitable vaccine supply.</p>
      <p>In this study, we only excluded individuals with current COVID-19 and included others regardless of their COVID-19 history. This was recommended by the Iran Food and Drug Administration committee based on several considerations. The study started about 15 months after the onset of the epidemic in Iran. Within this period, a substantial proportion of the population contracted SARS-CoV-2 infection. However, COVID-19 history could not be verified through PCR test records, as many individuals with actual positive infection did not undergo testing at the early stages of the epidemic. Even if we had used negative antibody titers at baseline as an inclusion criterion, we would have erroneously included many individuals with a history of COVID-19 who had been infected at the early stages of the pandemic and had undetectable antibody titers due to the waning of their antibodies over time. The effect of serostatus on VE still needs further investigation. In a well-powered study conducted at the same time as our trial,<sup><xref rid="zoi230329r32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref></sup> large proportions of the participants were seropositive at baseline (44%), and the VE for the complete analysis set (66.7%) and seronegative population (67.2%) were similar. These data are comparable with our results, which showed that the VE in the naive population was similar to that of the total population.</p>
      <sec id="H2-10-ZOI230329">
        <title>Limitations</title>
        <p>We acknowledge the limitations of this study. First, up to 30% of volunteers with controlled underlying diseases were selected. This may have impacted the overall VE in this study. Second, we could not run serologic tests for all our participants at baseline due to our limited resources in Iran. Third, given that the older population had undergone mass vaccination during the study period, this age group underwent premature unblinding to receive approved vaccines, a procedure that could lead to selection bias due to the nonrandom exclusion of this group postrandomization; it was an ethical decision and was based on good clinical practice standards. Also, the magnitude of this bias was not considerable (approximately 2.5% of the total sample).</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="H1-5-ZOI230329">
      <title>Conclusions</title>
      <p>In this multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, phase 3 clinical trial, the study findings indicate that the FINLAY-FR-2 vaccine, in addition to the third dose of FINLAY-FR-1A, has acceptable VE against symptomatic cases and high efficacy against severe cases of COVID-19 and COVID-19&#x2013;related hospitalizations. FINLAY-FR-2 and FINLAY-FR-1A also appeared to be well tolerated and have a favorable safety profile.</p>
    </sec>
