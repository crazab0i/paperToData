Its intracellular survival and replication depend on a functional virB system, an operon encoded by VirB1&#8211;VirB12. (ER)-derived replicative <italic toggle="yes">Brucella-</italic>containing vacuole (rBCV), the effectors of T4SS and the cellular pathways it subverts, which will help better understand the pathogenic sec-type="intro" id="sec1-ijms-22-13637"><title>1. Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is a debilitating zoonotic disease affecting animals and humans all over the world [<xref rid="B1-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. The sick animals are characterized by infertility, abortion and decline in milk production, resulting in great economic losses to the livestock husbandry industry [<xref rid="B2-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>]. Human disease is caused by direct contact with the blood or tissue of infected animals or by consumption of contaminated dairy products, with clinical symptoms such as intermittent fever, arthritis, orchitis, hepatitis, and depression [<xref rid="B2-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>,<xref rid="B3-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species frequently isolated from job-related infections are <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella canis</italic> [<xref rid="B1-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. are gram-negative; their main target cells are macrophages, dendritic cells and trophoblast cells [<xref rid="B4-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>], and the main virulence factors are lipopolysaccharides (LPS), T4SS and the BvrR/BvrS system [<xref rid="B5-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>].</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> causes host pathogenesis through survival and replication in target cells, an intracellular process dependent on T4SS encoded by the VirB operon [<xref rid="B6-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>]. During intracellular infection, VirB T4SS is rapidly activated and reaches its maximum activity in five hours. When the replication niche is established, VirB T4SS is inhibited. During this period, VirB T4SS can subvert the cell pathway of host cells, which is beneficial to the bacterial reproduction and pathogenicity [<xref rid="B7-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>]. Although the mechanism of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> VirB system in the intracellular circulation has been largely elucidated, there are still some deficiencies. Therefore, this paper will elaborate on the mechanism of VirB action to provide a theoretical basis for the pathogenesis of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and clinical vaccine development.</p></sec><sec id="sec2-ijms-22-13637"><title>2. Composition of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> VirB Operon</title><p>The VirB system was first discovered in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic>, subsequently confirmed to be present in all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species and highly conserved [<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. It is composed of 12 open reading frames (ORFs) co-linked and located on the SpeI fragment of chromosome 2 [<xref rid="B9-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>,<xref rid="B10-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>]. In the VirB region of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, an operon is formed by VirB1&#8211;VirB12, whose expression is regulated by environmental signals [<xref rid="B11-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>]. Hartigh et al. [<xref rid="B12-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>] carried out infection experiments in mice with the nonpolar VirB1 mutant (&#916; <italic toggle="yes">virB1</italic>) and nonpolar VirB2 mutant (&#916; <italic toggle="yes">virB2</italic>). Facts have proved that VirB2 plays an important role in the continuous infection of mice caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, but VirB1 is dispensable [<xref rid="B12-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>]. Similarly, although VirB12 is able to encode a 17 kDa protein that elicits a host immunogenic response during infection, it is not essential for persistent infection in mice and macrophages [<xref rid="B13-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>]. In addition, VirB3&#8211;VirB11, except for VirB7, are important components of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence in mice [<xref rid="B14-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>] (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-t001" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>).</p><p>Each gene in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> VirB operon has a specific function and encodes proteins with different structures and functions. VirB proteins can be divided into four groups according to their functions: ATPase, core components, surface exposure components and other components (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>).</p><sec id="sec2dot1-ijms-22-13637"><title>2.1. ATPase</title><p>VirB4 and VirB11 are ATPases composed of two domains (NTD and CTD). VirB4 usually appears as a monomer, dimer or hexamer which provides energy for T4SS assembly and substrate transport through NTP binding [<xref rid="B15-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>,<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. The active function of ATP is performed by a six-ring compound formed by the interaction between monomers [<xref rid="B17-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>]. Phospholipids can significantly enhance the active function of ATP [<xref rid="B17-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>,<xref rid="B18-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot2-ijms-22-13637"><title>2.2. Core Component VirB6&#8211;VirB10</title><p>The core components consist of VirB6&#8211;VirB10, which are present in the periplasm and contain distinct domains [<xref rid="B19-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>] (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). They interact with each other to form a translocation channel for T4SS, thereby transporting effectors into the host cell [<xref rid="B19-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>]. Among them, VirB6 is an endosomal protein with a periplasmic n-terminal, five transmembrane domains and a cytoplasmic c-terminal, and the n-terminal periplasmic domain can interact with VirB8 and VirB10, which is essential for secreting substrates through the inner membrane [<xref rid="B19-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>,<xref rid="B20-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>,<xref rid="B21-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>]. Bound to VirB6, VirB8 exists as a dimer, containing a periplasmic domain, a single transmembrane helix structure and a cytoplasmic c-terminus [<xref rid="B22-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>]. The amino acid residues at the dimerization interface determine the function of VirB8 [<xref rid="B22-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>,<xref rid="B23-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>]. VirB10 can pass through the entire outer membrane of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> T4SS structure. Therefore, VirB10 is an important functional protein for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref rid="B21-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>]. The protein consists of an N-terminal domain; it is found that the domain contains four parts: a cytoplasmic component, a TM helix, a bendable component and a spherical CTD [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B24-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. These four parts lead to the central function of the VirB10 protein in T4SS, that is, connecting many different proteins and sending interactions so as to mediate the signal transmission process [<xref rid="B24-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. VirB9 encapsulated by VirB10 (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>) is a periplasmic protein with two domains of NTD and CTD; can interact with VirB7 to form the inner wall of T4SS core complex [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B25-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>]. While VirB7 is a lipoprotein whose n-terminus is acetylated and inserted inside the outer membrane, the remainder of the lipoprotein is located in the periplasm, and the lipidation is essential for maintaining the stability of T4SS [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B26-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot3-ijms-22-13637"><title>2.3. Surface-Exposed Components VirB2 and VirB5</title><p>The surface-exposed component contains VirB2 and VirB5 (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f001" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>), which interact to form T-pilus [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. VirB2 is the main component of T-pilus, forms a columnar structure on the bacterial surface to transfer the effector protein and also delivers the target signal peptide via the inner membrane [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>,<xref rid="B27-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. VirB5 located at the tip of T-pilus is a minor component of T-pilus that targets host cell receptors as a specific adhesin and helps to integrate the major component VirB2 into T-pilus [<xref rid="B28-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>,<xref rid="B29-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec2dot4-ijms-22-13637"><title>2.4. Other Components VirB1 and VirB12</title><p>Other components include VirB1 and VirB12. VirB1 does not bind to any other VirB protein and exist alone in the periplasm; it is the first product of the VirB operon; can cleave the &#946;-1,4-glycosidic bond between MurNAc (N-acetylmuramic acid) and GlcNAc (N-acetylglucosamine) [<xref rid="B24-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. Its n-terminus is a lytic transglycosylase, which may facilitate T4SS assembly by local destruction of peptidoglycans [<xref rid="B15-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. The c-terminal protein VirB1* can be cleaved from VirB1 and secreted outside bacterial cells, interacting with the T-pilus subunit to promote T-pilus assembly [<xref rid="B30-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>]. The exact location of VirB12 is still unclear; it is only confirmed that it is a cell surface protein that induces antibody response during animal infection [<xref rid="B31-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>,<xref rid="B32-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>].</p></sec></sec><sec id="sec3-ijms-22-13637"><title>3. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Type IV Secretion System</title><sec id="sec3dot1-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.1. Formation of T4SS</title><p>T4SS is encoded by the VirB operon and divided into three basic parts: the inner membrane complex (IMC: VirB3, VirB4, VirB6, VirB8), the outer membrane complex (OMC: VirB7, VirB9, VirB10) and the outer column (VirB2, VirB5) [<xref rid="B16-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>]. OMC is a large structure inserted into the inner membrane and similar to the core complex. It is connected by a flexible central stalk to a larger complex, IMC [<xref rid="B24-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>]. Among all the VirB proteins, VirB4 and VirB11 have ATPase activity; they are located in the cytoplasm, providing energy for T4SS assembly or substrate transport [<xref rid="B20-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>]. VirB4 can interact with itself, VirB8, VirB10 and VirB11; its stability depends on the formation of the VirB7&#8211;VirB9 heterodimer, while VirB6 also affects the stability of VirB4. Besides VirB4, VirB11 can also interact with other proteins to form the VirB11&#8211;VirB9&#8211;VirB7&#8211;VirB2/VirB5 action pathway [<xref rid="B15-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>]. In the basic structure of T4SS, the substrate transmembrane transport channel in the periplasm connects the outer membrane and the inner membrane. The inner membrane of the channel is composed of VirB3, VirB6 and VirB8 [<xref rid="B20-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>,<xref rid="B21-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>,<xref rid="B33-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>]. VirB8 acts as an assembly factor to bring VirB9 and VirB10 to the poles of bacteria; then, the intermediate complex VirB9 interacts with VirB10 to induce conformational changes and reduce the level of nonproductive aggregation, and then VirB9 dissociates the VirB8&#8722;VirB10 dimer, separates VirB10 from VirB8 [<xref rid="B34-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>]. During T-pilus (the outer column) biogenesis, VirB2 is first absorbed by the inner membrane; subsequently, VirB5 interacts with VirB8 and VirB10 with the assistance of the ATPases VirB4 and VirB11, which may be necessary for VirB5 binding to VirB2, and is then processed and assembled to form T-pilus [<xref rid="B32-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>]. In non-substrate-contacting VirB proteins, VirB4 coordinates substrate translocation to the VirB6 and VirB8 subunits; thereafter, VirB3, VirB5 and VirB10 facilitate substrate translocation from VirB6 and VirB8 to the VirB2 and VirB9 subunits [<xref rid="B35-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>]. A portion of VirB proteins interact with each other to maintain T4SS stability. The VirB4&#8211;VirB8&#8211;VirB5&#8211;VirB2 interaction promotes the assembly and stability of T4SS, the binding of VirB1 with VirB8 and of VirB9 with VirB11 is also an important factor in promoting T4SS transmembrane assembly [<xref rid="B36-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>,<xref rid="B37-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>]. In conclusion, formation of T4SS depends on the interaction of various VirB proteins, but the mechanism of this interaction is not sufficiently clear, which needs to be further studied.</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot2-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2. T4SS Effectors</title><p>Although T4SS was discovered in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. about 15 years ago, the T4SS effectors were confirmed only recently. A candidate protein can be identified as a T4SS effector protein if it meets two criteria. First, the protein must be secreted by VirB T4SS. Second, this protein must be able to enter the host cell [<xref rid="B7-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. The first criterion can be verified by constructing a T4SS-defective strain; the second standard can be verified by TEM-1 lactamase or calmodulin-dependent adenylate cyclase assays [<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. There are currently 15 known VirB T4SS effectors: RicA, VceC, VecA, BtpA, BtpB, BspA, BspB, BspF, BPE005, BPE123, BPE043, BPE275, SepA, BspC, and BspE. They manipulate host cell pathways and responses [<xref rid="B7-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>,<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>].</p><sec id="sec3dot2dot1-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2.1. RicA</title><p>RicA is a single-domain protein composed of 175 amino acids assembled in the form of a trimer [<xref rid="B38-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>]. Each monomer consists of a left-handed parallel &#946;-helix and an &#945;-helix that is inversely parallel to the &#946;-helix, with 13 hydrogen bonds formed at the interface where each two monomers interact. The three-dimensional structure of RicA can be divided into two parts: the n-terminal L&#946;H and the c-terminal &#945;-helix. L&#946;H is a protein superfamily including acetyltransferases, ferripyochelin-binding proteins, acyltransferases, carbonic anhydrases and some proteins of unknown functions [<xref rid="B39-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]. In L&#946;H, there are three &#946;-layers, two of which are embedded in the trimeric structure and the other is externally exposed [<xref rid="B38-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="B39-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>,<xref rid="B40-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>]. Rab2 GTPase is a cellular host factor critical for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intracellular proliferation. RicA can interact with it in two possible interfaces; one is a &#946;-lamellar interface and the other is a ring called the isoleucine&#8211;glycine&#8211;phenylalanine&#8211;proline (IGFP) loop [<xref rid="B39-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>]; RicA relies on its molar affinity to bind to guanosine diphosphate (GDP) and non-GDP forms of the Rab2 GTPase [<xref rid="B40-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot2dot2-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2.2. VceC and VecA</title><p>VceC is highly conserved in all sequenced <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomes, contains 418 amino acids; the 20 amino acids at the c-terminal are necessary for its transfer to the host cell after entering the host cell; it is activated by a quorum-sensing regulatory protein VjbR and targets the endoplasmic reticulum. The interaction with the endoplasmic reticulum requires 37 amino acids at the n-terminal of the transmembrane domain [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B42-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>,<xref rid="B43-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>].</p><p>VceA contains 105 amino acids, is identical to VceC, conserved in all sequenced <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomes, shares an 18-base-pair (bp)-long conserved promoter with VceC; transcription of VceA is also regulated by VjbR [<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>,<xref rid="B44-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>]. VecA is related to autophagy and apoptosis. The expression levels of the autophagy genes of Atg5 and LC3-II in the VceA mutants constructed by Zhang et al. were higher than those in the wild type, while the expression levels of the autophagy genes of p62 and LC3-I were decreased. Atg5 and LC3-II are genes that promote autophagy. P62 and LC3-I are genes that inhibit autophagy, indicating that VceA has a function of inhibiting autophagy. At the same time, the content of caspase-3 mRNA in VceA mutants decreased and the expression level of Bcl-2 mRNA increased. Caspase-3 is a gene that promotes apoptosis, Bcl-2 is an anti-apoptosis gene, indicating that VceA can promote apoptosis [<xref rid="B43-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>].</p><p>In conclusion, VceC and VceA are important T4SS effectors which have important effects on autophagy and apoptosis.</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot2dot3-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2.3. BtpA and BtpB</title><p>The BtpA gene is located in a 20 kb genomic island on chromosome 1, present in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> and absent in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>
<italic toggle="yes">suis</italic>. BtpA is a protein composed of 250 amino acids that contains two domains: one is the n-terminal domain which can bind to phosphoinositide and the other is a c-terminal TIR domain [<xref rid="B45-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>,<xref rid="B46-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>,<xref rid="B47-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>,<xref rid="B48-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>]. In addition to subverting the innate immune signaling pathway, purified BtpA has also been shown to inhibit CD8+ T cell-mediated killing response, suggesting it may also control the acquired immune response [<xref rid="B47-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>].</p><p>Unlike BtpA, BtpB, which is present in all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, is formed by 292 amino acids and can downregulate the total level of NAD in host cells through direct enzymolysis of metabolic cofactors, thus directly controlling host energy metabolism [<xref rid="B49-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>,<xref rid="B50-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>].</p><p>BtpA and BtpB play different roles in host cells. BtpA mainly causes host immunity, BtpB is mainly related to energy metabolism.</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot2dot4-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2.4. BspA, BspB, BspF</title><p>BSPA, BspB and BspF consist of 191, 187 and 428 amino acids, respectively, and contain the DUF2062 domain (unknown functional domain 2062, Pfam database), the SCOP domain (flanked by two transmembrane domains) and the Gcn5-associated N-acetyltransferase (GNAT) family acetyltransferase domain [<xref rid="B51-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec3dot2dot5-ijms-22-13637"><title>3.2.5. Other Effectors</title><p>T4SS effectors also include BPE005, BPE123, BPE043, BPE275, SepA, BspC and BspE. BPE005, BPE275 and BPE043 are widespread in a variety of bacteria, while BPE123 is only found in <italic toggle="yes">Bartonella bacilliformis</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Ochrobactrum anthropi</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">O. intermedium</italic> [<xref rid="B8-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>]. In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, BPE123 can act on the &#945;-enolase of host cells to promote the intracellular life of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> [<xref rid="B52-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>]. At the same time, SepA is involved in the early stage of intracellular survival; its secretion occurs 30 min after infection [<xref rid="B53-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>]. There is a lack of studies on the structure and function of BPE043, BPE275, BspC, and BspE.</p></sec></sec></sec><sec id="sec4-ijms-22-13637"><title>4. Effect of VirB T4SS on the Intracellular Circulation of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic></title><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can invade the body through skin mucosa, digestive tract and respiratory tract; then, it is enclosed in a membrane to form a <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (BCV) [<xref rid="B54-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>]; subsequently, early endosomal markers (EEA-1, Rab5) and late endosomal markers (LAMP-1, Rab7, CD63, RILP) are obtained [<xref rid="B55-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>,<xref rid="B56-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>,<xref rid="B57-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>], which decreases PH and induces expression of VirB T4SS. Immediately after, <italic toggle="yes">B</italic><italic toggle="yes">rucella</italic> interacts with endoplasmic reticulum exit sites (ERES) and transforms into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER)-derived replicative <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (rBCV) with an endoplasmic reticulum structure and function [<xref rid="B54-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>]. After replication in the endoplasmic reticulum, rBCV becomes an autophagy-related <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>-containing vacuole (aBCV); aBCV maturation completes the intracellular cycle of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and facilitates subsequent cell-to-cell infection [<xref rid="B58-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>] (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f002" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p><sec id="sec4dot1-ijms-22-13637"><title>4.1. VirB T4SS Action Stage</title><p>Various VirB system mutants are nontoxic in mouse experiments, suggesting that a functional VirB system is required for persistent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in host cells [<xref rid="B59-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>]. Celli et al. [<xref rid="B57-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>] constructed VirB10 mutants to invade mouse BMDM cells; the survival rate of the VirB10 mutants was similar to that of wild-type S2308 during the first 4 h of infection, showing that the VirB system is not essential for the short-term survival of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. However, 4 h after infection, VirB mutants persistently fuse with lysosomes and do not effectively control the maturation of bacterial vacuoles [<xref rid="B60-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>], whereas the wild type, upon interfusion with lysosomes, is able to lose the lysosomal component LAMP-1 through gaining the partner calnexin by capturing vesicles from ERES, and mediating the maturation of bacterial vacuoles (i.e., the biogenesis of rBCV) [<xref rid="B27-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>]. Persistent fusion of VirB mutants with lysosomes leads to bacterial killing, but interaction of VirB T4SS-released effectors with ERES to establish a replication niche can prevent this process [<xref rid="B60-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>,<xref rid="B61-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">61</xref>,<xref rid="B62-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">62</xref>].</p><p>ERES is a discrete region formed by the coated complex COPII on the endoplasmic reticulum [<xref rid="B63-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>,<xref rid="B64-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>], VirB acting on ERES is consistent with that Celli et al. [<xref rid="B54-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>] have shown eBCVs are converted to rBCVs via interaction with COPII-positive compartments mediated by VirB [<xref rid="B54-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>,<xref rid="B55-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>]. After rBCV formation, as <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proliferates, rBCVs are phagocytosed into autophagosome-like structures to become aBCVs [<xref rid="B58-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>]. Smith et al. [<xref rid="B65-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>] showed that aBCV formation and subsequent bacterial excretion are also dependent on VirB T4SS [<xref rid="B65-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>]. As the formation of aBCVs requires the involvement of host autophagy mechanisms [<xref rid="B59-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>], it is possible that specific VirB T4SS effectors regulate the related pathways to promote aBCV formation, but this result is not widely accepted at present. Taken together, we can speculate that the VirB system plays an essential role in the biogenesis of rBCV; in addition, it may also be involved in the formation of aBCV [<xref rid="B66-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="sec4dot2-ijms-22-13637"><title>4.2. Cellular Pathways Affected by VirB T4SS</title><p>VirB T4SS secretes effectors into the host cell and alters specific cellular pathways, thus contributing to the virulence and the biogenesis of rBVC (<xref rid="ijms-22-13637-f003" ref-type="fig">Figure 3</xref>).</p><p>In pathogenetic terms, the T4SS effector VecC induces host inflammatory response via the unfolded protein response (UPR). VceC targets the ER and binds to Bip, leading to activation of the UPR. In mammalian cells, the UPR is activated by three different sensors on the endoplasmic reticulum (ER): inositol-requiring enzyme 1 (IRE1), protein kinase RNA (PKR)-like ER kinase (PERK) and activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6) [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. Yuki et al.&#8217;s [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>] experiments showed that during <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection, the UPR can only be activated through the IRE1 pathway in a time-dependent manner [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>]. Once activated, IRE1&#945; transfers TNF receptor-associated factor 2 (TRAF2) to the ER and induces endoplasmic reticulum stress via the NF-&#954;B pathway [<xref rid="B67-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">67</xref>,<xref rid="B68-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>]. Then, endoplasmic reticulum stress through the NOD1/2-dependent TUDCA/KIRA6-sensitive pathway leads to inflammation [<xref rid="B68-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">68</xref>]. When the ER is persistently or excessively stressed beyond the UPR limit, apoptosis is induced. The C/EBP-homologous protein (CHOP) is the most important UPR-mediated apoptotic pathway, VceC can interact with GRP78 to inhibit CHOP-induced apoptosis and promote persistent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in cells [<xref rid="B42-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>].</p><p>The other two T4SS effectors related to inflammation are BtpA and BtpB; both of them contain the TIR domain. The TIR domain is a vital molecular component of TLR-mediated innate immune response. BtpA bears resemblance to the TIR domain adapter protein MAL and simulates the MAL function by combining with the plasma membrane using its N-terminal domain, thus competing with MAL for binding to TLR4 [<xref rid="B46-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>,<xref rid="B47-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>]. It can also interact with MAL through the Box1 region to decrease MAL phosphorylation levels, thereby inhibiting TLR4- and TLR2-mediated NF-&#954;B activation and DC maturation [<xref rid="B45-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>,<xref rid="B46-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>]. BtpA interacts not only with MAL, but also with the death domain (DD) of MyD88, and this interaction is stronger than the interaction of BtpA with MAL [<xref rid="B49-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>]. As MyD88 serves as the central hub in inflammatory responses, BtpA may cause inflammation through it. Another T4SS effector, BtpB, has similar functions to BtpA, working with BtpA to control DC maturation and host inflammatory responses by inhibiting transduction of TLR2, TLR4, TLR5 and TLR9 signal pathways in vitro [<xref rid="B48-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>]. In conclusion, BtpA and BtpB can both induce inflammation and control host immunity.</p><p>In addition to BtpA and BtpB, T4SS effector BEP005 also controls host immune response by interfering with the cAMP/PKE signal pathway. The cAMP/PKE signaling pathway is thought to be involved in activation and proliferation of rat hematopoietic stem cells [<xref rid="B69-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>]. cAMP is a messenger for many hormones and neuropeptides, and the function of cAMP in eukaryotic cells is activated by PKE [<xref rid="B70-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">70</xref>]. When hepatic stellate cells are infected, the cyclic nucleotide monophosphate-binding domain (cNMP) of the effector BPE005 blocks the binding of PKE and cAMP, thus inhibiting the secretion of MMP-9, inducing collagen deposition and TGF-&#946;1 secretion. All that leads to partial suppression of the immune response, thereby establishing chronic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection [<xref rid="B69-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">69</xref>].</p><p>In the aspect of rBVC biogenesis, T4SS effectors like BspB and RicA play a vital role. The biogenesis of rBVC needs functions of the host&#8217;s early secretory pathway, including Rab2, Rab1, Arf1 and host small GTPases Sar1. The eukaryotic protein component, Rab2, controls membrane transport between the ER and the Golgi, the T4SS effector RicA can interact with it directly or indirectly to control Rab2 replenishment on eBCV membranes, thereby reducing rBCV biogenesis and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication [<xref rid="B38-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>,<xref rid="B71-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>]. However, the T4SS effector BspB compensates RicA&#8217;s harmful activity on rBCV biogenesis and coregulates Golgi-derived vesicle transport in a compensatory manner [<xref rid="B71-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">71</xref>]. BspB targets the Golgi and the ERGIC, wherein it interacts with the Golgi apparatus-associated conserved oligomeric Golgi (COG) complex, a regulator of vesicular traffic, and recruits COG-containing vesicles to the eBCV membrane, promoting the biogenesis of rBCV and bacterial replication [<xref rid="B51-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>,<xref rid="B72-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">72</xref>].</p><p>Besides being regulated by the T4SS effectors identified above, the biogenesis of rBCV may also be affected by other unclear effectors. During infection, T4SS may secrete an effector to trigger the activation of IRE1, and IRE1 forms higher-order complexes with the assistance of host factor Yip1A, thus triggering the biogenesis of ER-derived autophagic vacuoles [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B60-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>]. These vacuoles then fuse with endolysosomal vesicles; <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> possibly intercept the vacuolar formation and obtain an ER-derived membrane [<xref rid="B41-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>,<xref rid="B60-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>,<xref rid="B73-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">73</xref>]. Another hypothesis is that T4SS effectors activate IRE1 alpha-associated kinases (ASK1 and JNK1) and subvert a new host &#945;-ulk1 signal axis. Kinases drive downstream proteins (ULK1, Atg9a, WIPI1 and Beclin1) assembly, which leads to the remodeling of the eBCV membrane and enables <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to establish a replication niche in cells [<xref rid="B74-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">74</xref>]. It should also be noted that there is a eukaryotic protein component glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH) on the rBCV membrane. GAPDH is normally located on VTCs between the ER and the Golgi apparatus, forms active complexes with Rab2, protein kinase C (PKC&#953;/&#955;), and then constitutes a secretory pathway with COPI; the secretory pathway GAPDH/COPI/Rab2/pkc&#953;/&#955; is essential for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication; inhibition of GAPDH leads to a reduction in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> replication [<xref rid="B66-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>,<xref rid="B75-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">75</xref>]. This suggests that T4SS effectors may also affect the biogenesis of rBCV through this secretory pathway.</p></sec><sec id="sec4dot3-ijms-22-13637"><title>4.3. Regulatory Factors of VirB T4SS</title><p>The expression of the VirB system peaked during rBCV biogenesis, but the bacteria gradually began to decrease when replicating in the host cell, which indicates that the expression of the VirB system is strictly regulated in the host cell [<xref rid="B76-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">76</xref>]. We divide the regulatory factors of the VirB system into direct regulatory factors and indirect regulatory factors.</p><p>Factors that directly regulate the VirB system include HutC IFH, quorum-sensing regulatory protein BlxR and autoinducers in the quorum-sensing system. The transcriptional repressor of the histidine utilization (Hut) genes HutC as a transcription factor directly controls the activity of the VirB promoter (P<italic toggle="yes"><sub>VirB</sub></italic>). This regulation is related to the induction of the histidine utilization pathway. It may be a method to escape host immunity by sensing self-metabolism and developing adaptive response [<xref rid="B65-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>]. The other regulatory factor, the host integration factor IHF, competes with HutC for the binding of P<italic toggle="yes"><sub>virB</sub></italic>. IHF is a protein known to be involved in transcriptional regulation of multiple bacterial genes. It specifically interacts with P<italic toggle="yes"><sub>virB</sub></italic> and induces DNA bending with a bending angle of 50.36&#176;, playing a vital role in the expression of VirB T4SS [<xref rid="B65-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>,<xref rid="B77-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">77</xref>]. Quorum-sensing system is also directly involved in the expression of VirB T4SS. Autologous inducer is the first quorum-sensing regulator found in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, contains N-dodecyl-DL-homoserine lactone (C12-HSL); can change the conformation of the quorum-sensing regulatory protein VjbR by binding to it [<xref rid="B78-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">78</xref>]. One of the functions of VjbR is to confine individual bacteria to phagosomes during the early stages of intracellular infection [<xref rid="B79-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">79</xref>]. Cell infection and cell death experiments have shown that the absence of VjbR in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> rough mutants downregulates VirB expression, thereby completely eliminating its toxicity in macrophages [<xref rid="B80-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">80</xref>]. The combination of an autologous inducer and VjbR makes VjbR no longer act as a transcription activator or bind to P<italic toggle="yes"><sub>virB</sub></italic>, thus inhibiting the expression of the VirB operon [<xref rid="B78-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">78</xref>]. Another quorum-sensing regulatory protein BlxR negatively affects genes related to energy production and transformation, translation and protein modification, ribosome structure and biogenesis, carbohydrate and amino acid metabolism, directly binds to P<italic toggle="yes"><sub>virB</sub></italic> and represses transcription of the VirB operon [<xref rid="B81-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">81</xref>].</p><p>The other two factors indirectly regulating VirB T4SS are UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (UGPase) and Hfq. UGPase is a pyrophosphatase; can regulate the expression of virB proteins (virB3, virB4, virB5, virB6, virB8, virB9, virB10 and virB11) and T4SS effectors (vceC, btpA, btpB, ricA, bspB, bspC and bspF) by promoting the expression of topA, rpsL and BMEI1825 [<xref rid="B82-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">82</xref>]. Another indirect regulator Hfq, a bacterial protein mediating RNA&#8211;RNA interactions, is induced at the later stage of infection and affects VirB1 protein production, probably by mediating the sRNA&#8211;mRNA interaction [<xref rid="B83-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">83</xref>,<xref rid="B84-ijms-22-13637" ref-type="bibr">84</xref>].</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="conclusions" id="sec5-ijms-22-13637"><title>5. Conclusions Remarks</title><p>The intracellular replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> relies on the formation of T4SS encoded by VirB, which regulates host cell pathways through the release of effectors. VirB T4SS modifies the original vacuole into a replicative vacuole associated with the ER, ensuring bacterial survival, proliferation and egress. The VirB system plays a crucial role in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intracellular infection, but the process of T4SS formation encoded by VirB protein interaction and the mechanism of T4SS effectors remain to be further investigated. It is of great significance not only for understanding the pathogenic mechanism of bacteria, but also for the immune response of eukaryotes, and will contribute to the development of clinical vaccines.</p></sec></body><back><ack><title>Acknowledgments</title><p>We are very grateful to Huping Jiao from the College of Animal Sciences, Jilin University for editing the English language of this manuscript.</p></ack><fn-group><fn><p><bold>Publisher&#8217;s Note:</bold> MDPI stays neutral with regard to jurisdictional claims in published maps and institutional affiliations.</p></fn></fn-group><notes><title>Author Contributions</title><p>X.X. and H.J. wrote the manuscript.
