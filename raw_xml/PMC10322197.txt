      <abstract>
        <p>Over the past 10 years, immunization of cattle in Russia has been performed using vaccines from <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> strains 82, 19 and 75/79. To prevent brucellosis in small ruminants, two vaccines have been used, from the <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> strain REV-1 and the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 19; note that twice as many animals have been immunized with the former vaccine than with the latter vaccine. The disadvantage of using these preparations is the formation of prolonged post-vaccination seropositivity, which is especially pronounced in animals after immunization with vaccines from <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 19 and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain REV-1. This study aims to perform the whole genome sequencing of <italic>Brucella</italic> vaccine strains from the Russian collection. A bioinformatics analysis of the genomic data proved that the vaccine strains 75/79AB, 82, R-1096, and the KV 17/100 belong to ST-2, 104 M to ST-1, KV 13/100 to ST-5. This analysis allowed us to characterize vaccine strains&#x2019;s phylogenetic relationships and to prove the close relation of vaccine strains 75/79AB, 82, R-1096. Also, we defined candidate mutations in genes <italic>pmm, wbdA, wbkA, wboA,</italic> and <italic>eryB</italic>, which could be responsible for the attenuated virulence of vaccine strains. The complete genomic sequences of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains make further studies of bacterial pathogenicity determinants and virulence phenotype feasible, as well as their use in quality control of animal medicines.</p>

    <sec sec-type="intro" id="sec1">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>The development of measures for specific prophylaxis of brucellosis in the world began almost immediately from the moment of discovery of its causative agent. It was started by Bang who for the first time isolated microorganisms from the amniotic fluid of a cow that suffered abortion, selected the correct medium and determined optimal conditions for their cultivation (<xref rid="ref1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>). By infecting animals, he also proved that the isolated microorganisms are the causative agent of infectious abortion; he later noted that infected cows acquired certain immunity to this disease. It was further evidenced by observing cases of self-recovery of animals from infectious abortion. Based on this, Bang concluded that it is possible and necessary to develop measures for specific disease prophylaxis. Many researchers began to work in this direction, and, first of all, Bang himself.</p>
      <p>In 1906, Bang reported the results of experiments which allowed him to form an immunity in pregnant sheep, goats and cows by inoculating them with live virulent cultures of <italic>Brucella</italic>. In 1909, he for the first time in the field experiments performed the intravenous administration of live <italic>Brucella</italic> broth cultures to animals, as a result of which they developed a high degree of immune response, which, however, was accompanied by signs of anaphylactic shock.</p>
      <p>Since 1910, in England, Germany, Denmark, United States, and Argentina, agar swabs of <italic>Brucella</italic> cultures were used for mass vaccination of young and non-pregnant cows. Although the number of abortions in animals was slightly decreasing, the damage from the infection was about the same as in the natural course of the disease. The widespread use of virulent cultures caused abortions in pregnant animals and essentially led to their massive artificial infection. Thus, vaccinated animals presented an epizootic and epidemic danger.</p>
      <p>Due to this, later on strains with attenuated virulence were used for the production of vaccines. Attenuated virulence was achieved by treating virulent cultures of <italic>Brucella</italic> with chemical, physical and biological methods. Some of the researchers were able to achieve significant success.</p>
      <p>In 1934, Corner et al. (<xref rid="ref2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>) worked with a culture of <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> strain <italic>19</italic> isolated by Buck in 1923 from the milk of a cow of the third calving and found that after storage for one year at room temperature the culture spontaneously reduced its virulence. The authors were able to select an immunogenic and stable strain which subsequently was named Buck-19 [B-19] in honor of the author of the original culture. From that moment on, a new stage in the development of anti-brucellosis vaccines has begun, while the vaccine from strain 19 remains the standard of immunogenicity until now.</p>
      <p><italic>Brucella</italic> species are characterized by high invasiveness which allows them to successfully multiply in macrophages and lymphocytes (<xref rid="ref3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>). At the same time, <italic>Brucella</italic> lack virulence factors such as the capsule, flagella, fimbriae, pili, plasmids, toxins, exotoxins, cytolysins, and secreted proteases found in other bacteria (<xref rid="ref4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>, <xref rid="ref5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). Various virulence factors, the mechanism of evasion from the host defense systems and the method of intracellular survival of <italic>Brucella</italic> were reviewed in detail in (<xref rid="ref6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>). It was shown that the virulence of <italic>Brucella</italic> can be determined by: lipopolysaccharide (LPS) (<xref rid="ref7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>), &#x3B2;-glucan (&#x3B2;-cyclic glucan) (<xref rid="ref8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>, <xref rid="ref9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>), BvrS/BvrR, BacA (<xref rid="ref10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>), outer membrane proteins (Omps) (<xref rid="ref11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>), BmaC (<xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>), SagA (<xref rid="ref13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>), BtaE (<xref rid="ref14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>), BetB (<xref rid="ref15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>), MucR and T4SS genes found in <italic>vir</italic>B operon (<xref rid="ref16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>). The vital virulence factor, LPS, determines the morphology of <italic>Brucella</italic> colonies in culture. The smooth phenotype (S-form) is formed due to complete LPS, consisting of lipid A, core oligosaccharide, and O-side chains of the polysaccharide. The strains of <italic>Brucella</italic> with attenuated virulence often form rough colonies (R-form) due to a deficiency of O-side chains of the polysaccharide (<xref rid="ref17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>).</p>
      <p>In accordance with the List of Prokaryotic Names with Standing in Nomenclature (LPSN), the causative agent of brucellosis is assigned to the genus <italic>Brucella</italic>, family <italic>Brucellaceae</italic>, order <italic>Rhisobailes</italic>, class <italic>Alphaproteobacteria</italic>. The genus <italic>Brucella</italic> consists of 13 independent species. A total of six typical and seven new <italic>Brucella</italic> species were identified in a wide range of susceptible hosts. There are seven species that infect terrestrial animals, including <italic>B. abortus, Brucella melitensis, Brucella suis, Brucella ovis, Brucella canis, Brucella neotomae,</italic> and <italic>Brucella microti</italic> (<xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>); two more species, <italic>Brucella ceti</italic> and <italic>Brucella pinnipedialis</italic>, infect marine mammals (<xref rid="ref19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>). <italic>Brucella papionis</italic> was isolated from baboons and <italic>Brucella vulpis</italic> from red foxes (<xref rid="ref20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>). Seven biovars have been recognized for <italic>B. abortus</italic>, three for <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and five for <italic>B. suis</italic>. Other species are not subdivided into biovars. <italic>Brucella</italic> nomenclature is based on the species of the main hosts (<xref rid="ref21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>). As the list of species grows, it is important to identify more effective prophylaxis measures to control the spread of the disease in humans.</p>
      <p>Considering the complexity of differentiation of various species of <italic>Brucella</italic> bacteria, including using 16sRNA analysis for typing and comparison with other strains, we carried out whole genome sequencing and phylogenetic analysis by single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in order to comparatively assess the molecular-genetic characteristics of strains and analyze alternative options for the production of vaccines in Russia.</p>
    </sec>

      <sec id="sec5">
        <title>Microbiological cultivation methods</title>
        <p>Bacteria were cultivated on <italic>Brucella</italic> agar at 37&#xB0;C for 48&#x2009;h. A microbial suspension with a concentration of 1.7&#x2009;&#xD7;&#x2009;10<sup>9</sup> CFU/mL was disinfecte&#x2014;d by adding sodium merthiolate (0.01%) and incubating at 56&#xB0;C for 30 min. Additional incubation with CO<sub>2</sub> was not performed.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec6">
        <title>Method for studying immunogenicity</title>
        <p>The immunogenicity of the microorganism strains was studied in guinea pigs by introducing 10&#x2013;15 minimal infectious doses of the culture of the control virulent strain (<italic>B. abortus</italic> 54 VGNKI).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec7">
        <title>Extraction of genomic DNA</title>
        <p>Total genomic DNA was isolated using the DNA-sorb-V Kit (Central Research Institute for Epidemiology, Russia) in accordance with the manufacturer&#x2019;s instructions. DNA concentration was measured on a Quantus fluorimeter (Promega, United States) using the QuantiFluor<sup>&#xAE;</sup>ONE dsDNA System Kit (Promega, United States).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec8">
        <title>Genome sequencing, bioinformatics analysis</title>
        <p>DNA libraries were prepared using the Nextera XT DNA Sample Preparation Kit in accordance with the manufacturer&#x2019;s instructions. Whole genome sequencing was performed on a MiSeq system (Illumina, United States) in accordance with the standard operating procedure.</p>
        <p>For bioinformatics analysis of data of whole genome sequencing and <italic>de novo</italic> genome assembly, the following programs were used: FastQC 0.11.17 (<xref rid="ref31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>), Trimmomatic v.0.36 (<xref rid="ref32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>), SPAdes 2.11.1 (<xref rid="ref33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>), QUAST 4.6.3 (<xref rid="ref34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>), MAUVE v.20150226 (<xref rid="ref35" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>). Bacterial species identification and multilocus sequence typing (MLST) were performed <italic>via</italic> the online service of the Center for Genomic Epidemiology of the Danish University of Technology (CGE) using the KmerFinder server (version 3.0.2) and MLST server (version 2.0.4) (<xref rid="ref36" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>, <xref rid="ref37" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>). Annotation of bacterial genomes was performed using the RAST server (<xref rid="ref38" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>).</p>
        <p>The search for antibiotic resistance genes was carried out using the ResFinder 4.1 online service (<xref rid="ref39" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>) as well as the Arg-ANNOT, CARD and NCBI BARRGD databases (<xref rid="ref40" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>). Identification was carried out with the ABRicate program (<xref rid="ref41" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>) using BLASTN and BLASTX against nucleotide and amino acid sequences from various databases. To search for the main virulence factors in bacterial genomes, the Virulence Factor Database (VFDB) (<xref rid="ref42" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>) and Victors database were used (<xref rid="ref43" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>); to search for integrons, the IntegronFinder v5 program was used (<xref rid="ref44" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>). For phylogenetic analysis, the kSNP v.3.1.2 program was used (<xref rid="ref45" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>). For visualization, the service iTOL v.6.4.3 (Interactive Tree of Life) was used (<xref rid="ref46" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>).</p>

    <sec sec-type="results" id="sec9">
      <title>Results and discussion:</title>
      <p>An analysis of epizootic data from 1946 to the present day shows that brucellosis in cattle on the territory of Russia was not regx+istered only in the Kamchatka. Circulation of a total of 6 species of <italic>Brucell</italic>a was detected: <italic>B. abortus, B. melitensis, B. suis, B. neotomae, B. ovis</italic> and <italic>B. canis</italic>. The most virulent for humans are <italic>B. melitensis, B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. suis</italic>, and to a lesser extent <italic>B. canis, B. ceti</italic> and <italic>B. pinnipedialis</italic>. Therefore, when conducting laboratory diagnostics for brucellosis, it is important to perform not only the isolation of the culture of the pathogen, but also the identification of the specific species. To use as part of the composition for specific prophylaxis in animals, different authors proposed 25 strains of microorganisms of the genus <italic>Brucella</italic>, mainly <italic>B. abortus, B. melitensis</italic> and rarely <italic>B. suis</italic>. So far, however, only 9 of them were used in the composition of vaccines that were used to treat farm animals (<xref rid="tab2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>).</p>

      <p>For the mass prophylaxis of brucellosis in cattle, live vaccines from <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains 19, 82 and 75/79-AB were used in different volumes, in small ruminants&#x2014;from <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain Rev-1 and <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 19. It is important to note that there exist no <italic>Brucella</italic> vaccines for medical and veterinary use guaranteed to protect all those immunized. The degree of their protection depends on the virulence and dose of field <italic>Brucella</italic> cultures (<xref rid="ref56" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>). Until 1952, the measures against brucellosis in the Russia consisted of conducting diagnostic studies and removing sick animals from herds, without the use of anti-brucellosis vaccines. Due to the aggravation of the epizootic situation in 1953, the system of anti-brucellosis measures was expanded: it included immunization of animals with a vaccine from the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 19. This vaccine was used until 1975. During the period of active implementation of the vaccine, the disease was eliminated from many farms and even entire regions. However, in regions with a wide spread of brucellosis, the effectiveness of these sanation measures was insufficient. This was primarily due to the high agglutinogenicity of the vaccine. Agglutinins and complement-binding antibodies are preserved in the bodies of repeatedly immunized animals for up to 5&#x2013;8&#x2009;years, which makes it extremely difficult to differentiate such animals from those with brucellosis.</p>
      <p>According to official data (<xref rid="ref57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>), in the period of 2010&#x2013;2021 three vaccines were used in Russia for immunization of cattle (<xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure. 1</xref>), the main of which was the vaccine from the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 82. To prevent brucellosis in small ruminants, two vaccines were used (<xref rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">Figure. 2</xref>); among them, the vaccine from the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> REV-strain 1 was used to immunize twice as many animals as the vaccine from the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 19.</p>

      <p>As seen from the <xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figures 1</xref>, <xref rid="fig2" ref-type="fig">2</xref>, over the past 11&#x2009;years up to two million heads of cattle have been immunized annually with a vaccine from the <italic>B. aborus</italic> strain 82 (from 1,261.3 thousand heads in 2017 to 1,915.3 thousand heads in 2014). Despite that, today this vaccine, as almost any other live vaccine, does not fully meet the requirements of veterinary practice due to its abortogenic properties. Nonetheless, to a large extent, this problem can be solved by immunizing heifers at the age of 4&#x2013;6 and/or 12&#x2013;14&#x2009;months. Subsequently, sensitized animals can be immunized against brucellosis during pregnancy, but only if the infection with field cultures of <italic>Brucella</italic> is completely ruled out. However, the bulk of the breeding stock of cattle is immunized after calving.</p>
      <p>The <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain KV 17/100-VGNKI was obtained by targeted selection for cultural, morphological, biochemical and antigenic properties is very promising for the production of an inactivated vaccine with an adjuvant when constructing and testing a mini-batch (<xref rid="ref58" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>). This vaccine does not induce the synthesis of S-<italic>Brucella</italic> antibodies in diagnostic titers in animals not infected with <italic>Brucella</italic> but induces it in cattle with a latent form of brucellosis, which allows, due to the rapid removal of such animals from herds, to accelerate the recovery of affected farms from brucellosis. At the same time, it does not have abortogenic properties and can be used for immunization of pregnant cows and heifers. Moreover, since it is in an inactivated state, it does not pose an environmental hazard.</p>
      <p>Next, we performed whole genome sequencing of six vaccine strains: <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> strain 82, <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 75/79-AB and <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain R-1096; <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain KV 17/100 and <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain 104 M used as an antigen to provoke latent forms of brucellosis; and <italic>B. abortus</italic> strain KV 13/100 used to control the immunogenic activity of inactivated vaccines (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>).</p>

      <sec id="sec11">
        <title>Phylogenetic analysis of <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> vaccine strains</title>
        <p>For typing and comparison with other strains, a phylogenetic analysis was carried out using single nucleotide substitutions.</p>
        <p>To construct a phylogenetic tree based on SNP loci, we selected the genomes of vaccine strains and field isolates from the NCBI database that were assembled completely or at the scaffold level and added 6 vaccine strains from the All-Russian State Collection of Microorganisms Used in Veterinary Medicine and Animal Husbandry. The list of strains from the NCBI database is presented in <xref rid="tab1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>. Pan-genome analysis was carried out using the program kSNP v.3.1. In total, we identified 14,768 SNPs, of which 11,510 are core SNPs. The rooted tree of all SNPs was constructed using the maximum parsimony method (<xref rid="fig3" ref-type="fig">Figure. 3</xref>). When constructing the tree, the genome of <italic>Brucella melitensis bv</italic>. 1 str. 16 M (NC_003317.1) was used as an outgroup.</p>

            <p>The bold font indicates missense mutations occurred in conservative sites of proteins.</p>

        <p>So, the virulence genes <italic>wbdA</italic> and <italic>wboA</italic> is absent in the vaccine strain R-1096. The IS711-like insertion sequence destroys the <italic>pmm</italic> gene in the vaccine strain 13/100. The <italic>eryB</italic> gene in strain 104 M codes a non-functional truncated protein because of nonsense mutation W215X. The attenuated phenotype of the strains KV 17/100, 75/79-AB, and 82 can be explained by missense mutations that occur in conservative sites of functional domains in virulence proteins. The strain KV 17/100 has a substitution mutation G255V in a very conservative site of the glycosyl transferase domain of the wbkA protein (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref>). Both strains 75/79-AB and 82 have a substitution W219L located in a conservative site of the glycosyl transferase domain of the <italic>wboA</italic> protein (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S2</xref>). The strain KB 17/100 has two substitutions N255K in the <italic>pmm</italic> gene and A321 in the <italic>wbkA</italic> gene, which can also negatively impact the function of corresponding proteins because they are in semi-conservative sites (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figures S3</xref>, <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">S4</xref>).</p>
        <p>Thus, the five vaccine strains analyzed here are deficient in classical virulence genes responsible for the biosynthesis of LPS O-chain: <italic>pmm</italic>, <italic>wbdA</italic>, <italic>wbkA</italic>, <italic>wboA</italic> (<xref rid="ref63" ref-type="bibr">63</xref>). These mutations can be responsible for these vaccine strains&#x2019; R-form type of colonies (<xref rid="tab3" ref-type="table">Table 3</xref>). The deficiency of the <italic>wboA</italic> gene observed here in strains R-1096, 75/79-AB, and 82 was reported earlier for vaccine strain RB51 (<xref rid="ref64" ref-type="bibr">64</xref>).</p>
        <p>Yu et al. (<xref rid="ref22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>) described a set of candidate genes associated with virulence attenuation in vaccine strain 104 M. We complement this set with gene <italic>eryB</italic>, whose function is impaired by preliminary stop codon W215X. The gene <italic>eryB</italic> is a part of operon eryABCD encoding the erythritol dissimilative pathway (<xref rid="ref65" ref-type="bibr">65</xref>, <xref rid="ref66" ref-type="bibr">66</xref>) reported that the growth of vaccine strain B19 was inhibited by erythritol due to mutations in genes <italic>eryC</italic> and e<italic>ryD</italic>.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="conclusions" id="sec13">
      <title>Conclusion</title>
      <p>This study represents a comprehensive bioinformatics analysis of genomes of 6 <italic>Brucella</italic> vaccine strains. The results of our study serve as a prerequisite for improving the diagnostics of brucellosis in animals and will allow to reduce the risks associated with the spread of this infection in Russia, as well as to improve the control over the quality and safety of anti-brucellosis immunobiological agents.</p>
