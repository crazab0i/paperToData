The primary objective of this study was to present a comprehensive understanding of the prevalence and features of <italic>Brucella</italic> strains within the industrial dairy farming sector in Iran. Rose Bengal plate test, standard agglutination test, and indirect enzyme linked immunosorbent assay tests were used to confirm all seropositive animals. A total number of 1,311 bovine samples from seropositive animals including were collected from 224 farms in 21 provinces of different regions of Iran and examined. The discovered <italic>Brucella</italic> isolates were phenotyped and molecularly characterized. The isolates were all <italic>B. abortus</italic> or <italic>B. melitensis</italic>. Bacteria analysis revealed that 70.53% of seropositive farms were tested positive for <italic>Brucella</italic> strains, predominantly <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 (43.42%) and <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 (27.11%). Also, the prevalence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 2, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 3, <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1, <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 2 and RB51 vaccine were restricted to certain provinces. AMOS (<italic>abortus melitensis ovis suis</italic>)-polymerase chain reaction and Bruce-ladder PCR confirmed species identification. These results highlighted the complexity of bovine brucellosis in Iran and illustrated that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> was spread from small ruminants to cattle. This study provided important epidemiological insights for targeting future brucellosis control programs in the Iranian dairy farms. </p></abstract><kwd-group><title>Key Words</title><kwd>Agricultural economies</kwd><kwd>Bovine brucelosis</kwd><kwd>Brucella abortus</kwd><kwd>Brucella melitensi</kwd><kwd>Industrial dairy cattle farms</kwd></kwd-group></article-meta></front>

    <sec sec-type="intro">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>Zoonotic infection of brucellosis, is caused by various bacterial species from the <italic>Brucella</italic> genus, predominantly <italic>Brucella abortus</italic>, <italic>B.</italic>
<italic>melitensis</italic>, and <italic>B.</italic>
<italic>suis</italic>. This disease is closely linked to development of agricultural communities where animal husbandry plays a vital role. <italic>B. abortus</italic> is facultative intracellular pathogen that is capable of causing long-lasting infections in animals and has been found in various livestock species. More specifically, <italic>B. abortus</italic> is frequently found in cows which act as its primary host and cause bovine brucellosis.<sup><xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref></sup> This bacterial infection is a major concern for both farmers and policy makers because of its negative impact on animal health and the agricultural industry as a whole.<sup><xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup> Bovine brucellosis, a highly contagious and economically significant disease, continues to pose a significant threat to livestock in Iran. In recent years, there has been an alarming increase in bovine brucellosis cases in Iran. This increase can be attributed to several factors including ineffective control strategies, limited public awareness and inadequate veterinary services in certain regions.<sup><xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref></sup> Also, the practice of diverse agriculture, which includes the keeping of buffalo, cows, goats, and sheep has resulted in a higher risk of brucellosis. In this scenario, small ruminants serve as primary hosts for <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, while cattle serve as secondary hosts.<sup><xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref></sup> In this regard, many farmers face significant losses as infected cattle experience reduced milk production, reproductive problems and even death. In an infected herd, some animals show symptoms while others remain latent. The most common sign is miscarriage as well as other signs such as frequent and unsuccessful inseminations, reduced milk production, retained placenta, metritis, arthritis and orchitis.<sup><xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref></sup> In Iran, the key causative agents responsible for bovine brucellosis have been reported as different types of <italic>B. abortus</italic> and occasionally, <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, that play a significant role in spread of the disease among cattle herds.<sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> Furthermore, a different investigation found that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and <italic>B. abortus</italic> were prevalent in cattle.<sup><xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref></sup> As a result, it is crucial to accurately determine the specific <italic>Brucella</italic> species and biotypes in order to effectively implement control and eradication initiatives. This serves to clearly indicate the prevailing <italic>Brucella</italic> species and biotypes in a particular region or country as well as monitoring vaccine strains used during vaccination campaigns. Also, this information can aid in tracking and managing newly introduced strains and it is essential for accurately assessing the epidemiological situation of livestock herds and countries. </p>
      <p>The evaluation of <italic>Brucella</italic>-specific genes plays a crucial role in assessing the efficacy of PCR-based detection methods for <italic>Brucella</italic> detection. Currently, there exist specialized primers and probes that are tailored for the purpose of detecting <italic>Brucella</italic>. Numerous researches have been conducted, primarily centering around the genes <italic>IS711</italic>, <italic>16S rRNA</italic>, <italic>bcsp31</italic>, <italic>BMEI1162</italic>, <italic>BMEII0466</italic>, <italic>eryC</italic>, <italic>alkB</italic>, and <italic>per</italic>.<sup><xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref></sup> The <italic>IS711</italic> gene, along with other genes, serves as a valuable molecular marker in the diagnosis and characterization of <italic>Brucella. </italic>However, the <italic>IS711</italic> gene is highly specific to the <italic>Brucella</italic> genus and is present in multiple copies within the genome of <italic>Brucella</italic> species. Different <italic>Brucella</italic> species possess distinct patterns and numbers of <italic>IS711</italic> copies allowing for the differentiation of various <italic>Brucella</italic> strains. These tools contribute not only to accurate and rapid identification of <italic>Brucella</italic> species but also play a crucial role in understanding the epidemiology, evolution and dynamics of brucellosis.<sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref></sup> On the other hand, AMOS (<italic>abortus melitensis ovis suis</italic>)- and Bruce-ladder polymerase chain reaction (PCR) are molecular based techniques that leverage the variability in tandem repeat regions of the <italic>Brucella</italic> genome to differentiate between strains and species providing valuable information for epidemiological studies and disease tracking. Bricker and Halling developed the AMOS PCR assay to differentiate four <italic>Brucella</italic> species including <italic>B. abortus</italic>, <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, <italic>Brucella ovis</italic>, and <italic>B. suis</italic>.<sup><xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref></sup></p>
      <p>The primary aim of this study was to distinguish <italic>Brucella</italic> isolates in industrial dairy cattle farms through the utilization of both traditional methods and molecular approaches. Additionally, we aimed to monitor the occurrence of common biotypes <italic>Brucella</italic> strains, and vaccine strains in the field. </p>

    <sec sec-type="materials|methods">
      <title>Materials and Methods</title>
      <p>
<bold>Ethical statement. </bold>All dairy cattle in this investigation were treated considering to ethical standards for field study approved by the Iranian Veterinary Organization (Tehran, Iran). Approval for the research was granted by Ethics Committee for Health Research at Razi Vaccine and Serum Research Institute in June 2022 (Reference: IR.RVSRI.REC. 1402.002). The animals were slaughtered according to the test-and-slaughter programs of Iranian Veterinary Organization. The purpose of the study was explained to the dairy farmers and their consent was obtained.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Study area. </bold>This study was performed from June 2016 to June 2023, on 364 milk samples, 907 bovine lymph nodes, nine samples of abomasum content, and 31 aborted fetuses to evaluate the presence of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. in seropositive dairy cattle that tested by Rose Bengal plate test (RBPT), standard agglutination test (SAT), and indirect enzyme linked immunosorbent assay tests (I-ELISA) methods. A variety of specimens were collected from 21 provinces located across different regions of Iran (Tehran, East Azerbaijan, Alborz, West Azerbaijan, Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari, Ardabil, Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad, Kermanshah, Markazi, Lorestan, Isfahan, Qazvin, Hamadan, Ilam, Qom, Zanjan, Semnan, Yazd, Fars, Kerman and Mazandaran). These regions are critical for the dairy industry and boast a vibrant agricultural sphere.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Sample collection. </bold>Sampling was conducted on available seropositive lactating cattle within the herds of industrial dairy cattle farms using RBPT, Wright, and I-ELISA tests. A total of 1,311 specimens were sampled from 224 farms (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). The milk and lymph node samples from different seropositive farms were collected by the veterinary organization of each province and were sent to department of Brucellosis, Razi Vaccine and Serum Research Institute for analysis (Karaj, Iran). Lymph node specimens were collected from supra-mammary and retropharyngeal regions of slaughtered animals in sterile plastic bags and placed on ice and immediately transported to the laboratory. These preserved samples were intended for subsequent <italic>Brucella</italic> culture and isolation procedures.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Serological analysis. </bold>The sera of 1,311 blood samples were isolated using a centrifugation process at 3,000 rpm for 5 min. The serum from each sample was subjected to testing using RBPT, SAT (Razi Vaccine and Serum Research Institute, Karaj, Iran), and I-ELISA methods (IDEXX, Montpellier, France). The I-ELISA test was conducted according to the guidelines specified by the manufacturer, available at this link: (https://www.id-vet.com/produit/id-screen-brucellosis-serum-indirect-multi-species/). Serum specimens were deemed positive if the titers were equal to or exceeded 1:80. Dairy cattle farm are known as reactors when their serum of blood in official serological tests was positive. </p>
      <p>
<bold>
<italic>Brucella</italic>
</bold>
<bold> isolation. </bold>Bacteriological examinations were carried out within the protective environment of safety hoods. Bacterial culture was performed on samples of milk, aborted fetal organs and abomasum content of dairy cattle. The initial isolation of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. was involved by inoculating the clinical samples onto a specialized medium of <italic>Brucella</italic> Selective Supplement (Oxoid, Basingstoke, UK). This supplement contained various components such as cycloheximide (50.00 mg), nystatin (50.00 IU), bacitracin (12.50 IU), polymyxin B (2.50 IU), vancomycin (10.00 mg) and nalidixic acid (2.50 mg). Additionally, the culture medium used was comprised of inactivated horse serum (5.00%) in <italic>Brucella</italic> agar (HiMedia,&#xA0;Thane, India). These cultures were then incubated for a period of 10 days maintaining a temperature of 37.00 &#x2DA;C while also sustaining an atmosphere of 10.00% CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup> The milk samples underwent a 15-min centrifugation at 3,500 rpm followed by the cultivation of both sediments and the creamy upper layer. If no visible growth occurred after a 14-day incubation, the bacterial cultures were disposed. Distinct colonies representing <italic>Brucella</italic> spp. were then transferred to new culture media of <italic>Brucella</italic> agar for detailed identification and biotyping analysis.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Biotyping. </bold>The classic method of biotyping followed the protocol outlined previously.<sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup> At this facility, standard diagnostic procedures utilized <italic>Brucella</italic> monospecific antisera A and M, along with the <italic>Brucella</italic> reference phage from Tbilisi, Izzatnagar. A diverse range of biotyping tests were conducted including assessments for H<sub>2</sub>S production, dependence to CO<sub>2</sub>, agglutination using specific <italic>Brucella</italic> antisera, agglutination with acriflavine, growth in media with thionine and basic fuchsin. Interpretation of the test results was in accordance with the guidelines provided in the manual of World Organization for Animal Health.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Molecular typing. </bold>The genomic DNA was obtained by subjecting a small amount of bacterial material suspended in 300 &#xB5;L of high-quality water for molecular biology to a heat treatment at 100 &#x2DA;C for a duration of 15 min.<sup><xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref></sup> The mixture was thoroughly mixed using a vortex and then subjected to centrifugation at a force of 13,000 <italic>g</italic> for 5 min. Following this, the liquid portion containing DNA was gathered and kept at a temperature of &#x2013; 20.00 &#x2DA;C until it was needed for subsequent analysis.<sup><xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref></sup> The DNA isolated underwent a PCR assay utilizing the <italic>IS711</italic> marker to detect the presence of <italic>Brucella</italic> spp (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>).<sup><xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15&#xA0;</xref></sup>Species-level molecular characterization was additionally carried out through a composite Bruce-ladder PCR (Bio-Rad&#xA0;Laboratories, Hercules, USA) utilizing the subsequent parameters (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>): Step 1 (first denaturation): 95.00 &#x2DA;C for 5 min, step 2 (second denaturation): 95.00 &#x2DA;C for 30 sec, step 3 (annealing): 56.00 &#x2DA;C for 90 sec, step 4 (first extension): 72.00 &#x2DA;C for 3 min, step 5 (final extension): 72.00 &#x2DA;C for 10 min. Step 2, 3, and 4 were reiterated for a total of 30 cycles.<sup><xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref></sup> The resulting PCR products were resolved through electrophoresis (Bio-Rad&#xA0;Laboratories), utilizing a gel made from 1.50% agarose. </p>

    <sec sec-type="results">
      <title>Results</title>
      <p>A total number of 420 <italic>Brucella</italic> strains were extracted from a diverse range of seropositive sources originating from 158 out of 224 distinct farms (70.53%). These sources encompassed lymph nodes (239 isolates), milk (161 isolates), aborted fetuses (14 isolates), and abomasum contents (six isolates). These isolated bacteria exhibited typical phenotypic features commonly associated with the <italic>Brucella</italic> genus. The growth behaviour of all strains was observed in an environment with 10.00% carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) following an incubation period of 3 to 5 days at a temperature of 37.00 &#x2DA;C. Gram-negative characteristics were evident in the isolated bacteria and they formed small colonies with a distinctive translucent, honey-colored and glossy appearance, featuring a smooth surface. The characterization of the isolates extended to the biovar level with the identification of each isolate being confirmed to the species or vaccine level using AMOS PCR and Bruce-ladder techniques. These isolates represented either <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, <italic>B. abortus</italic>, or the <italic>B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain RB51.</p>
      <p>
<bold>Brucella</bold>
<bold>species</bold><bold>/</bold><bold>biovars</bold>
<bold>geographical</bold>
<bold>distribution</bold><bold>. </bold>It has been shown that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 was the most prevalent biovar in dairy cattle farms of 16 provinces in Iran (Alborz, Tehran, Fars, Qom, Kerman, Isfahan, Semnan, Qazvin, Lorestan, Chaharmahal and Bakhtiari, Zanjan, Hamedan, Kermanshah, Kohgiluyeh and Boyer-Ahmad, West Azerbaijan and, East Azerbaijan). The <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 2 was only isolated in Kerman province, while <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 3 was isolated from the four provinces of Fars, Kerman, Isfahan and Ardabil. <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 was identified from samples the three provinces of Fars, Qom and Yazd. <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 2 was only isolated in Yazd Province, and <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 was identified in six provinces of Tehran, Fars, Qom, Isfahan, Lorestan, and Hamedan. Also, the strain of RB51 vaccine was detected from provinces of Kermanshah and Alborz (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Fig. 1</xref>).</p>
      <p>
<bold>
<italic>Brucella abortus</italic>
</bold>
<bold>. </bold>A total number of 213 different strains of <italic>B. abortus</italic> were found across 61 farms spanning various provinces, including Tehran, Fars, Yazd, Qom, Isfahan, Lorestan, and Hamedan. The majority of <italic>B. abortus</italic> cases (105 cases) were linked to lymph nodes with 86 cases originating from milk samples, six cases from abomasum content, and 14 cases from aborted fetuses. </p>
      <p>The biotyping results were revealed with the presence of <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 (5 cases), biovar 2 (1 case), and biovar 3 (205 cases). The final two cases isolated from lymph node samples represented the strain of <italic>B. abortus</italic> RB51 vaccine according to Bruce-ladder typing. Notably, the AMOS PCR detected the <italic>B. abortus</italic> specific band (498 bp) exclusively in biovar 1 and 2 isolates, in line with its capability to identify biovars 1, 2, and 4 (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2A</xref>).<sup><xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref></sup> It is worth noting that all isolates were confirmed as <italic>B. abortus</italic> through the Bruce ladder PCR, revealing PCR products with sizes of 152, 450, 587, 794, and 1,682 bp (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Fig. 2B</xref>). </p>

    <sec sec-type="discussion">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>Given the widespread occurrence of brucellosis in Iran, it is essential to have a deep understanding of the clinical and epidemiological aspects of virulent <italic>Brucella</italic> species. This knowledge is critical for enhancing the accuracy of diagnosis, prevention and control measures. The most reliable method for diagnosing brucellosis in animals remains bacterial culture and isolation followed by comprehensive bacteriological testing and biotyping.<sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref></sup> In numerous studies conducted in Iran, <italic>Brucella</italic> infection has been primarily reported through PCR and serology tests. However, there has been a limited focus on directly identifying the presence of <italic>Brucella</italic> species and their biovars in recent research.<sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup> To address this gap, we conducted a thorough analysis using a combination of bacteriological and molecular techniques. Our study aimed to provide a comprehensive characterization of <italic>Brucella</italic> biodiversity within the industrial dairy cattle farms affected by the infection in Iran. The results we presented here represented a passive surveillance effort spanning seven years. These findings significantly contributed to our understanding of the specific <italic>Brucella</italic> species and biovars currently linked to the disease in industrial dairy cattle farms of Iran, clearly highlighting a notable prevalence of both <italic>B. melitensis </italic>and<italic> B. abortus</italic>. When focusing on dairy cattle farms, our observations indicated that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> was predominantly associated with cattle in different provinces of Iran. In contrast, <italic>B. abortus</italic> was prevalent in some provinces in dairy cattle farms. These findings were consistent with the growing trend of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> isolation, particularly in cattle, especially in regions such as Africa and the Middle East,<sup><xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref></sup><sup>-</sup><sup><xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref></sup> and with previous observations in Iran.<sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> In this study, bovine brucellosis primarily seemed to be linked to <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, affecting 43.50% of infected farms. There was a lesser prevalence of <italic>B. abortus</italic>, impacting 27.20% of these farms. This pattern was consistent with the belief that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> is present in dairy cattle.<sup><xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref></sup><sup>-</sup><sup><xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref></sup> Despite the small ruminant vaccination program, our findings indicated that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> has spilled over from these ruminants to cattle. Specifically, in the Iran, cattle must be regarded as a potential reservoir of both <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> for transmission to human. According to our results, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 and <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 was the biovar predominantly isolated from dairy cattle with rarer isolation of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovars 2, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovars 3,<italic> B. abortus</italic> biovar 2 and <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1. These findings were in agreement with previous research conducted in various regions across the globe, indicating that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovars 1 are extensively prevalent in cattle.<sup><xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref></sup>
</p>
      <p>According to our findings, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovars 1 (94.74%), 2 (1.00%) and 3 (4.33%) were the species that have been isolated in 97 dairy cattle farms from lymph node and milk samples. <italic>B. </italic>melitensis biovar 1 initially emerged after being identified in a sheep located in Isfahan (the center of Iran). Subsequently, it spread to various regions within Iran, causing infections in not only sheep and goats, but also in cattle, camels, dogs and humans.<sup><xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> The investigation performed by Zowghi <italic>et al</italic>., involving the culture of animal fetuses, placentas, lymph nodes, milk, vaginal swabs and human bone marrow and blood identified a total number of 2,413 strains of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> that underwent the process of identification and revealed that <italic>B.</italic>
<italic>melitensis</italic> strains were isolated from cattle, sheep, goats, camels, dogs, and humans in Iran.<sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> The <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 was also isolated in cows from Egypt,<sup><xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref></sup> South Africa,<sup><xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref></sup> Azerbaijan,<sup><xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref></sup> Kenya,<sup><xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref></sup> Spain,<sup><xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref></sup> China,<sup><xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref></sup> Kuwait,<sup><xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref></sup> Syria,<sup><xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref></sup> Algeria,<sup><xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref></sup> and Uganda.<sup><xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref></sup> The present study also showed the common prevalence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in dairy cattle farms of Iran. </p>
      <p>The <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 was emerged as the dominant strain in bovines consistent with a prior epidemiological investigations conducted in Iran identifying this particular biovar as the primary and most virulent variant affecting cattle.<sup><xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref></sup> Nevertheless, our findings indicated that <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 was similarly implicated in causing abortions among dairy cows in the provinces of Fars and Isfahan much like documented occurrences in Europe, Kenya, China and T&#xFC;rkiye.<sup><xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref></sup> Based on our findings, the occurrence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 2 was solely documented in Kerman bovine population. Despite its predominance in China,<sup><xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref></sup> this particular biovar appears to exhibit a reduced presence in regions encompassing the Middle East and Mediterranean. Records indicate prior identifications of<italic> B. melitensis </italic>biovar 2 in Saudi Arabia, Iran and T&#xFC;rkiye.<sup><xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref></sup>
</p>
      <p>Notably, our findings corroborated the presence of this variant in cattle. It appears that a proportion of 207 out of 420 cows with detectable culture-positive results were found to carry <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in their milk and lymph node. This indicated the presence of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> infection in small ruminants which might have been transmitted to dairy cattle. As a result, it is crucial to thoroughly assess the effectiveness of the Rev.1 vaccination program in small ruminants. One way to achieve this was by examining the antibody titers of vaccinated animals, which could provide insights into the extent of vaccination coverage and its efficacy. Recent research conducted in Mongolia proposed that a protective herd immunity could be attained by vaccinating around 60.00% of the small ruminant population.<sup><xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref></sup> Due to the discovery that 97 out of 158 dairy cattle farms with positive <italic>Brucella</italic> results carried an infection of <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, it is proposed that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> infections in small ruminants still play a significant role in cattle infections. To verify this issue, the isolation of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains from sheep and goats is necessary for genetic contrast. The potential transmission of the infection to other cattle and humans could arise from cows expelling <italic>B. melitensis</italic>. Two isolations of <italic>B. aborts</italic> RB51 from two bovine lymph node confirmed that they received RB51 dosage. However, it proposes relying solely on RB51 vaccination proves inadequate for managing brucellosis in areas where the disease is prevalent. To effectively address this widespread issue, it is essential to integrate vaccination with comprehensive control measures. These measures encompass implementing livestock management practices among farmers, initiating educational initiatives to enforce stringent hygiene protocols and undertaking the removal of seropositive dairy cattle. By combining these approaches, awareness about this pervasive ailment among farmers can be heightened, potentially leading to a reduction in the prevalence of brucellosis.<sup><xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref></sup><sup>,</sup><sup><xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">35</xref></sup>
</p>
      <p>In conclusion, the outcomes of our study, employing classical and molecular techniques, revealed the manifestation of brucellosis infection in dairy cattle farms of Iran despite of vaccination. This manifestation indicated the proliferation of diverse biovars of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> and <italic>B. abortus</italic>. Geographical distribution revealed that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 1 was the most common in dairy cow farms that this issue highlighted the complexity of bovine brucellosis in Iran and illustrated that <italic>B. melitensis</italic> was spread from small ruminants to cattle. </p>
      <p>Serological methods unquestionably play a crucial role in identifying and detecting brucellosis in domesticated Bovidae. The techniques of RBPT, SAT, and I-ELISA are frequently employed to assess animal sera in initiatives aimed at managing livestock disease. The RBPT, functioning as rapid and cost-effective point-of-care assessments, carry the risk of producing inaccurate positive outcomes due to their potential to react with various non-<italic>Brucella</italic> antigens. This particular method has found widespread application as a rapid test, offering greater sensitivity albeit at the expense of reduced specificity. A few comprehensive investigations regarding the circulating <italic>Brucella</italic> isolates in dairy cattle farms of Iran are available. Nevertheless, despite this recent information, there are significant gaps that still exist in Iranian literature about how prevalent <italic>B. melitensis</italic> is in cattle. It is absolutely necessary to conduct more extensive research in order to fully understand the disease spread in dairy cattle farms. This understanding will eventually serve as the foundation for creating potential strategies to manage and stop the spread of bovine brucellosis in the endemic area. The Iranian government has recognized the severity of the situation and has made efforts to combat bovine brucellosis through vaccination campaigns, educational programs and improved diagnostic methods. However, these measures have been met with limited success due to financial constraints and the vast geographical spread of the disease.</p>
