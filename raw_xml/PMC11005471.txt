While most <italic>B. abortus</italic>, <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, and <italic>B. suis</italic> biovars grow slowly in complex media, they multiply intensely in livestock genitals and placenta indicating high metabolic capacities. Mutant analyses <italic>in vitro</italic> and in infection models emphasize that erythritol (abundant in placenta and genitals) is a preferred substrate of brucellae, and suggest hexoses, pentoses, and gluconeogenic substrates use in host cells. While <italic>Brucella</italic> sugar and erythritol catabolic pathways are known, growth on 3&#x2013;4 carbon substrates persists in Fbp- and GlpX-deleted mutants, the canonical gluconeogenic fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (F1,6bP) bisphosphatases. Exploiting the prototrophic and fast-growing properties of <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5, we show that gluconeogenesis requires fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba); the existence of a novel broad substrate bisphosphatase (Bbp) active on sedoheptulose 1,7-bisphosphate (S1,7bP), F1,6bP, and other phosphorylated substrates; that <italic>Brucella</italic> Fbp unexpectedly acts on S1,7bP and F1,6bP; and that, while active in <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, GlpX is disabled in <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5. Thus, two Fba-dependent reactions (dihydroxyacetone-phosphate + glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate &#x21CC; F1,6bP; and dihydroxyacetone-phosphate + erythrose 4-phosphate &#x21CC; S1,7bP) can, respectively, yield fructose 6-phosphate and sedoheptulose 7-phosphate for classical gluconeogenesis and the Pentose Phosphate Shunt (PPS), the latter reaction opening a new gluconeogenic route. Since erythritol generates the PPS-intermediate erythrose 4-phosphate, and the Fba/Fbp-Bbp route predicts sedoheptulose 7-phosphate generation from erythrose 4-phosphate, we re-examined the erythritol connections with PPS. Growth on erythritol required transaldolase or the Fba/Fbp-Bbp pathway, strongly suggesting that Fba/Fbp-Bbp works as a PPS entry for both erythritol and gluconeogenic substrates in <italic>Brucella</italic>. We propose that, by increasing erythritol channeling into PPS through these peculiar routes, brucellae proliferate in livestock genitals and placenta in the high numbers that cause abortion and infertility, and make brucellosis highly contagious. These findings could be the basis for developing attenuated brucellosis vaccines safer in pregnant animals.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd><italic>Brucella</italic></kwd><kwd>erythritol</kwd><kwd>gluconeogenesis</kwd><kwd>placenta</kwd><kwd>abortion</kwd></kwd-group><funding-group><funding-statement>The author(s) declare financial support was received for the research, authorship, and/or publication of this article.     <sec sec-type="intro" id="sec1">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>Members of the genus <italic>Brucella</italic> cause brucellosis, a highly contagious zoonosis severely affecting animal production and human health worldwide (<xref rid="ref1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>). Phylogenomic analyses show that most brucellae form a core group (the &#x201C;classical&#x201D; species <italic>B. abortus, B. melitensis, B. suis, B. neotomae, B. ovis,</italic> and <italic>B. canis</italic>, and their biovars, plus <italic>B. pinnipedialis, B. ceti</italic>, <italic>B. papionis,</italic> and <italic>B. microti</italic>) separated from early diverging brucellae and environmental <italic>Ochrobactrum</italic> species (<xref rid="ref2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>, <xref rid="ref3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>).</p>
      <p>The best-known brucellae are facultative intracellular parasites able to establish long-lasting infections. While their ability to hinder innate immunity detection and to control intracellular trafficking are key traits of their pathogenicity (<xref rid="ref4" ref-type="bibr">4&#x2013;8</xref>), the metabolism underpinning their capacity to multiply in the hosts is imperfectly known. In livestock, these bacteria display a characteristic tropism for genitals and placenta, where their multiplication in high numbers (up to 10<sup>14</sup> bacteria per conceptus) causes intense tissue damage leading to infertility, abortion, and a copious release of the pathogen. Such intense multiplication is thought to be linked to the use of erythritol, abundant in those tissues (<xref rid="ref9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>) and, as these brucellae do not multiply in the environment, it plays a key role in pathogen transmission. Moreover, studies in infection models suggest that brucellae multiply in host cells through the combined and/or sequential use of hexoses, pentoses, amino acids, and gluconeogenic substrates (<xref rid="ref10" ref-type="bibr">10&#x2013;14</xref>). While pathways for erythritol, hexose, and pentose metabolism are known (<xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>), mutants simultaneously disabled in Fbp and GlpX (the only known fructose 1,6-bisphosphate bisphosphatases, [FBPases]) grow on 3- and 4-carbon substrates (<xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>, <xref rid="ref14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>). This is unexpected because these enzymes should be essential under these conditions as they catalyze the only irreversible gluconeogenic step linking 3 and 4 carbon precursors with glucose synthesis and the Pentose Phosphate Shunt (PPS) (<xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>). Since glucose and the PPS provide indispensable precursors for envelope and nucleic acid synthesis, this observation suggests a new FBPase and/or gluconeogenic pathway in this intracellular parasite.</p>

      <p>Described as fastidious because of their slow growth on the rich media used routinely and for primary isolation, most core brucellae grow slowly on relatively simple synthetic media (<xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">12&#x2013;16</xref>), and <italic>B. microti</italic> and <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5 are comparatively fast-growing (<xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>, <xref rid="ref17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>). Specifically, <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5 grows fast on combinations of lactate, glycerol, and glutamate, thus showing broad biosynthetic abilities obviously including gluconeogenesis (<xref rid="ref12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>). Taking advantage of these characteristics of <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5, we first show that gluconeogenesis in these bacteria requires fructose-bisphosphate aldolase (Fba), an enzyme involved in the synthesis of fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (F1,6bP) and also able to catalyze sedoheptulose 1,7-bisphosphate (S1,7bP) generation. Then, we identified a broad-spectrum phosphatase (Bbp) active on F1,6bP, S1,7bP plus other phosphorylated polyols, and that <italic>Brucella</italic> Fbp (a classical fructose 1,6-bisphosphatase) is also active on these two bisphosphorylated substrates, while GlpX is inactive in <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5 (but not in <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic>). Accordingly, the <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5 model shows that two <italic>Brucella</italic> enzymes, Fbp and Bbp enable gluconeogenesis through both the classical fructose 6-phosphate pathway and sedoheptulose 7-phosphate, the latter being a new route. Significantly, the new route makes erythritol fueling into PPP very efficient, thus accounting for the intense multiplication of these bacteria in genital tissues, with the subsequent pathological effects.</p>

    <sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="sec2">
      <title>Materials and methods</title>
      <sec id="sec3">
        <title>Bacterial strains and plasmids</title>
        <p>These materials are listed in <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Tables 1, 2</xref>, respectively. This study uses strains gifted from Instituto Nacional de Investiga&#xE7;&#xE3;o Agr&#xE1;ria e Veterin&#xE1;ria (<italic>B. suis</italic> 513), Virginia Tech (<italic>E. coli</italic> JLD2402), Technische Universit&#xE4;t Braunschweig (<italic>E. coli</italic> S17&#x3BB;pir), and Colorado State University (<italic>E. coli</italic> SM10&#x3BB;pir [pTNS2] and <italic>E. coli</italic> HB101 [pRK2013]). The University of Navarra did not require the study to be reviewed or approved by an ethics committee because the strains were a gift from the above-mentioned institutions. All brucellae were handled under BSL-3 containment.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec4">
        <title>Culture conditions</title>
        <p><italic>Brucella</italic> strains were routinely grown in peptone-glucose broth (tryptic soy broth [TSB], Biomerieux) or in TSB-agar (TSA). When appropriate, the following antibiotics (all from Sigma) were used: kanamycin (Km; 50&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL), ampicillin (Amp; 200&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL), polymyxin (Pmx; 1.5&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL), and/or chloramphenicol (Cm; 20&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL). When needed, media were supplemented with 5% sucrose. The media used to study the phenotype of the metabolic mutants were peptone-glucose, Gerhardt&#x2019;s defined medium (glutamate-lactate-glycerol) (<xref rid="ref16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>), or Plommet&#x2019;s vitamin-mineral salts (<xref rid="ref15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>) modified by Barbier et al. (<xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>) (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table 3</xref>). BL21 cells were grown in Luria-Broth (LB) or in M9 minimal medium (Sigma) supplemented with glucose (1&#x2009;mM), MgSO<sub>4</sub> (1&#x2009;mM) and CaCl<sub>2</sub> (1&#x2009;mM).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec5">
        <title>Growth curves</title>
        <p>To avoid carryover of media, the inocula were prepared as follows. First, bacteria were grown in 50&#x2009;mL flasks containing 10&#x2009;mL of peptone-glucose for 18&#x2009;h at 37&#xB0;C with orbital shaking. These exponentially growing bacteria were harvested (5&#x2009;min at 13,000&#x2009;&#xD7;&#x2009;<italic>g</italic>), resuspended in 10&#x2009;mL of fresh test medium at an OD<sub>600</sub> of 0.1, and grown for 18&#x2009;h at 37&#xB0;C with orbital stirring. These preconditioned bacteria were harvested, resuspended at an OD<sub>600</sub> of 0.1 in 1&#x2009;mL of fresh test medium, dispensed as technical triplicates in multi-well plates (200&#x2009;&#x3BC;L/well), incubated at 37&#xB0;C in a Bioscreen C apparatus with continuous shaking, and the OD at 420&#x2013;580&#x2009;nm measured at 0.5&#x2009;h intervals. All experiments were repeated at least three times. Controls with medium and no bacteria were included in all experiments.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="sec6">
        <title>DNA manipulations</title>
        <p>Genomic sequences were obtained from the National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) databases. Searches for DNA and protein homologies were performed using the NCBI BLAST tool (<xref rid="ref19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>). DNA sequencing was performed by the &#x201C;Servicio de Secuenciaci&#xF3;n del Centro de Investigaci&#xF3;n M&#xE9;dica Aplicada&#x201D; (CIMA, Universidad de Navarra, Pamplona, Spain).         <title>Protein expression and purification</title>
        <p><italic>E. coli</italic> BL21(DE3) carrying the expression vectors were grown with aeration at 37&#xB0;C in LB-Amp overnight. Using these cultures, two flasks with 200&#x2009;mL of LB-Amp and two flasks with 200&#x2009;mL of M9-Amp were inoculated (2&#x2009;mL/flask) and incubated with stirring at 37&#xB0;C until an OD<sub>600</sub> of approximately 0.6. Then, they were cooled for 10&#x2009;min in an ice bath and one flask of each medium was supplemented with IPTG (0.4&#x2009;mM final concentration) to induce protein expression (the remaining two flasks were used as non-induced controls). To allow for protein expression, the bacteria were grown overnight at 30 or 18&#xB0;C, after which they were recovered by centrifugation (7,000&#x2009;&#xD7;&#x2009;<italic>g</italic>, 10&#x2009;min, 4&#xB0;C) and the pellet was resuspended in 10&#x2009;mL of lysis buffer containing 1&#x2009;mg/mL lysozyme, 5&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL antipain, 5&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL leupeptin, and 300&#x2009;mM NaCl in 50&#x2009;mM Na phosphate buffer (pH 8). Bacteria were lysed by three cycles of freezing and thawing in liquid N<sub>2</sub>, the lysates were treated with DNAse I (125&#x2009;units/mL), and the insoluble material was removed (40,000&#x2009;&#xD7;&#x2009;<italic>g</italic>, 40&#x2009;min, 4&#xB0;C). A 10&#x2009;&#x3BC;L aliquot of the soluble fraction was used for expression analysis of the proteins by SDS-PAGE in 12&#x2013;14% gradient gels and Page Blue Protein Staining (Thermo Scientific) and Western blotting. For the latter, a 6X-HisTag monoclonal (1/2000 dilution, 4&#xB0;C, overnight) and a peroxidase-conjugated anti-mouse antibody (1/10000, 2.5&#x2009;h at room temperature) (both from Thermofisher) were used. Blots were developed with peroxide solution and then with luminol enhancer solution (Thermofisher).</p>
        <p>Lysates expressing the highest amount of each recombinant protein were used for protein purification. To this end, they were filtered (0.2&#x2009;&#x3BC;m pore Vivaspin; Sartorius), diluted 1:3 in 20&#x2009;mM imidazole, 300&#x2009;mM NaCl, 50&#x2009;mM sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), and recombinant proteins purified by metal-affinity chromatography. To this end, samples were loaded onto a 1&#x2009;mL HisTrap FF column (GE Healthcare) at a flow of 1&#x2009;mL/min, the column was washed with 20&#x2009;mM imidazole, 300&#x2009;mM NaCl, 50&#x2009;mM sodium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4), and proteins eluted with a 0&#x2013;300&#x2009;mM imidazole linear gradient in the same buffer. Fbp, GlpX, and Bbp were eluted in a single peak that was concentrated using a Vivaspin Turbo 15 device (Sartorius). Then, imidazole was removed by gel filtration using a PD-10 column (GE Healthcare) equilibrated with 25&#x2009;mM HEPES (pH 7.4) containing 5&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL antipain, 5&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL leupeptin, 1&#x2009;mM DTT, 100&#x2009;mM NaCl, and 5&#x2009;mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, and proteins eluted in 5&#x2009;mL of the same buffer were aliquoted and stored at &#x2212;80&#xB0;C until analysis. For measuring enzyme activity, stocks were diluted adequately in a buffer containing 25&#x2009;mM HEPES (pH 7.4), 10% glycerol, 0.5&#x2009;mg/mL BSA, and 1&#x2009;mM DTT.</p>

        <title>Phosphatase quantitative colorimetric assay</title>
        <p>Phosphatase activity was measured using a colorimetric assay for inorganic phosphate. The assay mixture (90&#x2009;&#x3BC;L final volume) contained 25&#x2009;mM HEPES (pH 7.4), 0.1&#x2009;mg/mL BSA, 1&#x2009;mM MgCl<sub>2</sub>, 0.1&#x2009;mM EDTA, and 0.25&#x2009;mM of the indicated substrate. The tubes were pre-warmed at 30&#xB0;C and the reaction was started by adding the appropriate amount of Fbp (0.3&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL; 6&#x2009;&#x3BC;L of a 500-fold dilution of the stock of purified protein) or Bbp 0.6&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/mL (6&#x2009;&#x3BC;L of a 40-fold dilution of the stock of purified protein). After 10&#x2009;min, the reaction was stopped by the addition of 10&#x2009;&#x3BC;L of 2&#x2009;M HCl and placing the tubes on ice for 10&#x2009;min. After a 3&#x2009;min centrifugation at top speed to remove precipitated material, 80&#x2009;&#x3BC;L of the supernatant were dispensed into 96-flat bottom well microtiter plates to which 150&#x2009;&#x3BC;L of a solution containing 1 vol. of 6.3% (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>Mo<sub>7</sub>O<sub>24</sub> (ammonium heptamolybdate) in 7.5&#x2009;N HCl and 3 vols. of 0.3% malachite green were added (<xref rid="ref23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>). Blanks without addition of enzymes as well as a standard curve, with known concentrations of inorganic phosphate (0 to 8&#x2009;nmol of KPi in 80&#x2009;&#x3BC;L), were prepared and run in parallel in the same assay conditions. These were necessary for computing the enzymatic activities. The amount of enzyme added in the assay was previously tested in order to ensure the linearity of the reaction during the 10-min incubation at 30&#xB0;C. Statistical significance was evaluated using One-sample <italic>t</italic>-test (*<italic>p</italic> &lt;&#x2009;0.05, **<italic>p</italic> &lt;&#x2009;0.01, ***<italic>p</italic> &lt;&#x2009;0.001, ****<italic>p</italic> &lt;&#x2009;0.0001).</p>

        <title><italic>Brucella</italic> carries a putative phosphatase of the histidine superfamily</title>

      <sec id="sec17">
        <title><italic>Brucella</italic> Fbp, GlpX, and Bbp have fructose 1,6-bisphosphatase activity</title>
        <p>To obtain <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 Fbp, GlpX, and Bbp, the corresponding open reading frames were cloned into a bacterial expression vector allowing for the overexpression in <italic>E. coli</italic> of the respective recombinant proteins. While this resulted in the expression of soluble Fbp and Bbp, GlpX appeared in insoluble inclusion bodies suggesting an inactive configuration. A close examination of the sequence revealed that <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 GlpX had only two amino acid changes with respect to the homologous protein in <italic>B. abortus</italic> 2308W. One of these changes, an Arg316Pro mutation, affects a highly conserved arginine that is predicted to be in the C-terminal &#xDF;-strand 8. Since proline is incompatible with a &#xDF;-strand, this mutation likely results in improper folding (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 3</xref>). Furthermore, &#xDF;-strand 8 is largely responsible for the dimerization of GlpX that is required for the activity of the enzyme. On the other hand, the second mutation (Gly139Asp) is predicted to have only a minor effect as it is localized in a loop. These results strongly suggested that GlpX is not active in <italic>B. suis</italic> 513. To test this hypothesis, we used site-directed mutagenesis to replace Arg316 by proline and Asp139 by glycine in the GlpX expressing vector (henceforth named <sub>Bs513</sub>GlpX139Gly316Arg) obtaining a GlpX that was similar to that of <italic>B. abortus</italic> 2308W (whose activity was previously proved; <xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure 2</xref>). These changes resulted in the production of a soluble GlpX that was purified by Ni-affinity chromatography yielding an active protein of the expected molecular weight. Then, we studied the activity and kinetic properties of <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 Bbp and Fbp, and of <sub>Bs513</sub>GlpX139Gly316Arg. <xref rid="tab1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref> shows that both Fbp and Bbp are able to catalyze the dephosphorylation of F1,6bP and S1,7bP with Kcat/Km values that did not markedly differ between the 2 enzymes. In contrast, GlpX preferred F1,6bP to S1,7bP as substrate.</p>

        <title>Bbp is a metal-independent broad-range phosphatase</title>

    <sec sec-type="discussion" id="sec20">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>As expected from a fructose-bisphosphate aldolase involved in the trioses-P &#x21CC; F1,6bP interconversion and in agreement with the homology analyses, we found that the putative aldolase gene (<italic>fba</italic>) is required for growth on gluconeogenic substrates, supporting that gluconeogenesis in <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 requires the action of F1,6bP bisphosphatases. Then, we confirmed the dispensability of Fbp and GlpX for growth on gluconeogenic substrates and found that the genomes of <italic>B. suis</italic> 513, <italic>B. abortus</italic> 2308W, and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> 16&#x2009;M carry a gene coding for a broad substrate phosphatase (Bbp). Concerning <italic>glpX</italic>, we found that it codes for a non-functional enzyme in <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 which, however, is active in at least <italic>B. abortus</italic>. These differences may indicate the dispensability of GlpX in this pathogen but, while they do not rule out a contribution of GlpX in other brucellae, the results show that <italic>fbp</italic> and <italic>bbp</italic> (together or individually) can sustain growth on gluconeogenic substrates. Consistent with this, proof of their functionality was also provided by enzymatic analyses.</p>
      <p>Bbp does not belong to any of the five groups of FBPases known (<xref rid="ref28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>), differing in structure, metal requirements, sensitivity to inhibitors, and substrate specificity. Remarkably, Bbp was active on S1,7bP, F1,6bP, and other bisphosphate sugars, and <italic>Brucella</italic> Fbp was active on both F1,6bP and S1,7bP. Their FBPase activity supports that <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 Fba and Fbp-Bbp can generate F6P from trioses via the sequential activities of the classical gluconeogenesis (i.e., DHAP + GAP &#x21CC; F1,6bP, and F1,6bP&#x2009;&#x2192;&#x2009;F6P&#x2009;+&#x2009;Pi; <xref rid="fig1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>), which can explain the phenotypes observed for the Fbp and Bbp mutants. However, their SBPase activity is intriguing. In bacteria, this activity has been described in the facultative H<sub>2</sub>-chemolitotroph <italic>Cupriavidus necator</italic> H16 (formerly <italic>Alcaligenes eutrophus</italic> H16), where it accomplishes an essential role in the Calvin cycle (<xref rid="ref29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>). Also, a dual FBPase/SBPase activity was described for the plasmid-encoded GlpX<sup>P</sup> of <italic>Bacillus methanolicus</italic> (<xref rid="ref28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>). In combination with Fba, this class II FBPase plays a role in the methylotrophic ribulose-phosphate pathway (classical FBPase activity) and in a new variant in which S1,7bP is dephosphorylated to S7P. None of these pathways is related to gluconeogenesis but the dual FBPases/SBPases could be related to a metabolic adaptation of brucellae facilitating the use of erythritol.</p>
      <p>In <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 and absence of erythritol, in addition to the classical pathway (GA3P &#x21CC; DHAP; DHAP + GAP &#x21CC; F1,6bP; F1,6bP&#x2009;&#x2192;&#x2009;F6P&#x2009;+&#x2009;Pi), gluconeogenesis produces F6P through the following three reactions: DHAP + E4P &#x21CC; S1,7bP (catalyzed by Fba); S1,7bP&#x2009;+&#x2009;H<sub>2</sub>O&#x2009;&#x2192;&#x2009;S7P&#x2009;+&#x2009;Pi (catalyzed by Fbp and Bbp); S7P&#x2009;+&#x2009;GAP &#x21CC; F6P&#x2009;+&#x2009;E4P (catalyzed by Tal). While F6P is produced from DHAP and GAP, it is important to note that E4P plays a catalytic role in this set of reactions, and therefore it must be formed in some way to initiate the bypass. When the bacteria are growing on gluconeogenic substrates (and no erythritol), E4P is necessarily produced via the transketolase of PPS (F6P&#x2009;+&#x2009;GAP &#x21CC; xylulose 5-phosphate + E4P). This implies that some F6P needs to be first formed and, as F1,6bP (from DHAP and GAP) is the only possible source of F6P under these growth conditions, FBPase activity is necessary and a strictly specific SBPase would not be able to support growth <italic>in vitro</italic> of the Fbp mutant of <italic>B. suis</italic> 513 (where GlpX is inactive) on gluconeogenic substrates. Therefore, a dual role for Bbp is necessary, consistent with the hypothesis that the bypass is functional. Remarkably, there is another source of E4P not connected to Fba that is very relevant in <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogenesis. Elucidation of the full pathway for erythritol catabolism in these bacteria has shown to yield only E4P, and not DHAP as previously thought (<xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>). Noteworthy, erythritol stimulates <italic>Brucella</italic> growth in minute amounts (0.4-4&#x2009;&#x3BC;g/ml in McCullough and Dick&#x2019;s media), an effect that has remained unexplained since first observed (<xref rid="ref30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>) and that could be explained by the catalytic role of E4P discussed above. Moreover, it has been proposed that, coupled to the erythritol pathway, synthesis of F6P and GAP through PPS occurs (<xref rid="ref18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>), which could be coupled to both gluconeogenesis and the reactions leading to the Krebs cycle. However, if erythritol is abundant, high amounts of E4P would be formed, which could make significant the production of S1,7bP by Fba and, therefore, the SBPase bypass a relevant way to increase the use of the abundant erythritol in the genitals and placenta of natural hosts.</p>
      <p>While <italic>B. suis</italic> biovar 5 is more prototrophic than <italic>B. abortus</italic> 2308W, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> 16&#x2009;M, or the slow-growing <italic>B. suis</italic> biovars (<xref rid="ref31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>), sequence analyses show that <italic>fba</italic>, <italic>fbp,</italic> and <italic>bbp</italic> are highly conserved, and at least <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Bbp show activity in gluconeogenic-deficient <italic>E. coli</italic>, strongly suggesting that the gluconeogenic pathways proposed here are functional in those species. This inference and the connection with erythritol metabolism in other brucellae are being currently verified.</p>
      <p>As indicated in the Introduction, the brucellae can reach exceedingly high numbers in the placenta. Reaching such high numbers necessarily requires a very active and efficient use of available substrates, among which erythritol is conspicuous. Therefore, the intense multiplication and tissue damage, subsequent abortion and infertility, and pathogen release in high numbers are characteristics of brucellosis in all likelihood linked to the peculiar metabolic characteristic described here.</p>
      <p>As with infection with field strains, the most effective vaccines against brucellosis&#x2014;<italic>B. abortus</italic> S19 for cattle and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> Rev1 for small ruminants&#x2014;induce abortion, an undesirable effect that hinders their use in mass vaccination campaigns. A possible strategy to avoid or reduce this abortifacient effect is to suppress their genital tropism. Since disrupting the use of erythritol might reduce the bacterial load in the placenta and subsequent reproductive failure, the results of this work provide the basis for the development of safer vaccines. Also, an improved understanding of bacterial metabolism offers opportunities for exploring new therapeutic agents.</p>
    </sec>
