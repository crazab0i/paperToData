In this study, we determined the whole-genome sequence of M5, and conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis against the whole-genome sequence of the virulent strain 16 M and other reference strains. This analysis revealed 11 regions of deletion (RDs) and 2 regions of insertion (RIs) within the M5 genome. Among these regions, the sequences encompassed in 5 RDs and 1 RI showed consistent variation, with a large deletion between the M5 and the 16 M genomes. RD4 and RD5 showed the large diversity among all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomes, both in RD length and RD copy number. Thus, RD4 and RD5 are potential sites for typing different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains. Other RD and RI regions exhibited multiple single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). In addition, a genome fragment with a 56 kb rearrangement was determined to be consistent with previous studies. Comparative genomic analysis indicated that genomic island inversion in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> was widely present. With the genetic pattern common among all strains analyzed, these 2 RDs, 1 RI, and one inversion region are potential sites for detection of genomic differences. Several SNPs of important virulence-related genes (<italic toggle="yes">motB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">dhbC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">sfuB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">dsbAB, aidA, aroC</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">lysR)</italic> were also detected, and may be used to determine the mechanism of virulence attenuation. Collectively, this study reveals that comparative analysis between wild-type and vaccine strains can provide resources for the study of virulence and microevolution of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p></abstract><funding-group><funding-statement>This work was supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (81271900). id="s1"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis is one of the most common bacterial zoonoses endemic in many countries, particularly those with developing livestock industries <xref rid="pone.0070852-Food1" ref-type="bibr">[1]</xref>. Among these, China is a large stock-raising country with approximately 300 million sheep <xref rid="pone.0070852-Shang1" ref-type="bibr">[2]</xref>. Along with the increased number of animals, the incidence of human brucellosis has also increased rapidly over the last 10 years, which has negatively affected the public health and the livestock industry <xref rid="pone.0070852-Cui1" ref-type="bibr">[3]</xref>. <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> was the predominant strain associated with human brucellosis in China from 1950 to 2010 <xref rid="pone.0070852-Shang1" ref-type="bibr">[2]</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0070852-Zhang1" ref-type="bibr">[4]</xref>. As a result, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccination has become one of the most important measures for preventing and controlling brucellosis in animals, but different vaccine strains are used for different animals. For example, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> B19 and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> RB51 are used to vaccinate cattle <xref rid="pone.0070852-Cutler1" ref-type="bibr">[5]</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0070852-Nicoletti1" ref-type="bibr">[7]</xref> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev. 1 to vaccinate sheep and goats <xref rid="pone.0070852-Blasco1" ref-type="bibr">[8]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-GarinBastuji1" ref-type="bibr">[9]</xref>.</p><p>In addition to these strains, the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain M5 was developed in 1962 by the Harbin Institute of Veterinary Medicine at the Academy of Agricultural Sciences. This vaccine was derived from the virulent strain of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M28 and used mainly to vaccinate both pregnant and non-pregnant sheep and goats <xref rid="pone.0070852-Shang1" ref-type="bibr">[2]</xref>. <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain M5 exhibited superior protection against brucellosis in sheep and goats as demonstrated by the first large-scale use in 1970 <xref rid="pone.0070852-Shang1" ref-type="bibr">[2]</xref>. However, the molecular and physiological mechanism causing the loss of virulence of this strain is not well understood. Species identification and subtyping of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> isolates are very important for epidemiologic surveillance, especially for differential diagnosis between infected and vaccinated animals. To address this problem, a series of methods have been reported. For example, Cloeckaert et al. discovered a unique mutation in the <italic toggle="yes">rpsL</italic> gene of Rev. 1 that eliminates an NciI restriction site; thus, this mutation can be detected by polymerase chain reaction&#8211;restriction fragment length polymorphism <xref rid="pone.0070852-Cloeckaert1" ref-type="bibr">[10]</xref>. In addition, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain-specific PCR assays were conducted to identify and distinguish specific <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> field strains from the vaccine strains S19 and RB51 <xref rid="pone.0070852-Sangari1" ref-type="bibr">[11]</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0070852-Crasta1" ref-type="bibr">[14]</xref>. Several other <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> attenuated strains have also been sequenced and the data are available <xref rid="pone.0070852-Ding1" ref-type="bibr">[15]</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="pone.0070852-Yang1" ref-type="bibr">[17]</xref>. However, none of these studies addressed factors affecting virulence in the M5 vaccine strain. Thus, the primary aim of this study was to identify candidate ORFs associated with virulence in 16 M or lack of virulence in M5. Using bioinformatics, this study is the first to perform whole genome analysis directed toward identifying the sequence differences between the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain M5 and the virulent strain 16 M and reveals a mechanism of attenuation genes.</p></sec><sec id="s2"><title>Results and Discussion</title><sec id="s2a"><title>General features of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain M5 genome</title><p>High-throughput bioinformatics revealed 26 genes present in the M5 genome. These genes were compared to those of the 16 M stain, and all were found to be homologues of the 16 M genome. These data suggest that the genomes of M5 and 16 M have a very similar genomic structure.</p><sec id="s2a1"><title>Genome assembly and annotation</title><p>The draft-quality genome of the vaccine strain M5 was sequenced and assembled into 95 contigs with N50&#8202;=&#8202;775,745 bp (<xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0070852.s002">Table S1</xref>). Scaffold 1, the largest scaffold, comprised most of chromosome II. Further analysis by PCR sequencing showed that this scaffold contained a complete 16S rRNA gene which was different from the copy in chromosome I. However, we did not continue to close the whole gap caused by duplication of the 16S-23S-5S rRNA operon. Compared to the sequenced genome of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16 M, the size, number of open reading frames (ORFs), guanine&#8211;cytosine (GC) content, gene length, and gene density were largely conserved in the genome of M5 (<xref ref-type="table" rid="pone-0070852-t001">Table 1</xref>).</p><table-wrap id="pone-0070852-t001" orientation="portrait" position="float"><object-id However, 2 SNPs in the rRNA sequences within chromosome II of M5 were identified when compared to those of chromosome I.</p></sec></sec><sec id="s2b"><title>Genome region differences</title><sec id="s2b1"><title>Genomic arrangement</title><p>Although there was homology between the genes of M5 and 16 M, the genome layout of M5 suggests a &#8764;56 kb inversion or rearrangement in the head of scaffold 1, which mapped to chromosome II of 16 M. This region is flanked by two duplicate sequences of the insertion sequence (IS) IS1953. Two genes located upstream (BMEII0183/BMII0184) and downstream (BMEII0227/BMEII0228) of these inversion sequences were associated with transposes. In addition, inactivated derivatives were found in the IS elements. These findings suggest that the inversion was due to the duplication of insertion elements.</p><p>Aligning this 56 kb inversion region with other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains revealed that genomic inversions vary among available genomes including; <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic> CCM 4915, <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> ATCC 23365, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> ATCC 23445, and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 1330; similar to <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> ATCC 25840 and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> ATCC 23457; and missing in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus bv.1</italic> str.9-941, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S19, and <italic toggle="yes">B. Abortus</italic> 2308 (<xref ref-type="table" rid="pone-0070852-t002">Table 2</xref>). However, absence of these transporters can be negative effects. Pairwise analysis demonstrated that there were 2819 SNPs between the genomes of 16 M and M5. Among these SNPs, 585 were in the intergenic regions and 2234 were in the genetic regions. Although the genome divergence time can be calculated by non-synonymous intra-species mutations, the splice time of these two genomes is difficult to estimate because the genome is under manual selection pressure. The 2234 SNPs in the genetic regions included 798 synonymous SNPs and 1436 non-synonymous SNPs (<xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0070852.s004">Table S3</xref>). The non-synonymous SNPs only resulted in amino acid changes, with no frame shift or nonsense mutations.</p><p>Delrue et al. presented a list of 196 unique virulence-related genes divided into 12 functional classes <xref rid="pone.0070852-Delrue1" ref-type="bibr">[19]</xref>. Our comparative analysis of these two genomes suggests that SNPs widely exist in these genes. A total of 183 SNPs were present in 197 genes, among which 101 SNPs were non-synonymous mutations. Compared with SNPs occurring at other sites of the genome, these genes presented higher positive selection pressure, with Ka/Ks of 1.23. Detailed function analysis of these virulence-related genes revealed that most functional categories were involved in fast evolution (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="pone-0070852-g002">Figure 2</xref> and <xref ref-type="table" rid="pone-0070852-t005">Table 5</xref>). Within all known functional groups, both total SNP rates and non-synonymous SNP rates were higher in the genes of &#8220;metal acquisition&#8221; and &#8220;nitrogen metabolism,&#8221; and both Ka/Ks values were greater than 1. The Ka/Ks values were also large in classical virulence factors, including genes of envelope molecules and secretion or transport system. These data suggest that mutations in these genes can play important roles in virulence attenuation of the vaccine strain. id="s2b5"><title>Virulence-associated metabolism and virulence factors</title><p>Comprehensive analysis of the 16 M and M5 genomes revealed variations in genome deletion, insertion, and SNPs. Given these genomic differences, we next sought to identify candidate ORFs associated with virulence in 16 M or lack of virulence in M5. To improve the accuracy of our analysis, we first performed an independent literature search to identify published differences between virulence factors and important metabolic pathways. Following this, large-scale screens and testing in the model systems were performed in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to confirm and/or identify virulence factors associated with metabolism and virulence. The mutations of each virulence-associated gene between M5 and 16 M are listed in <xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0070852.s005">Table S4</xref>.</p></sec><sec id="s2b6"><title>Envelope molecule</title><p>The LPS O-polysaccharide (O-PS) is the first molecule clearly involved in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intracellular entry <xref rid="pone.0070852-FernandezPrada1" ref-type="bibr">[20]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-Porte1" ref-type="bibr">[21]</xref>. Genetic studies demonstrated that the O-PS is synthesized by the so-called ABC transporter-dependent pathway, which characterizes homopolymetric polysaccharides. In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, the genes involved in the synthesis of the O-PS and its translocation to the periplasm are clustered in the Wbk region of chromosome I (ORF BMEI1393 to BMEI1427) <xref rid="pone.0070852-Zygmunt1" ref-type="bibr">[22]</xref>. In our analysis of M5, no non-synonymous mutations were found in the core genes of this region (<italic toggle="yes">wbpZ</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">wbkA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">rfbD</italic>, and others); however, several mutations were present in the ORFs BMEI1399 [G238C(A80P)] and BMEI1400 [C61T (P21S)], which are transposases in the cluster. In addition, other mutations involved in the synthesis of LPS were identified and included <italic toggle="yes">pmm</italic> [T397C(F133L)], <italic toggle="yes">pgm</italic> [A752G(H251R)], <italic toggle="yes">wbdA</italic> [T347C(L116S), G1103C(R368P)], <italic toggle="yes">lpsA</italic> [C278T(S93L)], <italic toggle="yes">mgtA</italic> [C142T(P48S), G380A(R127K)], and <italic toggle="yes">amiC</italic> [A545T(N182I)]. These data suggest that the reduced virulence of M5 is due, in part, to defects in O-PS synthesis.</p></sec><sec id="s2b7"><title>Secretion or transport system</title><p>In addition to envelope molecules, the type IV secretion system (T4SS) of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> encoded by the VirB operon is a major virulence factor <xref rid="pone.0070852-Boschiroli1" ref-type="bibr">[23]</xref>. This operon is composed of 12 ORFs clustered in chromosome II (BMEII0025 to BMEII0036). We identified three ORFs associated with the M5 T4SS that contained single or two sites of non-synonymous mutations. These included: A463C (K155Q) in inner membrane ATPases (<italic toggle="yes">virB4</italic>, BMEII0028), A622G (T208A) and T1007A (F336Y) in the channel protein (<italic toggle="yes">virB6</italic>, BMEII0030), and C700G (R234G) in the channel protein (<italic toggle="yes">virB9</italic>, BMEII0033). The <italic toggle="yes">virB</italic> mutants cannot reach the replicative niche and reside in a membrane-bound vacuole <xref rid="pone.0070852-Comerci1" ref-type="bibr">[24]</xref>. In addition to mutations in the T4SS mutations C613G(P205A) in the ABC transporter (BMEII0923), C188T(A63V) and C659T(T220I) in the flagellar gene BMEII0154, and A427G(I143V) in the flagellar gene BMEII0159 were also identified. Recent studies showed that some proteins involved in the export of flagellar components are similar to the components of the type III secretion machinery, and flagellar apparatus itself can secrete virulence factors <xref rid="pone.0070852-Blocker1" ref-type="bibr">[25]</xref>. These data suggest that defects in virulence factor secretion may affect the replicative ability of M5.</p></sec><sec id="s2b8"><title>Regulation</title><p>
<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> must survive under varying conditions ranging from the open environment to the intracellular medium. Thus, the bacterium must coordinate an intricate network of factors to generate a suitable adaptive response to various signals. The adaptive response is mediated by two-component regulatory systems (TCSs) and transcriptional regulators (TRs) <xref rid="pone.0070852-Kohler1" ref-type="bibr">[26]</xref>. To date, BvrS/R is the best characterized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> TCS <xref rid="pone.0070852-Viadas1" ref-type="bibr">[27]</xref>. Moreover, BvrS/R regulates several critical cellular functions, including cell division, chemotaxis, metabolism, and expression of toxins and other proteins important for pathogenesis. TRs important for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> survival are the GntR and LysR families. In addition, LysR-type transcriptional regulators (LTTRs) are widely distributed in the diverse genera of prokaryotes <xref rid="pone.0070852-Schell1" ref-type="bibr">[28]</xref>. Many virulence factors of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are regulated by LTTRs <xref rid="pone.0070852-Foulongne1" ref-type="bibr">[29]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-Haine1" ref-type="bibr">[30]</xref>. Although no mutations were observed in the common TCSs or TRs, we did identify a non-synonymous mutation of T176C(L59P) and a 3 bp deletion from 391 to 393 at the M5 locus encoding LTTR (BMEI0116 in 16 M). These data suggest that M5 may have reduced ability to regulate survival in certain environmental conditions.</p></sec><sec id="s2b9"><title>Metal acquisition</title><p>Iron acquisition and transport systems are essential for intracellular survival ability. For instance, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> with a mutation in the Fe<sup>3+</sup> uptake gene <italic toggle="yes">dhbC</italic>, which affects DBHA siderophore synthesize, exhibits a high degree of attenuation <xref rid="pone.0070852-Eskra1" ref-type="bibr">[31]</xref>. In <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, DHBA is important for bacterial growth on erythritol, explaining its potential role in cattle abortion <xref rid="pone.0070852-Bellaire1" ref-type="bibr">[32]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-Parent1" ref-type="bibr">[33]</xref>. Detailed comparison between the <italic toggle="yes">in vitro</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">in vivo</italic> phenotypes of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis dhb</italic> mutants and strains with defined mutations in the erythritol catabolic genes is necessary to more accurately define the relationship among DHBA production, erythritol catabolism, and virulence in the natural ruminant host. The role of the TonB transport system is well documented in <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella entrica</italic>
<xref rid="pone.0070852-Tsolis1" ref-type="bibr">[34]</xref> and S<italic toggle="yes">higella dysenteriae</italic>
<xref rid="pone.0070852-Reeves1" ref-type="bibr">[35]</xref>. The <italic toggle="yes">tonB</italic> mutants in these bacteria exhibit reduced growth in host cells, and the TonB complex is required for optimal uptake of DHBA siderophore and/or citrate in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>
<xref rid="pone.0070852-Danese1" ref-type="bibr">[36]</xref>. However, few studies have evaluated the expression of iron-related genes in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. <xref rid="pone.0070852-Eskra1" ref-type="bibr">[31]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-Bellaire1" ref-type="bibr">[32]</xref>. The comparison between the transcript levels of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16 M and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M5 during macrophage infection led us to presume that some common and differentially expressed transcripts of iron acquisition genes could be identified. In addition, previous proteomics studies of the vaccine strain M5 showed altered expression of proteins associated with iron utilization (data unpublished). To verify this hypothesis, we next focused on analyzing differences metabolic activity between M5 and 16 M.</p></sec><sec id="s2b10"><title>Amino acid metabolism</title><p>Among the 19 synthesis subclass genes of these two strains, 10 genes of M5 contained point mutations. These data suggest that M5 may have some amino acid metabolism defects; yet failure of M5 to resist a sudden lack of nutrients requires further investigation.</p></sec><sec id="s2b11"><title>Sugar metabolism</title><p>Maltose, ribose, arabinose, galactose, glucose, glycerol, erythriol, and inositol degradation pathways are essential for the intracellular survival of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>
<xref rid="pone.0070852-Essenberg1" ref-type="bibr">[37]</xref>. For example, mutants of genes encoding sugar transport systems, such as pyruvate carboxylase (BMEI0266), ribokinase (BMEII0089), 6-phosphogluconate dehydrogenase (BMEII1124), inosamine methylase (BMEI1301), glycosyl transferase (BMEII1101), ribose transport (BMEII0300), and glucose/galactose transport (BMEII1053) limit carbon or energy supply and affect gene regulation. In addition, the production of cyclic &#946;-1,2 glucan (BMEI1837) is necessary for the intracellular replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>
<xref rid="pone.0070852-Briones1" ref-type="bibr">[38]</xref>. Upon examination of these pathways, SNP mutations in the ORF BMEI1837 of M5 caused five residue mutations at the positions 3587 to 3594, 3631 to 3638, and 3774 to 3782, and another non-synonymous mutation at position 4996. These data suggest that M5 may have defects in cyclic &#946;-1,2 production.</p></sec><sec id="s2b12"><title>DNA/RNA</title><p>Purine/pyrimidine synthesis, repair, and regulatory genes are essential for the intracellular replication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. A study by Crawford RM indicated that deletion of the <italic toggle="yes">purE</italic> gene from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> dramatically reduced bacterial virulence and suggested that this gene should be further evaluated as a potential vaccine <xref rid="pone.0070852-Crawford1" ref-type="bibr">[39]</xref>. Other genes under the same classification, such as <italic toggle="yes">xseA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">purL</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">purD</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">mgpS</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">tldD</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">cobB</italic> mutants, appear to be targets for attenuation vaccine strains.</p></sec><sec id="s2b13"><title>Stress proteins/Chaperones</title><p>Genes encoding stress proteins have been an obvious choice for directed mutagenesis for virulence studies and vaccine generation. Within the M5 genome, genes encoding chaperones (BMEI2002 and BMEI1513) showed one non-synonymous mutation, namely, A37C(M13L) and G398A(G133D), respectively.</p></sec><sec id="s2b14"><title>Oxidoreduction and nitrogen metabolism</title><p>DsbA has been shown to be involved in toxin production, adhesion, motility, extracellular enzyme production, and type III secretion system <xref rid="pone.0070852-Jiang1" ref-type="bibr">[40]</xref>. Few studies have investigated the role of DsbB in pathogenicity <xref rid="pone.0070852-Raczko1" ref-type="bibr">[41]</xref>. A G120A(M40I) mutation was detected in the <italic toggle="yes">DsbB</italic> (BMEI0384) gene of M5. In addition, published genome analysis indicated that <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> can adapt to either aerobic or anaerobic environments. Among the genes involved in the process, we detected non-synonymous mutations in <italic toggle="yes">FdhA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">glnA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">nifS</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">glnD</italic> of M5.</p></sec><sec id="s2b15"><title>Unknown function</title><p>Seven ORFs encoding hypothetical protein were slightly different between the attenuated M5 strain and the virulent strain 16 M. Among these ORFs, BMEII0128 was identified as a virulence-associated ORF in another attenuated vaccine strain S19. Mutations of A31C(M11L), C589T(H197S), and A590C(H197S) were also identified in the vaccine strain M5.</p></sec></sec></sec><sec id="s3"><title>Conclusions</title><p>The vaccine strain M5 has been widely used in Asia for decades to vaccinate sheep against brucellosis <xref rid="pone.0070852-Shang1" ref-type="bibr">[2]</xref>. While extensive studies have been performed on the phenotype and physiology of this strain, the molecular mechanisms responsible for loss of virulence are unclear. In this study, we determined the whole-genome sequence of M5 and conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis against the whole-genome sequence of the virulent strain 16 M. This comparative analysis revealed that the genome sequences were highly conserved with more than 99% identity, which agreed with previous studies. However, we identified 11 RDs and 2 RIs by the reads-to-genome comparisons. The gene and genomic structure analysis of RDs and RIs revealed that only 5 RDs and 1 RI showed a consistent variation with a large deletion in the M5 genome compared to 16 M. The other RDs and RIs displayed multiple SNPs, which caused the low read coverage. In addition, a genome fragment with a 56 kb rearrangement was determined to be consistent with the previous study. Based on these results, we suggest the use of genome sequence differences to create a precise map for effective vaccine detection and diagnosis. RD4 and RD5 indicated a large diversity among all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genomes (<xref ref-type="supplementary-material" rid="pone.0070852.s006">Table S5</xref>) not only in RD length but in copy number as well. Thus, they are strong candidate sites for typing different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains.</p><p>RD4 is a typical random repeat region consisting of the sequence &#8220;GGGGATCAAGAGATCGCCCCCGTTTCCGTCGCCGTTCCCGTGCTGCCGAAGGCACACAAAGAGAAGAAACATGGCGGCGACGGCAATTGTTGAAAGCGCCATCATTTTTCGTGGGTGACGATGAC,&#8221; which repeats 1 to 2 times in each genome and can be considered the candidate VNTR loci for MLVA genotyping. RD5 is 883 bp and overlaps with two resolvase family genes in 16 M. The ORFs BMEI1661 and BMEI0902 belong to this family, which are 231 and 747 bp respectively. The proteins encoded by these ORFs have the same core functions but different protein and structure combinations. Comparative genome analysis in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> revealed two typical sequence types present in this region. The first sequence was approximately 139 bp and contained a gene similar to BMEI0902. The second was 883 bp and contained a gene similar to BMEI1661. In our study, RI2 belongs to the first sequence. Compared with the 16 M genome, the M5 strain had an additional RI2 region but the RD5 was missing. Further analysis in all <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains presented different patterns of these two regions in the various genomes. This bias of the selected site-specific recombinase has been shown to be involved in the acquisition of drug resistance genes and alteration of gene expression <xref rid="pone.0070852-Eskra2" ref-type="bibr">[42]</xref>, suggesting that this unique gene affects virulence.</p><p>Considering the genetic patterns widely present in all strains, we consider the 2 RDs, 1 RI, and the inversion region as candidates for detection of genome differences. Our analysis was also aimed at identifying 1) the genes or genome fragments that only existed in our genome,2) the mutations specific toward the virulent strain and the attenuated strain, and 3) the molecular mechanisms of M5 strain attenuation . Among the 11RDs identified, RD1, RD2, and RD7 were in M5, whereas they are consistent with other strains in 16 M. Furthermore, RD3, RD6, RD8, RD9, RD10, and RD11 were highly differentiated regions with a large number of mutations. All of these regions are possible candidates for distinguishing M5 from 16 M. Notably, RD9 includes an ORF encoding an ABC-type Fe<sup>3+</sup> transport system (<italic toggle="yes">sfuB</italic>, BMEII0566).</p><p>In <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, SfuA binds free Fe <sup>3+</sup> in the periplasm, and SfuB and C transport this cation across the cytoplasmic membrane. Several studies suggested that the ferric dicitrate complex itself represents important substrates for the SfuABC type transporters <xref rid="pone.0070852-Bellaire1" ref-type="bibr">[32]</xref>, <xref rid="pone.0070852-Parent1" ref-type="bibr">[33]</xref>. On the basis of the potential relevance of ferric dicitrate as an iron source for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in vitro, the <italic toggle="yes">sfuB</italic> mutation seemingly is responsible for the loss of virulence in vaccine strain M5. Surveys of the genome sequences of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16 M and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 1330 suggest that transport systems that allow the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to utilize heme and ferric dicitrate as iron sources are important for sustaining the intracellular lifestyle of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> in host macrophages <xref rid="pone.0070852-Braun1" ref-type="bibr">[43]</xref>.</p><p>The whole-genome sequences analyzed in this study clearly revealed large genome differences between 16 M and M5. Many of these differences were in genes associated with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> metabolism and survival, and thus serve as potential detection sites for distinguishing between the virulent 16 M and attenuated M5 strains. In addition, our analysis revealed two novel sites for typing <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. Furthermore, 3 RDs and SNPs of important virulence-related genes such as <italic toggle="yes">motB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">dhbC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">sfuB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">dsbAB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">aidA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">aroC</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">lysR</italic>, were considered to be sites causing virulence loss in the M5 strain. Collectively, this study reveals that comparative analysis between the wild-type and vaccine strains can provide resources for the study of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> microevolution. Further detailed analysis of the gene function of deletion genes can elucidate their roles in virulence attenuation of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis.</italic>
</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="s4"><title>Materials and Methods</title><sec id="s4a"><title>Isolation extraction and identification</title><p>
<italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> M5 was obtained from the National Institute for Communicable Disease Control and Prevention Center. This strain was submitted for classical identification procedures as follows: CO<sub>2</sub> requirement, H<sub>2</sub>S production, and inhibition of growth by basic fuchsin and thionin, agglutination with monospecific antisera, and phage typing. Total genomic DNA was extracted using the DNeasy Blood and Tissue Kit (QIAGEN, China Ltd., China). An aliquot of the DNA was subjected for analysis using the Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies) and was confirmed for absence of DNA degradation.</p></sec><sec id="s4b"><title>Genome sequencing, assembly, and annotation</title><p>A genome shotgun method was used to collect the genome sequence. We constructed two DNA libraries: 500 bp and 3 kb&#8764;5 kb DNA fragments for high-throughput genome sequencing with SOLEXA. Pair-end 75-bp reads were collected. We obtained 518 Mb and 7,000,000 reads covering &#8764;157 folds for the 16 M genome. The draft genome sequence of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain M5 is available in GenBank under accession number <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="AONT00000000.1">AONT00000000.1</ext-link>.</p><p>The SOLEXA data were assembled using the SOAP <italic toggle="yes">de-novo</italic> software. Genome sequences were analyzed using an automatic genome analysis pipeline. Gene prediction was conducted following the method used in the study by Glimmer <xref rid="pone.0070852-Delcher1" ref-type="bibr">[44]</xref> with default parameters. Gene annotation was performed with BLAST compared with nt, nr, and SwissProt databases with an e-value of 1e-5. Gene function prediction was conducted using COG and InterproScan. Gene pathways were annotated based on Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes.</p></sec><sec id="s4c"><title>Comparative genomic analyses</title><p>High-throughput SOLEXA reads mapping to 16 M was used in the whole-genome comparison because that the draft genome had limits in gene prediction and alignment. Genome differences were valued by each site of SOLEXA coverage, which is standardized by calculating the CDM (read coverage of each site/mean coverage of the whole genome). The log2 value of the CDM (Log2CDM) was used to improve the description.</p><p>We compared the sequences of two strains by MUMmer <xref rid="pone.0070852-Delcher2" ref-type="bibr">[45]</xref> and removed unclear fragments by BLAST. The IS elements were identified and compared based on the IS database (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://www-is.biotoul.fr/">http://www-is.biotoul.fr/</ext-link>). Genes lost or gained were selected by PERL scripts, and their functions were determined. A synteny map between the two strains was constructed by the OrthoMCL pipeline <xref rid="pone.0070852-Li1" ref-type="bibr">[46]</xref>. Whole-genome raw SNPs were detected following the method published by Mummer et al. with default parameters. The detailed variations in virulence-associated genes between M5 and 16 M, including SNPs, deletion, and insertion, were further identified by alignment using CLUSTALW and then calculated by PERL scripts. A Ka/Ks calculator was used <xref rid="pone.0070852-Zhang2" ref-type="bibr">[47]</xref>.</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="supplementary-material" id="s5"><title>Supporting Information</title><supplementary-material content-type="local-data" id="pone.0070852.s001" position="float" orientation="portrait"><label>Figure S1</label><caption><p>
