The objective of this study was to design and evaluate <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">in vivo</italic> a multivalent vaccine based on the immunogenic potential of three selected open reading frames (ORFs) of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p></sec><sec><title>Methods</title><p>The designed construct, named S22, was analyzed <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> to evaluate its physicochemical properties, antigenicity, allergenicity and toxicity. This construct was modeled and subjected to molecular dynamics analysis. Additionally, the antigenicity and protection induced by this construct was evaluated through <italic toggle="yes">In vivo</italic> assays immunizing BALB/c mice with protein (S22), DNA (pVS22) and combining both vaccine formats using a prime boost immunization strategy.</p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p>All bioinformatics analyses showed safe and high quality structural features, revealing favorable interactions between S22 and the TLR4/MD2 complex. Moreover, results from <italic toggle="yes">in vivo</italic> assays indicated that the S22 protein induced robust levels of IgG1 and IgG2a, suggesting a balanced Th1 and Th2 immune response. The DNA construct (pVS22) elicited primarily a Th1 response, whereas the use of a prime boost strategy, which combines both formats resulted in a balanced immune response with significant induction of lymphoproliferation and elevated.</p></sec><sec><title>Discussion</title><p>Although our assays did not demonstrate the induction of a substantial protective response against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, this construct was capable of inducing immunogenicity. This study highlights the utility of <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> design for predicting and optimizing candidate vaccines and underscores the potential of using strategies such as prime boost, which incorporate antigens of different biological nature to modulate the immune response, while balancing parameters such as stability of the antigens and the cost of production.</p></sec></abstract><kwd-group><kwd>prime-boost strategy</kwd><kwd>multivalent vaccines</kwd><kwd>immunoinformatic</kwd><kwd>molecular dynamics</kwd><kwd>immunogenicity</kwd><kwd>protection</kwd><kwd>interferon gamma</kwd></kwd-group><funding-group><award-group><funding-source id="cn001"><institution-wrap><institution>Fondo Nacional de Innovaci&#243;n y Desarrollo Cient&#237;fico&#8211;Tecnol&#243;gico

and Molecular Therapeutics</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec sec-type="intro" id="s1"><label>1</label><title>Introduction</title><p>The genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> comprises small, non-motile, and non-sporulating Gram-negative coccobacilli. These facultative intracellular pathogens lack classical virulence determinants such as capsules and plasmids (<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp are known for their ability to infect mammalian cells and establish chronic infections in both wildlife and domestic animals, as well as in humans, leading to brucellosis. In cattle, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> causes sterility in males and abortion in pregnant females. In humans, this bacterium is responsible for a zoonotic disease primarily acquired through contact with infected animals or consuming contaminated dairy products. Human brucellosis can progress to a chronic condition with symptoms like intermittent fever, myalgia, and headache, potentially escalating to hepatitis, osteoarthritis, endocarditis, and neurobrucellosis (<xref rid="B2" ref-type="bibr">2</xref>). This chronic condition is attributed to the tropism of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> for the lymphoreticular and reproductive systems, facilitating its survival within phagocytic cells and evasion of the host&#8217;s innate and adaptive immune responses (<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>).</p><p>Upon infection, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> evades the microbiocidal action of the complement system and the recognition by the pathogen-associated pattern recognition receptors (PPRs), thereby limiting Toll-like receptor (TLRs) signaling pathways and innate functions of several leukocytes (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>). The stealthy nature of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> has been largely attributed to the smooth structure of its lipopolysaccharide (Br-LPS). The Br-LPS, with an elongated fatty acid in the lipid A component, exhibits low toxicity and a reduced capacity to activate the immune response by evading TLR4 recognition (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> also expresses immunoregulatory elements such as BtpA and BtpB proteins, which translocate through the type IV secretion system (T4SS), inhibiting TLR signaling in innate cells and dampening the adaptive immune responses. BtpA specifically inhibits TLR2 and TLR4, while BtpB is a potent inhibitor of TLR2, TLR4, and TLR9 signaling (<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>, <xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>). This stealth strategy challenges the induction of protective immunity, which is dependent on cellular immunity mediated by CD4<sup>+</sup> T helper type 1 and CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells (<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>).</p><p>Vaccination remains a principal method for preventing, controlling, and eradicating brucellosis. Currently, two live attenuated vaccines based on <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 19 (S19) and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain RB51 are widely used in preventing bovine brucellosis (<xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>, <xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>). Despite their effectiveness, these vaccines can induce adverse effects in cattle, such as abortion, and pose a risk to humans, with the RB51 strain showing resistance to rifampicin, a primary antibiotic used to treat human brucellosis (<xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>, <xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>). Furthermore, the administration of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S19 strain, due to its LPS, generates diagnostic problems, since it does not allow differentiating between immunized animals and those that are naturally infected (<xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>). These safety concerns underscore the urgent need for safer vaccine alternatives.</p><p>Recent vaccine candidates against brucellosis have shown promising results, including DNA vaccines encoding genes for lumazine synthase (BLS) (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>), Cu/Zn superoxide dismutase (SOD) protein (<xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>), and various open reading frames (ORFs) such as BAB1_0267, BAB1_0270, BAB1_0278, BAB1_0278a, as well as a multiepitope DNA vaccine designed using immunoinformatic approaches (<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>). Despite their demonstrated immunogenicity and significant protection levels in mice infected with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308, these vaccine candidates exhibit considerable variability in protection levels. This observation was described by the BAB1_0267 and BAB1_0270 ORFs coding for a ZnMP and a SH3-like domain-containing protein, respectively, which are DNA vaccines that induced significant levels of IgG antibodies, cytokines (IFN-gamma) and lymphoproliferative responses; however, they conferred low levels of protection (<xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>). Therefore, the prime-boost immunization strategy, either with the same (homologous prime-boost) or a different formulation (heterologous prime-boost), has emerged as an effective approach to enhance the immunogenicity of DNA vaccines (<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>). Notably, the DNA prime-protein boost approach has successfully induced both humoral and cellular immune responses against several pathogens (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>), suggesting its potential applicability in enhancing the immunogenicity of DNA vaccine candidates against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>. In this study, we aim to contribute to developing an effective and safer vaccine against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus.</italic> Utilizing bioinformatics tools, we designed a multivalent DNA vaccine and a homologous recombinant protein vaccine based on Cu/Zn SOD, BAB1_0270 ORF (ZnMP), and BAB1_0267 ORF (SH3-like domain) proteins from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308. We evaluated the immunogenicity and protective response conferred by these vaccines in BALB/c mice, both administered alone and through a DNA prime-protein boost strategy.</p></sec><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="s2"><label>2</label><title>Materials and methods</title><sec id="s2_1"><label>2.1</label><title>An <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> design of the multivalent protein and its physicochemical and immunological parameters was used</title><p>The multivalent protein, designated S22, was designed using amino acid sequences from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 2308, which is available in the NCBI GenBank database. The S22 protein incorporates sequences of the ZnMP protein (Accession number: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="CAJ10226">CAJ10226</ext-link>), SH3-like domain protein (Accession number: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="CAJ10223">CAJ10223</ext-link>), and the SOD protein (Accession number: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-protein" xlink:href="CAJ12701">CAJ12701</ext-link>), linked by a (GGGGS)<sup>4</sup> peptide. The physicochemical properties of the S22 protein were analyzed using the Expasy ProtParam tool (<xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>). Its solubility was evaluated using the SOLpro server (Magnan et&#160;al., 2009). Immunological parameters, such as antigenicity, were assessed using VaxiJen v2.0 and ANTIGENpro (<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>), while the allergenicity and toxicity were evaluated using AllerTOP v.2.0 and ToxinPred2 servers, respectively (<xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>, <xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="s2_2"><label>2.2</label><title>Tertiary structure modeling and molecular docking of the multivalent protein</title><p>The tertiary structure of the S22 protein was predicted using the IntFold7 server (<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>) and refined with the GalaxyRefine web server (<xref rid="B25" ref-type="bibr">25</xref>). Model quality was evaluated using ProSA-web (<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>), and stereochemical quality was validated using the MolProbity server (<xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>). Molecular docking was performed with ClusPro 2.0 (<xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>), using the crystal structure of the TLR4/MD-2/lipid IVa complex from <italic toggle="yes">Mus musculus</italic> (PDB accession number: 3VQ1) (<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>). For adjuvating functions, one chain of Lymphocyte antigen 96 and one of the Toll-like receptor 4 were selected and docked against the S22 construct using ClusPro 2.0 server (<xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="s2_3"><label>2.3</label><title>Molecular dynamics simulations</title><p>The stability of S22, both alone and in complex with TLR4, was confirmed through Molecular Dynamics Simulations (MDS). CHARMM-GUI server&#8217;s Solution Builder tool constructed the input for GROMACS, using the TIP3P water model for both MDS setups. Monte Carlo methods were used to add K<sup>+</sup> and Cl<sup>-</sup> ions to each system until neutralization (<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>). The Amber Force Field ff19sb was used for the system description (<xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>). System potential energy minimization employed the steepest descent algorithm. The NVT and NPT ensembles were applied at 300K and 1 bar. The final MDS, with a 2 fs integration time step, was conducted for 45 ns. Energy recordings were made every 10 ps. The conformational stability of S22 and the S22-TLR4 complex was assessed by generating RMSD (root mean square deviation) profiles for backbone residues, RG (radius of gyration), and RMSF (root mean square fluctuation) from C-alpha atoms.</p></sec><sec id="s2_4"><label>2.4</label><title>Production of multivalent recombinant protein</title><p>The S22 gene was synthesized by GenScript (Piscataway, NJ, USA) with a 6-his tag at the carboxy terminal. The recombinant S22 protein was expressed and purified in <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> by Novoprotein Scientific Inc. (New Jersey, USA), whose description is that it would have &#8805;85% purity, with an endotoxin level of 1 EU (endotoxin units)/&#956;g of protein, using for this detection a polymyxin B bead column (Polymyxin Affi-Prep; Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, California).</p></sec><sec id="s2_5"><label>2.5</label><title>Construction and purification of the multivalent DNA vaccine</title><p>The gene for the S22 expression in eukaryotic cells was synthesized and codon-optimized by GenScript (Piscataway, NJ, USA) with a Kozak sequence addition. It was then subcloned into the pVAX eukaryotic expression vector (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., USA), resulting in the construct pVS22, used as a DNA vaccine. Large-scale plasmid purification followed the method described by (<xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="s2_6"><label>2.6</label><title>Evaluation of immune response</title><sec id="s2_6_1"><label>2.6.1</label><title>Animals</title><p>Animal experiments received approval from the ethics, bioethics, and biosafety committee of the University of Concepci&#243;n, certificate CEBB 1466-2023. Female BALB/c mice, aged 10 weeks, were obtained from the Institute of Public Health (ISP), Santiago, Chile. Mice were randomly distributed into groups of five and housed with food and water ad libitum at the Molecular Immunology Laboratory, Department of Microbiology, University of Concepci&#243;n.</p></sec><sec id="s2_6_2"><label>2.6.2</label><title>Schedule of immunization</title><p>The BALB/c mice were immunized with either the recombinant multivalent protein S22, the plasmid pVS22 DNA vaccine, or both in a DNA prime-protein boost strategy, administered at 15-day intervals. One group received an initial intradermal immunization with 20 &#181;g of S22 (50 &#181;l) emulsified in 50 &#181;l of Freund&#8217;s Complete Adjuvant (FCA) (Sigma), followed by subcutaneous and intraperitoneal administrations with 20 &#181;g of S22 (50 &#181;l) emulsified in 50 &#181;l of Freund&#8217;s Incomplete Adjuvant (FIA) (Sigma). Another group received three intramuscular immunizations with 50 &#181;g of the pVS22 DNA vaccine in 50 &#181;l of PBS in each tibialis anterior muscle (100 &#181;g of DNA/mouse). For the prime-boost strategy, the initial two immunizations were administered intramuscularly with 20 &#181;g of pVS22 DNA vaccine, followed by an intradermal dose of 20 &#181;g S22 in FCA. The control groups received either the pVAX empty vector (100 &#181;g of DNA/mouse) or 100 &#181;l of sterile PBS through intramuscular injections. A positive control group received a single intraperitoneal dose of 5 x 10^8 CFU of the <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> RB51 vaccine (<xref rid="f1" ref-type="fig">
<bold>Figure&#160;1</bold>
</xref>). Serum samples were obtained from blood drawn from the caudal tail vein two days before each immunization and were stored at -20&#176;C until use (<xref rid="f1" ref-type="fig">
<bold>Figure&#160;1</bold>
</xref>).</p><fig position="float" id="f1" orientation="portrait"><label>Figure&#160;1</label><caption><p>Schematic representation of the immunization and challenge protocol. Briefly, 96-well plates were coated with 3 &#956;g/ml of S22 protein diluted in carbonate-bicarbonate buffer (pH 9.4) and incubated overnight at 4&#176;C. After the plate was washed three times (3X), serial dilutions of the serum, starting at 1:200, were added. Plates were incubated for three hours at room temperature, followed by three washes with PBS.Anti-S22 IgG1 and IgG2a were detected using horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated rat anti-mouse IgG1 (US Biological Life Sciences, Salem, MA, USA) and HRP-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG2a (Jackson ImmunoResearch Inc., West Grove, PA, USA), respectively. Both secondary antibodies were diluted 1:3000 and incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature. Finally, the reaction was measured through the colorimetric response of o-phenylenediamine dihydrochloride (Sigma-Aldrich) with HRP, read at 450 nm using&#160;an Infiniti M Nano instrument (TECAN Group Ltd. Switzerland) (<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="s2_6_4"><label>2.6.4</label><title>Lymphoproliferative response</title><p>Forty-five days post-immunization, mice were euthanized to harvest spleens. Spleens were disaggregated to prepare cellular suspensions, and red blood cells were lysed using AcK solution (Promega, USA). After washing the splenocytes thrice with PBS, they were adjusted to 4 x 10^6 cells/mL in RPMI-1640 supplemented with fetal bovine serum and antibiotic-antimycotic solution (Sigma). Each well of a 96-well cell culture plate received 100 &#181;l of splenocytes. Later, the cells were stimulated by adding 100 &#181;l of S22 or total proteins from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> at concentrations of 0.5, 2.5, and 12.5 &#181;g/ml. The cultures were incubated for 72 hours at 37&#176;C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere. After this, the proliferation assay involved a pulse with 0.5 &#181;Ci/well of [Methyl-3H] thymidine for 8 hours, followed by harvesting and assessing the incorporated thymidine (c.p.m) using a scintillation counter. Concanavalin A (Merck) at a concentration of 10 &#181;g/ml was used as a positive control, and a complete RPMI 1640 medium was used as a negative control. All assays were performed in triplicate.</p></sec><sec id="s2_6_5"><label>2.6.5</label><title>Production of cytokines</title><p>Levels of cytokines produced by T helper lymphocytes were determined in the supernatants of splenocyte cultures. Briefly, 500 &#181;L of splenocytes (4 x 10^6 cells/ml) were stimulated with 500 &#181;L of S22 at different concentrations. These cultures were incubated in 24-well plates for 72 hours at 37&#176;C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere. After incubation, supernatants were collected for cytokine quantification using sandwich ELISA. The production of IFN-gamma, TNF-alpha, and IL-4 by splenocytes was measured using the Invitrogen ELISA kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc., USA). Standard curves were established for cytokine quantification (<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>, <xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>). Finally, absorbance generated by HRP enzyme-binding secondary antibodies was read at 450 nm using the Infiniti M Nano (TECAN Group Ltd. Switzerland). All assays were performed in triplicate.</p></sec><sec id="s2_6_6"><label>2.6.6</label><title>Challenge assays</title><p>The protective efficacy of the vaccine formulations, including a positive control group immunized with 5x10^8 CFU of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> RB51, was evaluated 45 days after the last immunization (<xref rid="f1" ref-type="fig">
<bold>Figure&#160;1</bold>
</xref>). For this, experimental and control mice were intraperitoneally challenged with 1 x 10^4 CFU of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308. Then, fifteen days post-challenge, mice were euthanized, and their spleens were aseptically removed and homogenized in 2 ml of sterile PBS. The homogenates were serially diluted and cultured through microdrops on Columbia agar plates supplemented with 5% sheep blood (bioM&#233;riex, France). After 72 h of incubation at 37&#176;C, colonies were counted to determine each animal&#8217;s CFU count per spleen. The level of protection was quantified subtracting the log10 of CFU counts from spleens of unimmunized (negative control) and log10 of immunized mice (<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>). All assays were done in duplicate.</p></sec></sec><sec id="s2_7"><label>2.7</label><title>Statistical analysis</title><p>Statistical analyses were performed using the GraphPad Prism 9 software (GraphPad Software Inc., La Jolla, CA, USA). Our engineered construct, depicted in <xref rid="f2" ref-type="fig">
<bold>Figure&#160;2A</bold>
</xref> and called S22, underwent extensive <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> analyses to evaluate its physicochemical properties, antigenicity, allergenicity, and toxicity. Using bioinformatic tools (SOLpro), we ascertained that S22 exhibits favorable solubility and hydrophobicity characteristics, essential for efficient expression and purification in <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>. Furthermore, evaluations using the VaxiJen v2.0 server and ANTIGENpro suggest that S22 is highly immunogenic. ToxinPred2 classified S22 as non-toxic, and AllerTOP v.2.0 server data suggests it is non-allergenic (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">
<bold>Table&#160;1</bold>
</xref>). These comprehensive analyses underscore that our designed S22 protein is not only immunogenic but also viable for production and safe for host administration. The efficacy of a vaccine in eliciting a stable immune response depends on its interaction with receptors of immune cells (<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>). TLR4, a pathogen recognition receptor expressed in several types of immune cells, including monocytes, macrophages, and dendritic cells, plays a pivotal role in this process (<xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>). Unlike other TLRs that assemble on the cell surface, a subset of TLR4- lymphocyte antigen 96 (MD2)-LPS complexes can be recruited to an endosomal compartment to activate an alternative signal transduction pathway for the induction of proinflammatory cytokines and type I interferons (IFNs) (<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>). The main agonist of TLR4 is LPS; however, other ligands, such as proteins or synthetic agonists different from LPS, can interact with this receptor (<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>, <xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>). Recognizing this, we explored the binding affinity of S22 to TLR4. Our approach involved constructing a 3D model of the tertiary structure of S22 using the Intfold7 server, recently enhanced with cutting-edge deep learning methods for accurate protein structure prediction (<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>). This model, subsequently refined with GalaxyRefine, showed energetically favorable and high-quality characteristics similar to native proteins clarified through X-ray crystallography. Validation by the ERRAT server confirmed the superior quality of the model, with a quality factor higher than 97.5, indicating a highly reliable structure (<xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>) (<xref rid="f2" ref-type="fig">
<bold>Figure&#160;2</bold>
</xref>).</p><p>The refined S22 model and the crystallized TLR4/MD2 complex from the PDB database were subjected to a molecular docking analysis. This revealed that S22 engages favorably with the TLR4/MD2 complex at three distinct sites. A subsequent molecular dynamic simulation (MDS), a critical step for assessing vaccine stability under <italic toggle="yes">in vivo</italic> conditions (<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>), predicted the stabilization of the S22 protein, both in its free form and in conjunction with the TLR4/MD2 complex, at 15 ns. A lower RMSF value indicates less flexibility and higher stability (<xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>). High RMSF values associated with regions of the linker and SH3 domain-like protein region of the S22 vaccine were reduced by forming the S22-TLR4/MD2 complex, suggesting a stable interaction. This interaction is crucial for activating the innate immune response and, in turn, potentiating the adaptive immune response.</p><p>In this work, we also evaluated the immune response triggered by vaccinating mice with the S22 protein, the pVS22 DNA construct, and the DNA prime-protein boost strategy. The use of different immunization routes is mainly based on the fact that subunit-based vaccines, such as the S22 protein, are administered with some type of adjuvant and the route of administration depends on the adjuvant. In the case of CFA, this is normally administered i.d. and IFA by s.c. or i.p. On the other hand, DNA vaccines have always been administered i.m (<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>). That is why in the primary immunization group with DNA/protein booster, the DNA doses were administered i.m. and the protein dose i.d. The immunization with S22 protein induced robust levels of both IgG1 and IgG2a, suggesting the activation of a comprehensive and balanced Th1 and Th2 immune response. The administration of the pVS22 DNA construct elicited a marked increase in IgG2a antibodies, a marker typically indicative of Th1 responses in mice (<xref rid="B43" ref-type="bibr">43</xref>). Notably, a balanced immune response was observed in the DNA prime-protein boost group. In this case, the initial responses were similar to those observed in the pVS22 group; however, with the third immunization with S22, the immune response evolved towards a mixed response characterized by higher production of both IgG1 and IgG2a. We also observed that antigen-specific splenocytes from mice immunized with either the S22 protein or pVS22 DNA construct displayed significant proliferation upon stimulation with the S22 protein, surpassing the response of the control groups. Intriguingly, the pVS22 group required higher protein concentrations for optimal lymphoproliferation than the S22 group. This finding suggests differential sensitivities in immune cell activation between the two vaccine candidate formulations. Additionally, splenocytes from all immunized mice exhibited significant proliferation when stimulated with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> total proteins. Under the evaluated conditions, splenocytes from the S22 protein and DNA prime-protein boost groups secreted elevated levels of IFN-&#947;. The importance of IFN-&#947;, especially in orchestrating macrophage bactericidal activity and favoring the production of protective IgG2a antibodies, cannot be overstated in the context of immunity against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (<xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>).</p><p>The potential of a heterologous DNA prime-protein boost strategy has been primarily explored in the context of viral pathogens, including COVID-19, Tembusu virus, HIV, and hepatitis C virus (<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>), with emerging applications against bacterial pathogens like <italic toggle="yes">Leptospira</italic> spp, <italic toggle="yes">Mycobacterium tuberculosis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">Mycobacterium kansassi</italic> (<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>, <xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>). In our study, while the DNA prime-protein boost did not significantly enhance overall protective levels, it effectively modulated antibody production from a predominantly IgG2a response to a balanced IgG1 and IgG2a profile. Moreover, this strategy successfully stimulated lymphoproliferation against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> total proteins and promoted IFN-&#947; production, an outcome not achieved solely with the DNA vaccine. This underscores the nuanced yet critical role of vaccine strategies in shaping immune responses, a factor that could be pivotal in developing more effective interventions against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>.</p><p>Immunoinformatic tools made it possible to generate a new multivalent vaccine candidate against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus.</italic> Administered intramuscularly and/or intradermally, this vaccine candidate demonstrated significant immunogenicity. Although it failed to elicit a substantial protective response against the pathogen, our results underscore the utility of the <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> design. This approach effectively predicted and optimized expression, purification, and immune response induction against customized antigens, significantly reducing the development time and costs. Such advantages align with those reported in the design of vaccines for other pathogens, including SARS-CoV-2 (<xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>).</p><p>The licensed RB51 vaccine, used as a positive control in our study and known for inducing strong cellular immunity, which predominantly stimulates cell-mediated immunity (Th1 type responses), conferred a high level of protection against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">
<bold>Table&#160;2</bold>
</xref>). Interestingly, our findings using specifically S22, induced high levels of immunogenicity but low levels of protection, demonstrating a discrepancy between our experimental vaccines and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> RB51, which suggests that the protection against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> involves a complicated balance between Th1/Th2- immune response (<xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>, <xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>). Moreover, these experimental vaccines (S22 and pVS22) induced lower immunogenicity and protective response compared to the SOD-only vaccine (<xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>). Thus, we hypothesize that this effect is due to competition among epitopes from SOD, ZnMP and SH3 proteins, which could specifically reduce (ZnMP and SH3 protein epitopes) the immune response conferred by SOD, recognized as the most immunogenic protein used in the construction of our experimental vaccines against <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>. Our results demonstrate that the same antigenic construct can elicit different immune responses, depending on its formulation, either as a purified protein (S22) or as a gene in a DNA vector (pVS22). In addition, our study illustrates the potential of the DNA prime-protein boost strategy in modulating immune responses. This immunization scheme shifted the pVS22-induced response, characterized by IgG2a production without IFN-&#947;, towards a mixed Th1/Th2 response with IFN-&#947; production, similar to the response induced by the S22 protein alone. Therefore, these findings open up new avenues for vaccine development and underscore the importance of careful antigen selection in the design of multivalent vaccines. The combination of antigens can lead to suboptimal immune responses due to potential antagonism or competition between epitopes, which may reduce the vaccine efficacy, an important aspect that must be considered in future studies.</p></sec></body><back><sec sec-type="data-availability" id="s5"><title>Data availability statement</title><p>The original contributions presented in the study are included in the article/supplementary materials, further inquiries can be directed to the corresponding author/s.</p></sec><sec sec-type="ethics-statement" id="s6"><title>Ethics statement</title><p>The animal study was approved by Ethics, bioethics, and biosafety committee of the University of Concepci&#243;n, Chile.
