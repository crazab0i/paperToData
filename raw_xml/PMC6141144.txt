However, despite extensive research, the molecular mechanisms underlying the attenuation of this strain are still unknown. In the current study, we conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis of the whole-genome sequence of Rev.1 against that of the virulent reference strain, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M. This analysis revealed five regions of insertion and three regions of deletion within the Rev.1 genome, among which, one large region of insertion, comprising 3,951 bp, was detected in the Rev.1 genome. In addition, we found several missense mutations within important virulence-related genes, which may be used to determine the mechanism underlying virulence attenuation. Collectively, our findings provide new insights into the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence mechanisms and, therefore, may serve as a basis for the rational design of new <italic sec-type="intro" id="S0001"><title>Introduction</title><p>Brucellosis, also known as undulant fever or Malta fever, is the most common worldwide zoonotic bacterial disease, infecting over half a million people annually [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>]. The cause of brucellosis is <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species: intracellular [<xref rid="CIT0002">2</xref>], Gram-negative bacteria, which were first isolated by Sir David Bruce (Malta, 1887) from the spleens of soldiers with fatal cases of the disease [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>]. There are 10 known <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, based on host specificity: <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> (goats and sheep), <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (cattle), <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (swine), <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> (dogs), <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> (sheep and rams), <italic toggle="yes">B. neotomae</italic> (desert wood rats), <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic> (cetacean), <italic toggle="yes">B. pinnipedia</italic> (seal), <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic> (voles), and <italic toggle="yes">B. inopinata</italic> (unknown) [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus, B. melitensis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> are the most pathogenic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species for humans. The three <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, <italic toggle="yes">B. canis, B. ovis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. neotomae</italic> are of low pathogenicity for humans [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>].</p><p><italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> have a particular tropism toward the reproductive system of their primary animal hosts, which often leads to abortion in pregnant females and to sterility in males [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>, <xref rid="CIT0003">3</xref>]. Inside their hosts, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> reside within various types of cell, where they establish a replicative niche and remain protected from the immune response of their host [<xref rid="CIT0002">2</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> can be passed on to humans upon direct contact with fluid discharges from an infected animal or by the consumption of dairy products made of unpasteurized milk, mainly goat milk and fresh soft cheese. Containment of human brucellosis depends upon the successful vaccination of livestock and imposing strict farming hygiene, surveillance, and infection control measures [<xref rid="CIT0001">1</xref>].</p><p>In the mid-1950s, Elberg and Herzberg developed a live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain, known as Rev.1, from the virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 6056 strain (biovar 1). This strain was shown to successfully protect and reduce abortions in small ruminants [<xref rid="CIT0004">4</xref>], and it possesses several unique characteristics &#8211; including susceptibility to high concentrations of basic fuchsin and thionin (20&#160;&#181;g/ml), resistance to 2.5&#160;&#181;g/ml streptomycin, and susceptibility to 5 IU penicillin G [<xref rid="CIT0005">5</xref>] &#8211; factors that enable a clear distinction between the vaccine and virulent strains by using bacteriological tests. The original Rev.1 strain was later passaged by Elberg and, in 1970, passage 101 was made available as a freeze-dried seed stock culture. The strain originating from passage 101 resembles the original parental seed material, making it suitable for the prophylactic vaccination of sheep and goats [<xref rid="CIT0005">5</xref>].</p><p>To date, the attenuation mechanisms of the Rev.1 have not been fully characterized. Comparative genomic analysis of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine and virulent strains, had been widely used in order to reveal insights into virulence attenuation [<xref rid="CIT0006">6</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="CIT0009">9</xref>].A few comparative genomic and proteomic analyses of Rev.1 and the virulent reference strain 16M (biovar 1) have been conducted to elucidate the molecular mechanism underlying virulence attenuation [<xref rid="CIT0010">10</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="CIT0012">12</xref>]. These analyses revealed a panel of 32 genome-specific markers of the Rev.1 strain, as well as various differentially expressed proteins involved in iron metabolism, sugar transport, lipid metabolism, and protein synthesis. However, the genomic analyses were based on an assembled draft version of the Rev.1 genome, as there was no complete genomic sequence available at the time. In a recent study [<xref rid="CIT0013">13</xref>], we sequenced and annotated the whole genome of the original Elberg <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain, passage 101, 1970. Using this information, we report, here, the first comprehensive comparative analysis of the complete genomic sequence of Rev.1 against the virulent strain 16M, aimed at elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying the virulence attenuation of Rev.1. We have identified several candidate genes that could be related to the virulence attenuation of Rev.1 and may, therefore, facilitate the design of novel and better vaccines to control brucellosis.</p></sec><sec id="S0002"><title>Results and discussion</title><sec id="S0002-S2001"><title>General genomic sequence features</title><p>We recently reported the complete genomic sequence of the original Elberg <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain [<xref rid="CIT0013">13</xref>]. </p><sec id="S0002-S2002-S3001"><title>Genomic insertions</title><p>ChrI of Rev.1 contains three small RIs (174 bp, 135 bp, and 32 bp; RI1, RI2, and RI3, respectively) and one large RI (3,951 bp; RI4). A bioinformatic analysis revealed the location of RI1 within the ORF BMEI1729, which encodes the mercuric resistance operon regulatory protein, MerR, leading to the addition of 58 amino acids (<xref rid="F0002">Figure 2(A</xref><underline>)</underline>). DNA-binding transcriptional regulators of the MerR family had previously been found in a wide range of bacterial genera and shown to respond to environmental stimuli, such as oxidative stress, heavy metals, or antibiotics [<xref rid="CIT0015">15</xref>]. As the MerR response to mercury(II) was previously shown to rely on the specific positioning and orientation of metal-binding amino acids within the protein [<xref rid="CIT0015">15</xref>], mutations in <italic toggle="yes">merR</italic> may play a role in bacterial attenuation.<fig id="F0002" fig-type="figure" orientation="portrait" position="float"><object-id pub-id-type="doi">10.1080/21505594.2018.1511677-F0002</object-id><label>Figure 2.</label><caption><p>Regions of insertions within the MerR (<bold>a</bold>) and BMEI1570 (<bold>b</bold>) proteins of the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 strain. Pairwise alignment between the Rev.1 (top) and 16M (bottom) sequences was visualized using the Clustal X color scheme for residues coloring [<xref rid="CIT0063">63</xref>]. (<bold>c</bold>) Crystal structure of the <italic toggle="yes">E. Coli</italic> copper efflux regulator homodimer (4WLS). DNA is shown as white surface. The inserted sequence 1 (RI1) is shown in red. (<bold>d</bold>) Crystal structure of the <italic toggle="yes">T. maritima</italic> glycerate kinase (2B8N). The inserted sequence 2 (RI2) is shown in yellow. Active site residues are shown as spheres.</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" specific-use="web-only" content-type="color" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="kvir-09-01-1511677-g002.jpg"/></fig></p><p>RI2 is located within the ORF BMEI1570, which encodes a putative hydroxypyruvate reductase, leading to the addition of 45 amino acids to the N-terminus of the protein (<xref rid="F0002">Figure 2(B</xref>)). Hydroxypyruvate reductase metabolizes the toxic intermediate glyoxylate &#8211; a small and very reactive dicarboxylic acid molecule synthesized by most eukaryotes and prokaryotes [<xref rid="CIT0016">16</xref>]. Notably, the hydroxypyruvate reductase of the attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> &#916;<italic toggle="yes">bpdA</italic> phosphodiesterase mutant, which produces excess c-di-GMP, was shown to be downregulated, emphasizing the relevance of this enzyme in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence [<xref rid="CIT0017">17</xref>].</p><p>Next, to evaluate the possible effect of RI1 and RI2 on protein functionality, we conducted a structural analysis of the amino acid sequences of the Rev.1 MerR and hydroxypyruvate reductase. The RI1 of MerR is located within two known domains: MerR (Pfam PF00376), positioned between residues 1&#8211;24, and the DNA-binding domain MerR-type helix-turn-helix domain (Prosite PS50937), positioned between residues 1&#8211;43. A homologue of MerR &#8211; the copper efflux regulator homodimer (PDB 4WLS) of <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic> &#8211; was recently crystalized with DNA [<xref rid="CIT0018">18</xref>] and was shown to be a dimer comprising two N-terminal DNA binding domains connected by a dimerization helix [<xref rid="CIT0018">18</xref>]. Our analysis revealed that RI1 is located within the dimerization helix of MerR (<xref rid="F0002">Figure 2(C</xref>)); thus, the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strain 16M, which lacks RI1, may possess a deficient dimerization of the DNA binding domain. RI2 of hydroxypyruvate reductase is located within a kinase domain (between residues 33 and 190), which, according to Pfam belongs to the DUF4147 family. The ORF BMEI1570 shares a 37% identity with the glycerate kinase of <italic toggle="yes">Thermotoga maritima</italic> (PDB 2B8N) [<xref rid="CIT0019">19</xref>]. The position of RI2, according to the crystal structure of the glycerate kinase of <italic toggle="yes">T. maritima</italic>, is indeed within its kinase domain (PDB 2B8N [19,<xref rid="F0002">Figure 2(D</xref>)). The inserted sequence folds into a helix and two &#946;-strands, which are part of a 6-strand &#946;-sheet, and contains one of the active-site residues. Notably, aligning 57 and 88 non-redundant protein sequences of the MerR and hydroxypyruvate reductase homologous (Supplementary Table S1A and S1B, respectively), revealed that most sequences contain RI1 and RI2, respectively (data not shown). We assume that RI1 and RI2 affect protein activity, although further studies are required to determine the possible association between these RIs and Rev.1 attenuation.</p><p>RI3 is located at the 3&#697; end of the ORF BMEI1203, which encodes ribonuclease D. An <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> analysis revealed an identical sequence immediately downstream of RI3 (data not shown); therefore, we assume that RI3 does not modify the translated protein. RI4 contains an ORF that encodes a transcriptional regulator belonging to the Cro/CI family. This region is inserted between a transposase element of the IS6 family (BMEI0200) and a hypothetical protein (BMEI0199). Transcriptional regulators, such as Cro/CI, regulate the expression of effector genes by binding to the promoter region, thereby positively or negatively affecting transcription to consequently modify the expressed mRNAs and the produced proteins [<xref rid="CIT0020">20</xref>]. A novel function of Cro/CI as a transcriptional regulator has recently been reported in <italic toggle="yes">Staphylococcus aureus</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0021">21</xref>], where the predominant clinical strain USA300 was shown to secrete reduced levels of virulence-associated proteins as a result of single-point mutations inside or immediately upstream the gene encoding Cro/CI [<xref rid="CIT0021">21</xref>]. Blat search analysis revealed homologs of the transcriptional regulator Cro/CI in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, including <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (strains 544, 2308, and S19), <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (strain 1330), <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic>. Further studies are required to determine the role of Cro/CI in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> transcriptional regulation and its possible association with bacterial attenuation. RI5 is located immediately downstream of BMEII0307, which encodes the WD40 repeat domain-containing protein, and does not affect the protein sequence.</p></sec><sec id="S0002-S2002-S3002"><title>Genomic deletions</title><p>We identified three RDs within the Rev.1 genome, as compared with the 16M genome (<xref rid="T0002">Table 2</xref>). ChrI of Rev.1 contains two small RDs of 27 bp and 24 bp (RD1 and RD2, respectively), while chrII contains one small RD of 40 bp (RD3). RD1 is located within the ORF BMEI1651, which encodes the urease accessory protein UreE1, leading to an in-frame deletion of His149-Gly157. Most members of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> show strong urease activity [<xref rid="CIT0022">22</xref>], and it has been suggested that urease protects <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> during their infection via the oral route, which is the major route of infection leading to human brucellosis [<xref rid="CIT0022">22</xref>]. Blat search analysis revealed a similar RD in <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> 1330. The importance of this RD in the attenuation of Rev.1 is questionable, as <italic toggle="yes">ureE</italic> in cluster 1 had previously been shown to be a pseudogene in <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0023">23</xref>]. RD2 is located downstream of BMEI1888, which encodes lactoylglutathione lyase, and RD3 is located at the 5&#697; end of BMEII0784, which encodes a putative hydroxlase. Both BMEI1888 and BMEII0784 belong to the glyoxalase pathway, which plays an important role in detoxification by converting methylglyoxal into D-lactic acid [<xref rid="CIT0024">24</xref>]. The gene <italic toggle="yes">glxI</italic>, which encodes lactoglyglutathione lyase in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308, had previously been shown to be activated in macrophages 4&#160;h post-infection [<xref rid="CIT0025">25</xref>], and its induction was suggested to contribute to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proliferation and intracellular survival [<xref rid="CIT0025">25</xref>]. Our genomic comparative analysis revealed a similar RD3 in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, including <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (strains 544, 2308, and S19), <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (strain 1330), <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic>, and <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic>. Notably, we noticed a larger RD2, consisting of 40 bp, in the genome of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (strains 544, 2308, and S19); an insertion of 56 and 72 bp in the genomes of <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic>, respectively; and a similar sequence to that of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M in the <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (strain 1330) genome. As RD2 is divergent among <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, we may consider it a potential target for the typing and detection of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p></sec></sec><sec id="S0002-S2003"><title>Genomic SNPs</title><p>Genome-wide comparisons between Rev.1 and 16M were conducted to identify all the single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) between the two genomes. We detected a missense mutation (c.86C&gt;&#160;T) in the ORF BMEII0695 of Rev.1, which encodes PCS. The role of PCS in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> pathogenicity was reported by Comerci et al. [<xref rid="CIT0030">30</xref>], who showed that <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> possessing a <italic toggle="yes">pcs</italic> mutation display a reproducible virulence defect in mice. As the phospholipid composition of the membrane is critical for the interaction of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> with the host cell [<xref rid="CIT0031">31</xref>], the Rev.1 <italic toggle="yes">pcs</italic> mutation may contribute to its attenuation.</p></sec><sec id="S0002-S2004-S3002"><title>Stress proteins/chaperones</title><p>Facultative intracellular <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are known to be capable of employing various strategies to survive the bactericidal environment within phagocytes and, thereby, to avoid the immune system of the host [<xref rid="CIT0032">32</xref>]. During this process, molecular chaperones, peroxiredoxins, and siderophores were shown to play an important role in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> survival [<xref rid="CIT0033">33</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="CIT0035">35</xref>]. Our SNP analysis revealed mutations in the ORFs BMEI2002 (c.25A&gt;&#160;C; a missense mutation), BMEII1048 (c.850T&gt;&#160;C; a missense mutation), and BMEI1619 (c.738T&gt;&#160;A; a missense mutation), which encode the molecular chaperones DnaK, GroEL, and Hsp33, respectively. The induction of DnaK has been reported in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0036">36</xref>], and the induced DnaK enabled <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to adapt to the hostile environment of the macrophage and was essential for the intracellular multiplication of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0033">33</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0036">36</xref>]. We also found missense mutations in the ORFs BMEI1049 (c.167C&gt;&#160;A) and BMEII0078 (c.485A&gt;&#160;C), which encode a peroxiredoxin and the 2,3-dihydroxybenzoate-AMP ligase EntE, respectively. The <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> peroxiredoxin AhpC had previously been shown to detoxify endogenous H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> generated via aerobic metabolism [<xref rid="CIT0034">34</xref>]; the catecholic siderophores dihydroxybenzoate acid (DHBA) and brucebactin were shown to be produced and utilized as iron sources by <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 [<xref rid="CIT0035">35</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0037">37</xref>]; and 2,3-DHBA has been assumed to be required by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> at the state of gestation after changes in the iron status [<xref rid="CIT0035">35</xref>]. These data suggest that Rev.1 may have deficiencies in proteins that are crucially involved in stress tolerance.</p></sec><sec id="S0002-S2004-S3003"><title>Regulation</title><p>To survive extreme environmental changes and respond well to various stress conditions within their hosts, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> require a suitable regulation system [<xref rid="CIT0032">32</xref>]. Our SNP analysis revealed missense mutations in the ORF BMEI0685 (c.896C&gt;&#160;G and c.884C&gt;&#160;T), which encodes an AraC-like transcriptional regulator, and in the ORF BMEI1816 (c.443G&gt;&#160;C), encoding the sensor histidine kinase RegB two-component system. The AraC-like transcriptional regulator DhbR was shown to regulate the biosynthesis of 2,3-DHBA and brucebactin, which play an important role in the virulence of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> in its natural ruminant host [<xref rid="CIT0038">38</xref>]. RegA, the regulator of the RegB/RegA two-component system of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic>, was shown to control oxidative respiration and denitrification, which enables <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to adapt to oxygen-limited conditions, produce sufficient energy under low oxygen conditions, and eliminate toxic NO produced by macrophages [<xref rid="CIT0039">39</xref>]. Thus, the regulatory capabilities of Rev.1 appear to be reduced, which may contribute to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec><sec id="S0002-S2004-S3004"><title>Amino acid metabolism</title><p>Previous studies found that the replicative compartment of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is poor in nutrients, such that the synthesis of amino acids is obligatory for its intracellular multiplication [<xref rid="CIT0040">40</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0041">41</xref>]. Indeed, in <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic>, mutations in genes that are involved in amino acid synthesis result in bacterial attenuation [<xref rid="CIT0040">40</xref>]. Our SNP analysis revealed missense or frameshift mutations in 18 genes involved in amino acid metabolism (Supplementary Table S5). Notably, we found that the ORF BMEI1759 possesses a missense mutation (c.523G&gt;&#160;C) in the Rev.1 genome. This ORF encodes the vitamin B12-dependent methyltransferase MetH, which is involved in methionine biosynthesis and was previously screened in a murine infection model aimed at identifying pathogenic <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genes expressed <italic toggle="yes">in vivo</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0028">28</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0042">42</xref>]. Thus, we suggest that mutations in Rev.1 genes that encode enzymes involved in amino acid biosynthesis may contribute to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec><sec id="S0002-S2004-S3005"><title>Cell-wall synthesis and penicillin resistance</title><p>The cell wall is the major shape-maintaining element in bacteria, and its integrity is crucial for cell viability. In both Gram-positive and -negative bacteria, the cell wall comprises a layer of the cross-linked polymer peptidoglycan (PG), which is composed of polysaccharides with alternating <italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-acetylglucosamine (GlcNAc) and <italic toggle="yes">N-</italic>acetylmuramic acid (MurNAc) saccharide groups. PG synthesis begins in the cytoplasm, where UDP-GlcNAc is synthesized from fructose-6-phosphate by the Glm enzymes, while UDP-<italic toggle="yes">N</italic>-acetylmuramyl-pentapeptide is synthesized from UDP-GlcN by the Mur enzymes (MurA, MurB, MurC, MurD, MurE and MurF) [<xref rid="CIT0043">43</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0044">44</xref>]. The proteins that catalyze the final steps of the PG synthesis include the bifunctional penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), which catalyze the polymerization of the glycan strand (transglycosylation) and the cross-linking between glycan chains (transpeptidation). The penicillin-binding domain possesses a transpeptidase, a carboxypeptidase, or an endopeptidase activity. To destruct the PG layer of pathogenic bacteria, the PG synthesis pathway is often targeted, for example with &#946;-lactam antibiotics. In contrast to 16M, Rev.1 is susceptible to 5IU penicillin G, thus allowing a clear distinction between virulent strains and the vaccine strain [<xref rid="CIT0005">5</xref>]. In <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus pneumoniae</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Neisseria meningitidis</italic>, the main mechanism of penicillin G resistance is the alteration of PBPs [<xref rid="CIT0045">45</xref>,<xref rid="CIT0046">46</xref>]. Point mutations in PBPs reduce the affinity for penicillin, which may increase penicillin resistance [<xref rid="CIT0047">47</xref>]. Additionally, penicillin resistance may be the consequence of penicillin acylase activity [<xref rid="CIT0048">48</xref>], which has been reported in many species of Gram-negative bacteria [<xref rid="CIT0049">49</xref>&#8211;<xref rid="CIT0051">51</xref>], leading to the hydrolysis of penicillin, with the production of the relatively inactive 6-aminopenicillanic acid. Another common mechanism underlying penicillin resistance is the activity of &#946;-lactamase, which hydrolyzes the &#946;-lactam ring and, consequently, deactivates &#946;-lactam antibiotics [<xref rid="CIT0052">52</xref>].</p><p>Our comparative analysis of PBPs and penicillin acylases (ORFs BMEI0573, BMEI0914, BMEI1055, BMEI1351, BMEI1831, BMEII0253, BMEII0211, BMEII0212, BMEI0913, and BMEI0814) in the 16M and Rev.1 strains revealed a 100% identity in nucleotide and amino acid sequences (data not shown). Furthermore, we did not find homologs of genes encoding &#946;-lactamase enzymes in the genomes of the two strains. However, our SNP analysis revealed three genes in the Rev.1 genome, encoding proteins involved in cell-wall synthesis, which may be associated with penicillin susceptibility of this strain: BMEI0574 (c.4G&gt;&#160;A; a missense mutation), which encodes the UDP-N-acetylmuramoyl-L-alanyl-D-glutamate&#8211;2,6-diaminopimelate ligase MurE; BMEI1302 (c.968G&gt;&#160;A; a missense mutation), which encodes soluble lytic murein transglycosylases; and BMEII0839 (c.421A&gt;&#160;C; a missense mutation), which encodes the undecaprenyl-phosphate/decaprenyl-phosphate GlcNAc-1-phosphate transferase. MurE has been previously associated with resistance to &#946;-lactam antibiotics in <italic toggle="yes">S. aureus</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0053">53</xref>], and a mechanism was suggested whereby the suppression of &#946;-lactam antibiotic resistance in MurE mutants is related to the reduced cellular activity of PBP2A and PBP2 &#8211; the two main proteins involved in the &#946;-lactam antibiotic resistance in <italic toggle="yes">S. aureus</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0053">53</xref>]. Recently, MurE has been implicated in the transformation of high &#946;-lactam antibiotic resistance from <italic toggle="yes">Streptococcus oralis</italic> into <italic toggle="yes">S. pneumoniae</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0054">54</xref>].</p><p>Another important finding in our analysis is a missense mutation in the ORF BMEI0123 (c.33C&gt;&#160;G), which encodes the peptidyl-prolyl cis-trans isomerase. This protein had previously been reported to be one of the four proteins uniquely expressed by the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M strain, as compared with Rev.1 [<xref rid="CIT0012">12</xref>]. A recent study determined the role of PrsA &#8211; a peptidyl-prolyl cis-trans isomerase &#8211; in the folding of <italic toggle="yes">Bacillus subtilis</italic> PBPs [<xref rid="CIT0055">55</xref>]; in the absence of PrsA, PBP2a, PBP2b, PBP3, and PBP4 are unstable, which results in growth arrest. Furthermore, PrsA was shown to be involved in glycopeptide and oxacillin resistance in <italic toggle="yes">S. aureus</italic> [<xref rid="CIT0056">56</xref>], where the disruption of <italic toggle="yes">prsA</italic> dramatically decreases the resistance of methicillin-resistant <italic toggle="yes">S. aureus</italic> to oxacillin [<xref rid="CIT0056">56</xref>]. Our data suggest that impairments in one or more genes involved in PG synthesis may affect the penicillin susceptibility of Rev.1 and contribute to the attenuation of this strain.</p></sec></sec></sec><sec id="S0003"><title>Conclusions</title><p>The widely used live attenuated <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev.1 vaccine strain successfully protects and reduces abortions in small ruminants [<xref rid="CIT0005">5</xref>], but the molecular mechanisms responsible for its attenuation remain unclear. Here, we performed a comparative genomic analysis between the whole-genome sequence of the original Elberg Rev.1 strain, passage 101, and the 16M virulent reference strain. Although the genomes of the two strains are 99.9% identical, we identified five RIs and three RDs in the Rev.1 genome, as compared with that of 16M. Two RIs are located within the ORFs BMEI1729 and BMEI1570, resulting in the addition of 58 and 45 amino acids, respectively. A structural analysis of the sequence of these proteins showed that these RIs are positioned in highly important locations within the proteins, namely, in the dimerization helix of MerR and in the kinase domain of hydroxypyruvate reductase, which presumably contributes to the activity of these proteins. The third RI, encoding a transcriptional regulator that belongs to the Cro/CI family and is absent in the 16M reference strain, is located between the ORFs BMEI0200 and BMEI0199.</p><p>A SNP analysis revealed non-synonymous and frameshift mutations in important virulence-related genes involved in lipid metabolism, stress response, regulation, amino acid metabolism, and cell-wall synthesis. We suggest that these genes are involved in the molecular mechanisms underlying Rev.1 attenuation, although conclusively determining their roles in the virulence attenuation of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> requires further characterization, for example, through mutation, complementation, transcriptomic, and host-response studies.</p></sec><sec id="S0004"><title>Materials and methods</title><sec id="S0004-S2001"><title>Genomic data</title><p>Rev.1 and 16M genomes and annotations were downloaded from NCBI (accession numbers: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_003317.1">NC_003317.1</ext-link> and <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_003318.1">NC_003318.1</ext-link> for 16M; <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="CP024715">CP024715</ext-link> and <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="CP024716">CP024716</ext-link> for Rev.1).</p></sec><sec id="S0004-S2002"><title>COG analysis</title><p>Reverse position-specific BLAST (RPS-BLAST) [<xref rid="CIT0057">57</xref>] was used to search protein sequences against a local COG BLAST database, which was downloaded from NCBI. The expectation value (E) threshold was set to 0.01, and the BLAST output was parsed using an updated version of the cdd2cog.pl script [<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://github.com/aleimba/bac-genomics-scripts/tree/master/cdd2cog">https://github.com/aleimba/bac-genomics-scripts/tree/master/cdd2cog</ext-link>] to obtain the assignment statistics of the COGs. COG assignment was performed to selected protein sequences (16M proteins whose corresponding Rev.1 genes carry a potentially functional mutation) and to the full list of the 16M annotated proteins. The enrichment p-value was calculated using the hypergeometric test, in which we set the population size to be the number of proteins out of all annotated proteins that had at least one COG assignment. Similarly, the sample size was the number of proteins with functional mutations that have COG assignments.</p></sec><sec id="S0004-S2003"><title>Comparative genomic analysis</title><p>The average nucleotide identity (ANI) between the Rev.1 and 16M genomes was calculated using the ANI calculator (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://enve-omics.ce.gatech.edu/ani/">http://enve-omics.ce.gatech.edu/ani/</ext-link>). The whole-genome alignment between 16M and Rev.1 strains was conducted using the progressive Mauve algorithm included in the Mauve genome alignment package [<xref rid="CIT0058">58</xref>]. The Rev.1 genome was reversed and complemented before being subjected to the software. The locations of conserved and unique regions were retrieved from the backbone output file. Deletions and insertions were validated by retrieving the indel area together with flanking regions from each side, and these regions were aligned, using the blat software [<xref rid="CIT0059">59</xref>], against the relevant genome, following a correction of the indel borders. A dot plot was generated using the last-dotplot tool within the LAST local alignment package [<xref rid="CIT0060">60</xref>]. Pairwise alignment between the Rev.1 and 16M sequences was conducted using MUSCLE software [<xref rid="CIT0061">61</xref>] and visualized with Jalview [<xref rid="CIT0062">62</xref>]. Residues are colored according to the Clustal X color scheme [<xref rid="CIT0063">63</xref>].</p></sec><sec id="S0004-S2004"><title>SNP detection</title><p>The MUMmer [<xref rid="CIT0064">64</xref>] SNP detection pipeline was used to detect SNPs. Briefly, the Rev.1 assembly was aligned to the 16M strain genome using the NUCmer program. The SNPs contained in the generated delta-encoded alignment file were fetched and reported by the show-snp utility. Next, output SNPs were converted to a VCF format using an in-house script and were subjected to annotation by the SnpEff tool [<xref rid="CIT0065">65</xref>]. The SnpEff database of the 16M genome (2002 assembly) was built based on a GenBank file that was downloaded from NCBI.</p></sec><sec id="S0004-S2005"><title>Structural analysis</title><p>The Pfam [<xref rid="CIT0066">66</xref>] and Prosite [<xref rid="CIT0067">67</xref>] databases were used to obtain known functional domains of the protein sequence. The protein sequences for alignment were obtained from the universal protein knowledgebase, UniProt. Sequence alignments were viewed using Jalview [<xref rid="CIT0062">62</xref>]. Proteins with structural data from these families were used to inspect the 3D location of the inserted sequences and to predict their functional effect. 3D structures were viewed in PyMOL (The PyMOL Molecular Graphics System, Version 2.0 Schr&#246;dinger, LLC).</p></sec></sec></body><back><sec id="S0005"><title>Disclosure statement</title><p>No potential conflict of interest was reported by the authors.</p></sec><sec id="S0006" sec-type="supplementary-material"><title>Supplementary Material</title><p>The supplementary data for this article can be accessed <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://doi.org/10.1080/21505594.2018.1511677">here</ext-link>.</p><supplementary-material content-type="local-data" id="SM4018" position="float" orientation="portrait"><caption><title>Supplemental Material</title></caption><media xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" mimetype="application" mime-subtype="zip" xlink:href="kvir-09-01-1511677-s001.zip" orientation="portrait" id="d37e1458" position="anchor"/></supplementary-material></sec><ref-list><title>References</title><ref id="CIT0001"><label>[1]</label><mixed-citation publication-type="journal"><person-group person-group-type="author"><name name-style="western"><surname>von Bargen</surname><given-names>K</given-names></name>, <name name-style="western"><surname>Gorvel</surname><given-names>J-P</given-names></name>, <name name-style="western"><surname>Salcedo</surname><given-names>SP.</given-names></name></person-group><article-title>Internal affairs: investigating the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> intracellular lifestyle</article-title>. <source>FEMS Microbiol Rev</source>.
