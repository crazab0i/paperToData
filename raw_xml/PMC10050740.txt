      <abstract>
        <sec>
          <title>Introduction</title>
          <p>Brucellosis, a serious public health issue affecting animals and humans, is neglected in West Africa (WA).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec>
          <title>Methods</title>
          <p>In the present study, bio-typing, multi-locus sequence typing (MLST), multiple-locus variable-number tandem repeat analysis (MLVA), and whole genome sequencing single-nucleotide polymorphism (WGS-SNP) analysis were used to characterize the <italic>Brucella abortus (B. abortus</italic>) strains from WA.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec>
          <title>Results</title>
          <p>All of the 309 strains analyzed in this study were extracted and downloaded from the international MLVA bank and were from 10 hosts (cattle, humans, ovine, buffalo, dromedaries, horse, sheep, zebu, dog, and cat) distributed in 17 countries in WA. Based on the bio-typing, three biovars, dominated by <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv.3, were observed and reported across seven decades (1958&#x2013;2019). With MLST, 129 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from the present study were sorted into 14 STs, with ST34 as the predicted founder. These 14 STs clustered into the global MLST data into three clone complexes (C I&#x2013;C III) with the majority of strains clustering in C I, while C II forms an independent branch, and C III harbors three STs shared by different continents. These data revealed that most cases were caused by strains from native lineages. According to the MLVA-11 comparison, 309 strains were divided into 22 MLVA-11 genotypes, 15 of which were unique to WA and the remaining seven had a global distribution. MLVA-16 analysis showed that there were no epidemiological links among these strains. Based on the MLVA data, <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from WA have high genetic diversity, and predominated genotypes were descended from a native lineage. While the MLVA-16 globally highlights that the dominant native and few introduced lineages (from Brazil, the USA, South Korea, Argentina, India, Italy, Portugal, the UK, Costa Rica, and China) co-driving the <italic>B. abortus</italic> ongoing prevalence in WA. The high-resolution SNP analysis implied the existence of introduced <italic>B. abortus</italic> lineages, which may be reasonably explained by the movement and trade of dominant hosts (cattle) and/or their products.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec>
          <title>Discussion</title>
          <p>Our results indicated that <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains in WA consist of native and introduced strains that necessitate control such as vaccination, testing, slaughtering, and movement control by the relevant country authorities to reduce brucellosis in livestock.</p>
        </sec>

    <sec sec-type="intro" id="s1">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p>Brucellosis, a widespread bacterial zoonotic disease that can cause considerable suffering in humans and massive economic losses in the animal industry, is caused by Gram-negative facultative intracellular bacteria of the genus <italic>Brucella</italic> (<xref rid="B1" ref-type="bibr">1</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B3" ref-type="bibr">3</xref>). The genus includes 12 species, among which <italic>Brucella melitensis, B. abortus</italic>, and <italic>B. suis</italic> are the most important species and responsible for the majority of human and animal brucellosis cases (<xref rid="B4" ref-type="bibr">4</xref>). It has been reported that there are ~500,000 new cases of human infection annually, especially in low-income tropical countries (<xref rid="B5" ref-type="bibr">5</xref>). Cattle are natural hosts of the intracellular pathogen <italic>B. abortus</italic>, which can also be isolated in other hosts, such as sheep, camels, and horses, and imposes a significant burden on the health and reproduction of these important livestock (<xref rid="B6" ref-type="bibr">6</xref>, <xref rid="B7" ref-type="bibr">7</xref>). Infected animals can have live offspring following the initial abortion, and they may continue to shed the bacteria (<xref rid="B8" ref-type="bibr">8</xref>). Transmission to human occurs through unprotected handling of tissues or body fluids from infected animals, consumption of unpasteurized milk and milk products, or inhalation of <italic>Brucella</italic>-contaminated aerosols (<xref rid="B9" ref-type="bibr">9</xref>, <xref rid="B10" ref-type="bibr">10</xref>). Although <italic>B. abortus</italic> causes less severe infections than <italic>B. melitensis</italic> (<xref rid="B11" ref-type="bibr">11</xref>), back pain and arthralgia are common symptoms in patients infected with <italic>B. abortus</italic> in Turkey (<xref rid="B12" ref-type="bibr">12</xref>).</p>
      <p>A previous study reported that the annual economic burden of bovine brucellosis in Latin America is ~$600 million (<xref rid="B13" ref-type="bibr">13</xref>). In 2015, in Kazakhstan, around $21 million was spent on compensation for slaughtered animals with brucellosis, and an additional $24 million was spent on testing and screening of animals (<xref rid="B14" ref-type="bibr">14</xref>). Therefore, brucellosis can not only lead to substantial economic losses in the animal industry but also pose an ongoing threat to public health.</p>
      <p>Countries declared brucellosis-free are located in Europe and Oceania, whereas the prevalence is high in enzootic countries in central and South America, Africa, and parts of Asia (<xref rid="B15" ref-type="bibr">15</xref>). In these developing countries, especially in Africa, livestock husbandry development is continuously challenged by brucellosis (<xref rid="B16" ref-type="bibr">16</xref>, <xref rid="B17" ref-type="bibr">17</xref>). Bovine brucellosis remains the most widespread disease in animals and the main concern in sub-Saharan countries (<xref rid="B18" ref-type="bibr">18</xref>). In African countries, <italic>B. abortus</italic> has been reported in Sudan, in both cattle and their handlers (<xref rid="B19" ref-type="bibr">19</xref>), as well as in Gambia, Mali, Niger, and more frequently in Nigeria, Senegal, and Ivory Coast (<xref rid="B20" ref-type="bibr">20</xref>). Therefore, a comprehensive analysis of the genetic diversity of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains and the epidemiology of the disease in animals and humans in sub-Saharan countries is necessary.</p>
      <p>Molecular characterization of the predominated circulating strains is critical to understanding <italic>B. abortus</italic> diversity and epidemiology in the country. Multi-locus sequence typing (MLST) is a reliable tool for the characterization of <italic>Brucella spp</italic>. populations and the determination of phylogenetic relationships (<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>, <xref rid="B22" ref-type="bibr">22</xref>); however, this method yields less detailed typing results than MLVA and WGS-SNP due to the lower-resolution power (<xref rid="B21" ref-type="bibr">21</xref>, <xref rid="B23" ref-type="bibr">23</xref>). Multiple locus variable-number tandem repeat analysis (MLVA) enables <italic>Brucella</italic> genotyping to infer genetic diversity and investigate the geographic clustering of the isolates (<xref rid="B24" ref-type="bibr">24</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>). Moreover, whole genome sequencing single-nucleotide polymorphism (WGS-SNP) has higher discriminatory power to efficiently track the origin and spread of <italic>Brucella</italic> strains, leading to be targeted and effective control of disease spread (<xref rid="B27" ref-type="bibr">27</xref>, <xref rid="B28" ref-type="bibr">28</xref>). At present, MLVA has been used to explore the diversity of <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 isolated in West Africa (<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>) and investigate the epidemiological links of the <italic>B. melitensis</italic> in Egypt (<xref rid="B30" ref-type="bibr">30</xref>). Moreover, studies that used MLVA and WGS-SNP (<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>, <xref rid="B32" ref-type="bibr">32</xref>) indicated that WGS-SNP analysis allows a better differentiation than MLVA-16. WGS-SNP analysis has been used to type, discriminate and track outbreak strains (<xref rid="B31" ref-type="bibr">31</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>). Therefore, the purpose of this study was to use a series of molecular typing assays, including MLST, MLVA, and WGS-SNP, to investigate the species population, genetic diversity, geographical origin, and molecular epidemiology of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from West Africa (WA), to provide insight into the comprehensive understanding of the <italic>B. abortus</italic> brucellosis epidemiology features in WA, and to facilitate surveillance and control strategy development.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="methods" id="s2">
      <title>Methods</title>
      <sec>
        <title>Source of MLST and MLVA genotyping data</title>
        <p>The MLST genotyping data of 129 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>; <xref rid="SM3" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>) were downloaded from the PubMLST (<ext-link xlink:href="https://pubmlst.org/organisms/brucella-spp" ext-link-type="uri">https://pubmlst.org/organisms/brucella-spp</ext-link>), including the key (code) of isolates, sequence type (ST), allotype of nine loci (aroA/cobQ/dnaK/gap/glk/gyrB/int_hyp/omp25/typE), <italic>Brucella</italic> species/biovars, host, country, continent, and years isolated. Moreover, the MLVA-16 genotyping data of 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>; <xref rid="SM4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>) from 17 WA countries, including Senegal, Egypt, Togo, Nigeria, Rwanda, Kenya, Guinea-Bissau, Uganda, Niger, Zimbabwe, Cameroon, Sudan, Tanzania, Guinea, Ivory Coast, Chad, and Gambia, were abstracted from the MLVA bank database (<ext-link xlink:href="https://microbesgenotyping.i2bc.paris-saclay.fr/databases" ext-link-type="uri">https://microbesgenotyping.i2bc.paris-saclay.fr/databases</ext-link>). The key (code) of strains, number of tandem repeats at each of the MLVA-16 loci, MLVA-8 genotype, MLVA-11 genotype, hosts, species/biovars, isolated regions, years isolated, and lineage groups were obtained. The MLVA-8 genotype, MLVA-11 genotype, and lineage group data from 19 <italic>B. abortus</italic> MLVA-8 and 21 MLVA-11 genotypes were not previously identified genotypes in the international MLVA bank database.</p>

      <sec>
        <title>Analysis and visualization of MLST and MLVA data</title>
        <p>All data in this study were cleaned and processed using Excel 2016 software (Microsoft Corporation, Redmond, WA, USA). For MLST analysis, genetic similarities between the STs of 129 strains from WA and 631 strains from other continents (PUBMLST) (<xref rid="SM3" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S1</xref>) were investigated using eBURST software 2.0. Finally, the genetic relationships between the isolates and allelic profiles of MLST data were analyzed with the software PHYLOViZ version 2.0 using the goeBURST Full Minimum Spanning Tree algorithms (<xref rid="B34" ref-type="bibr">34</xref>). Subsequently, MLVA data were processed according to genotype information, species/biovars, hosts, location, and years isolated for all <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains. A minimum spanning tree (MST) of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains was constructed using BioNumerics 8.0 software based on the 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains' MLVA-11 data (<xref rid="SM4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>) to investigate the host lineage, genotype distribution, and the geographical origin of the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains. At the global level, an MST was constructed using the MLVA-11 data of 1,746 strains (<xref rid="SM4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>) to explore the geographical origin feature of genotypes from this study at the global level. Moreover, the MST based on the MLVA-16 data of 1,746 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains (<xref rid="SM4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>) was used to determinate the relationship among strains at a global scale. In this study, the MST trees were weighted according to MLVA panels, as described by previous reports (<xref rid="B35" ref-type="bibr">35</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec>
        <title>Phylogenetic analysis based on the SNPs of 80 strains at the global level</title>
        <p>WGS-SNP analysis was performed as previously described (<xref rid="B36" ref-type="bibr">36</xref>). Briefly, genomic alignment between the sample genome and reference genome (<italic>B. abortus</italic> 2308; Assembly ID: GCA_000054005.1) was performed using the MUMmer (<xref rid="B37" ref-type="bibr">37</xref>) and LASTZ (<xref rid="B38" ref-type="bibr">38</xref>) tools. The phylogenetic tree of 18 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from WA and 62 strains from outside WA (<xref rid="SM5" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S3</xref>) in GenBank was constructed using TreeBeST (<xref rid="B39" ref-type="bibr">39</xref>) based on the Maximum-Likelihood Phylogenies (PHYML) algorithm with 1,000 bootstrap replicates to investigate the genetic relationships of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains.</p>

        <title>Species/biovars, host, and geographic distribution of 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains</title>
        <p>Of the identified biovars of 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> African strains, 239 <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 3, 9 <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 1 and 6 <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 6 were identified with 55 not typed to biovar level (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>; <xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). The <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv. 1 was distributed in Egypt (<italic>n</italic> = 6), Zimbabwe (<italic>n</italic> = 2), and Senegal (<italic>n</italic> = 1). <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv. 6 was found in Chad (<italic>n</italic> = 2), and the location of the remaining six is unknown. However, the <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv. 3 strains were distributed in 15 countries, i.e., all 17 countries except Egypt and Zimbabwe (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>; <xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). Based on the host, 280 strains were found in cattle, 8 in humans, 4 in ovine, 4 in buffalo, 3 in the dromedary, 2 in horse, 2 in sheep, 2 in zebu, 1 in a dog, and 1 in a cat, and the remaining two were vaccine strains (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). A total of 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> were collected from 17 countries, including 138 in Senegal, 56 in Egypt, 31 in Togo, 19 in Nigeria, 14 in Rwanda, and remaining countries contained 1&#x2013;10 different numbers strains (<xref rid="F1" ref-type="fig">Figure 1</xref>; <xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>). According to the MLVA data, strains were divided into the abortus B group (<italic>n</italic> = 209), the abortus C group (<italic>n</italic> = 55), and the abortus C_RB51_2308 group from Egypt (<italic>n</italic> = 11), and the remaining 24 strains were unknown. These strains were isolated between 1958 and 2020, including 5 in the 1950s, 147 in the 1970s, 43 in the 1980s, 7 in the 1990s, 13 in the 2000s, 46 in the 2010s, and 2 in 2020, and the year of isolation of the remaining isolates is unknown (<xref rid="T2" ref-type="table">Table 2</xref>; <xref rid="SM4" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Table S2</xref>).</p>

        <title>MLST genotyping characteristic of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains in the present study</title>
        <p>The results of eBURST analysis showed that the 129 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from the present study could be sorted into 14 STs, including ST1 (<italic>n</italic> = 16), ST2 (<italic>n</italic> = 8), ST5 (<italic>n</italic> = 3), ST6 (<italic>n</italic> = 9), ST32 (<italic>n</italic> = 8), ST33 (<italic>n</italic> = 4), ST34 (<italic>n</italic> = 31), ST36 (<italic>n</italic> = 8), ST37 (<italic>n</italic> = 5), ST38 (<italic>n</italic> = 3), ST72 (<italic>n</italic> = 31), ST82 (<italic>n</italic> = 1), ST83 (<italic>n</italic> = 1), and ST84 (<italic>n</italic> = 1) (<xref rid="T1" ref-type="table">Table 1</xref>). Of these, ST34 was the predicted founder and included two single locus variants, zero double locus variants, one triple locus variant, and 10 more distantly related (satellites) (<xref rid="SM1" ref-type="supplementary-material">Supplementary Figure S1</xref>). The ST34 is comprised of strains from three countries, including Sudan (<italic>n</italic> = 24), Nigeria (<italic>n</italic> = 4), and Chad (<italic>n</italic> = 3). The MST at the global scale indicated that among those strains (<italic>n</italic> = 760) from six continents, 14 STs in the present study were divided into three clone complexes (C I&#x2013;C III) (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>), of which C I and C II comprised strains from the present study and formed the independent complex. C I comprised eight STs (ST6, 32, 33, 34, 36, 82, 83, and 84; and strains from Cameroon, Chad, Kenya, Niger, Nigeria, Rwanda, Senegal, Sudan, Tanzania, Togo, Uganda and Zimbabwe) and C II contained three STs (ST37, 38, and 72; and strains from Ethiopia, Kenya, and Mozambique) (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). C III harbored three single STs (ST 1, 2 and 5; and strains from Chad, Egypt, Mozambique, Sudan, Uganda, Zambia, and Zimbabwe) that were shared between three and five continents: ST1 was shared by strains from Asia, Europe, North America, Oceania, and South America (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>); ST2 was shared by strains from Asia, Europe, and South America; and ST5 was shared by strains from four continents, including Asia, Europe, North America, and South America (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>).</p>

    <sec sec-type="discussion" id="s4">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p>In this study, three molecular methods, including MLST, MLVA, and WGS-SNP, were applied to analyze the genetic diversity, population structure, and molecular relationship among 309 <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from WA with global strains. Our analysis highlights that <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains have a high species/biovars, host diversity, as well as wide geographic distribution in WA. In the present study, more than 90% (280/309) of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains were isolated from cattle, and the remaining strains were obtained from 10 other hosts, including wildlife. <italic>B. abortus</italic> predominantly infect cattle but also infect other host species and seems to be a spill-over from the dominant host species to other species due to farming and grazing (<xref rid="B40" ref-type="bibr">40</xref>), which is also evident from in the present study. A previous study showed that 273 <italic>Brucella</italic> strains from Africa were identified and typed since 1976, among which 272 strains were from cattle and ~95% (260/270) were from native animals showing hygromas (<xref rid="B41" ref-type="bibr">41</xref>). Similar when focusing on WA, <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains were historically prevalent mainly in cattle (<xref rid="B42" ref-type="bibr">42</xref>&#x2013;<xref rid="B44" ref-type="bibr">44</xref>). Bio-typing identified three biovars in WA, and <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv.3 was the predominant biovar (<xref rid="B45" ref-type="bibr">45</xref>).</p>
      <p>MLVA11 identified <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 3 isolated from cattle as new major clusters from WA. In contrast to WA where <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 3 strains occur in cattle, <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 is the dominate biovar in South Africa (<xref rid="B46" ref-type="bibr">46</xref>). These data suggest that the predominated <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovars are unique in WA compared to southern Africa, but further investigation is needed to better understand the epidemiological trait of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in Africa.</p>
      <p>Cattle production plays a crucial role in Africa including WA, as cattle are traded for status and serve as a &#x201C;savings account&#x201D; in nomadic systems in Nigeria (<xref rid="B47" ref-type="bibr">47</xref>). It has been reported that 20% of cattle are imported in Nigeria, mostly from Chad and Niger (<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>). Moreover, according to literature, brucellosis is one of the major transboundary animal diseases in North African countries, while the illegal animal movement was identified as one of the major constraining factors (<xref rid="B49" ref-type="bibr">49</xref>).</p>
      <p>In cattle, <italic>B. abortus</italic> mainly invades the reproductive organs and causes symptoms such as abortion and a decline in milk productivity, resulting in substantial economic losses (<xref rid="B50" ref-type="bibr">50</xref>). Therefore, strengthening disease surveillance in cattle is necessary to map the epidemiology of <italic>B. abortus</italic>. However, strains were found in 10 wildlife species in WA, indicating that the <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains circulate among animal species that are not the preferential hosts. These reservoirs pose a crucial risk to domestic animals and humans. A previous study showed that exploring the wildlife reservoir of brucellosis may represent a new challenge to be faced by the medical and veterinarian community in the twenty-first century (<xref rid="B51" ref-type="bibr">51</xref>). Brucellosis in wildlife in this region cannot be neglected, detection and surveillance should be implemented to block the transmission chain.</p>
      <p>Although MLVA has a higher resolution power for <italic>Brucella</italic> strains than MLST, both the MLST and MLVA are crucial to epidemiologic surveillance and investigation into the geographical distribution of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains. MLST analysis highlighted that the predominant circulating <italic>B. abortus</italic> population of WA was native lineages, while three STs were shared by many continents (<xref rid="F2" ref-type="fig">Figure 2</xref>). However, these shared populations were predominant in Europe (ST1 and 2) and America (ST1 and 5), implying that the introduced <italic>B. abortus</italic> lineage was from outside WA, but a further survey into the trade activities of cattle is needed.</p>
      <p>The MLVA-11 highlights the high genetic diversity and multiple geographic original of strains from the present study as reflected by the 37 MLVA-11 genotypes were identified among 309 strains that include 22 known and 15 new genotypes. Two predominant MLVA-11 genotypes (317 and 64) were observed in WA (<xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>, <xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>), while genotype 317 was distributed in Kenya, Senegal, Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, and Rwanda (<xref rid="B52" ref-type="bibr">52</xref>, <xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>), and 64 was also distributed in Senegal and Togo (<xref rid="B53" ref-type="bibr">53</xref>). Comparison analysis of global MLVA-11 data suggests that these genotypes originate from WA. Three MLVA-11 genotypes (66, 79, and 82) were shared with strains from Brazil (<xref rid="B54" ref-type="bibr">54</xref>), the USA (<xref rid="B55" ref-type="bibr">55</xref>), Italy, Portugal (<xref rid="B56" ref-type="bibr">56</xref>), France, and South Korea (<xref rid="B26" ref-type="bibr">26</xref>). The WA <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains from the present study existed multiple geographic original that suggest introduction from multiple regions outside WA similar to study by Wareth et al. (<xref rid="B57" ref-type="bibr">57</xref>).</p>
      <p>The global analysis of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains base on MLVA-16 highlights that the native dominant strains in WA are driving the ongoing prevalence of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in this region. This was similar to findings by Liu et al. (<xref rid="B58" ref-type="bibr">58</xref>) suggesting that <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains were transmitted within the national borders of a country despite common geographical origins of the strain in countries along the silk road. <italic>B. abortus</italic> bv 3 strains from Algeria grouped in two separate clusters using MLVA11 which most strains clustering with European isolates from France and Spain while a few strains cluster in African lineage (<xref rid="B59" ref-type="bibr">59</xref>). These studies thus indicate presence of introduced strains as well as native strains in WA. Therefore, further extensive bacteriological and molecular investigations are necessary for a comprehensive understanding of the epidemiology of cattle brucellosis in African countries.</p>
      <p>WGS-SNP analysis identified <italic>B. abortus</italic> lineages circulating in WA was introduced through the import of animals and/or animal products from multiple countries as 12 of 17 SNP genotypes were from nine countries (regions). The MLST, MLVA, and SNP analyses demonstrated that native strains and introduced lineages are co-driving with the persistence of <italic>B. abortus</italic> circulating in WA. Similarly, WGS-SNP analysis demonstrated that brucellosis in South Africa spreads within the herd on some farms, whereas the introduction of infected animals is the mode of transmission on other farms (<xref rid="B33" ref-type="bibr">33</xref>). WGS phylodynamics analysis identified the main <italic>B. abortus</italic> lineage circulate in Costa Rica are widespread while new introductions seem to be more geographically restricted, which might be similar in various other middle- and low-income countries where brucellosis is endemic with similar farming practices and lack of control (<xref rid="B60" ref-type="bibr">60</xref>).</p>
      <p>Therefore, a control program involving improved surveillance, animal movement restrictions, public health education is suggested. Moreover, there is a strong need for more sustainable molecular data on prevailing <italic>Brucella</italic> strains in WA, and on all susceptible species, including humans, to comprehensively analyze the relatedness between field strains of <italic>Brucella</italic> and the epidemiology of brucellosis within WA countries (<xref rid="B29" ref-type="bibr">29</xref>).</p>
    </sec>
    <sec sec-type="conclusions" id="s5">
      <title>Conclusions</title>
      <p>The present study revealed the constant circulation of <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains in cattle throughout WA and neighboring countries. These strains exhibited high species/biovars, host spectrum, and genetic diversity as well as multiple geographic origins. Moreover, most cases were caused by native strains, and few cases resulted from introduced lineages. The surveillance and control of <italic>B. abortus</italic> in WA should be made a priority which can be enhanced by improving molecular databases that can elucidate epidemiological relationships between strains. Regulations should be strictly implemented when introducing animals to prevent the spread of this species (<xref rid="B48" ref-type="bibr">48</xref>).</p>
