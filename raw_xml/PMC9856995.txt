      <abstract>
        <title>Abstract</title>
        <sec id="vms31048-sec-0010">
          <title>Background</title>
          <p>Brucellosis, caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>., is a major zoonotic public health threat. Although several <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines have been demonstrated for use in animals, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>. can cause human infection and to date, there are no human&#x2010;use vaccines licensed by any agency. Recently, methods in vaccine informatics have made major breakthroughs in peptide&#x2010;based epitopes, opening up a new avenue of vaccine development.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms31048-sec-0020">
          <title>Objectives</title>
          <p>The purpose of this article was to identify potential antigenic peptides in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> by proteome and peptidome analyses.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms31048-sec-0030">
          <title>Methods</title>
          <p>Mouse infection models were first established by injection with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and spleen protein profiles were then analysed. Subsequently, the major histocompatibility complex class I or II (major histocompatibility complex [MHC]&#x2010;I/II)&#x2010;binding peptides in blood samples were collected by immunoprecipitation and peptides derived from <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins were identified through liquid chromatography&#x2013;mass spectrometry (LC&#x2010;MS/MS). These peptides were then evaluated in a variety of ways, such as in terms of conservation in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and synchronicity in predicted peptides (similarity and coverage), which allowed us to more effectively measure their antigenic potential.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms31048-sec-0040">
          <title>Results</title>
          <p>The expression of the inflammatory cytokines IL1B and IFN&#x2010;&#x3B3; was significantly altered in the spleen of infected mice and some <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins, such as Muri, AcpP and GroES, were also detected. Meanwhile, in blood, 35 peptides were identified and most showed high conservation, highlighting their potential as antigen epitopes for vaccine development. In particular, we identified four proteins containing both MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010; and MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding peptides including AtpA, AtpD, DnaK and BAbS19_II02030. They were also compared with the predicted peptides to estimate their reliability.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms31048-sec-0050">
          <title>Conclusions</title>
          <p>The peptides we screened could bind to MHC molecules. After being stimulated with antigen T epitopes, Memory T cells can stimulate T cell activation and promote immune responses. Our results indicated that the peptides we identified may be good candidate targets for the design of subunit vaccines and these results pave the way for the study of safer vaccines against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p>
        </sec>
      </abstract>
      <abstract id="vms31048-abs-0001" abstract-type="graphical">
        <p>Based on proteomics and peptidomics, we identified 35 peptides from the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteome, which have the ability to bind MHC molecules and have the potential to be used in vaccine design. In addition, we also found four <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins containing the identified MHC&#x2010;I and MHC&#x2010;II binding peptides, which may be good candidate targets for designing subunit vaccines.

    <sec id="vms31048-sec-0060">
      <label>1</label>
      <title>INTRODUCTION</title>
      <p>
<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is a gram&#x2010;negative facultative intracellular bacterium, that causes a chronic zoonotic infection called brucellosis (Glynn and Lynn, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>). There are 12 recognised species of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, which in humans is rarely fatal but can be severely debilitating or disabling (Rajendhran, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0018" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>). Skin wounds and mucous membranes are the primary targets of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in humans, and such infections can damage the reproductive system and joints, resulting in genitourinary complications and peripheral arthritis. In endemic areas, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> is usually transmitted through the consumption of unpasteurised dairy products and fresh soft cheeses made from unpasteurised milk (Glynn and Lynn, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>). In the grassland, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species can also infect other mammals in addition to their preferred hosts. For example, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> can both infect cows and contaminate their milk, resulting in huge economic losses to grazing lands. Therefore, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> has the potential to cause great harm to human health and seriously hinder the development of grazers.</p>
      <p>Vaccination is an effective method to reduce the incidence of brucellosis in livestock, which can reduce the risk of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infections in humans. At present, several kinds of live attenuated vaccines have been used in livestock. For example, <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 19 (S19) is currently the most effective vaccine used in cattle. Researchers have found that vaccination with S19 provide 70%&#x2013;91% protection against bovine abortion caused by infection with <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (Confer et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0004" ref-type="bibr">1985</xref>; Wyckoff et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0026" ref-type="bibr">2005</xref>) and subcutaneous inoculation of S19 in 3&#x2013;6&#x2010;month&#x2010;old female calves yields high immune efficacy. In addition, some other live attenuated vaccines, such as RB51 and Rev 1, also have been shown to provide protection to animals. Although these live attenuated vaccines have been widely used in animals, the safety of them still needs to be considered due to their pathogenicity in humans. With the development of subunit vaccines, a variety of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> protective antigens, such as BLS, Omp19, DnaK and Bp26, have been used as candidate vaccine targets (Pan et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0016" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>; Yang et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0027" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). At the same time, single component antigens provide an opportunity for the introduction of widely used delivery carriers, which could greatly enhance specific immune responses. To date, many promising delivery systems, such as viral vectors, bacterial vectors, chitosan derivatives, liposomes, polymers and inorganics, have been used to explore <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines (Pan et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0016" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>). Considering the stability of these delivery systems, loading simpler antigen epitopes, which have less interference with the carrier, may be a promising strategy for producing safe and efficient brucellosis vaccines.</p>
      <p>Generally speaking, bacterial vaccines are internalised by phagocytes through different mechanisms in an organism. In the traditional research paradigm, major histocompatibility complex class I major histocompatibility complex (MHC&#x2010;I)&#x2010;binding peptides are mostly derived from intracellular antigens, while MHC class II (MHC&#x2010;II)&#x2010;binding peptides are derived from external antigens. Both antigens are processed and presented by antigen&#x2010;presenting cells (APCs) to trigger subsequent immune processes. MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;antigenic peptides are generated from the proteasome, then translocated into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and loaded onto MHC&#x2010;I molecules (L&#xF3;pez et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0010" ref-type="bibr">2001</xref>). MHC&#x2010;II molecules are synthesised and transferred from the ER to the endomembrane compartment, where these derived peptides bind to their respective antigen&#x2010;binding domains. These MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;peptide complexes exist on the surface of APCs and can be recognised by CD4<sup>+</sup> T cells, which secrete cytokines to further activate humoral immunity. By contrast, MHC&#x2010;I molecules bind to antigen&#x2010;binding domains on the surface of most cells and present antigen fragments to CD8<sup>+</sup> T cells, which may play an important role in the eradication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> by cell lysis of infected macrophages (Oliveira and Splitter, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">1995</xref>). Therefore, the affinity between antigen epitopes and MHC&#x2010;I/II molecules impact the type of immune response and the identification of antigen epitopes is great significance for vaccine design and development.</p>
      <p>Subunit vaccines use certain surface structural components from microorganisms to make a vaccine that does not contain nucleic acids and can induce a patient to produce antibodies. Subunit vaccines have only a few major surface proteins, avoiding the production of antibodies induced by many unrelated antigens, thereby reducing the incidence of related diseases caused by vaccines. Polypeptide vaccines are a tape of subunit vaccine prepared using chemical synthesis technology, mainly based on known or predicted amino acid sequences for an antigen epitope from a pathogen. The greatest advantage of peptide vaccines lies in the aggregation of immune epitopes, which not only improves the immunogenicity of these vaccines, but also reduces their side effects (Gong et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0008" ref-type="bibr">2022</xref>). The prediction and screening of epitopes is therefore the most important step in their production. Epitope screening methods include nuclear magnetic resonance and X&#x2010;ray crystallography for the structural identification of antigen and monoclonal antibody complexes, and enable atomic level interactions to be determined. There are also methods to predict T and B cell epitopes using specific computer algorithms, which can also identify monoclonal antibody epitopes by mass spectrometry (MS), or identify epitopes containing known T cell epitopes or peptides. As peptide&#x2010;based vaccines are completely synthesised, there is no reversion to virulence or inactivation deficiencies, which might be the major drawback in live attenuated vaccines. Furthermore, compared with these subunit vaccines, the design of peptide&#x2010;based vaccines is convenient and fast. Peptide&#x2010;based vaccines have also been developed to treat dengue fever (Chen et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0003" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>), malaria, influenza (L&#xF3;pez et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0010" ref-type="bibr">2001</xref>), multiple sclerosis (Shahsavandi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0021" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>), tuberculosis (Gong et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0008" ref-type="bibr">2022</xref>) and even cancers, such as colorectal cancer, myeloid leukemia and stomach cancer (Bourdette et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0001" ref-type="bibr">2005</xref>; Maslak et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0012" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>; Sundar et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0022" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>). Based on the above research background, the purpose of this study was to identify potential antigenic peptides in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> by proteome and peptidome analyses.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="vms31048-sec-0070">
      <label>2</label>
      <title>METHODS</title>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0080">
        <label>2.1</label>
        <title>Infection of mice</title>
        <p>A total of six female BALB/c mice (6&#x2010; to 8&#x2010;week old) were purchased from Beijing Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). All animal procedures were approved by The Academy of Military Medical Sciences Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (Ethics Approval Code IACUC&#x2010;DWZX&#x2010;2021&#x2010;008) and performed in accordance with commonly accepted procedures.</p>
        <p>The six female BALB/c mice were randomly divided into infected and uninfected groups, three per group. S19 was inoculated into a sterile Tryptic Soy Broth (TSB) liquid medium and cultured at 37&#xB0;C. After 16 h, the concentration of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> was measured with a spectrophotometer. Using TSB medium as a blank control to zero the instrument and measuring the absorbance of four folds diluted bacterial solution. When the OD600 was 2.0, the number of colonies on plates was counted. A total of 100 &#x3BC;l of each bacterial solution was taken and added to 900 &#x3BC;l of normal saline, mixed well, and diluted to make the dilution 10<sup>&#x2212;5</sup>. A total of 100 &#x3BC;l of diluted liquid was taken and spread on Tryptic Soy Agar medium in triplicate. The plates were placed at 37&#xB0;C, and after 3 days, the number of colonies was counted. The calculation formula was as follows: total viable bacteria per ml = average number of colonies from three counts at the same dilution &#xD7; dilution ratio &#xD7; 50. Based on the calculation results, 2OD = 1 * 10<sup>8</sup> CFU/ml. A total of 20 &#x3BC;l of each bacterial solution was taken and added to 380 &#x3BC;l of normal saline to prepare a premix. The injection dose was 5 &#xD7; 10<sup>5</sup> CFU/100 &#x3BC;l in the infected group, while 100 &#x3BC;l of sterile saline was used in the uninfected group, and both groups were injected intraperitoneally. Six days after infection, blood was collected after mice were anesthetised by subcutaneous injection of sodium barbital at a dose of 200 mg/kg. On the same day, the mice were sacrificed by cervical dislocation, and then the spleens were collected for further analysis.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0090">
        <label>2.2</label>
        <title>Isolation of mouse spleen cells</title>
        <p>All spleens were individually dry&#x2010;pulverised using an automated cryogenic grinding system (Shanghai Jingxin Industrial Development Co., Ltd.). All tissues were homogenised in lysis buffer (4% SDS) supplemented with a protease inhibitor cocktail (A32953, Thermo Scientific). Spleen homogenates were subsequently incubated at 4&#xB0;C for 30 min and then centrifuged at 16,000 <italic toggle="yes">g</italic> and 4<sup>o</sup>C for 20 min. The supernatant was transferred to a new tube, and the protein concentration was then determined using a BCA protein assay kit (23225, Pierce).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0100">
        <label>2.3</label>
        <title>Liquid chromatography tandem&#x2010;mass spectrometry (LC&#x2010;&#x2010;MS/MS)&#x2010;based spleen proteome</title>
        <p>Peptides were resolved by capillary chromatography using an Easy nano&#x2010;LC&#x2010;1200 nano&#x2010;capillary UHPLC, coupled with an electrospray interface, on a Q Exactive HF&#x2010;X mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The peptides were eluted with a linear, 120&#x2010;min, 6%&#x2013;38 % acetonitrile gradient in 0.1% formic acid, at a flow rate of 0.25 &#x3BC;l/min. Data&#x2010;dependent acquisition (DDA) was performed using the following parameters: MS1 resolution was set to 60,000 with the automatic gain control (AGC) target set to 750%. Dynamic exclusion was set to 30 s. For precursor fragmentation in high energy collision dissociation (HCD), the collision energy was set to 27%. For MS2, the resolution was set to 15,000 with an AGC target of 200% and a maximum inject time of 50 ms. Label&#x2010;free quantification was also performed using Peaks Studio based on results with a 1% false discovery rate (FDR). The relative abundances of peptide features (precursor peak area) were detected using multiple samples. Feature detection was performed separately on each sample, and then the features of the same peptide from different samples were reliably aligned together using a high&#x2010;performance retention time alignment algorithm. Normalisation was performed using the total ion current of each sample, and the normalised abundance was calculated from the raw abundance divided by the normalisation factor.</p>
        <p>Peaks Studio was used to process all data. The settings for the database searching were Orbitrap for identified instrument, HCD fragmentation and no digestion enzyme. Precursor and fragment mass tolerances of 10 ppm and 0.02&#x2009;Da, respectively, were used. Peptide identification was performed against a combination of mouse and S19 Uniprot database (updated on December 2019, URL of mouse Uniprot database: <ext-link xlink:href="https://rest.uniprot.org/proteomes/stream?compressed" ext-link-type="uri">https://rest.uniprot.org/proteomes/stream?compressed</ext-link>= true&amp;fields = upid%2Corganism%2Corganism_id%2Cprotein_count%2Cbusco%2Ccpd&amp;format = tsv&amp;query = upid%3AUP000000589; URL of S19 Uniprot database: <ext-link xlink:href="https://rest.uniprot.org/proteomes/stream?compressed" ext-link-type="uri">https://rest.uniprot.org/proteomes/stream?compressed</ext-link>= true&amp;fields = upid%2Corganism%2Corganism_id%2Cprotein_count%2Cbusco%2Ccpd&amp;format = tsv&amp;query = upid%3AUP000002565). Analysis was carried out with oxidation [+15.99] and deamidation [+0.98] set as variable peptide modifications, with a maximum of three modifications per peptide. The peptide&#x2010;spectrum match (PSM) level threshold was set to 1%.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0110">
        <label>2.4</label>
        <title>Peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) isolation</title>
        <p>Whole blood was collected into EDTA tubes (366643, BD Vacutainer) 6 days after infection. PBMCs were isolated using Ficoll&#x2013;Paque gradient centrifugation according to the manufacturer's instruction. Briefly, 3 ml of Ficoll&#x2013;Paque plus (17144003, Cytiva) was preloaded into a 10 ml LeucoSep tube (163288, Greiner Bio&#x2010;One) by centrifugation for 1 min at 1000 <italic toggle="yes">g</italic>. The whole blood was then diluted 1:1 with phosphate&#x2010;buffered saline (PBS) and added to a Ficoll&#x2013;Paque preloaded LeucoSep tube. The tubes were then centrifuged for 15 min at 1000 <italic toggle="yes">g</italic> at room temperature. The PBMC layer was collected, and washed twice with PBS.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0120">
        <label>2.5</label>
        <title>MHC&#x2010;bound peptides purification</title>
        <p>MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010; and &#x2010;II&#x2010;bound peptide libraries were generated based on the isolation of MHC complexes and subsequent dissociation of bound peptides using an immunoprecipitation protocol as previously described (Rijensky et&#xA0;al., 2020). Briefly, cells were lysed and homogenised by incubation with rotation for 45 min at 4&#xB0;C. Immunoprecipitation was performed using a crosslink IP kit (26147, Pierce) according to the manufacturers&#x2019; recommendations. A primary anti&#x2010;MHC&#x2010;I antibody (MA5&#x2010;18004, Invitrogen) and an anti&#x2010;MHC&#x2010;II antibody (14&#x2010;5321&#x2010;82, eBioscience) were covalently bound to mouse protein A/G sepharose to immunoprecipitate MHC&#x2010;I and MHC&#x2010;II complexes, respectively. The immunoprecipitated peptides were eluted twice with 1% TFA and were subjected to desalting using a SepPak (WAT054955, Waters).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0130">
        <label>2.6</label>
        <title>LC&#x2010;MS/MS analysis of MHC&#x2010;binding peptides</title>
        <p>Peptides were resolved by capillary chromatography using an easy nano LC&#x2010;1200 nano&#x2010;capillary UHPLC, coupled by electrospray interface, to a Oritrap Fusion Lumos MS (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The MHC peptides were eluted with a linear, 60 min, 6%&#x2013;36 % acetonitrile gradient in 0.1 % formic acid, at a flow rate of 0.3 &#x3BC;l/min. DDA was performed using the following parameters: the MS1 resolution was set to120,000 with a normalised AGC target 200%. And the dynamic exclusion was set to 30 s. Mass tolerance of &#xB1;10 ppm was allowed. For precursor fragmentation in HCD, collision energies of 25%, 30%, and 35% were used. For MS2, the resolution was set to 50,000 with a normalised AGC target of 200%, and a maximum injection time of 86 ms.</p>
        <p>Proteins were identified and quantified with Peaks Studio. The parameters were similar to the previous proteome database search as highlighted in the previous section. Briefly, precursor and fragment mass tolerances, as well as peptide modifications, were the same, and the target database was the S19 Uniprot database (updated in December 2019), the PSM and protein level FDR threshold were set to 1%.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0140">
        <label>2.7</label>
        <title>Statistical analysis</title>
        <p>After MS analysis, the count number of proteins was obtained. The R package &#x2018;DESeq2&#x2019; was used for differential expression analysis to determine the expression changes of each protein. | log2FoldChange | &#x2265;1 was used as the threshold, an adjusted <italic toggle="yes">p</italic> value (FDR) &lt; 0.05 was regarded as significant, and then differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were obtained. We then summarised the upregulated and downregulated genes using the R packet &#x201C;Clusterprofiler&#x201D; for GO analysis and KEGG pathway enrichment, respectively, in which the GO analysis included secondary&#x2010;level term and full&#x2010;level term analyses. R (v4.1.2) was used for statistical analyses in this study, and a <italic toggle="yes">p</italic> (adjusted <italic toggle="yes">p</italic>) value &lt; 0.05 was regarded as significant.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0150">
        <label>2.8</label>
        <title>Immunoinformatics analysis</title>
        <p>In this study, the prediction of MHC&#x2010;binding antigenic peptides from bacterial proteins was performed using the Immune Epitope Database (IEDB) (Buus et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0002" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>; Wang et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0023" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>). A half&#x2010;limiting dose (IC50) rank &lt; 2% was regarded as strong binding, and MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;binding antigenic peptides were predicted based on the IEDB&#x2013;ANN method with a 9&#x2010;mer. The prediction of MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding antigenic peptides was performed using the IEDB&#x2010;recommended method with a 12&#x2010;mer, and an adjusted rank &lt;2% was regarded as strong binding. VaxiJen was used for immunogenicity analysis with the &#x2018;bacteria&#x2019; mode as the default setting.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="vms31048-sec-0160">
      <label>3</label>
      <title>RESULTS</title>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0170">
        <label>3.1</label>
        <title>Differential protein expression analysis from the spleens of infected mice</title>
        <p>There has been a great development in epitope&#x2010;based peptide vaccines in recent years. A short antigenic epitope is important for vaccine rational design and delivery. To obtain reliable <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antigen peptides, we infected mice with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, and the proteomic data from spleen and blood samples were then analysed. MHC&#x2010;binding peptides were obtained by immunoprecipitation and identified through MS analysis (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0001" ref-type="fig">1</xref>).</p>
        <fig position="float" fig-type="FIGURE" id="vms31048-fig-0001">
          <label>FIGURE 1</label>
          <caption>
            <p>
<bold>
Schematic diagram of this study</bold> . Mice were injected with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> intraperitoneally and then the spleen cells and PBMCs were isolated 6 days after infection. Proteins from spleen were extracted and assessed by LC&#x2013;MS/MS. MHC&#x2010;binding peptides in PBMCs were extracted by immunocoprecipitation and further detected through proteome analysis.</p>
          </caption>
          <graphic xlink:href="VMS3-9-523-g001" position="anchor" id="jats-graphic-3"/>
        </fig>
        <p>As known, the spleen is the main target of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection. When mice are infected with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, Splenomegaly is one of the most obvious phenotypic changes. At the same time, as the most important immune organ, the spleen is usually used to analyse the degree of infection and the immune response (Jacob and Curtiss, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0009" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>); Wang et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0024" ref-type="bibr">2020</xref>). Therefore, to determine the reliability of the infection model and further comprehensively analyse the immunological changes after infection, we used the spleen of uninfected and infected mice. Since the spleen is the main target of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection and the main organ of the immune response, we first analysed the changes in the spleen proteome of infected mice. These MS proteomics data were deposited in the ProteomeXchange Consortium (<ext-link xlink:href="http://proteomecentral.proteomexchange.org" ext-link-type="uri">http://proteomecentral.proteomexchange.org</ext-link>) via the iProX partner repository (Ma et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0011" ref-type="bibr">2019</xref>) with the dataset identifier PXD032856. The data from the infected group and the control group were obtained by LC&#x2013;MS/MS. A total of 2914 mouse DEGs were identified in a comparison of gene expression levels between the infected group and the control group (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0002" ref-type="fig">2a</xref>). The expression levels of 1628 genes were significantly increased, while 1286 genes were significantly decreased (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0002" ref-type="fig">2b</xref>). Specifically, there were significant changes in the expression of several inflammatory cytokines in infected mice, such as IL1B and IFN&#x2010;&#x3B3;, indicating a serious inflammatory reaction caused by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>. (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0002" ref-type="fig">2c</xref>). In addition, three <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins, MurI, AcpP and GroES, were detected in the infected group, but not in the control group (Figure <xref rid="vms31048-supitem-0001" ref-type="supplementary-material">S1</xref>). The MurI protein is mainly involved in the biosynthesis of peptidoglycans and the regulation of cell shape. AcpP presents substrates to enzymes involved in fatty acid biosynthesis or in polyketide secondary metabolite biosynthesis. GroES, related to bacterial metabolism processes, is a well&#x2010;known <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> immunogen from <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16 M. Furthermore, APCs in the spleen can process and present foreign antigens. Thus, by analysing the level of MHC&#x2010;related proteins, we found that both MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;and MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;mediated immune responses were enhanced (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0002" ref-type="fig">2d</xref>).</p>

      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0180">
        <label>3.2</label>
        <title>GO analysis and KEGG pathway enrichment analysis</title>
        <p>To further analyse the function of these DEGs, GO analysis was performed. Secondary&#x2010;level GO analysis results from upregulated genes including biological process (BP) and molecular function (MF) and a total of nine terms were enriched, such as the &#x201C;detoxification&#x201D; process, which has a close relationship with infection (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0003" ref-type="fig">3a</xref>). The downregulated genes were enriched into seven terms in BP and MF by secondary&#x2010;level GO analysis. However, all of them were routine cellular processes and functions (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0003" ref-type="fig">3b</xref>). In our secondary&#x2010;level GO analysis, we noticed that both upregulated genes and downregulated genes were enriched in BP and MF, but no cellular component terms were found. This phenomenon may indicate that the infection process affects gene expression in these mice, but the cellular structure is relatively stable.</p>

(c, d) KEGG pathway enrichments, including upregulated genes (c) and downregulated genes (d).           </caption>
          <graphic xlink:href="VMS3-9-523-g006" position="anchor" id="jats-graphic-7"/>
        </fig>
        <p>By performing KEGG pathway enrichment analysis on upregulated genes, these genes were found to be enriched in &#x2018;biosynthetic of antibiotics&#x2019;, &#x2018;bacterial invasion of epithelial cells&#x2019; and &#x2018;antigen processing and presentation&#x2019; pathways (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0003" ref-type="fig">3c</xref>). The downregulated genes were enriched in the &#x2018;bacterial invasion of epithelial cells&#x2019; pathway and correlated significantly with the &#x2018;B cell receptor signaling pathway&#x2019; (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0003" ref-type="fig">3d</xref>). These pathways are closely related to bacterial infection, indicating that <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection impacted the physiological condition of infected mice.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms31048-sec-0190">
        <label>3.3</label>
        <title>MHC&#x2010;binding peptides</title>
        <p>After immunoprecipitation and elution, samples were analysed by MS to identify MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;binding peptides. The MS proteomics data were also deposited in the ProteomeXchange Consortium (<ext-link xlink:href="http://proteomecentral.proteomexchange.org" ext-link-type="uri">http://proteomecentral.proteomexchange.org</ext-link>) via the iProX partner repository (Ma et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0011" ref-type="bibr">2019</xref>) with the dataset identifier PXD032855. After searching the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> protein sequence database, 19 peptides were identified, which matched 12 proteins in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> S19. The shortest peptide consisted of 8 amino acids and the longest contained 17 amino acids. In addition, multiple peptides derived from different regions of the same protein could be found (Table&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-tbl-0001" ref-type="table">1</xref>). MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding peptides were also identified in the same way. After searching this database, 16 peptides were matched to 11 proteins in the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> S19 (Table&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-tbl-0002" ref-type="table">2</xref>). The shortest peptide consisted of 7 amino acids and the longest contained 21 amino acids. Consistent with the well&#x2010;known different spatial structures between the two classes of MHC molecules (the peptide&#x2010;binding clefts are more open in MHC&#x2010;II molecules compared with MHC&#x2010;I molecules), the length range of MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding peptides identified was wider than for MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;binding peptides. Interestingly, we found four proteins (including AtpA, AtpD, DnaK and BAbS19_II02030) that had both MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010; and MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding peptides, indicating the potential to simultaneously activate humoral and cellular immunity. It has been reported that DnaK can be used as an antigen for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine development and provides good protection against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection (Delpino et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0005" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Minhas et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0013" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>). However, the other three candidate targets had not been reported yet and their immunogenicity needed to be further verified.</p>

        <label>3.4</label>
        <title>Conservation analysis of proteins and peptides identified in this study</title>
        <p>Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) was used to analyse the sequence similarity of these proteins against the RefSeq Select proteins database. We would like to introduce this analysis to explore the possibility of its widespread application. Our previous analysis has yielded certain peptides in several proteins, so we further analysed them at both protein and peptide levels. The conservation among multiple <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains was calculated for each identified protein by BLAST method (each protein in different strains). Then, we further amplified the details of the target peptides to obtain individual conservation of each peptide (each peptide on one protein in different strains). Most of the proteins had high conservation among <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>., with about 90% similarity (Figure <xref rid="vms31048-supitem-0001" ref-type="supplementary-material">S2</xref>). Yet, there were a few proteins, such as BAbS19&#x2010;II02030, that showed relatively lower similarities, which may have narrow&#x2010;range protection between <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. The same scenario was observed in a conservation analysis of peptides identified (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-fig-0004" ref-type="fig">4</xref>). For instance, &#x2018;VVDLLAPYAK&#x2019; (I&#x2010;03) was a highly conserved sequence in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella spp</italic>., while &#x2018;VVARADNHVIL&#x2019; (I&#x2010;09) was less conserved.</p>

        <label>3.6</label>
        <title>Correlation of MHC&#x2010;binding peptides with MHC&#x2010;predictive peptides</title>
        <p>In this study, a sequence correlating MHC&#x2010;predictive peptides at more than three amino acid repeats to an MHC&#x2010;binding peptide was regarded as a relevant peptide. We compared the identified MHC&#x2010;binding peptides with the predictive results obtained above, and found that there were five MHC&#x2010;binding peptides, which could match the relevant predictive peptides, and their corresponding proteins were AtpD, LeuB, MutS, BAbS19_II02030 and BAbS19 II10850 (Table&#xA0;<xref rid="vms31048-tbl-0003" ref-type="table">3</xref>). In other words, they are approved both in silico calculates and biological tests, which effectively reducing the false positives of their immune capacities. In particular, the peptide of &#x2018;FTQAGSEVSALLGR&#x2019; (AtpD) was found in both MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010; and MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;predictive peptides, and its overlap sequence &#x2018;FTQAGSEV&#x2019; showed antigenic potential in immunogenicity analyses. The other four binding peptides from BAbS19_II02030, BAbS19_II10850, LeuB and MutS only have relevant MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010;predictive peptides. In particular, the &#x2018;FTQAGSEVSALLGR&#x2019; peptide (AtpD) was found in the prediction of both MHC&#x2010;I and MHC&#x2010;II, and its repeat sequence &#x2018;FTQAGSEV&#x2019; showed antigen potential in immunogenicity analyses. The other four binding peptides from BAbS19_II02030, BAbS19_II10850, LeuB and MutS only have relevant peptides in only one class of MHC.</p>

    <sec id="vms31048-sec-0230">
      <label>4</label>
      <title>DISCUSSION</title>
      <p>In contrast to experiments using a specific cell type (such as PBMCs) to identify potential bacterial epitopes in vitro, the spleens of infected mice were collected and analysed in this in vivo study. We first compared the spleen proteomic changes between infected and control mice. In the spleen of infected mice, we detected three <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins, MurI, AcpP and GroES, it may be that our samples came from mouse spleens, which are not easy for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> to multiply. They involved in common functions, such as amino acid racemic, fatty acid chain synthesis or molecular chaperone, so they may be high in content and easy to detect. After confirming the infection status and the activation of spleen immune responses, MHC&#x2010;binding peptides were isolated and identified by LC&#x2013;MS/MS. Due to their different conservation, certain screening could be carried out. Through MHC epitope antigen prediction, some relevant peptides also showed MHC affiliative abilities, suggesting that the peptides obtained in this research could be used as candidate antigenic peptides with great potential for the development of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines. We found that the proteins were encoded by five genes, including AtpD, LeuB, MutS, BAbS19_II02030 and BAbS19_II10850. AtpD, has proton&#x2010;transporting ATP synthase activity (<ext-link xlink:href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0046933" ext-link-type="uri">https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0046933</ext-link>), and LeuB has been shown to be involved in the leucine biosynthetic process (Essenberg and Sharma, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0006" ref-type="bibr">1993</xref>; Rajasekaran et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0017" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>). MutS is involved in the repair of mismatches in DNA (Bellacosa, <xref rid="vms31048-bib-1027" ref-type="bibr">2001</xref>), BAbS19_II02030 with transmembrane transporter activity (<ext-link xlink:href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0022857" ext-link-type="uri">https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0022857</ext-link>) and BAbS19_II10850 is related to the regulation of transcription (<ext-link xlink:href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0006355" ext-link-type="uri">https://www.ebi.ac.uk/QuickGO/term/GO:0006355</ext-link>). Among these proteins, the &#x2018;FTQAGSEV&#x2019; sequence has been found both in the binding and predictive peptides of MHC&#x2010;I and MHC&#x2010;II. In general, in this study, we identified 35 MHC&#x2010;binding peptides by experimental methods and provide candidate antigen targets for further vaccine design.</p>
      <p>At present, several studies on <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> epitope vaccines have been published (Chen et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0003" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>; Oliveira et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0014" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>; Sha et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0020" ref-type="bibr">2020</xref>), and in most of which epitopes were predicted using only bioinformatic software. In this study, the MHC&#x2010;binding peptides in infected mice were combined with predicted peptides to obtain more reliable MHC&#x2010;binding antigenic peptides. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> mainly infects livestock, rather than traditional laboratory animals such as mice, so there have been no suitable animal models presented date. MHC molecules vary greatly between species, and polymorphisms in MHC loci have also been observed within species, suggesting that specific epitopes may only cover part of a population. However, studies based on this method can further explore the antigenic peptides of interest in MHC alleles. Previous studies have shown that human leukocyte antigen (HLA) transgenic mice can be used to effectively screen epitope peptides from specific alleles (Weingarten&#x2010;Gabbay et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0025" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>). Thus, in the future, we will carry out similar studies to identify antigenic peptides with binding abilities in human MHC molecules using HLA transgenic mice. Furthermore, these peptides can be also loaded onto nanoparticles to improve the immunogenicity of these peptides to produce high&#x2010;performance nanovaccines (Pan et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms31048-bib-0016" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>).</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="vms31048-sec-0240">
      <label>5</label>
      <title>CONCLUSION</title>
      <p>Although several <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines have been used in animals, they can cause human infection and as yet, there are no licenced human&#x2010;used vaccines. Based on proteomics and peptidomics, we identified 35 peptides from the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteome, which have the ability to bind with MHC molecules and have potential for vaccine design. In addition, we also found four <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> proteins containing both identified MHC&#x2010;I&#x2010; and MHC&#x2010;II&#x2010;binding peptides, which might be good candidate targets for the design of subunit vaccines. Thus, these results pave the way for the study of safer vaccines against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>.</p>
    </sec>
