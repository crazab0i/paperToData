      <abstract>
        <title>Abstract</title>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0010">
          <title>Introduction</title>
          <p>Brucellosis is one of the infectious diseases that has the greatest impact on the productivity of sheep and goats. A cross&#x2010;sectional study followed by a simple random sampling technique was used to investigate the seroprevalence of brucellosis (Rose Bengal plate test; RBPT and complement fixation test; CFT) in small ruminants and its related risk variables from November 2019 to June 2020 in Kolme and Abala Abaya districts. A questionnaire was also given to owners to assess their existing knowledge of the disease.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0020">
          <title>Result</title>
          <p>Using the RBPT and CFT, 28 (4.1%) and 23 (3.33%) of the 690 animals were found to be seropositive for brucellosis, respectively. In this study, the seroprevalence of brucellosis detected in the Kolme district (5.3%) was greater when compared to Abala Abaya (1.0%). The odds of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection were greater for goats (odds ratio [OR] 6.0, 95% confidence interval [CI] 16 0.8&#x2013;44.9) than for sheep. The odds of adult animals (OR 0.05, 95% CI 0.03&#x2013;0.07) being positive for brucellosis was higher than young animals. A statistically significant difference in the seropositivity of brucellosis was detected in univariate logistic regression among districts, different age groups, herd size, parity number, and reproductive health problems except for species and sex, but in multivariate logistic regression, only reproductive health problems were revealed a statistically significant difference. Out of 138 families, 100% of respondents were unaware of brucellosis, 94.5% drink raw milk, and 74% handle animals with retained fetal membranes with their bare hands.</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0030">
          <title>Conclusion</title>
          <p>This study showed that brucellosis was a widely spread disease in the study areas and poses a substantial public health danger. To reduce the spread of the disease in small ruminants, public health risks, and economic losses, stringent vaccination application and awareness of personal hygiene are critical.</p>
        </sec>

    <sec id="vms3992-sec-0040">
      <label>1</label>
      <title>INTRODUCTION</title>
      <p>Goats and sheep are major domestic animals in African tropical livestock production systems, accounting for 21% of the global small ruminant population (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>). Ethiopia is one of East Africa's developing countries, with 31.30 million sheep and 32.74 million goats primarily raised in the country's lowland and pastoral regions (Addis &amp; Desalegn, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0004" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>). Because of their high proliferation rate and great ability to adapt to various environmental conditions, these small ruminants represent an important export commodity that significantly contributes to the livelihood and national economy of rural farmers as a source of food (milk and meat), wool, skins, source of income, and monetary asset, especially in pastoral and lowland areas of Ethiopia (Adams et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0003" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>). Small ruminants are a vast resource; however, production from this valuable asset is not fully realized due to several technical and non&#x2010;technical factors, including nutritional deficiency, husbandry difficulties, water scarcity, inadequate marketing, and losses associated with infectious diseases, primarily brucellosis (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Geletu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0017" ref-type="bibr">2021</xref>; Tewodros &amp; Dawit, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0043" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>).</p>
      <p>Brucellosis is a zoonotic disease (Franc et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>) that is caused by microscopic coccobacilli bacteria of the genus <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. This Gram&#x2010;negative bacterium is slow growing and capable of surviving and replicating within epithelial cells, placental trophoblasts, dendritic cells, and macrophages (Tekle et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0039" ref-type="bibr">2019</xref>).</p>
      <p>In goats and sheep, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> produces the disease; however, in cattle, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> can cause clinical brucellosis, and in rams, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella ovis</italic> causes epididymitis (Kusiluka &amp; Kambarage, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0020" ref-type="bibr">1996</xref>). The disease affects the animal's reproductive system, causing significant production and productivity losses such as decreased milk production, abortion, stillbirth, acute orchitis, weak offspring, weight loss, culling, and condemnation of infected animals due to infertility, lameness, and trade and export restrictions (Makita et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0021" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>).</p>
      <p>In endemic areas, brucellosis is primarily transmitted between animals through direct contact with infected animals or contact with an environment that has been contaminated with infected birthing tissues or fluids, including the placenta, aborted fetuses, or uterine discharge. Humans most frequently acquire brucellosis by consuming infected raw animal products (raw, unpasteurized milk, and soft cheeses) but can also develop the disease following contact with infected animal tissues or secretions (especially unpasteurized milk and soft cheeses) (Gupte &amp; Kaur, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0018" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Radostits et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0033" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>).</p>
      <p>Different species of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and their biovars has distinct distributions. The bacterium <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> can be found all over the world. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> have a sporadic distribution (Acha &amp; Szyfres, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0002" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>). The disease's occurrence in animals is related to a variety of factors. Animals' age, sex, and reproductive status are all host variables that can influence their susceptibility to infection (Nicoletti, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0025" ref-type="bibr">2010</xref>). Farmers, veterinarians, and others in the livestock industry are at high risk of contracting the disease (Sintayehu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0036" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Tewodros &amp; Dawit, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0043" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>).</p>
      <p>The World Health Organization has designated brucellosis as one of the world's main &#x2018;neglected zoonotic diseases&#x2019;, owing to the disease's disproportionate impact on low&#x2010;income countries (Franc et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>). In Ethiopia, the first case of brucellosis was reported almost 50 years ago (Domenech &amp; Lefevre, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0013" ref-type="bibr">1974</xref>). Many studies detailing the prevalence and associated risk factors of small ruminant brucellosis throughout different parts of Ethiopia have been reported (Ashagrie et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0006" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Asmare et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0008" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>; Ferede et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0014" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Melese, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0022" ref-type="bibr">2016</xref>; Sintayehu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0036" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Teshale et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0041" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>; Tewodros &amp; Dawit, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0043" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Yesufa et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0050" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>). Ethiopia is a large country with a huge livestock population, and previously published work has only described seroprevalence for specific areas and not the whole country (Tamrat et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0038" ref-type="bibr">2020</xref>). Furthermore, no published research work on small ruminant brucellosis was found in the current study region. Therefore, this study was initiated to investigate the seroprevalence and the associated risk factors of small ruminant brucellosis in the Kolme and Abala Abaya districts.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="vms3992-sec-0050">
      <label>2</label>
      <title>MATERIALS AND METHODS</title>
      <sec id="vms3992-sec-0060">
        <label>2.1</label>
        <title>Study area</title>
        <p>The study was undertaken in the Southern Nation Nationalities Peoples (SNNP) Region in Kolme and Abala Abaya districts from December 2019 to May 2020. The districts of Kolme and Abala Abaya are about 600 and 345 km south of Addis Ababa, respectively (Figure&#xA0;<xref rid="vms3992-fig-0001" ref-type="fig">1</xref>). Geographically, the Abala Abaya district lies between 37&#xB0; 44' 10" and 37&#xB0; 54' 20" E longitudes and 6&#xB0; 35' 0" to 6&#xB0; 45' 10" N latitudes, whereas the Kolme district lies between 36o 53' 20" and 37o 23' 50" E longitudes and 5o 13' 40" to 5o 23' 50" N latitudes. In the Kolme area, the altitude ranges from 500 to 2500 m above sea level, whereas in the Abala Abaya district, the altitude ranges from 700 to 2275 meters. The annual average rainfall in the area was reported as 570&#xA0;mm at Kolme and 854&#xA0;mm in Abala Abaya, according to the local meteorological station, while the average annual temperature in the Abala Abaya district was 31.59&#xB0;C (21&#x2013;27.5&#xB0;C in sub&#x2010;zone ranges), and the temperature typically varies between 17.22 and 35.55&#xB0;C throughout the year in Kolme, with temperatures rarely falling below 15.55&#xB0;C or rising above 37.77&#xB0;C. Agro&#x2010;pastoralism in Kolme and the lowland mixed agricultural system in Abala Abaya are the main economic activities in the research area. Cattle are the most common animal species in terms of population number, followed by small ruminants. According to each woreda Livestock and Fishier Office report, Kolme district had 140,834 goats and 75,804 sheep, whereas Abala Abaya district had 25,000 sheep and 53,706 goats (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>).</p>
        <fig position="float" fig-type="FIGURE" id="vms3992-fig-0001">
          <label>FIGURE 1</label>
          <caption>

          </caption>

      </sec>
      <sec id="vms3992-sec-0070">
        <label>2.2</label>
        <title>Study populations</title>
        <p>This study focused on indigenous sheep and goats in the Kolme and Abala Abaya districts, which were kept in the extensive production system. The study comprised sheep and goats aged 6 months and above, as well as both sexes in the selected flock that had never been vaccinated against brucellosis. Information regarding putative risk factors including origins of the animal, species, sex category, parity, and reproductive health concerns (abortion, stillbirth, retained fetal membrane) was documented as previously described (Abebe &amp; Yami, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0001" ref-type="bibr">2008</xref>; Shively, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0035" ref-type="bibr">1985</xref>). The age of sheep and goats was determined based on their dental eruption patterns. Animals were classified as young (1&#x2013;3 years old) if they had four or fewer permanent teeth and adult (4&#x2013;5 years old) if they had more than four permanent teeth (Steel, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0037" ref-type="bibr">1996</xref>; Vatta et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0046" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>).</p>
        <p>From December 2019 to May 2020, a cross&#x2010;sectional study was carried out in the Kolme and Abala Abaya districts to investigate the seroprevalence and the associated risk factors of small ruminant brucellosis.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms3992-sec-0080">
        <label>2.3</label>
        <title>Sample size determination</title>
        <p>The sample size was calculated according to the approach suggested by Thrusfield (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0044" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>). The seroprevalence of small ruminant brucellosis was estimated at 5.9% using previously reported results in districts near those studied herein (Melese, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0022" ref-type="bibr">2016</xref>; Wakene et&#xA0;al. <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0047" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>). Six hundred and ninety goats and sheep were sampled in this study, which is twice as large as the sample size calculated to achieve a level of significance of 0.05% and a 95% level of confidence. Thus, 345 goats and sheep were chosen as the overall sample size. However, the sample size was enlarged two times to maximize precision, resulting in 690 goats and sheep being sampled for the seroprevalence investigation (Thrusfield, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0044" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms3992-sec-0090">
        <label>2.4</label>
        <title>Sampling techniques and sample collection</title>
        <p>A multistage sampling technique was carried out to select the zone from the southern region. Among different zones of the region, the zones (Jinka and Wolaita) and districts (Kolme and Abala Abaya districts) were selected purposively based on the population density of sheep and goats. Peasant association: three from Kolme (Masoya, Abaya, and Gete) and three from Abala Abaya (Abala Maraka, Abala Sipa, and Abala Faracho), households, and the herd were selected randomly from both districts. The households that have an average animal number of 10 in Abala Abaya districts and 30 in Kolme districts were included to collect the sample. Accordingly, a total of 690 small ruminants (<italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 546 goats and <italic toggle="yes">n</italic> = 144 sheep) were sampled.</p>
        <p>After handling the animal carefully, about 10&#xA0;ml of blood was obtained aseptically from the jugular vein of each animal using plain vacutainer tubes. Using a permanent marker, all samples were serially identified and correctly labelled. The blood sample was spun at 3000&#xA0;rpm for 4 min before being gently split into 2&#xA0;ml cryovials tubes and delivered to the Sodo Regional Veterinary Laboratory using an icebox. The Rose Bengal plate test (RBPT) was used to screen sera samples. After using the RBPT test, all positive samples were sent to the National Veterinary Institute for confirmation using the complement fixation test (CFT) (OIE, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0027" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>).</p>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0100">
          <label>2.4.1</label>
          <title>Rose Bengal plate test</title>
          <p>The whole serum sample was first examined using the RBPT, which was done by adding 25&#xA0;&#x3BC;l of antigen and 75&#xA0;&#x3BC;l of serum onto a plate. The antigen and test serum were then completely mixed with the plastic applicator, shaken for 4 min, and the degree of agglutination was visually inspected and recorded as positive or negative for the presence or absence of agglutination (OIE, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0026" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>). The degree of agglutination was then recorded as 0, +, ++, +++, with 0 indicating no agglutination, + indicating barely visible agglutination, ++ indicating fine agglutination, and +++ indicating coarse clumping. The samples with no agglutination (0) were considered negative, whereas those with +, ++, and +++ were considered positive and recorded (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Rose &amp; Roepke, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0034" ref-type="bibr">1957</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0110">
          <label>2.4.2</label>
          <title>Complement fixation test</title>
          <p>The CFT was used to confirm the results of those sera that tested positive by the RBPT. Anti&#x2010;<italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> antibodies were detected in the serum using standard <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> antigen for CFT (from the Veterinary Laboratories Agency, Addlestone, United Kingdom). The control sera and complement were obtained from the German Federal Institute for Consumer and Veterinary Medicine in Berlin. The minimal positive threshold was set at a 25% haemolytic response at a dilution of 1:5. Sera with a strong reaction of approximately 100% fixation of the complement (4+) at a dilution of 1:5, sera with about 75% fixation of the complement (3+) at a dilution of 1:10, sera with about 50% fixation of the complement (2+) at a dilution of 1:20, and sera with about 25% fixation at a dilution of 1:40 (+) will be classified as positive (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
        <sec id="vms3992-sec-0120">
          <label>2.4.3</label>
          <title>Questionnaire survey</title>
          <p>A pre&#x2010;tested semi&#x2010;structured questionnaire was given to animal owners to know their knowledge about brucellosis, consuming raw animal products (milk, blood, or meat), handling aborted fetuses and retained placentas by bare hand, reproductive health problems, isolation of diseased animals from the herd, herd size, and history of contact with another herd. As a result, 138 households (90 from Kolme and 48 from Abala Abaya) took part in the research. The overall sample size for a household interview was calculated according to Cochran (Cochran, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0010" ref-type="bibr">1977</xref>).</p>
        </sec>
      </sec>
      <sec id="vms3992-sec-0130">
        <label>2.5</label>
        <title>Data analysis</title>
        <p>A Microsoft Excel spreadsheet was used to store the information gathered from the questionnaire and laboratory testing. Following coding and editing, STATA/IC 13 was used to do the appropriate statistical analysis (2013, College Station). The frequency and proportion of both dependent and independent variables were determined using descriptive statistics. The <italic toggle="yes">&#x3C7;</italic>
<sup>2</sup> test was also employed to see if there was a significant difference between the independent variables (origin of animals, species, herd size, sex, age, parity, and reproductive health problems) (abortion, stillbirth, and RFM) with expected outcomes (brucellosis). Using univariate logistic regression analysis, the different possible risk factors were evaluated for their association with the seroprevalence of brucellosis in small ruminants. To develop the likely model (<italic toggle="yes">p</italic> &lt; 0.05), non&#x2010;collinear variables with <italic toggle="yes">p</italic>&#x2010;value &#x2264;0.25 were all subjected to multivariable logistic regression analysis (Dohoo et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0012" ref-type="bibr">2003</xref>).</p>

    <sec id="vms3992-sec-0200">
      <label>4</label>
      <title>DISCUSSION</title>
      <p>Out of 690 (546 goats and 144 sheep) sera samples tested for small ruminant brucellosis in the Kolme and Abala Abaya districts, 28 (4.1%) tested positive on the RBPT, and 23 (3.33%) tested positive for CFT for brucellosis. The high seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection when using the RBPT was in line with the previous reports of Ashagrie et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0006" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>) who indicated a seroprevalence of 4.2%. Contrary to our findings, a lower seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in Ethiopia ranging from 0.7% to 3.7% has been previously reported (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Asmare et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0008" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>; Ferede et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0014" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Habtamu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0019" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Sintayehu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0036" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Teshale et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0041" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>; Tewodros &amp; Dawit, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0043" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). Furthermore, the prevalence ranges from 1.6% to 14.6% and 1.7% to 16.45% in sheep and goats, respectively, in Afar and Somali regions (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Teshale et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0041" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>).</p>
      <p>The overall seroprevalence of brucellosis in CFT (3.3%) was found to agree with the previous report (Melese, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0022" ref-type="bibr">2016</xref>; Sintayehu et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0036" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Teklue et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0040" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>). In contrast, a higher overall seroprevalence was previously described by Wakene et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0047" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>) in northern parts of Ethiopia. The CFT test is commonly used as a confirmatory test because of its high specificity (Padilla Poester et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0029" ref-type="bibr">2010</xref>). When using this test, the number of positive animals declined from 28 to 23 with a false&#x2010;positive result of 5/28 (17.8%). This is likely due to the CFT reducing the occurrence of false positives due to cross&#x2010;reacting bacteria that can occur with RBPT. Indeed, the RBPT is susceptible to cross&#x2010;reaction with other Gram&#x2010;negative bacteria such as <italic toggle="yes">Yersinia enterocolitica</italic>, O:9, <italic toggle="yes">Escherichia coli</italic>, O:157, and some <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella</italic> spp (Garin&#x2010;Bastuji et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0016" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>; OIE, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0026" ref-type="bibr">2009</xref>).</p>
      <p>The seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in Kolme and Abala Abaya districts showed a statistically significant difference (<italic toggle="yes">p</italic> &lt; 0.05) with the occurrence of brucellosis. Similar findings have been previously reported from different parts of the country (Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Deddefo et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0011" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>; Mengistu, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0023" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Tigist et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0045" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Yibeltal, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0051" ref-type="bibr">2005</xref>). The difference in the seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection could be associated with unrestricted movement of animals for searching pasture and water nearby watering points, especially during the dry season, environmental factors, breed differences, sample size, and the tests used.</p>
      <p>A statistically significant difference in the seropositivity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection was observed between species. The seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection in goats (4.03%) was higher than in sheep (0.7%). Similar to this finding, a higher seroprevalence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection ranging from 15.4% to 1.9% has been previously reported (Adugna et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0005" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>; Ashenafi et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Bekele et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0009" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>; Mengistu, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0023" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>; Teshome et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0042" ref-type="bibr">2018</xref>; Wedajo et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0049" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>) in goats and sheep. On contrary, Deddefo et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>) in Arsi and East Shoa recorded a higher seroprevalence of brucellosis in goats (4.9%) and sheep (4.4%) with no statistically significant difference. This difference might be due to the differences in flock sizes and the number of goats and sheep kept in the herd. Most breeds of goats are fully susceptible, but the susceptibility of sheep breeds differs widely (Radostitis et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0031" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>). The breed&#x2010;specific difference in the case of goats may be due to persistent infection of mammary glands and supramammary lymph nodes because of constant or intermittent shedding of organisms within the milk during succeeding lactations. However, in the case of sheep, the differences may due to the self&#x2010;limiting nature of the disease which is seldom accompanied by prolonged excretion of the bacteria (Radostitis et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0031" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>).</p>
      <p>The seropositivity of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection between sex was not revealed as a statistically significant difference as previously described by Ashenafi et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>). The lack of statistically significant difference between sexes could be due to the smaller sample size of males, and the habit of keeping males in the herd for a shorter period may decrease their exposure to the disease. Yet, Adugna et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0005" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>) have also reported that males are less susceptible to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection due to the absence of erythritol.</p>
      <p>A statistically significant difference that was observed among age groups with the occurrence of brucellosis was found to be in line with Radostits et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0033" ref-type="bibr">2006</xref>) and Quinn et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0030" ref-type="bibr">2004</xref>) who have reported that brucellosis is essentially a disease of sexually mature animals. However, young animals may acquire latent infections even though they are more resistant to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection as they frequently clear an established infection (Walker, <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0048" ref-type="bibr">1999</xref>). This is due to sex hormones and erythritol, which stimulate the growth and multiplication of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> organisms, which tend to increase in concentration with age and sexual maturity (Radostits et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0032" ref-type="bibr">2000</xref>).</p>
      <p>In this study, a statistically significant difference (<italic toggle="yes">p</italic> &lt; 0.05) was observed between herd&#x2010;level prevalence and individual animal&#x2010;level prevalence of brucellosis in the Kolme and Abala Abaya districts. This result was in agreement with the report of Wakene et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0047" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>) and Adugna et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0005" ref-type="bibr">2013</xref>) who appreciated a high seroprevalence of brucellosis at the herd level but contrary to Mohammed et&#x2010;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0024" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>) and Ferede et&#xA0;al. (2011). Although a statistically significant association was observed as parity increased with the seropositivity of brucellosis. This was compatible with Melese (2016), Ashagrie et&#x2010;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0006" ref-type="bibr">2011</xref>), and Deddefo et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>). This difference could be due to the effect of reproductive physiology and hormone on the genetic performance of individual animals and differences in the animal population in their respective niche. Furthermore, ewes having a history of abortion, stillbirth, and retained fetal membranes have revealed a statistically significant difference with the seroprevalence of brucellosis. This finding was in agreement with the report of Wakene et&#xA0;al. (Wakene et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0047" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>) but contrary to Deddefo et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0015" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>) and Adugna et&#xA0;al. (2013). The difference might be due to the effect of the reproductive and sexual hormones on the pathogenesis of the disease in small ruminants (Radostits et&#xA0;al., <xref rid="vms3992-bib-0032" ref-type="bibr">2000</xref>).</p>
      <p>Among small ruminant owners, 62.32% were illiterate. However, 100% of owners were unaware of the disease, and 79.7% of them used their bare hands during parturition. This was in agreement with the report of Wakene et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0047" ref-type="bibr">2017</xref>). This study also revealed that 67.4% of owners are not aware of the transmission of brucellosis through contact with other herds during feeding and watering times, and consumption of raw animal products, so they have not practiced the isolation of healthy animals from diseased ones. Approximately 57.2% of respondents&#x2019; herds had experienced reproductive health problems such as abortion, stillbirth, or retained fetal membrane due to a lack of using any control measure to prevent abortion in small ruminants. Hence, this is the most common means of exposure of the rural community to the disease in endemic areas as reported by Olsen and Palmer (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0028" ref-type="bibr">2014</xref>).</p>
      <p>Brucellosis is a severe health danger for the entire community due to close contact between humans and their animals, which sometimes share the same housing enclosures. In Ethiopia, reproductive issues such as abortion, stillbirth, and retained fetal membranes are the most common causes of reproductive wastage in small ruminants, and humans may become infected while handling infected material. It has yet to be determined if brucellosis or malaria is the source of human illness with symptoms such as fever, aches and pains, weariness, and debility. Consequently, there is a high risk of human brucellosis in the study area. This outcome is in agreement with the study conducted by Ashenafi et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0007" ref-type="bibr">2007</xref>) and Habtamu et&#xA0;al. (<xref rid="vms3992-bib-0019" ref-type="bibr">2015</xref>).</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="vms3992-sec-0210">
      <label>5</label>
      <title>CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS</title>
      <p>This study revealed a high seroprevalence of brucellosis in goats as compared to sheep in Kolme and Abaya Abala districts. All potential risk factors, including district, herd size, parity, age, and a history of reproductive health problems (abortion, stillbirth, and retained fetal membrane), have shown a statistically significant difference with the occurrence of small ruminant brucellosis. In the current study area, smallholder farmers were found to be at high risk of acquiring <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection due to a tradition of ingesting raw milk, traditional animal husbandry practices, handling animals with reproductive difficulties with their bare hands, and lack awareness on the zoonotic nature of the disease. As a result, improved management systems and the implementation of appropriate control measures in the smallholder production system are recommended in the study areas. Further epidemiological research, as well as the identification and isolation of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> biotype responsible for the infection, should be carried out.</p>
