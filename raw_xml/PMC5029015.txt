In the late fifties, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> vaccine strain S2 with reduced virulence was obtained by serial transfer of a virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovar 1 strain in China. It has been widely used for vaccination in China since 1971. Until now, the mechanisms underlie virulence attenuation of S2 are still unknown.</p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p>In this paper, the whole genome sequencing of S2 was carried out by Illumina Hiseq2000 sequencing method. We further performed the comparative genomic analysis to find out the differences between S2 and the virulent <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> strain 1330. We found premature stops in outer membrane autotransporter <italic toggle="yes">omaA</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> genes. Single mutations were found in phosphatidylcholine synthase, phosphorglucosamine mutase, pyruvate kinase and FliF, which have been reported to be related to the virulence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> or other bacteria. Of the other different proteins between S2 and 1330, such as Omp2b, periplasmic sugar-binding protein, and oligopeptide ABC transporter, no definitive implications related to bacterial virulence were found, which await further investigation.</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusions</title><p>The data presented here provided the rational basis for designing <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccines The Author(s) 2016</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec id="Sec1"><title>Background</title><p>Brucellosis is a worldwide zoonotic disease caused by the infection of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species, a Gram-negative and facultative intracellular bacterium. Brucellosis can be acquired by direct contact with infected animals including cattle, sheep, goats and swine. <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infects approximately 500,000 humans worldwide annually according to the World Health Organization (WHO) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">1</xref>]. The infection can cause reproductive failure in food animals and even the loss of human productivity. The clinical symptoms of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> infection include undulant fever, abortion, asthenia, endocarditis, and encephalitis. Brucellosis in food animals could be controlled by vaccination, while human brucellosis can be treated with antibiotics.</p><p>Currently, there are ten recognized <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species based on their host preference and phenotypic differences: <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> (goats), <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> (cattle), <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> (swine), <italic toggle="yes">B. canis</italic> (dogs), <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> (sheep), <italic toggle="yes">B. neotomae</italic> (desert mice), <italic toggle="yes">B. ceti</italic> (cetaceans), <italic toggle="yes">B. pinnipedialis</italic> (pinnipeds), <italic toggle="yes">B. microti</italic> (common voles) and <italic toggle="yes">B. inopinata</italic> (unknown) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2</xref>]. The genome of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strain 16M is the first one to be sequenced [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">3</xref>], followed by <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strain 1330 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">4</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">5</xref>] and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 2308 and 9&#8211;941 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">6</xref>]. New <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> genome sequences are increasing, and at least 39 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains have been sequenced. These genomic resources can be served for identifying <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> gene variability that associated with their host preference, pathogenesis, and virulence.</p><p><italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strain 1330 (hereafter referred to as 1330) is a swine isolate and used as&#160;the standard reference strain for <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovar 1 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR7">7</xref>]. According to phylogenetic analysis of 10 <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> has a broader host specificity without any identified species-specific markers [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">8</xref>].</p><p>The <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strain S2 is isolated from swine fetus by the Institute of Inspection for Veterinary Medicine in 1952 in China. It possesses all of the characteristics of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> biovar 1 and a smooth colonial morphology [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>]. After serial transfer on media for years, <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strain S2 is naturally attenuated to a vaccine strain designated as <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> vaccine &#8220;strain 2&#8221; or S2 (hereafter referred to as S2). The avirulent property of S2 is stable when inoculated into susceptible animals, and it does not cause the abortion of pregnant female [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>]. Therefore, it has been widely used in China since 1971 to vaccinate sheep and goats [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>]. The vaccine is administered through drinking water, a dose of 10 billion bacteria provides the protection for 2&#8211;3 years. In the middle of 1980s, S2 is introduced to the other countries [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">10</xref>]. Although studies suggest that the protection rate of S2 is less than that of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> &#8220;strain 19&#8221; or S19 (hereafter referred to as S19) and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Rev. 1 (hereafter referred to as Rev. 1) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">12</xref>], a large-scale animal test shows that S2 can provide a satisfactory protection rate in the field [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">13</xref>]. Based on these encouraging results, the low virulence compared with S19 and Rev.1, the low cost of production, the safety for pregnant sheep and the feasibility of administration by the oral route in cattle, pigs, sheep and goats, S2 is used successfully in China to vaccinate all target species against brucellosis by administration through drinking water [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">9</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">12</xref>]. In mice, S2 has low residual virulence and less persistence time compared with S19 and Rev.1. It provides short-time protection against virulence <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> challenge compared with the other two vaccines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">14</xref>]. However, it does not show significant protection against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 53H38 challenge when tested in lambs [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">11</xref>].</p><p>The underlying molecular and physiological mechanisms of the low virulence of S2 are not well understood so far. In this study, we performed the paired-end sequencing combined with Sanger sequencing method to determine the complete genome sequences of S2. We further identified the different genes between S2 and 1330 by genome comparison. Several putative factors were identified as the candidate genes that might be related to the virulence attenuation of S2. Further investigation of these target genes will be important for designing better vaccines to provide efficient control for brucellosis.</p></sec><sec id="Sec2" sec-type="discussion"><title>Results and discussion</title><sec id="Sec3"><title>Genome sequence properties</title><p>The S2 genome was comprised of two circular chromosomes (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab1" ref-type="table">1</xref>). Unsurprisingly, The S2 genomes exhibited significant similarities in size and structure compared to the genomic sequences of 1330. The S2 genome sequences showed over 99.9&#160;% similarities with the genomes of 1330. The diagram of S2 genomes is shown in Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig1" ref-type="fig">1</xref>. Six ORFs had 2, 6, 8, 16, 69 and 78&#160;bp insertion or deletion in the genes respectively (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab3" ref-type="table">3</xref>). The insertion or deletion in the gene caused either frameshifting (including premature stop) or residue insertion in the coding protein. The different ORFs in S2 that encoded the same protein sequences in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains were labeled &#8216;found&#8217; and omitted in discussions. The different ORFs labeled with &#8216;surface&#8217; suggested that the different residue was located on the surface of the modeled structure, which were also omitted from the analysis (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab3" ref-type="table">3</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="Sec5"><title>Known virulence associated differences</title><p>In chromosome I of S2, a 2&#160;bp deletion caused the premature stop of an outer membrane autotransporter OmaA. The autotransporters are secreted proteins that can translocate themselves through the membranes to the cell surface or to the extracellular environment. Bandara et al. studied the function of this <italic toggle="yes">OmaA</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">16</xref>]. In mice, the <italic toggle="yes">OmaA</italic>-deficient strain provides greater protection against the challenge of 1330 than that of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> mutant strain VTRS1. The deficient strain can induce a significantly higher level of serum IgG1 and IgG2a antibodies and can be cleared quickly from the mice. Although the OmaA activity is not necessary in the course of the acute phase of infection of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic>, it might still be a critical virulent factor during the chronic phase of the infection. Therefore, we suspected the lack of intact OmaA protein might be the major cause for S2 virulence attenuation.</p><p>The second difference between 1330 and S2 was found in the erythritol (<italic toggle="yes">ery</italic>) operon. The erythritol operon contains 4 ORFs (<italic toggle="yes">eryA, eryB, eryC and eryD</italic>) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>]. Erythritol metabolism by <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> has been known to be associated with the abortion in livestock. The high concentration of erythritol in the foetal tissue of the animal (cattle, sheep, goat and pig) might be advantageous for the growth of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. On the other hand, erythritol catabolism pathway might play the important physiological roles in bacteria growth. It has been suggested that erythrose 4-phosphate, the precursor for the biosynthesis of aromatic compounds, may be easily obtained from the intermediates of erythritol catabolism. Compared to 1330, a 16&#160;bp insertion caused the premature stop of the <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> gene in S2<italic toggle="yes">.</italic> The studies on <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> S19 and S2308 showed that <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> might act as a repressor of erythritol operon [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>]. The consequence of <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> ORFs deletion in S19 has been studied, which reveals that they are not sufficient or required for virulence in a murine model. No direct correlation between erythritol metabolism and in vivo colonization was found when S19 and 2308 strains were compared with three genetically engineered strains related to <italic toggle="yes">ery</italic> operon [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">18</xref>]. Considering its repressive control on <italic toggle="yes">ery</italic> operon, the lack of <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> might cause the uncontrolled erythritol catabolism. Nevertheless, its consequence on virulence attenuation needs to be further explored.</p><p>It has been reported that S19 does not grow on TSA (Ery). To test the growth capacity of S2 on erythritol, we chose S19 as the control. We found that S2 was able to grow on erythritol agar, suggesting it has the ability to metabolize erythritol (Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab4" ref-type="table">4</xref>). We compared the genomes of 1330, S2 and S19 around the <italic toggle="yes">ery</italic> operon. S2 and 1330 genomes have the same <italic toggle="yes">eryA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">eryB</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> genes, while <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> in S2 genome encodes a premature protein. Meanwhile, a 68&#160;bp deletion in the region of <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> results in an ORF encoding a putative sugar binding protein in S19. The other genes upstream and downstream of <italic toggle="yes">ery</italic> operon in these three genomes are highly conserved, except for some mutations in the encoded proteins (Fig.&#160;<xref rid="Fig3" ref-type="fig">3</xref>). Based on the analysis, the inviability of S19 on erythritol agar might be due to the lack of <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> genes in the genome. A recent paper describes a new pathway on erythritol metabolism. Except for the known <italic toggle="yes">eryA</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">eryB</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">eryC</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> genes, two critical genes downstream of the <italic toggle="yes">eryD gene</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">TpiA2</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">RpiB</italic>, in erythritol metabolism are demonstrated. The new model strongly suggests that erythritol metabolism needs three isomerization reactions sequentially catalyzed by EryC, TpiA2, and RpiB to produce D-erythrose-4-P, which will be converted into glyceraldehyde-3-P and fructose-6-P in the pentose phosphate pathway [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">19</xref>]. In bacteria, phosphatidylcholine can be synthesized through either the phospholipid N-methylation pathway or the phosphatidylcholine synthase pathway [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">20</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">21</xref>]. The correlation between bacterial virulence and membrane phosphatidylcholine has been reported in <italic toggle="yes">L. pneumophila</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">A. tumefaciens</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">22</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">24</xref>]. The cell envelope of the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> contains phosphatidylethanolamine and phosphatidylcholine. Although the phosphatidylcholine level in bacterial membrane does not affect some major virulence properties of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> such as invasion, intracellular traffic, and intracellular replication, it is necessary to maintain a chronic infection in mice [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">25</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">26</xref>]. A systematic study on phosphatidylcholine synthase from <italic toggle="yes">S. meliloti</italic> was performed by alanine scanning mutagenesis. Protein sequence alignment of PCSs from several bacteria species showed that only five of eight residues at position 32 are serine. Changing the conserved residues close to position 32 causes about 20&#160;% decrease in PCS activity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">27</xref>]. Further studies are needed to explore if the mutation at position 32 affects the PCS activity, which might decrease S2 virulence.</p><p>Peptidoglycan is a major component of the bacterial cell wall. Its main function is to preserve the cell integrity and resist osmotic pressure [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">28</xref>]. It has been shown that peptidoglycan synthesis genes affect bacterial shape, elongation, and cell division [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">29</xref>]. Therefore, disruption of peptidoglycan synthesis might affect the survival rate of the bacteria in the host. UDP-N-acetylglucosamine (UDP-GlcNAc) is one of the key building blocks in peptidoglycan biosynthesis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">30</xref>]. The second step in the formation of UDP-GlcNAc is the interconversion of glucosamine-6-phosphate to glucosamine-1-phosphate, which is catalyzed by the enzyme phosphorglucosamine mutase (PNGM). A growing body of research suggests the importance of PNGM in bacterial virulence and infectivity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">31</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">33</xref>]. The different residue in PNGM between S2 and 1330 was located at position 250. Although the residue was close to the substrate binding site, it did not interact with the substrate. The biochemical studies are needed to fully define the function of this residue on PNGM activity and bacterial virulence.</p><p>Pyruvate kinase (PK), the key enzyme in the glycolysis, is important for controlling the concentrations of glycolytic intermediates, biosynthetic precursors and nucleoside triphosphates in the cells. Pyruvate kinase transfers the phosphoryl group of phosphoenolpyruvate to ADP to yield pyruvate and ATP. Most eukaryotic PKs can be allosterically activated by fructose 1,6-bisphosphate (FBP), while bacterial PK could be activated by either FBP or AMP/sugar monophosphates [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">34</xref>]. The bacteria carrying the impaired PK activity might exhibit severe attenuation as suggested in the study in <italic toggle="yes">C. Neoformans</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">35</xref>]. In chromosome I of S2, there was one residue different (T462A) in PK compared to that in 1330. Based on result of the Protein Databank search by blastp program, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> PK shared some similarities with PKs from other species, such as human, rabbit, <italic toggle="yes">B. Stearothermophilus</italic>, S<italic toggle="yes">. aureus</italic> et al. In the structure of human pyruvate kinase isozyme M2, the corresponding threonine at position 462 was close to the FBP binding site and interacted with FBP through two water molecules [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">36</xref>]. The threonine residue is highly conserved in many PKs, except for the serine in <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>. Both threonine and serine contain hydroxyl groups, which might be important for its interaction with the activator. A study demonstrates that S2 grows normally in the medium containing glucose as the only carbon source [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">37</xref>]. We, therefore, suspected the mutant on PK might not be a major reason for S2 attenuation. Further biochemical studies are still necessary to clarify this conclusion.</p><p>Although <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> spp. is considered typically as non-motile, a study demonstrates that flagellar genes are necessary for the assembly of a functional flagellum [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">38</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">39</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> with <italic toggle="yes">fli</italic>F mutant is unable to replicate in macrophages and HeLa cells, suggesting the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> flagellar genes might be related to virulence [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">40</xref>]. Another study shows that in the early log phase of its growth in 2YT nutrient broth, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> expresses genes corresponding to the basal (MS ring) and the distal parts of the flagellar apparatus. A polar and sheathed flagellar structure has been observed under transmission electron microscopy. Although the mutations encoding different parts of the flagellum structure do not show a discernible phenotype when compared with the wild-type strain in cellular infection models, all these mutants are attenuated in mice infected via the intraperitoneal route in 4&#160;weeks after infection [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">41</xref>]. We found a mutation in fliF protein (M544L) located in MS ring. Whether the mutation decreases the virulence of S2 awaits further studies.</p></sec><sec id="Sec6"><title>Other different proteins found between S2 and 1330</title><p>Two <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic> regulatory genes in S2 were different from those in 1330. The <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic> regulatory gene located in chromosome I lacked 8&#160;bp that began at ATG, causing the loss of the coding protein. The other different <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic> regulatory gene located in chromosome II had a 1&#160;bp deletion, resulting in the premature stop in the coding protein. Members of the IclR transcription regulator family regulate a variety of metabolic processes in various microorganisms [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">42</xref>]. In one study, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M mutations in <italic toggle="yes">AraC</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">ArsR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">Crp</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">DeoR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">GntR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">LysR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">MerR</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">RpiR</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">TetR</italic> were constructed. <italic toggle="yes">GntR</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">LysR</italic> mutants showed the decreased virulence [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">43</xref>]. Two different <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic> regulatory genes found in S2 genome were correspondent to <italic toggle="yes">IclR5</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">IclR6</italic> in the study. Therefore, the lack of these two <italic toggle="yes">IclR</italic> regulatory genes might not be the major cause for S2 attenuation.</p><p>In chromosome I of S2, Omp2b had two residues different from that in 1330. Omp2b is bacterial porins present in the outer membrane of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. The differences in the Omp2b variants are located in the predicted external loops of the porin [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">44</xref>]. The first different residue (D79A) is highly conserved in different <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains, while the second different residue (R225H) is not strictly conserved. <italic toggle="yes">Omp2b</italic> is present in virulent <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>, <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strains that are pathogenic to humans, but is absent in non-pathogenic <italic toggle="yes">B. ovis</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">45</xref>]. Bacterial porins have been reported to be involved in apoptosis modulation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">46</xref>&#8211;<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR48">48</xref>]. Expression of <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> Omp2b in yeast prevents the cell from death induced by the mammalian pro-apoptotic protein Bax [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">49</xref>]. No further functional studies have been reported on <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> Omp2b.</p><p>High-affinity binding protein-dependent ABC transporter consists of a high-affinity periplasmic substrate-binding subunit, two hydrophobic membrane subunits and two additional cytoplasmic subunits. ATP hydrolysis by associated ATPase provides the energy for substrate accumulation across the inner membrane against the concentration gradient. Periplasmic substrate-binding subunits are composed of two separate but similarly folded globular domains, which are connected by a hinge region made of two or three short polypeptide segments [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">50</xref>]. In S2 chromosome I, 1&#160;bp difference in periplasmic sugar-binding protein caused one residue difference (S97Y) in protein sequence compared with that in 1330 strain. The sequence alignment with structure-solute receptor GacH of <italic toggle="yes">Streptomyces glaucescens</italic> (Protein Databank accession number: 3JZJ [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">51</xref>]) showed that this residue was located very close to the substrate binding site. Due to the bulky structure of tyrosine, we suspected the mutation might affect the sugar binding affinity or preference of the protein. The comparative proteome study on <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccine strain Rev 1 and virulent strain 16&#160;M reveals that four sugar-binding proteins are differentially expressed in Rev 1, although these two strains have similar physical properties in biotyping tests [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">52</xref>]. Therefore, it is possible that the substrate preference change might help bacteria adapt to different growth conditions.</p><p>In S2 chromosome II, 2&#160;bp differences resulted in 2 residues difference in oligopeptide ABC transporter. The first different residue (S136G) existed in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> strains, while the other different residue was only seen in S2 (P284Q). Bacteria utilize peptides as a source of amino acids, carbon, nitrogen, and/or energy. A variety of peptide uptake systems mediate the translocation of peptides across the bacterial cytoplasmic membrane. Recent studies show that OppA possesses preference in substrate peptide. <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> OppA prefers positively charged peptides with three or four amino acids in length. However, other studies also suggest that the substrate preference of the peptide uptake systems are more complicated [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">53</xref>, <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR54">54</xref>]. The sequence alignment with the homologous structure of oligopeptide-binding protein from <italic toggle="yes">Salmonella typhimurium</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR55">55</xref>] showed that residue 284 was located close to the substrate binding site, but not involved in direct interaction with the substrate. Compared with other available structures of oligopeptide-binding proteins, the residue 284 of OppA protein can be glutamate <italic toggle="yes">(S. typhimurium)</italic>, serine (<italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>), alanine (<italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic>) or tyrosine (<italic toggle="yes">B. Pseudomallei</italic>). Meanwhile, two oligopeptide-binding proteins were encoded in 1330 genome, which shared 84&#160;% similarity in protein sequences. The residue at 284 is aspartate in the other sequence, suggesting this residue was not conserved well in different bacteria. ATP-Binding Cassette Transporter in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella ovis</italic> is shown to be essential in mice [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR56">56</xref>]. The &#916;<italic toggle="yes">abc</italic>A<italic toggle="yes">B</italic> mutant strain can trigger host serologic responses similar to the WT strain and a significant cellular host response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR57">57</xref>].</p><p>One residue different in S2 was found in a DedA family protein. The DedA family proteins are integral inner membrane proteins, which are present in nearly all species of bacteria. <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> encodes 8 DedA proteins. Although each of them is nonessential, they are collectively essential [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR58">58</xref>]. An <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> mutant with two <italic toggle="yes">dedA</italic> genes deletion (&#916;<italic toggle="yes">yghB</italic>/&#916;<italic toggle="yes">yqjA</italic>) fails to complete cell division or grow at elevated temperatures [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR59">59</xref>]. <italic toggle="yes">B. burgdorferi</italic> possesses only one <italic toggle="yes">dedA</italic> gene. The gene deletion results in imbalanced membrane phospholipid composition, which is required for proper cell division and envelope integrity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR60">60</xref>]. No functional clue was suggested for <italic toggle="yes">Brucella DedA</italic> so far.</p><p>We identified a difference at residue 576 between S2 and 1330 in a putative molybdopterin-binding oxidoreductase. Members of the molybdopterin oxidoreductase family include formate dehydrogenase, nitrate reductase, DMSO reductase, TMAO reductase, pyrogallol hydroxytransferase and arsenate reductase. The protein in S2 showed about 40&#160;% similarity with formate dehydrogenase H from <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> at residues 54&#8211;420, as suggested by the protein databank search with blastp. The residues involved in Mo binding are conserved [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR61">61</xref>]. No significant sequence similarity was found between these two proteins around residue 576.</p><p>A 1&#160;bp insertion caused the premature stop of 3-hydroxyisobutyrate dehydrogenase in S2. 3-hydroxyisobutyrate dehydrogenase, a key enzyme for the metabolism of valine and some keto-bodies, exists widely in bacteria, yeast, and mammalian tissue. It catalyzes the reversible conversion of 3-hydroxyisobutyrate to methylmalonate semialdehyde [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR62">62</xref>]. Although the enzymatic properties of 3-hydroxyisobutyrate dehydrogenase have been studies in several organisms, its relevance to bacterial virulence awaits further studies.</p><p>NADH-quinone oxidoreductase subunit 1 (NuoF) in S2 had one different residue compared with that of 1330 strain. NuoF is part of the hydrophilic fragment of NADH dehydrogenase I, which represents the electron input part of the enzyme NADH: ubiquinone oxidoreductase. It couples electron transport to proton translocation across the membrane, which is partially responsible for generating the proton gradient necessary for ATP production. NADH: ubiquinone oxidoreductase serves as both a proton pump and an entry point for electrons into the respiratory chain [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR63">63</xref>]. Currently, no bacterial virulence is associated with this gene.</p><p>One residue difference was found in <italic toggle="yes">lepA</italic> gene between S2 and 1330. LepA protein encoded by this gene exists in most of the bacterial genomes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR64">64</xref>]. Although <italic toggle="yes">lepA</italic> knockout strain of <italic toggle="yes">E. coli</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR65">65</xref>] and <italic toggle="yes">Staphylococcus aureus</italic> [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR66">66</xref>] are viable, the gene product might be necessary for bacteria survival under certain growth conditions [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR67">67</xref>].</p><p>There are other different proteins between S2 and 1330 (labeled unknown in Table&#160;<xref rid="Tab3" ref-type="table">3</xref>). When we analyzed the sequences, we did not find any functional implications of the proteins in bacteria. Therefore, further studies are needed to clarify their links to virulence attenuation of S2 strain.</p></sec></sec><sec id="Sec7"><title>Conclusion</title><p>In this report, we sequenced the attenuated strain <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> S2 genome and performed comparative genome analysis between S2 and virulent strain 1330. During the analysis, we found out 59 different ORFs between these two strains. Of these different ORFs, several have been reported to be related to <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> virulence, such as outer membrane autotransporter, eryD. The proteins encoded by some of the different genes have been suggested to be essential for bacteria growth, such as dedA, phosphorglucosamine mutase and phosphatidylcholine synthase. These gene products might be responsible for the utilization of the materials from the environment, which are necessary for bacteria growth. We found several mutations in peptide-binding ATP transporter and sugar-binding ATP transporter, but their roles on S2 attenuation could not be ruled out without further investigation.</p><p>It has been shown that the high concentration of erythritol in the foetal tissues of cattle, sheep, goats and pigs might be beneficial for the growth of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. Erythritol catabolism pathway may play important physiological roles in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. The studies in S19 and S2308 showed that <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> might act as the repressor of erythritol operon [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">17</xref>]. In our experiment, we found that S2 but not S19 could grow in the media supplemented with erythritol, suggesting S2 has the ability to metabolize erythritol. Therefore, further investigation on the function of the <italic toggle="yes">eryD</italic> gene is necessary to clarify its relevance to virulence attenuation.</p><p>Although we identified some target proteins that might be related to the virulence, most of the identified proteins have not been investigated in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>. Therefore, all these genes have to be examined individually to ensure their relationships with the virulence. The modification of the genes identified in our study will help to reduce residual virulence, which is critical to developing more efficient <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> vaccine strains.</p></sec><sec id="Sec8"><title>Methods</title><sec id="Sec9"><title>Strain and genomic DNA preparation</title><p>The S2 used in sequencing was obtained from the China Institute of Veterinary Drugs Control. Total genomic DNA was extracted using the DNeasy Blood and Tissue Kit (QIAGEN, China Ltd., China). The DNA was fragmented by nebulization, and fragments ranging from 400 to 600&#160;bp were extracted from an agarose gel after size-fractionation, and then adaptors were added according to Illumina Sample Preparation Guide.</p></sec><sec id="Sec10"><title>Genome sequencing and assembly</title><p>After sequencing with Illumina Hiseq2000, 50,941,654 paired-end (2*101&#160;bp) reads were obtained. Base calling was performed with the software CAVASA. There were 41,608,314 paired reads passed the filter, which provided coverage of chromosome of ~1000-fold. Reads were assembled into contigs and scaffolds using SOAP de-novo (Release 1.04) [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR68">68</xref>]. Assembled contigs were compared to the published genome sequence of <italic toggle="yes">B. suis</italic> strain 1330 (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_017250.1">NC_017250.1</ext-link>, <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NC_017251.1">NC_017251.1</ext-link>) using BLAT v.34 [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR69">69</xref>]. The order and the orientation of the assembled contigs were determined in accordance with the control genome. Based on the extensive similarities between the published genomes of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic>, PCR primers were designed to link the gaps between two neighboring contigs. The leftover gaps were sequenced separately by conventional Sanger sequencing. The Illumina sequence reads for the genomes are deposited to NCBI, with the accession number of SAMN03068300.</p></sec><sec id="Sec11"><title>Gene prediction and annotation</title><p>Gene prediction was established with Glimmer 2.0 using the default settings [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR70">70</xref>]. All the predicted CDS and putative intergenic sequences were subjected to further manual inspections. Exhaustive BLAST searches with an incremental stringency against the NCBI non-redundant protein database were performed to determine the homology of the predicted coding sequences. Translational start codons were identified based on protein homology, proximity to the ribosome-binding site, relative positions to predicted signal peptide and putative promoter sequences. We also classified the putative proteins according to the COG database search results. Transfer RNAs were predicted with the tRNAscan-SE software. The repeat sequences were predicted by Trf program [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR71">71</xref>]. The results showed 55 and 23 tandem sequences in chromosome I and II. The average copy number is 2.79 and 2.86.</p><p>All the S2 proteins predicted have been compared to KEGG bacterial gene set with KAAS (KEGG Automatic Annotation Server <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://www.genome.jp/tools/kaas/">http://www.genome.jp/tools/kaas/</ext-link>) program by BBH (Bi-directional best hit) method to map the genes into bacterial pathways automatically.</p></sec><sec id="Sec12"><title>Comparative genomic analysis</title><p>MUMmer 3.0 program was used to investigate the differences between the genome sequences of 1330 and S2 with the default parameters [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR72">72</xref>]. The genomes were first automatically compared for SNPs and indels by MUMmer3.0. Then SNPs between 1330 and S2 were called by home-brew perl scripts. We classified SNPs as intergenic, frameshift and premature stop according to their positions and functions, and also counted the numbers of synonymous substitutions and non-synonymous substitutions. To identify potential genes that were different between 1330 and S2, we compared S2 genome sequences using blastp program with proteins download from NCBI RefSeq database.</p></sec><sec id="Sec13"><title>S2 and S19 erythritol sensitivity test</title><p>The Trypticase-soy agar plates in the presence of 1&#160;mg/ml erythritol [TSA (Ery)] plates were prepared [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR73">73</xref>]. The autoclaved TSA was cooled to 47&#160;&#176;C, and the appropriate amount of erythritol was added and mixed. The plates were poured. S2 and S19 were grown on TSA slants at 37&#160;&#176;C for 24&#160;h and resuspended in saline, and were tested by placing 20&#160;&#956;l of tenfold dilutions (10<sup>7</sup>, 10<sup>6</sup>, 10<sup>5</sup>, 10<sup>4</sup>, 10<sup>3</sup>, 10<sup>2</sup> and 10<sup>1</sup>&#160;CFU/ml) of the bacteria resuspension on TSA and TSA (Ery) plates and incubated at 37&#160;&#176;C. Plates were examined for 2 to 5&#160;days. The effect of erythritol on the growth of S2 was estimated from the size of the colonies on the test plates in comparison with the control plates (S2 and S19 inoculated on TSA plates and S19 inoculated on TSA (Ery) plates). The experiments were repeated three times.</p></sec></sec></body><back><app-group><app id="App1"><sec id="Sec14"><title>Additional files</title><p><media xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" position="anchor" xlink:href="12864_2016_3076_MOESM1_ESM.doc" id="MOESM1" orientation="portrait"><label>Additional file 1: Table S1.</label><caption><p>Results of genome-wide collinear analysis between attenuated strain S2 and virulent strain 1330 by Mummer.
