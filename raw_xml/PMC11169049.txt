In this study, eight <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> and eighteen <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> strains from Egypt were annotated and compared with RB51 and REV1 vaccines respectively. RAST toolkit in the BV-BRC server was used for annotation, revealing genome length of 3,250,377&#xA0;bp and 3,285,803&#xA0;bp, 3289 and 3323 CDS, 48 and 49 tRNA genes, the same number of rRNA (3) genes, 583 and 586 hypothetical proteins, 2697 and 2726 functional proteins for <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> respectively. <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains exhibit a similar number of candidate genes, while <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains showed some differences, especially in the SRR19520422 Faiyum strain. Also, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> clarified differences in antimicrobial resistance genes (KatG, FabL, MtrA, MtrB, OxyR, and VanO-type) in SRR19520319 Faiyum and (Erm C and Tet K) in SRR19520422 Faiyum strain. Additionally, the whole genome phylogeny analysis proved that all <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains were related to vaccinated animals and all <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains of Menoufia clustered together and closely related to Gharbia, Dameitta, and Kafr Elshiek. The Bowtie2 tool identified 338 (eight <italic>B. abortus</italic>) and 4271 (eighteen <italic>B. melitensis</italic>) single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) along the genomes. These variants had been annotated according to type and impact. Moreover, thirty candidate genes were predicted and submitted at GenBank (24 in <italic>B. abortus</italic>) and (6 in <italic>B. melitensis</italic>).     <sec id="Sec1">
      <title>Introduction</title>
      <p id="Par2"><italic>Brucella</italic> is a stealth pathogen that evades immune response and causes brucellosis which is regarded as a zoonotic infectious disease all over the world (Corbel <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR9">1997</xref>). The <italic>Brucella</italic> genus now includes twelve species that infect various domestic and wild-life animal species (Dawood et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR14">2023</xref>). Six <italic>Brucella</italic> species have been identified as correlating to their pathogenicity and natural hosts. They are <italic>Brucella abortus</italic> (cattle), <italic>Brucella melitensis</italic> (sheep and goats), <italic>Brucella canis</italic> (dogs), <italic>Brucella suis</italic> (pigs), and <italic>Brucella neotomae</italic> (desert wood rat), <italic>Brucella ovis</italic> (rams) (Whatmore <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR50">2009</xref>; Blasco and Molina-Flores <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR4">2011</xref>). In Egypt, <italic>B. melitensis</italic> biovar 3 is the most common cause of animal and human infection, subsequent to <italic>B. abortus</italic> biovar 1 (Hamdy and Zaki <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR23">2018</xref>). Regardless of the implantation brucellosis control strategy in 1981, it remains widespread in Egypt (Hegazy et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR24">2021</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par3"><italic>Brucella&#x2019;s</italic> genome is circular and very stable, and its size is 3.3&#xA0;Mb (approximately 2.1&#xA0;Mb on chromosome I and 1.2&#xA0;Mb on chromosome II) (Michaux et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">1993</xref>). There are striking similarities between <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> corresponding to the homology degree found in DNA-DNA hybridization studies (Wang et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR46">2012</xref>). Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) is regarded as the perfect technology for studying genomic variants of organisms in depth; it has become simple, cost-effective, and widely available (Tan et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR44">2015</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par4">Bioinformatics has significant roles not only in dealing with massive amounts of data, but also in the prediction, analysis, or interpretation of clinical and preclinical results, drug discovery, evaluation, and development (Khan et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR26">2022</xref>). The comprehensive genome analysis provides detailed insights into genome quality evaluation, a phylogenetic tree, subsystem overview, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes, and candidate genes (Wang et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR47">2020</xref>). Hence, in this study, we carried out detailed genetic characterization and comparative genomics analysis of some available Egyptian strains.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec2">
      <title>Materials &amp; Methods</title>
      <sec id="Sec3">
        <title>Retrieve Data</title>
        <p id="Par5">The sequences of <italic>B. abortus</italic> and<italic> B. melitensis</italic> strains in Egypt (from 2007 to 2019) were obtained from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) repository and downloaded from European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) in fastq format (Tables S1 and S2). Most of the genomic analysis tools used in this study were done at the Bacterial and Viral Bioinformatics Resource Center (BV-BRC) server. Therefore, comparative genomics of available Egyptian strains (<italic>B. abortus</italic>
<italic>n</italic>&#x2009;=&#x2009;8 and <italic>B. melitensis</italic>
<italic>n</italic>&#x2009;=&#x2009;18) have been used in the current research to determine their closeness with vaccine strains (RB51 and Rev.1) for <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> respectively.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec4">
        <title>Assembly and Annotation of Genomes</title>
        <p id="Par6">The processed fastq files are applied to the PATRIC assembly service which has seven different strategies. A Unicycler (V 0.4.8) pipeline used for the genome assembly of bacterial and archaeal genomes and the &#x201C;AUTO&#x201D; selection was applied to assemble the paired read to short read contigs. The unicycler employs the SPAdes de novo assembler to construct an initial assembly graph and subsequently refines by incorporating information from both short and long reads to generate high-quality, circular sequences and resolves complex repeat structures (Wick et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR52">2017</xref>). A bandage program (V 0.8.1) was employed for visualization of these de novo assembly contigs. It primarily helps to identify errors and structure rearrangements in next-generation sequencing (NGS) data (Wick et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR51">2015</xref>). Pilon (V 1.23) was used for correcting assembly errors for short reads to enhances the quality of draft genome assemblies producing more contiguous and accurate genome assemblies with better gene identification with remarkable accuracy in the identification of both small and large sequence variants (Walker et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR45">2014</xref>). Additionally, TrimGalore was used for trimming the reads. It enables the automation of adapter trimming, and the removal of low-quality reads to enhance the quality of sequencing data. Also, SAMtools with default parameters were employed for reading mapping (Li et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">2009</xref>). Finally, QUAST Version 5.0.2 is used for the automated and comprehensive analysis of genomics sequence assemblies to check the quality and completeness of the assembly output (Gurevich et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR22">2013</xref>). Genome annotation refers to the process of locating functional components along the sequence of a genome. To annotate genomic characteristics in <italic>Brucella</italic> strains, rapid annotation using a subsystems technology tool kit (RASTtk) in BV-BRC was utilized (Aziz et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR2">2008</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec5">
        <title>Comprehensive Genome Analysis</title>
        <p id="Par7">Comprehensive Genome Analysis was applied using the meta-service on the BV-BRC server that acquires raw data or the single or paired read to calculate genome assembly, annotation, quality control, AMR, and candidate genes that are specific for important functions (Davis et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR13">2019</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec6">
        <title>Alignment Analysis</title>
        <p id="Par8">Progressive Mauve application was used to perform Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA). Using MAUVE, the visually observable locally collinear blocks (LCBs), which are identified by specific color codes, were used to locate the genomic areas that were shared by all the matched sequences. LCBs indicate the homologous sequence found in two or more&#xA0;genomes&#xA0;without rearrangement (Darling et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR11">2010</xref>). <italic>B. abortus </italic>RB51-AHVLA (Taxonomy ID: 1198700) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain Rev.1&#xA0;(Genome ID: 29459.409) were used as reference genomes from the PATRIC database. Moreover, at the level of Menoufia Governorate, <italic>Brucella</italic> from different centers was aligned with reference vaccine genome Rev.1.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec7">
        <title>Phylogenetic Analysis</title>
        <p id="Par9">Performing phylogenetic relationships in bacterial populations is critical to understanding the molecular evolutionary chronicle (O&#x2019;Callaghan and Whatmore <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR37">2011</xref>). We used a BV-BRC phylogenetic tree called &#x201C;Codon Trees&#x201D; to know the evolutionary relationship between our strains and other outgroup strains to give an accurate view. It makes use of the predefined PATRIC protein global families (PGFams) (Davis et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">2016</xref>), choosing 10&#x2013;1000 single-copy families from among members of a genomic group. Alignments are produced for protein sequences of every family using Muscle (Edgar <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR16">2004</xref>), and for their corresponding nucleotide sequences, BioPython&#x2019;s codon-align function was employed (Cock et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR8">2009</xref>). To create confidence values, 100 rapid bootstrap rounds are executed within RaxML (Stamatakis et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR42">2008</xref>).</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec8">
        <title>Comparative Genomic Analysis</title>
        <p id="Par10">The comparative genome analysis was executed at the BV-BRC server. It combines two PATRIC tools (Protein Family Sorter and Comparative Pathways Viewer) as well as subsystems (Overbeek <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR38">2005</xref>; Overbeek et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR39">2014</xref>). All three systems use two protein families: the local families (PLFams) for intra-genus comparisons and the global families (PGFams) for cross-genus comparisons (Davis et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR12">2016</xref>). The PATRIC Protein Family Sorter tool is utilized to investigate the distribution patterns of protein families with different options: (1) present in all families to find the core genome, (2) absence from all families to examine the accessory genome and 3) mixed/either option which is the default is to determine the pangenome.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec9">
        <title>Variation Analysis</title>
        <p id="Par11">The variation analysis tool on the BV-BRC server finds the alterations from the reference (<italic>B. abortus</italic>&#xA0;RB51-AHVLA (Taxonomy ID: 1,198,700) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strain Rev.1&#xA0;(Genome ID: 29,459.409)) and identifies single nucleotide variants (SNVs) and short insertions/deletions (indels) from aligned NGS data. Numerous tools have been developed such as BWA-mem and BWA-mem-strict (Li <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR32">2013</xref>), Bowtie2 (Langmead and Salzberg <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR29">2012</xref>), and LAST (Frith et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR19">2010</xref>). FreeBayes (Garrison and Marth <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR20">2012</xref>) and SAMtools (Li et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR31">2009</xref>) are the two SNP callers. Nevertheless, even minor variations could result in a significant number of variant calls across the entire genome. Therefore, it is crucial to select a reliable variant caller for both SNVs and indels and further evaluate and refine it to achieve maximum efficiency when analyzing the data. The analysis was done using the four different aligners.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec10">
        <title>Gene Prediction</title>
        <p id="Par12">The assembled contigs files generated from the previous steps from the BV-BRC website were subjected to various gene prediction servers: GeneMark.hmm prokaryotic (version 3.25), GeneMarkS-2 (Lomsadze et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR33">2018</xref>), and EasyGene 1.2 Gene finding in prokaryotes (Larsen and Krogh <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR30">2003</xref>). To find the most concealed state sequence, the GeneMark.hmm server utilizes a hidden Markov model (HMM) architecture and the&#xA0;Viterbi algorithm, while GeneMarkS-2 is an ab initio technique that finds species-specific (native) genes using a model generated from self-training, as well as heuristic models meant to identify horizontal gene transfers. To evaluate GeneMarkS-2&#x2019;s precision, the identified genes were confirmed through Clusters of Orthologous Groups (COG) proteomics experiments, annotation and N-terminal protein sequencing. EasyGene is also based on an algorithmic estimation of an HMM for an entirely new genome. After identifying the putative genes, they are evaluated using the HMM score and according to their scores and the length of the open reading frame (ORF), the statistical significance of the genes is calculated.</p>
      </sec>
      <sec id="Sec11">
        <title>Molecular Evolutionary, Phylogenetic Analysis, and Conserved Regions of Predicted Genes</title>
        <p id="Par13">The 30 predicted genes underwent BLASTN and multiple sequence alignments from various genotypes via ClustalW multiple alignments impeded in BIOEDIT 7.2 software. The conserved regions refer to parts of DNA or protein sequences that have been preserved during evolution and contain functional elements such as protein-coding, non-coding regulatory domains, or structural motifs. In DNA sequences, the terms &#x201C;similarity&#x201D; and &#x201C;identity&#x201D; are used interchangeably to describe the degree of resemblance between two sequences, with identity specifically indicating that the two sequences are identical in that region. High sequence identity between regions of DNA typically indicates conservation, as these regions have been preserved across multiple organisms and may have important functional implications. These regions were identified by comparing sequences from multiple organisms or individuals. The &#x201C;find conserved regions&#x201D; option in the BIOEDIT 7.2 software with the default parameters of minimum length&#x2009;=&#x2009;15 and max average entropy&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.2. was applied to detect the conserved regions in the thirty predicted genes. These parameters ensure that identified regions meet a certain level of similarity and length to be considered conserved (Zhang et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR54">2000</xref>).</p>
        <p id="Par14">After that, MEGA11 software and the ClustalW tool that impeded in it were employed for the phylogenetic analysis of the thirty predicted genes (Tamura et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR43">2021</xref>). The multiple sequence alignment was performed using MAFFT server which offers various multiple alignment strategies. They are classified into three types, (a) the progressive method, (b) the iterative refinement method with the WSP score, and (c) the iterative refinement method using both the WSP and consistency scores. In order to obtain more accurate alignments, three new options, L-INS-i, G-INS-i, and E-INS-i, were used to align sequences globally using the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm. The five different constructed phylogenetic tree methods in MEGA11 software were used. Not only computing pairwise distances and overall mean distances but also different models were assessed by MEGA11 software. The best model selection &#x201C;find best DNA/protein models&#x201D; option was applied using MEGA to justify the choice of model for phylogenetics. Then the phylogenetic analysis was conducted on nucleotide substitution type and choice of the best model General Time Reversible Model (GTR). The GTR model is designed for inferring evolutionary relationships from DNA sequence data by accounting for different rates of nucleotide substitutions and the reversibility of these processes over time, making it an accurate tool for phylogenetic analysis. The initial tree(s) for the heuristic search were conducted automatically by applying Neighbor-Join and BioNJ algorithms to a matrix of pairwise distances estimated using bootstrap methods, and the GTR model and then selecting the topology with superior log likelihood value. Phylogenetic trees use bootstrap values to measure the confidence level of sequence-based phylogeny on a scale of 1 to 100.</p>
      </sec>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec12">
      <title>Results</title>

          <title>Core and Accessory Gene Compositions</title>
          <p id="Par24">Pangenome refers to the totality of shared and distinct genetic material present in a particular species that combines the genetic information of all the genomes sampled. It helps to better understand the comprehension composition and evolution of gene content within a population. The core genome is the identification of protein families that are either conserved across all the genomes, and that are conserved only in specific subsets of selected genomes are the accessory proteome.</p>
          <p id="Par25">In the case of <italic>B. abortus</italic> (8 genomes) and <italic>B. melitensis</italic> (18 genomes), the pan-genome analysis revealed 2677 and 2650 core genes and zero accessory genes of 2742 and 3911 gene pan-genome respectively. It is indicated that the genomes of these two <italic>Brucella</italic> species are highly similar, and they share most of their genetic material. The absence of accessory genes in the pan-genome analysis indicates that there is little genetic diversity among the sampled strains of these two species as shown in Fig. <xref rid="Fig6" ref-type="fig">6</xref>.</p>

    <sec id="Sec28">
      <title>Discussion</title>
      <p id="Par34">Brucellosis is often considered one of the world&#x2019;s most serious zoonotic diseases. It causes enormous economic losses in both developed and developing countries. Despite massive attempts to eradicate <italic>Brucella</italic> in many countries of the world, this disease has disseminated worldwide (Elrashedy et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">2022</xref>). The complete genome of <italic>Brucella</italic> is 3.3&#xA0;Mb (approximately 2.1&#xA0;Mb on chromosome I and 1.2&#xA0;Mb on chromosome II) (Michaux et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR34">1993</xref>). Genomic annotation and comparative genome analysis of <italic>Brucella</italic> species are important to reveal the genetic features and get a deep look at the genetic variation in the form of SNPs for validating the vaccine strain. In this study, <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains annotation displayed striking similarities in a variety of features to those previously reported in studies (Wang et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR47">2020</xref>; Yu et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR53">2015</xref>). On the other hand, annotation of <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains was agreed upon in a study performed by (Karthik et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR25">2021</xref>) and this may be due to the genome stability of <italic>Brucella</italic> this study&#x2019;s findings confirm that there are significant resemblances between the two <italic>Brucella</italic> species according to the homology degree.</p>
      <p id="Par35">In this study, there are 33 antimicrobial resistance genes identified by the PATRIC server in the two <italic>brucella</italic> species. The intracellular lifestyle of brucellae, which prevents the penetration of certain antimicrobials into the cells, may have a role in the emergence of resistance in these bacteria. As a result, resistance and virulence mechanisms at the genomic, proteomic, and transcriptome levels trigger the alert about an increase in the number of residence genes. The stealthy <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogen has different AMR mechanisms such as enzymatic activation and inactivation of the antimicrobial agent, efflux pumps, replacement of protein or alteration occurring in cell wall charge via protein, drug target modification and resistance genes regulating the gene expression (Biswas et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">2008</xref>; Dadar et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR10">2023</xref>). DNA gyrase enzyme (gyrA and gyrB) has the primary role in DNA supercoiling, and inhibition of this activity using quinolones or fluoroquinolones generation is combined with rapid killing of the pathogenic <italic>Brucella</italic> (Spencer and Panda <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR41">2023</xref>). However, rifampicin is composed of four distinguishing subunits (rpoA, rpoB, rpoC and rpoD) and it has a mode of action that inhibits the DNA-dependent RNA polymerase, and its resistance is caused by the mutation at 81&#xA0;bp in the rpoB gene (Portelli et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR40">2020</xref>). Also, FosX is an AMR gene that can inactivate Fosfomycin (Bolotin et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR6">2021</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par36">Furthermore, efflux pumps can be categorized into six groups, namely, the ATP-binding cassette (ABC), resistance nodulation cell division (RND), major facilitator superfamily (MFS), multi-antimicrobial and toxic compound extrusion (MATE), proteobacterial antimicrobial compound efflux (PACE) and small multidrug resistance (SMR) family (Du et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR15">2018</xref>). In this study, efflux pumps in two families (ABC and RND) were accompanied by antibiotic resistance. MacAB (macrolide-specific ABC-type efflux carrier) involves macA and macB genes that perform resistance against macrolides composed of 14- and 15-membered compounds (Greene et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR21">2018</xref>). The RND family is another efflux pump that features the triclosan-specific efflux protein TriABC-OpmH. In <italic>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</italic>, this protein comprises two membrane fusion proteins, TriA and TriB (Fabre et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR18">2021</xref>). Although its role in <italic>Brucella</italic> is not yet understood, increased resistance to ciprofloxacin and imipenem suggests that the efflux pump in <italic>Brucella</italic> may provide quinolone resistance.</p>
      <p id="Par37">What&#x2019;s more, folic acid (vitamin B9) is an important amino acid that enters the folate synthesis process which is vital for the replication of DNA. This process can be blocked by trimethoprim and sulfamethoxazole. The presence or absence of the folA (dihydrofolate reductase) or the folP (dihydropteroate synthase) can produce resistance to trimethoprim and sulfamethoxazole respectively (Biswas et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR3">2008</xref>; Nurjadi et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR36">2021</xref>). Additionally, gidB gene encodes a tRNA uridine 5-carboxymethyl aminomethyl modification enzyme that participates in post-transcriptional tRNA modifications. The gidB exhibited a decreased susceptibility to aminoglycoside antibiotics like gentamicin and streptomycin (Mikheil et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR35">2012</xref>).</p>
      <p id="Par38">In <italic>Brucella</italic>, OxyR is a transcription factor that affects the bacteria&#x2019;s ability to combat oxidative stress and resist reactive oxygen species (ROS) produced within the host cell. This protein controls the expression of different cellular components responsible for antioxidant defense mechanisms. It can trigger the activation of numerous genes, including catalase-peroxidase, superoxide dismutase, and other detoxification genes, which aid in the bacteria&#x2019;s protection against ROS-induced damage (Kim and Mayfield <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR28">2000</xref>; Wang et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR48">2022</xref>). Moreover, PgsA and GdpD contribute to the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) that has a very important role in <italic>Brucella</italic> virulence and survival (Elrashedy et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR17">2022</xref>). In addition, these genes Alr, Ddl, S10p, S12p, EF-G, inhA, EF-Tu, Iso-tRNA, Dfr, kasA, rho, MurA, and fabI have been implicated in various cellular functions and pathways in <italic>Brucella</italic>. While some of these genes have been implicated in antibiotic resistance in other bacteria, their role in <italic>Brucella</italic> antibiotic resistance may not be extensively studied or well-established.</p>
      <p id="Par39">In this study, there are some differences in Fayioum strains of <italic>B. meliteinsis</italic> revealed in antimicrobial resistance genes which can be explained on the basis that Fayioum main raising cattle is beef collected from different sources (local, European, and Latin) with no vaccination representing a pool for mixing different <italic>Brucella</italic> strains from different sources. KatG is a gene that encodes for the catalase-peroxidase enzyme. This enzyme is responsible for the detoxification of ROS within bacterial cells and is therefore considered to play a key role in bacterial defense mechanisms, including resistance to antibiotics. Although KatG is not typically considered as a primary antibiotic resistance gene in <italic>Brucella</italic>, studies have shown that mutations in this gene can result in resistance to isoniazid, an anti-tuberculosis drug that is also used to treat brucellosis (Bollela et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR5">2016</xref>). Additionally, The Erm(C) gene is a ribosomal RNA methylase gene that confers resistance to macrolide-lacosamide-streptogramin B (MLSB) antibiotics, including erythromycin, clindamycin, and streptogramin B (Khodabandeh et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR27">2019</xref>). From the above mentioned, antibiotic-resistance genes have the potential to spread between different bacterial species through horizontal gene transfer, leading to the emergence of multidrug-resistant strains that are difficult to treat. Hence, it is crucial to monitor antibiotic resistance patterns across various bacterial species and develop suitable treatment strategies to ensure the effective management of bacterial infections.</p>
      <p id="Par40">In this study, whole genome phylogeny analysis for all <italic>Brucella</italic> strains was classified into six clades: <italic>B. melitensis</italic> clade; <italic>B. abortus</italic> clade; <italic>B. canis clade</italic>; <italic>B. suis clade; B. microti</italic>;&#xA0;<italic>B. pinnipedialis</italic>;&#xA0;<italic>B. ceti</italic> clade; and <italic>B. ovis</italic> clade. The evolutionary relationship constructed here fits&#xA0;with previous results (Azam et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR1">2016</xref>). The transition mutations occur more frequently than transversion mutations and could have an impact on the evolution of protein-coding genes. This is because the DNA sequence undergoes less significant changes during transition mutations, which may result in fewer negative consequences on the protein-coding sequence. As a result, certain parts of the genome could experience higher rates of transition substitutions, possibly due to the less deleterious impact of these mutations on protein function in those regions.</p>
      <p id="Par41">By analyzing the pan-genome, we can gain a comprehensive understanding of the gene content and genetic evolution within a population, which provides valuable insights. Additionally, identifying core and accessory genes through pan-genome analysis can help us to better comprehend the genetic relatedness and variability of different strains of a particular species. When we apply this mechanism to <italic>B. abortus</italic> and <italic>B. melitensis</italic>, it suggests that these two species have a significant genetic resemblance, which is also supported by the absence of accessory genes in the sampled strains. In this study, the results are in agreement with (Wattam et al. <xref ref-type="bibr" rid="CR49">2014</xref>) who reported 2285 core genes in the <italic>Brucella</italic> species. Finally, This study enhances our knowledge of the epidemiology and evolution of brucellosis in Egypt by providing significant insights into the genetic diversity, potential virulence factors, and associations related to vaccines of <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogens.</p>
    </sec>
    <sec id="Sec29">
      <title>Conclusion</title>
      <p id="Par42">Our investigation into Brucellosis involves the genomic analysis of eight <italic>B. abortus</italic> and eighteen <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains from Egypt have yielded significant findings. The comprehensive comparison with RB51 and REV1 vaccines, annotation of genome characteristics, identification of antimicrobial resistance genes (AMR), and elucidation of phylogenetic relationships offer an understanding of the genetic landscape of this <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogen. The detection of AMR in two <italic>Brucella</italic> species indicates that Brucellae possess several strategies to withstand antimicrobial agents. One of these strategies is the intracellular lifestyle of <italic>Brucella</italic>, which hinders the entry of specific antimicrobials into the cells, contributing to the development of resistance. Also in this study, we predicted and submitted thirty candidate genes to GenBank. Interestingly, the distinct features observed in <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains, particularly in the Faiyum isolate, highlight potential variations in virulence and resistance profiles. The clustering patterns in the whole genome phylogeny further emphasize the geographical relevance, linking <italic>B. abortus</italic> strains to vaccinated animals and grouping <italic>B. melitensis</italic> strains from Menoufia, Gharbia, Dameitta, and Kafr Elshiek. The detection of numerous SNPs and subsequent annotation enhances our knowledge of the molecular intricacies of the <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogen. This research increases our understanding of the epidemiology and evolution of brucellosis in Egypt by providing useful insights into the genetic diversity, putative virulence factors, and vaccine-related associations of these <italic>Brucella</italic> pathogens.</p>
