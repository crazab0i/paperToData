xlink:href="1476-8518-7-1.pdf"/><abstract><sec><title>Background</title><p>There is no safe, effective human vaccine against brucellosis. Live attenuated <italic>Brucella </italic>strains are widely used to vaccinate animals. However these live <italic>Brucella </italic>vaccines can cause disease and are unsafe for humans. Killed <italic>Brucella </italic>or subunit vaccines are not effective in eliciting long term protection. In this study, we evaluate an approach using a live, non-pathogenic bacteria (<italic>E. coli</italic>) genetically engineered to mimic the brucellae pathway of infection and present antigens for an appropriate cytolitic T cell response.</p></sec><sec sec-type="methods"><title>Methods</title><p><italic>E. coli </italic>was modified to express invasin of <italic>Yersinia </italic>and listerialysin O (LLO) of <italic>Listeria </italic>to impart the necessary infectivity and antigen releasing traits of the intracellular pathogen, <italic>Brucella</italic>. This modified <italic>E. coli </italic>was considered our vaccine delivery system and was engineered to express Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) or Brucella antigens for <italic>in vitro </italic>and <italic>in vivo </italic>immunological studies including cytokine profiling and cytotoxicity assays.</p></sec><sec><title>Results</title><p>The <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector was able to infect all cells tested and efficiently deliver therapeutics to the host cell. Using GFP as antigen, we demonstrate that the <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector elicits a Th1 cytokine profile in both primary and secondary immune responses. Additionally, using this vector to deliver a <italic>Brucella </italic>antigen, we demonstrate the ability of the <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector to induce specific Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes (CTLs).</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusion</title><p>Protection against most intracellular bacterial pathogens can be obtained mostly through cell mediated immunity. Data presented here suggest modified <italic>E. coli </italic>can be used as a vaccine vector for delivery of antigens and therapeutics mimicking the infection of the pathogen and inducing cell mediated immunity to that pathogen.</p></sec></abstract><custom-meta-group><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-status-qastatus</meta-name><meta-value>0</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-status-live</meta-name><meta-value>yes</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-status-embargo</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-status-released</meta-name><meta-value>yes</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-open-access</meta-name><meta-value>yes</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-olf</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-manuscript</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-legally-suppressed</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-has-pdf</meta-name><meta-value>yes</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-has-supplement</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-pdf-only</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-suppress-copyright</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-is-real-version</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-is-scanned-article</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-preprint</meta-name><meta-value>no</meta-value></custom-meta><custom-meta><meta-name>pmc-prop-in-epmc</meta-name><meta-value>yes</meta-value></custom-meta></custom-meta-group></article-meta></front><body><sec><title>Background</title><p>There is no safe, effective human vaccine against brucellosis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B1">1</xref>]. Brucellosis is a zoonotic disease causing chronic fatigue, arthritis, recurrent fever, endocarditis, and orchitis in humans [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B2">2</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B3">3</xref>]. The etiologic agents for brucellosis are the closely related, facultative, gram-negative, intracellular coccobacilli, <italic>Brucella </italic>species [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B4">4</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B5">5</xref>]. The ease with which <italic>Brucella </italic>can be transmitted by aerosolization, and the unpredictable timing of the onset of symptoms raise the specter of a potentially insidious bioterror attack [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B6">6</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B9">9</xref>]. During the course of infection, <italic>Brucella </italic>are actively phagocytosed by macrophages or other phagocytic cells where they prevent phagosome-lysosome fusion, persist and replicate in endocytic compartments that acquire endoplasmic reticulum membranes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B11">11</xref>]. Bacteremia occurs during an acute phase that is hard to define or detect [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B12">12</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B13">13</xref>]. Live attenuated <italic>Brucella </italic>strains are widely used to vaccinate animals against brucellosis. However, these live <italic>Brucella </italic>vaccines can cause disease and are unsafe for humans [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B14">14</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B17">17</xref>]. Killed <italic>Brucella </italic>or subunit vaccines are not effective in eliciting long term protection [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B18">18</xref>]. Therefore, a new vaccine approach is needed.</p><p>Eliciting a specific T cell response is necessary to fight <italic>Brucella </italic>infection. Numerous studies have shown that Th1 or cell mediated immunity is crucial for protection against brucellosis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>] however Th2 or humoral immunity also participates in protecting the host [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B20">20</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B23">23</xref>]. Adoptive transfer of <italic>Brucella </italic>immune T cells protects mice against virulent <italic>Brucella </italic>infection [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B24">24</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B25">25</xref>] with both CD4<sup>+ </sup>and CD8<sup>+ </sup>T cells involved in immunity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B27">27</xref>]. Nevertheless, murine brucellosis is markedly exacerbated in MHC I knockout mice that lack CD8<sup>+ </sup>T cells compared to CD4<sup>+ </sup>T cell deficient mice or wild type mice [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B19">19</xref>]. In fact numerous studies have shown that a CTL response is key to effective <italic>Brucella </italic>immunity [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B26">26</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B28">28</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B30">30</xref>].</p><p>Our approach utilizes a non-pathogenic <italic>Escherichia coli </italic>to mimic the intracellular pathogen <italic>Brucella melitensis </italic>in delivery and presentation of antigens to stimulate a Th1 and CTL response. <italic>E. coli </italic>are normally extracellular while <italic>Brucella </italic>are intracellular bacteria. Therefore, we engineered <italic>E. coli </italic>(DH5&#945;) to express a plasmid containing the <italic>inv </italic>gene from <italic>Yersinia pseudotuberculosis </italic>and the <italic>hly </italic>gene from <italic>Listeria monocytogenes </italic>[<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>]. Introduction of <italic>inv </italic>confers <italic>E. coli </italic>invasion of host cells by binding the &#945;&#946;1-integrin heterodimer. Upon clustering of integrins, invasin activates signaling cascades. One signaling pathway causes activation of components of focal adhesion complexes including <italic>Src</italic>, focal adhesion kinase, and cytoskeletal proteins, leading to the formation of pseudopods that engulf the bacteria into the host cell. Binding of invasin to &#946;1-integrin is necessary and sufficient to induce phagocytosis of the bacteria even by non-professional phagocytic cells. A second pathway including activation of Rac1, NF-&#954;B, and mitogen-activated protein kinase, leads to production of proinflammatory cytokines [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B32">32</xref>]. After internalization, <italic>E. coli </italic>is taken into the phagosome/lysosome where lysis of the bacterium occurs. The <italic>hly </italic>gene product, along with other bacterial proteins, is release into the lysosomal vesicle. The sulfhydryl-activated hly, also known as listeriolysin O (LLO) is a pore-forming cytolysin capable of binding and perforating phagosomal membranes at low pH [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B33">33</xref>]. The cytoplasmic contents of the bacteria can then escape into the cytosolic compartment of the mammalian cell through the pores generated by LLO. This critical step exports antigen from the <italic>E. coli </italic>into the cytosol where further processing by proteosomes and translocation by TAP into the endoplasmic reticulum lumen occurs for MHC class I presentation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>]. LLO is sufficient for MHC class I presentation of Ag when co-expressed in <italic>E. coli </italic>that are phagocytosed by Antigen Presenting Cells (APC) such as macrophages and dendritic cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>]. Using similar recombinant <italic>E. coli</italic>, others have shown successful delivery of genes and molecules [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B31">31</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>]. In this study, we investigate the potential of <italic>inv-hly </italic>expressing recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>as a vaccine vector for immunization against the intracellular pathogen, <italic>Brucella</italic>.</p></sec><sec sec-type="methods"><title>Methods</title><sec><title>Cell culture</title><p>Cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 medium (Invitrogen) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum (FCS), 4.5% dextrose, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, and antibiotic-antimycotic solution (100 &#956;/ml penicillin G sodium, 100 &#956;g/ml streptomycin sulfate, 0.25 &#956;g/ml amphotericin B). In addition, drugs used for selection were: Blasticidin-S (Invivogen; 10 &#956;g/ml) and G418-sulfate (Alexis Biochemical; 400 &#956;g/ml). Cell lines included: D17 (ATCC CCL-183), TB1 (ATCC CCL-88), J774A.1 (ATCC TIB-67), HeLa S3 (ATCC CCL-2.2), RAW 264.7 (ATCC TIB-71), HEK 293 (ATCC CRL-1573), FLK [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>], and the cytotoxicity target cell line RAW/YFP [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>].</p></sec><sec><title>Mouse care and vaccination</title><p>BALB/c female mice (H-2<sup>d</sup>), 4&#8211;6 wks old were purchased from Jackson Laboratory and injected with 0.1 ml of PBS i.p. one day prior to <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccinations to prevent the mice from succumbing to LPS-induced endotoxic shock from live <italic>E.coli</italic>. Intraperatoneal (i.p.) route of vaccination was chosen to best deliver live <italic>E. coli </italic>vector vaccine to mice based on consistency of results and ease of method. Recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccines were injected i.p. with 2 &#215; 10<sup>7 </sup><italic>E.coli </italic>in PBS. PBS was used for negative controls. For experiments examining primary immune response cytokine profiles, mice were injected with <italic>E. coli </italic>vector vaccine and after 5 h, euthanized and spleens removed. For experiments enumerating antigen-specific CD8<sup>+ </sup>T cells, RAW264.7 macrophages (H-2<sup>d</sup>) expressing GFP (RAW/GFP; [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B45">45</xref>]) was subjected to gamma-irradation (2 KR) and 1 &#215; 10<sup>6 </sup>cells in PBS were vaccinated in mice i.p. following the same protocol as the <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccines. Animals were boosted with the same dose two weeks later. Four weeks after the final boost, animals were euthanized and spleens harvested and processed for CTL assays. Live imaging was performed (IVIS; Caliper Biosciences, Inc.) with animals anesthetized using Isofluorane. IVIS image analysis was performed using Living Image 3.0 software (Caliper Biosciences). Each group of mice consisted of 4 animals. All animal experiments were conducted with approval from the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.</p></sec><sec><title>Plasmid constructs</title><p>The prokaryotic expression vector pGB2&#937;inv-hly [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>](10.05 kb; spectinomycin resistance) was a gift from C. The prokaryotic expression vector pDEST17/BMEII1097 was engineered from pDEST17 (invtrogen) and pDONR201/BMEII1097.</p></sec><sec><title><italic>E. coli </italic>vector vaccines</title><p>All <italic>Escherichia coli </italic>used in these studies were strain DH5&#945;&#8482; (Invitrogen) except for recombinants expressing pDEST17 vectors were we used BL21-AI&#8482; (Invitrogen). BL21-AI&#8482;.</p></table-wrap-foot></table-wrap></sec><sec><title>Invasion and gene delivery assays</title><p>One day prior to cell infection, eukaryotic cell lines were seeded at 2 &#215; 10<sup>5 </sup>cells/well in a six-well plate (or two well chambered coverslips for fluorescent microscopy) in 2 ml/well RPMI with 10% fetal calf serum (Invitrogen) and grown in a humidified CO<sub>2 </sub>incubator at 37&#176;C. <italic>E. coli </italic>were grown overnight in a shaking incubator at 37&#176;C in LB broth (Difco) supplemented with appropriate antibiotic for plasmid selection. The following day, bacteria were counted by 600 nm absorbance spectrometry and added to washed eukaryotic cells in fresh medium without antibiotic at the specified MOI. Bacteria were then centrifuged onto the monolayer at 2 krpm for 5 min at room temperature. Cells were incubated for 90 min, washed and fresh medium added supplemented with 100 &#956;g/ml gentamicin to kill extracellular bacteria. For invasion assays, cells were incubated for an additional 90 min to kill extracellular bacteria, then washed and lysed in 200 &#956;l of 1% triton X-100 for 5 min at room temperature. Finally, 800 &#956;l of LB broth was added to each well and CFU were determined on LB agar plates supplemented with chloramphenicol, the selection drug for the GFP plasmid. For gene delivery assays, cells were incubated then analyzed by fluorescent microscopy. Random fields of cells were counted and scored for fluorescence at indicated times. For IL-12 assays, infected cells were fixed and permeabilized using Cytofix/Cytoperm&#8482; (BD Biosciences) following the manufacturer's protocol. Samples were stained using IL-12 (p40/p70) PE conjugated monoclonal antibody (BD Biosciences) and analyzed by flow cytometry.</p></sec><sec><title>MHC class I pentamer staining and cytokine profiling</title><p>Pooled splenocytes from four mice per immunization group were isolated and density gradient purified (Fico/Lite-LM (Mouse); Atlanta Biologicals). Leukocytes were subjected to non-T cell depletion using a Pan T Cell Isolation Kit and MACS separation (Miltenyi Biotec) following the manufacturer's protocol. Aliquots of 2 &#215; 10<sup>6 </sup>T cells were then used for flow cytometry or cytokine profiling. R-PE labeled Pro5<sup>&#174; </sup>MHC class I pentamers GFP antigen specific for T cell receptors of H-2K<sup>d </sup>HYLSTQSAL were co-stained with FITC labeled rat anti-mouse CD8&#945; and used for flow cytometry along with controls following the manufacturer's suggested protocol (Proimmune). Controls included R-PE labeled rat anti-mouse CD3&#949; (SouthernBiotech), and R-PE and FITC anti-rat IgG2a and anti-rat IgG&#954; (BD Biosciences). Flow cytometry analysis was performed on 3.5 &#215; 10<sup>5 </sup>cells for each immunization group. For cytokine profiling, T cells from immunized and control mice were incubated with gamma-irradiated (2 KR) RAW 264.7 macrophages on 6 well plates with or without the addition of 50 mM GFP peptide (HYLSTQSAL; A&amp;A Labs LLC) for 3 days. Supernatant was harvested, centrifuged to remove cell debris and processed using a Th1/Th2 cytokine kit by cytometric bead array (BD Biosciences). Data acquisition and analysis was performed according to the manufacturer's instructions using flow cytometry.</p></sec><sec><title>Cell mediated cytotoxicity</title><p>Splenocytes from immunized mice were isolated and gradient purified (described above) for use as effector cells. Transduced RAW 264.7 cells expressing GFP or BMEII1097 were cloned by limiting dilution and used as target cells. Cytotoxic effector cells were expanded <italic>in vitro </italic>by growth on confluent 2 KR gamma-irradiated target cells in six-well plates supplemented with 10% T-stim without Con A (BD Biosciences) for three days. Effector cells were then washed and purified through a density gradient. Cells were counted and assayed using a CytoTox 96<sup>&#174; </sup>Non-Radioactive Cytotoxicity kit (Promega) following the manufacturer's protocol with 4 h incubation.</p></sec><sec><title>Flow cytometry</title><p>Acquisition was performed on a FACSCalibur flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) and analyzed using FlowJo 8.7.1 software (Tree Star, Inc).</p></sec><sec><title>Cell transfection and transduction</title><p>Retrovirus-mediated gene transfer was accomplished using the BD Retro-X System (BD Biosciences) following the manufacturer's suggested protocol. Briefly, 100 &#215; 20 mm tissue culture dishes (Falcon) were seeded with the packaging cell line GP2-293 at 70&#8211;90% confluency. GP2-293 cells were co-transfected with 5 &#956;g each of retroviral vector and the envelope glycoprotein expression vector pVSV-G using 15 &#956;l/transfection of Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen) for 3 h in a total volume of 5 ml medium/dish. The ability of <italic>Brucella </italic>to chronically infect humans is related to its ability to avoid a protective Th1 response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>]. Chronic brucellosis patients display a Th2 immune response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B64">64</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B65">65</xref>]. Our objective was to analyze a novel vaccine approach engineering <italic>E.coli </italic>to mimic invasion, immunoregulation, and antigen expression of <italic>Brucella </italic>without the pathogenicity of <italic>Brucella</italic>.</p><p>Recombinant invasive <italic>E. coli </italic>have been used to deliver therapeutically relevant molecules to mouse and human professional and non-professional phagocytic cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B38">38</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>-<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B70">70</xref>]. To date, use of recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>as vectors has mainly been for delivering DNA for genetic vaccination. The ability to easily be engulfed by cells in addition to the absence of plasmid size restrictions make bacteria an interesting vector for gene therapy. In most cases, the recombinant invasive <italic>E. coli </italic>is used to efficiently enter eukaryotic cells where it is destroyed, releasing a eukaryotic vector to the host cell for expression of a therapeutic gene [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B66">66</xref>]. Using this basic approach, we modified <italic>E. coli </italic>to be a live vaccine that would efficiently invade host cells, deliver a eukaryotic gene expression vector to help modulate the proper immune response, and release a large amount of antigen efficiently produced by the prokaryotic expression system. <italic>E. coli </italic>infection would not be long-lived, unlike live <italic>Brucella</italic>, being cleared by the host relatively rapidly. Nevertheless, we found our invasive <italic>E. coli </italic>could survive in host cells up to 72 h after infection compared to control <italic>E. coli </italic>surviving less than 3 h post-infection (data not shown). These data had been confirmed by others [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B41">41</xref>] and suggest an alternate pathway of infection for our recombinant vaccine <italic>E. coli</italic>.</p><p>Bacteria enter cells through a variety of receptors. Host cell receptor(s) for binding and internalization of <italic>Brucella </italic>have not been identified but involve lipid rafts and components of this micro domain [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B71">71</xref>]. The <italic>Brucella </italic>endocytic pathway is distinct from the classical endosome-lysosome pathway in that <italic>Brucella </italic>inhibit phagosome-lysosome fusion [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>]. Further, smooth <italic>Brucella </italic>infection of macrophages is inefficient with only 40&#8211;60% of cells infected <italic>in vitro </italic>after 1 hour [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B72">72</xref>]. In contrast, <italic>E. coli </italic>are efficiently engulfed and processed through the classical endosome-lysosome pathway. However, this leads to rapid destruction of the bacteria and MHC class II presentation of antigen [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B73">73</xref>]. To avoid this destructive pathway, we modified our <italic>E. coli </italic>vector to express invasin from <italic>Yersinia</italic>. This effectively made the vector 80&#8211;100% invasive to not only professional phagocytic cells, but to all cells expressing &#946;1-integrin (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>, Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F1">1</xref>). Further, the endocytic pathway was changed as evidenced that live recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>could be isolated from macrophages after 3 hours (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>) whereas wild-type <italic>E. coli </italic>were destroyed. The pathway seemed to mimic that of <italic>Yersinia </italic>as demonstrated by TEM (Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F2">2</xref>) where the bacterium adheres to a filopodium then is internalized to individual endosomes [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B74">74</xref>]. The result is more cells internalizing the vaccine with potential to express antigen in association with MHC class I. Of great interest was the fact that <italic>in vivo</italic>, the vaccine expressed the reporter gene (<italic>lux</italic>) for a prolonged period at the site of immunization (Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F3">3</xref>) as only viable bacteria continue to express lux. This confirms broad cell-type internalization and probable increased antigen presentation.</p><p>In addition to <italic>invasin </italic>of <italic>Yersinia pseudotuberculosis </italic>our recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector co-expressed the <italic>hly </italic>gene of <italic>Listeria monocytogenes </italic>on the same vector. Modification of the bacterial vaccine to express listeriolysin O (LLO) was to increase MHC I presentation of the expressed antigen delivered by the vaccine. As reported by others [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>], the bacteria would be lysed in the phagosome/lysosome. Through the pore-forming action of LLO, the cytoplasmic contents of our bacterial vaccine vector (including the over expressed antigen) would then escape into the cytosol and thereby be processed by the proteasome. <italic>In vitro</italic>, this LLO-mediated process has been shown to improve MHC I presentation of antigens by macrophages and dendritic cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B34">34</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B35">35</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B44">44</xref>]. <italic>In vivo</italic>, <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccines expressing LLO induced a very strong anti-tumor CTL response [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B43">43</xref>]. We did not confirm improved MHC I presentation of GFP antigen by LLO in studies presented here. However, we did see less YFP gene delivery for mammalian cell expression using recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>without LLO (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T3">3</xref>; data not shown). Furthermore, a recent report demonstrated that the presence of LLO in a recombinant bacterial vaccine suppresses CD4<sup>+ </sup>regulatory T cell (Treg) inhibition of antigen-specific CD8<sup>+ </sup>T cell expansion [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B51">51</xref>]. Primary immune responses activate antigen induced Tregs limiting vaccine efficacy [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B75">75</xref>]. The cytokine profile of the primary immune response to our recombinant <italic>E.coli </italic>vaccine vector revealed a mixed Th1/Th2 profile suggesting a high population of CD4<sup>+ </sup>T cells and possibly Tregs (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T4">4</xref>). However, the secondary immune response to the vaccine shifted to a Th1 dominant cytokine profile (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T5">5</xref>) and subsequent generation of antigen specific CTLs (Figures <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F6">6</xref> and <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F7">7</xref>). It would be interesting to determine whether LLO expression in our vaccine vector affected successful CTL generation and long-term CD8<sup>+ </sup>effector memory T cells.</p><p>Three major regulatory cytokines, TNF&#945;, IL12, and IFN&#947;, were increased in expression relative to controls in both primary immune response (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T4">4</xref>) and secondary immune response (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T5">5</xref>) using our recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector indicating DC maturation and cell mediated immunity. TNF&#945; is a multipotent proinflammatory cytokine fundamental for defense against a variety of intracellular pathogens and is primarily involved in DC maturation [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B76">76</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B77">77</xref>]. DCs infected with <italic>E. coli </italic>clearly show a high capacity to induce the response of na&#239;ve T cells, and TNF&#945; secretion by DCs infected with <italic>Brucella </italic>as well as <italic>E. coli </italic>was directly implicated in the maturation of these cells, since TNF&#945; blocking antibodies cause a strong maturation decrease [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B61">61</xref>]. Invasive <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine, similar to <italic>Brucella</italic>, initiates the first phase of a T cell dependent adaptive immune response inducing the secretion of IL12 from APCs. IL12 then potently stimulates IFN&#947; production by activated na&#239;ve T cells [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B78">78</xref>]. Both IL12 and IFN&#947; are considered essential for protection against brucellosis [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B10">10</xref>]. Our inclusion of a murine IL12 mammalian expression plasmid in the <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector results in a high level of IL12 expression in the infected cell (Figure <xref ref-type="fig" rid="F4">4</xref>). This IL12 rich microenvironment surrounding the host antigen presenting cell (professional or non-professional; Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T2">2</xref>) may be involved in supporting the Th1 profile of the secondary immune response as indicated by the high levels of TNF&#945; and IFN&#947; (Table <xref ref-type="table" rid="T5">5</xref>). The resulting maturation of DCs and CD8<sup>+ </sup>T cells would lead to cell mediated immunity.</p><p>The initial host defense to infection is stimulated by pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPS) common to different groups of pathogens. The toll-like receptor (TLR) family has emerged as a major group of signaling receptors for PAMPs [<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B79">79</xref>,<xref ref-type="bibr" rid="B80">80</xref>]. Classical LPS activates macrophages and DCs through binding the TLR-4. Nevertheless, the respective effects of APC stimulation by isolated LPS or living bacteria are clearly distinct, even when the bacteria carry a highly active LPS like <italic>E. coli</italic>; the bacteria probably bind not only to TLR-4 but also to a set of various receptors. Our studies demonstrate a notable Th1, specific CTL response to antigen delivered by the invasive, recombinant <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector. However, the highly active LPS and PAMPS of <italic>E. coli </italic>may over stimulate the immune response to the vector. Engineering the <italic>E. coli </italic>genome to make the organism less stimulatory to the host would greatly improve the usefulness of this novel vaccine approach.</p></sec><sec><title>Conclusion</title><p>We began our studies with the goal of developing a live vaccine vector using an organism (<italic>E. coli</italic>) that was not pathogenic to the host and engineering it to mimic the bacterial pathogen <italic>Brucella </italic>intracellular infection to stimulate a protective cellular immune response. Our data show that this vaccine vector could efficiently infect cells of multiple tissues. These vaccine infected cells acting as antigen presenting cells can stimulate a cellular immune response with Th1 cytokine profile and specific CTLs. Studies are now in progress to determine whether this recombinant invasive <italic>E. coli </italic>vaccine vector, expressing pools of immunodominant <italic>Brucella </italic>antigens, would be sufficient to induce a protective immune response in mice. Our studies show that this novel vaccine could be applied to any disease where cellular immunity is required.</p></sec><sec><title>Competing interests</title><p>The authors declare that they have no competing interests.</p></sec><sec><title>Authors' contributions</title><p>JH, MD, and DD participated in mouse vaccination studies.
