abortus</italic>, which confers safe and effective protection of cattle from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> infection, was originally generated via serial passage of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 to generate spontaneous, attenuating mutations. While some of these mutations have been previously characterized, such as an insertional mutation in the <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> gene that contributes to the rough phenotype of the strain, a comprehensive annotation of genetic differences between RB51 and <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 genomes has not yet been published. Here, the whole genome sequence of the RB51 vaccine strain is compared against two available 2308 parent sequences, with all observed single nucleotide polymorphisms, insertions, and deletions presented. Mutations of interest for future characterization in vaccine development, such as mutations in <italic toggle="yes">eipA</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">narJ</italic> genes in RB51, were identified. Additionally, protein homology modeling was utilized to provide <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> support for the hypothesis that the RB51 <italic toggle="yes">capD</italic> mutation is the second contributing mutation to the rough phenotype of RB51, likely explaining the inability of <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic>-complemented strains of RB51 to revert to a smooth phenotype.</p></abstract><kwd-group><kwd>Brucella</kwd><kwd>RB51</kwd><kwd><italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic></kwd><kwd>Brucellosis</kwd><kwd>vaccine sequence</kwd></kwd-group><counts><fig-count a chronic and debilitating bacterial disease in humans, is spread zoonotically from infected ruminant species like goats and cattle, in which the disease causes reproductive dysfunction and abortion in pregnant animals (reviewed by <xref rid="bib6" ref-type="bibr">de Figueiredo <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2015</xref>). Multiple species of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> are infectious to humans; <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> is often found in cattle, <italic toggle="yes">Brucella suis</italic> in pigs and feral swine, and <italic toggle="yes">Brucella melitensis</italic> in sheep and goats.</p><p>Vaccination of young animals is an important strategy for protection of both animals and humans against infection with <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species. Multiple live, attenuated vaccines have been developed for protection of livestock against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic>, including RB51 (<xref rid="bib28" ref-type="bibr">Schurig <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1991</xref>; <xref rid="bib4" ref-type="bibr">Cheville <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1993</xref>), used in the U.S. in cattle herds. However, development of vaccines against <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species that are both safe and effective is made challenging by the intracellular nature of the bacterium.</p><p>Investigation of the characteristics of the RB51 <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> vaccine strain is of importance for development of future <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> vaccines, due to its ability to elicit a strong protective, cell-based immune response without causing disease in livestock (reviewed by <xref rid="bib29" ref-type="bibr">Schurig <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2002</xref>). While <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> is highly virulent in humans and causes zoonotic transmission and persistent infection (reviewed by <xref rid="bib3" ref-type="bibr">Atluri <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2011</xref>), no vaccines against <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> are currently approved in the U.S. as safe and effective for use in humans. RB51 was derived from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> strain 2308 by serial passage, and exhibits a rough phenotype in its cell envelope (<xref rid="bib28" ref-type="bibr">Schurig <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1991</xref>). It is well-known that an IS711 element-caused disruption is present in the <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> gene of RB51; <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> encodes a glycosyltransferase necessary for lipopolysaccharide (LPS) biosynthesis, and this disruption is at least partially responsible for the rough morphology of RB51 (<xref rid="bib31" ref-type="bibr">Vemulapalli <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1999</xref>). However, complementation of the defective gene with the wild-type <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> allele did not restore a smooth phenotype or restore virulence to RB51, so additional factors appear to contribute to the strain phenotype (<xref rid="bib32" ref-type="bibr">Vemulapalli <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2000</xref>). Not all rough strains of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> confer the protective properties of RB51 (<italic toggle="yes">i.e.</italic>, <xref rid="bib7" ref-type="bibr">Elzer <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1998</xref>), indicating that there are genetic changes specific to RB51 that contribute to its beneficial vaccine properties.</p><p>While sequencing of the RB51 genome has been attempted, previous sequences were either not closed to completion, or are disparate from expected allele sequences at the <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> gene locus (<xref rid="bib19" ref-type="bibr">Ma <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2014</xref>; Table S1; Figure S1). Additionally, a genome-wide (complete) comparison of RB51 to the parent <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308 strain has not yet been published. Therefore, to provide a complete picture of genetic changes that may contribute to RB51 properties, we present here a sequence of the RB51 vaccine strain closed to completion at the National Animal Disease Center, assess DNA mutations relative to the 2308 genome sequence, and compare findings to previous studies. Using protein modeling, we then predict the impact of mutations of particular interest for use in future vaccine development.</p><sec sec-type="materials|methods" id="s1"><title>Materials &amp; Methods</title><sec id="s2"><title>DNA isolation and purification</title><p>Genomic DNA was extracted as previously described (<xref rid="bib9" ref-type="bibr">Halling <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2005</xref>) from approximately 1 X 10<sup>10</sup> methanol-killed <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> RB51 (sub-strain ARS-1). After the second precipitation, the spooled DNA was washed for several days in periodic changes of 80% ethanol. The purified DNA was stored in 80% ethanol. As needed, precipitated flakes of DNA were removed and re-solubilized in 10 mM sodium hydroxide. When fully rehydrated, the pH was adjusted to 7.5 in the presence of 10 mM HEPES buffer, and stored at 4&#176;.</p></sec><sec id="s3"><title>DNA sequencing</title><p>The RB51 genomic DNA was sequenced by two methods. The first pass was done by Sanger sequencing of purified plasmid DNA from a shotgun library created from the RB51 genomic DNA. The DNA was sequenced on an ABI-PRISM DNA Sequencer, Model 3700, following treatment of the plasmid DNA with BigDye terminator nucleotides (ABI_PRISM BigDye Terminator Cycle Sequencing Kit ver. 3.0; Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA) as directed by the manufacturer. A second round of sequencing was done by pyrosequencing with the high-throughput Roche GS FLX Sequencing System per manufacturer&#8217;s directions (454 Life Sciences- A Roche company, Branford, CT, USA). Briefly, the RB51 purified DNA was sheared by nebulization to a range of approximately 500 to 700 base pairs. A single-strand template (sstDNA) shotgun library was created from the sheared fragments as directed in the GS FLX Standard Library Preparation Kit (Roche Applied Science, Indianapolis, IN, USA). DNA concentrations were measured with an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA, USA). The size range of the sheared DNA was analyzed on a DNA-7500 lab chip, while the concentration of the sstDNA library was determined on the Pico 6000 LabChip. The RB51-sstDNA library was amplified by emulsion-based clonal amplification (emPCR) as directed by the Shotgun GS FLX Standard emPCR kit (Roche Applied Science) manual. The DNA-bound beads were loaded onto a Pico Titer plate and sequenced with the GS LR70 Sequencing Kit per manufacturer&#8217;s instructions.</p></sec><sec id="s4"><title>Sequence assembly and annotation</title><p>Image processing of the raw data and signal processing (base calling) were performed using the Roche GS Run Processor pipeline according to the manufacturer&#8217;s recommendations. Assembly of sequence data into contigs was completed with Phred/Phrap and Consed software programs. Phred was used for base calling of the Sanger sequence reads, while Phrap was used to assemble both the Sanger and the pyrosequencing data. Consed was used for the graphical assembly and final editing. The annotated sequences were prepared for submission to GenBank with the IMG/ER software program (Joint Genomic Initiative; <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://img.jgi.doe.gov/er">http://img.jgi.doe.gov/er</ext-link>; <xref rid="bib21" ref-type="bibr">Markowitz <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2009</xref>). As needed, the annotated sequence was viewed with the Artemis sequence viewer and annotation tool (Genome Research Limited; <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://www.sanger.ac.uk/Software/Artemis">http://www.sanger.ac.uk/Software/Artemis</ext-link>; <xref rid="bib22" ref-type="bibr">Mural 2000</xref>). Results were compared manually with ACT, a DNA comparison viewer, part of the Artemis package. Genes were called using Glimmer (<xref rid="bib25" ref-type="bibr">Salzberg <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1998</xref>), and compared to homologs using BLAST (<xref rid="bib2" ref-type="bibr">Altschul <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1990</xref>). Single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were identified with MUMmer (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://www.tigr.org/software/mummer">http://www.tigr.org/software/mummer</ext-link>; <xref rid="bib16" ref-type="bibr">Kurtz <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2004</xref>) and sequence alignment with Mauve (<ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="http://gel.ahabs.wisc.edu/mauve">http://gel.ahabs.wisc.edu/mauve</ext-link>; <xref rid="bib5" ref-type="bibr">Darling <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2004</xref>).</p></sec><sec id="s5"><title>Protein modeling</title><p>Computational modeling of the C-terminal domain of CapD (residues 261 - 585) was performed using the program I-TASSER (<xref rid="bib24" ref-type="bibr">Roy <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2010</xref>; <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://zhanglab.ccmb.med.umich.edu/I-TASSER/">https://zhanglab.ccmb.med.umich.edu/I-TASSER/</ext-link>). I-TASSER has been the top-ranked server in the last few Critical Assessment of Techniques for Protein Structure Prediction (CASP 7-10) competitions for homology modeling and threading. The C-terminal domain structure of PgIF (PDB ID: 5BJU), which had a sequence identity of 41% with the C-terminal domain of CapD, was used as the template. The five structural models normally returned by the program was collapsed to one, indicating the generation of a high-quality model. This is confirmed by the high C-score (1.23) for the model (the C-score describes the quality of a predicted model, and ranges between -5 and 2). When the model was superposed with the C-terminal domain of PgIF, the only significant deviations occurred in two loop regions located far away from the active site of PgIF (Figure S2). PDB files were visualized using PyMol software.</p></sec><sec sec-type="data-availability" id="s6"><title>Data availability</title><p>The genome information is publicly available on NCBI GenBank (&#8220;whole genome sequence of <italic toggle="yes">Brucella abortus</italic> RB51, ARS-1 isolate&#8221;) under the BioProject accession PRJNA573988 and BioSample accession SAMN12837785. Strains are available upon request to the USDA ARS National Animal Disease Center. Interestingly, we did not identify any of the non-synonymous SNPs described here in the <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="pmc:entrez-nucleotide" xlink:href="NZ_AQIE00000000.1">NZ_AQIE00000000.1</ext-link> sequence. Alignment of the Broad RB51 genome with our RB51 genome indicated that the expected <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> insertion, which is a defining characteristic of RB51, is not present in the Broad sequence (Figure S1).</p><p>Using gene locations on the BioCyc browser, we examined the location of each of the intergenic mutations, to consider the potential for each of these mutations to influence gene expression. Potential implications of the intergenic mutations are limited; mutations in RB51 observed within 50 bp of the upstream end of a predicted gene are indicated in <xref rid="t2" ref-type="table">Tables 2</xref> and <xref rid="t3" ref-type="table">3</xref> (see Annotation column).</p><p>Notably, a previous study characterized the sequences of LPS synthesis genes in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> RB51 in an attempt to understand the basis of the rough phenotype. <xref rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">Adone <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> (2011)</xref> amplified 21 genes known to be involved in LPS synthesis and compared their sequences to the <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> 16M genome (Table S3). However, since RB51 is derived from <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308, the 2308 parent strain is the proper point of comparison. Therefore, we analyzed the RB51 mutations identified by <xref rid="bib1" ref-type="bibr">Adone <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> (2011)</xref> to compare them to the 2308 reference sequence. Consistent with our genome sequencing, only <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> (insertion) and <italic toggle="yes">wbkD</italic> (corresponds to <italic toggle="yes">capD</italic> in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic>; E559K mutation) carry mutations in RB51 as compared to <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> 2308; all other mutations identified by Adone <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> (Table S3) reflect sequence variation between <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic>.</p></sec><sec id="s9"><title>In silico evidence supports the importance of the CapD mutation to the rough phenotype of RB51</title><p>Based on its predicted function in bacterial envelope biosynthesis, the CapD mutation in RB51, relative to the 2308 sequence, is a promising candidate to explain the rough phenotype of the strain in conjunction with the <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> gene disruption. When the full-length CapD sequence (622 residues) was subjected to protein-protein BLAST against the Protein Data Bank (PDB) database, the top hit was the C-terminal domain of PgIF (E-value of 3e-82 for an alignment spanning 90% of residues of the PgIF C-terminal domain; Figure S2). PgIF is a sugar dehydratase in <italic toggle="yes">Campylobacter jejuni</italic>, for which the prokaryotic N-linked glycosylation pathway has been characterized in detail. More than sixty proteins in <italic toggle="yes">C. jejuni</italic> are glycosylated by a conserved heptasaccharide (<xref rid="bib30" ref-type="bibr">Szymanski <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1999</xref>; <xref rid="bib27" ref-type="bibr">Scott <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2011</xref>) that includes QuiNAc4NAc (2,4-diacetamido-2,4,6-trideoxy-&#945; -D -glucopyranose) as one of its components. PgIF catalyzes the first step in the synthesis of QuiNAc4NAc, the dehydration of UDP-GlcNAc to UDP-2-acetamido-2,6-dideoxy-&#945;-D-xylo-hexos-4-ulose. The enzymes involved in such 4,6-dehydratase events are known to belong to the NAD(H)- or NADP(H)-dependent short-chain dehydrogenase/reductase (SDR) superfamily (<xref rid="bib12" ref-type="bibr">Kallberg <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2002a</xref>; <xref rid="bib13" ref-type="bibr">Kallberg <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2002b</xref>; <xref rid="bib15" ref-type="bibr">Kavanagh <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2008</xref>; <xref rid="bib14" ref-type="bibr">Kallberg <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2010</xref>). In these enzymes, the dehydration step is flanked by dehydrogenation/ hydrogenation steps involving NAD(P).</p><p>PglF is a NAD<sup>+</sup>-dependent membrane associated protein (590 residues) composed of three domains: an N-terminal motif containing four transmembrane regions, a linker section of unknown function, and a C-terminal catalytic domain (<xref rid="bib26" ref-type="bibr">Schoenhofen <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2006</xref>). The structure of the C-terminal domain of PglF (Ala 244 to Lys 587) has been solved by x-ray crystallography (Protein Data Bank ID 5BJU; <xref rid="bib23" ref-type="bibr">Riegert <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2017</xref>). A BLAST alignment performed between the full-length sequences of CapD and PgIF (Figure S4) yields a highly significant E-value (7e-97), indicating that the homology between CapD and PgIF extends to the entire protein. Moreover, as in PgIF, CapD is also predicted to contain four transmembrane helices in the N-terminal region (data not shown).</p><p>Therefore, we utilized the PglF structure to model the impact of the CapD mutation, using the crystal structure of the C-terminal domain of PglF as a template to construct a model of the C-terminal domain (residues 261 &#8211; 585) of CapD. In the structural superposition (Figure S3), the key active site residues of PgIF superposed with residues of CapD (in parentheses) as follows: 395 Thr (413 Ser), 396 Asp (414 Asp), 397 Lys (415 Lys). Accordingly, two of the active site residues are strictly conserved in CapD, while the third is a conservative Thr to Ser substitution. Thr 395 Ser PgIF mutants have been shown to be fully active (<xref rid="bib23" ref-type="bibr">Riegert <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2017</xref>). Thus, the &#8220;CapD-C-term&#8221; model provides strong evidence that CapD acts as a sugar 2,4-dehydratase. PgIF belongs to a subclass of the SDR superfamily in which the Tyr in the YXXXK signature sequence (Kallberg <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2002) has been replaced by a Met. The structural alignment also suggests that CapD belongs to this same subclass (Met 423 of CapD superposes with Met 405 of PgIF).</p><p>Structural superposition of the CapD-C-term model with the C-terminal domain of PgIF (Figure S2) shows Glu 559 of CapD superposing with Glu 526 of PgIF. Importantly, Glu 526 is part of the binding pocket for one of the substrates of PgIF, uridine-5&#8242;-diphosphate (UDP); Glu 526 specifically forms hydrogen bonding interactions with the ribose moiety of UDP (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1A</xref>). In addition, Glu 526 makes salt bridge-type interactions with two arginine residues, stabilizing the binding pocket for UDP. The model for the C-terminal domain of CapD (CapD-C-term) does not contain UDP, but the close superposition of the C-terminal domains suggests that Glu 559 of CapD could make similar hydrogen bonding interactions with a putative UDP substrate. Further, in CapD-C-term, Glu 559 makes stabilizing salt bridge interactions with two Arg residues (<xref ref-type="fig" rid="fig1">Figure 1B</xref>) similar to that observed for Glu 257 in PgIF (as discussed above).</p><fig id="fig1" fig-type="figure" orientation="portrait" position="float"><label>Figure 1</label><caption><p>Glu 559 of CapD is predicted to be directly linked to enzyme activity. (A). In the CapD homolog PgIF (PDB ID 5BJU), the corresponding glutamate, Glu 526, makes hydrogen bonding interactions with the C-2 hydroxyl group of the ribose moiety in UDP. (B). In the model for the C-terminal domain of CapD (CapD-C-term), Glu 559 participates in salt bridge type interactions with Arg 488 and Arg 556 (similar interactions are seen in PgIF). The Glu 559 Lys mutation in strain RB51 would be predicted to create repulsive interactions between positively charged side chains, disrupting the putative binding site for UDP.</p></caption><graphic xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" position="float" orientation="portrait" xlink:href="1175f1.jpg"/></fig><p>Finally, additional evidence for the functional importance of Glu 559 of CapD comes from mutational studies previously performed on the related SDR superfamily member CapE of <italic toggle="yes">S. aureus</italic>. The structural superposition between CapD-C-term and the crystal structure of CapE (Figure S5) indicates that Glu 257 of CapE superposes with Glu 559 of CapD. Mutating Glu 257 of CapE to Ala leads to a loss of activity (<xref rid="bib20" ref-type="bibr">Miyafusa <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2013</xref>), suggesting that the Glu 559 Lys mutation observed in RB51 CapD would also lead to a similar loss of activity, and impaired glycosylation of proteins in the RB51 strain.</p></sec></sec><sec sec-type="discussion" id="s10"><title>Discussion</title><p>The presentation of a complete RB51 genome comparison to two published 2308 sequences defines a set of mutations for future genetic analysis of the properties of <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> and/or <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains modified at one or more of these genetic locations. Our data not only confirm the presence of the CapD mutation in RB51, but utilize <italic toggle="yes">in silico</italic> modeling to provide evidence supporting the hypothesis that this mutation is the second key RB51 mutation contributing to a rough strain phenotype. Previous studies have demonstrated that <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> strains carrying a <italic toggle="yes">wboA</italic> mutation are attenuated and have increased vaccination efficacy (<xref rid="bib17" ref-type="bibr">Li <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2015</xref>); therefore, the <italic toggle="yes">Brucella capD</italic> gene is a candidate for future mutation in vaccine development.</p><p>Additionally, the other mutations identified in RB51 chromosome II serve as the basis for future wet lab studies of the influence of these genes on <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> phenotypes. Recently, <xref rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">Herrou <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> (2019)</xref> reported on the first structural and functional characterization of the protein product of BAB1_1186, which they have named EipA; EipA is conserved in <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> and is proposed to be involved in cell envelope homeostasis. Deletion of <italic toggle="yes">eipA</italic> in <italic toggle="yes">B. abortus</italic> revealed that the protein is not required for initial spleen colonization in mice, but is involved in persistence in the spleen (<xref rid="bib11" ref-type="bibr">Herrou <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 2019</xref>). BLAST analysis reveals that the L &#8594; M mutation in EipA observed in RB51 is not present in other <italic toggle="yes">Brucella</italic> species; this biochemical change has the potential to influence function as a destabilizing mutation (<xref rid="bib18" ref-type="bibr">Lipscomb <italic toggle="yes">et al.</italic> 1998</xref>), and is a candidate for future investigation. Finally, the disruption of <italic toggle="yes">narJ</italic> is of phenotypic interest, due to the potential to compromise survival of the vaccine strain in macrophages. Introduction of the RB51 complement of genetic mutations, or a subset of these, into <italic toggle="yes">B. melitensis</italic> could provide the basis for a new attenuated vaccine candidate in this closely related, but challenging, species.</p></sec></body><back><fn-group><fn id="fn1"><p>Supplemental material available at figshare: <ext-link xmlns:xlink="http://www.w3.org/1999/xlink" ext-link-type="uri" xlink:href="https://doi.org/10.25387/g3.11864358">https://doi.org/10.25387/g3.11864358</ext-link>.</p></fn><fn id="fn2"><p>Communicating editor: Y.
